Typefaces Designed

I've spent the last twelve years learning how to get out of the way. Good design, I think, is mostly about knowing what to leave out—which typeface to choose, which details matter, and which clever ideas to quietly discard. Typography taught me that. It demands restraint. You can't trick your way to a comfortable reading experience.

Most of my work is for publications, cultural institutions, and small studios that care about how things feel. I've set books, designed identities, and built websites where the typography does most of the talking. I prefer projects that value clarity over novelty, though I'll admit I'm drawn to a well-placed flourish when the moment's right.

I believe that a well-set page is generous to its reader. It doesn't demand attention—it invites it. That's what I'm after, whether I'm working on a poster or a hundred-page report.

Approach

I work best with clients who trust the process and understand that good design takes time. I start most projects by reading—not just the content I'll be designing with, but around it. What's the context? Who's the audience? What are they used to, and what might surprise them in a good way?

From there, I make options. Not fifty of them, but three or four real directions. We talk through the choices together, and I refine from there. I don't believe in "revisions" as a separate phase—design is iterative by nature, and the best work emerges from ongoing conversation.
