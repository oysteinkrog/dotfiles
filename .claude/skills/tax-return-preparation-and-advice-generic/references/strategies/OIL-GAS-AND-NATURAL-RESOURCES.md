# OIL, GAS, AND NATURAL RESOURCES: TAX STRATEGIES

## Overview

Oil and gas investments offer some of the most aggressive tax deductions available in
the Internal Revenue Code. The combination of intangible drilling cost deductions,
depletion allowances, and the working interest exception from passive activity rules
makes this sector uniquely attractive for high-income taxpayers seeking large first-year
deductions. However, these investments carry substantial economic risk — most drilling
ventures are unprofitable — and the tax benefits must be weighed against the real
possibility of total economic loss.

---

## Intangible Drilling Costs (IDCs) — Section 263(c)

IDCs represent the non-salvageable costs of drilling a well. For domestic wells, an
operator (or working interest holder) may elect to DEDUCT 100% of IDCs in the year
incurred, rather than capitalizing and amortizing them.

**What qualifies as IDC:**
- Labor costs for drilling, testing, and completing the well
- Fuel and power for drilling operations
- Drilling mud, chemicals, and other materials consumed in drilling
- Ground clearing and preparation
- Surveys and geological work related to drilling
- Rig rental and tool rental
- Hauling and transportation of drilling equipment
- Well stimulation (fracturing, acidizing)

**What does NOT qualify (tangible costs):**
- Wellhead equipment, casing, and tubing that has salvage value
- Pumping equipment
- Storage tanks
- Pipelines
- Other equipment with a useful life that can be depreciated

**IDCs typically represent 60-80% of total well cost.** In a $100,000 drilling investment,
$60,000-$80,000 may be immediately deductible as IDCs.

**Election:** The deduction is elective. The taxpayer may choose to capitalize IDCs and
amortize them over 60 months under Section 59(e), which may be preferable for AMT
purposes (see below).

**Domestic wells only:** The 100% IDC deduction applies only to wells drilled in the
United States. Foreign IDCs must be capitalized and amortized over 10 years (or
deducted as part of the foreign tax credit computation).

**Dry hole costs:** If the well is a dry hole (non-productive), all costs — both IDCs
and tangible costs — are deductible as an ordinary loss in the year the well is
abandoned.

## Tangible Drilling Costs

Equipment and materials with salvage value must be capitalized and depreciated:

| Asset | MACRS Recovery Period |
|-------|----------------------|
| Drilling equipment | 7-year |
| Wellhead equipment | 7-year |
| Gathering pipelines (upstream) | 7-year |
| Transmission pipelines | 15-year |
| Storage tanks | 7-year |
| Natural gas processing plant | 7-year |
| Office buildings | 39-year (nonresidential) |

**Bonus depreciation** (80% in 2025, declining 20% per year) and **Section 179** apply
to qualifying tangible drilling equipment, providing additional first-year deduction
acceleration.

## Depletion Allowances

Depletion is the oil and gas equivalent of depreciation — it accounts for the exhaustion
of a natural resource. Two methods are available:

### Cost Depletion

Analogous to units-of-production depreciation:

**Formula:**
Depletion per unit = (Adjusted basis of the property) / (Estimated total recoverable units)
Annual depletion = Depletion per unit x Units extracted during the year

**Example:**
- Basis in mineral interest: $200,000
- Estimated recoverable barrels: 100,000
- Barrels extracted this year: 10,000
- Cost depletion: ($200,000 / 100,000) x 10,000 = $20,000

**Cost depletion reduces basis.** When basis reaches zero, no further cost depletion
is available.

### Percentage Depletion — Section 613 / Section 613A

Percentage depletion is a statutory deduction equal to a fixed percentage of gross
income from the property, regardless of the taxpayer's actual basis in the property.

**Oil and gas percentage depletion rate: 15% of gross income from the property**
(Section 613A(c)(1))

**Critical limitation for oil and gas:** Percentage depletion for oil and gas is
available ONLY to:
- Independent producers (not integrated oil companies)
- Royalty owners
- Limited to average daily production of 1,000 barrels of oil or 6,000 MCF of natural gas
  (Section 613A(c)(3))

**Section 613A(d)(1) — Net income limitation:** Percentage depletion cannot exceed 100%
of the net income from the property (computed without the depletion deduction). However,
for marginal wells, this limit is suspended.

**Section 613A(d)(2) — 65% of taxable income limitation:** Total percentage depletion
from all properties cannot exceed 65% of the taxpayer's taxable income (computed without
the depletion deduction, NOL deduction, and capital loss carryback).

### Percentage Depletion Exceeding Basis

**This is the remarkable feature of percentage depletion:** Once the taxpayer's adjusted
basis in the property reaches zero, cost depletion would stop — but percentage depletion
continues. The taxpayer can take deductions that exceed their total investment in the
property.

**Example:**
- Investment in mineral rights: $50,000 (basis)
- Over years 1-5: $50,000 of cost or percentage depletion taken (basis now $0)
- Year 6: property produces $40,000 gross income
- Percentage depletion: $40,000 x 15% = $6,000
- This $6,000 deduction has NO remaining basis to support it — pure tax benefit beyond
  the original investment
- This can continue for the remaining productive life of the well (potentially decades)

**Basis reduction rule:** Percentage depletion reduces basis, but not below zero. Once
basis is zero, percentage depletion is still allowable but does not further reduce basis
(which is already zero).

### Depletion for Other Minerals

| Mineral | Percentage Rate |
|---------|----------------|
| Oil and gas | 15% |
| Coal and lignite | 10% |
| Gold, silver, copper, iron ore | 15% |
| Gravel, sand, stone | 5% |
| Sulfur | 22% |
| Uranium | 22% |
| All other minerals | 14% |

## Working Interest Exception — Section 469(c)(3)

**A working interest in an oil or gas property is NOT treated as a passive activity,
regardless of whether the taxpayer materially participates.**

This is the ONLY blanket exception to the passive activity rules for a specific type
of investment. It means:

- Losses from oil and gas working interests can offset wages, salaries, portfolio
  income, and other non-passive income
- No material participation test required
- The IDC deduction and depletion can offset the taxpayer's active income

**Critical requirement:** The working interest must be held directly (as an individual
or through an entity that does not limit the taxpayer's liability). If held through
a **limited partnership**, the exception does NOT apply because the taxpayer's liability
is limited (Section 469(c)(3)(B)).

**Practical structure:** To use the working interest exception, hold the interest:
- Directly as an individual
- Through a general partnership
- Through an LLC (most courts and the IRS have treated LLC working interests as qualifying,
  since the taxpayer is not a limited partner — but this is not entirely settled)

**NOT qualifying:** Limited partnership interest in a drilling program. Such interests
ARE passive activities subject to the standard passive loss rules.

## AMT Considerations for Oil and Gas

### IDC Preference Item

The excess of IDC deductions over the amount that would have been allowed if IDCs were
capitalized and amortized over 10 years is an AMT preference item (Section 57(a)(2)).

**Exception (Section 57(a)(2)(E)):** The IDC preference does not apply if total IDC
deductions do not exceed 65% of the taxpayer's net income from oil, gas, and
geothermal properties.

### Section 59(e) Election: 60-Month Amortization

As an alternative to the immediate IDC deduction, the taxpayer may elect under Section
59(e) to amortize IDCs over 60 months (beginning with the month the costs are paid or
incurred).

**When to elect Section 59(e):**
- Taxpayer is already in or near AMT
- The immediate IDC deduction would create a large AMT preference item
- Spreading the deduction over 60 months avoids the AMT preference entirely
- The regular tax benefit of the deduction may be similar or better when AMT is avoided

**The election is made on a well-by-well basis** — the taxpayer can elect 59(e) for
some wells and take the immediate deduction for others.

## At-Risk Rules — Section 465

Oil and gas investments are subject to the at-risk rules. The taxpayer's deductions
are limited to the amount "at risk," which includes:

- Cash invested
- Adjusted basis of property contributed
- Amounts borrowed for which the taxpayer is personally liable (recourse debt)
- Amounts borrowed secured by property NOT used in the activity (qualified nonrecourse
  financing for real estate does NOT apply to oil and gas)

**Nonrecourse financing does NOT increase at-risk amount for oil and gas** (unlike real
estate). This limits the ability to leverage deductions beyond the actual capital invested.

## Tax Credits for Non-Conventional Fuels

### Section 45K (formerly Section 29) — Non-Conventional Source Credit

A production credit for fuels produced from non-conventional sources, including:
- Oil from shale and tar sands
- Gas produced from geopressured brine, Devonian shale, coal seams, tight formations,
  or biomass
- Synthetic fuels from coal (including lignite)

**Note:** This credit has expired for most new facilities (generally for wells drilled
after 2005 for some categories), but may apply to qualifying wells placed in service
during the credit period. Verify current availability.

### Section 45Q — Carbon Capture and Sequestration Credit

For oil and gas operations that capture CO2:
- $85 per metric ton for geological storage
- $60 per metric ton for enhanced oil recovery (EOR) or other qualified uses
- Available for facilities beginning construction before January 1, 2033 (IRA extension)

## Master Limited Partnerships (MLPs)

MLPs are publicly traded partnerships that typically operate in the midstream oil and gas
sector (pipelines, storage, processing, transportation).

### Tax Characteristics
- **K-1 reporting:** Income and deductions reported on Schedule K-1, not 1099
- **Tax-deferred distributions:** Most MLP distributions are return of capital
  (reducing basis) rather than taxable income, making the effective current tax rate
  very low (often 0-5% on distributions)
- **Depreciation and depletion pass-through:** Substantial cost recovery deductions
  flow through to unit holders, sheltering distributions
- **Passive income/loss:** MLP income is typically passive (unless the holder has a
  working interest or materially participates). Passive losses can only offset passive
  income.

### Basis Tracking Complexity
- Initial basis = purchase price
- Annually adjusted for: allocable income (+), allocable deductions (-), distributions (-),
  contributions (+)
- If basis reaches zero, additional distributions are taxable as capital gain
  (Section 731)
- Cumulative basis tracking over decades of ownership is extremely complex — maintain
  meticulous records

### Sale of MLP Units — Section 751 "Hot Assets"
When MLP units are sold, gain must be bifurcated:
1. **Ordinary income portion:** Attributable to the MLP's "hot assets" — unrealized
   receivables and substantially appreciated inventory (Section 751(a))
2. **Capital gain portion:** Remainder of the gain

This means MLP sales often generate a mix of ordinary income and capital gain, which is
reported on both Schedule D and Form 4797. The K-1 from the year of sale will include
the Section 751 allocation.

### MLPs in IRAs — UBTI Concern
MLP income in an IRA or other tax-exempt account may generate Unrelated Business Taxable
Income (UBTI) under Section 512. If UBTI exceeds $1,000 in a year, the IRA must file
Form 990-T and pay UBIT.

**Practical impact:** Holding MLPs in IRAs is generally discouraged unless the expected
UBTI is minimal or the investor is comfortable with the IRA paying UBIT.

## Mineral Rights and Royalty Income

### Royalty Income Reporting
- Mineral rights royalties are reported on **Schedule E, Part I** (not Schedule C,
  unless the taxpayer is in the business of managing mineral rights)
- Subject to Net Investment Income Tax (3.8%) if taxpayer's MAGI exceeds $250,000 (MFJ)
  or $200,000 (Single)
- Depletion deduction available (cost or percentage, taxpayer takes the larger each year)

### State Severance Taxes
Most oil/gas-producing states impose severance taxes on production:

| State | Oil Severance Rate | Gas Severance Rate |
|-------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Texas | 4.6% | 7.5% |
| North Dakota | 5.0% + 5.0% extraction | 0.2 cents/MCF |
| Oklahoma | 2-7% (varies) | 2-7% (varies) |
| Wyoming | 6.0% | 6.0% |
| New Mexico | 3.75% + conservation tax | 3.75% + conservation tax |
| Alaska | 35% (net, with credits) | varies |

Severance taxes are deductible on the federal return as a business expense or as a
deduction against royalty income.

### Leasing Mineral Rights
- **Bonus payment (lease bonus):** Upfront payment for signing the lease. Ordinary income
  in the year received. Some courts have allowed this to be treated as advance royalty
  eligible for depletion (fact-specific).
- **Delay rental:** Annual payment to maintain the lease during the primary term before
  production begins. Ordinary income.
- **Royalty income:** Percentage of production value (typically 12.5% to 25%). Subject to
  depletion deduction.
- **Shut-in royalty:** Payment when the well is capable of producing but is shut in
  (not producing). Ordinary income.

## Risk Factors and Due Diligence

### Economic Risks
- **Dry hole risk:** 60-80% of exploratory wells are non-productive (industry average
  varies by formation and operator). Development wells in proven formations have higher
  success rates (70-90%).
- **Commodity price risk:** Even a productive well may be uneconomic if oil/gas prices
  decline below the well's operating cost.
- **Decline curve:** Oil and gas production declines over time (typically 30-50% annual
  decline in tight shale formations in year 1). Revenue front-loads while operating
  costs continue.
- **Operating cost escalation:** Lifting costs, workover expenses, regulatory compliance,
  and plug/abandonment liability can erode returns.

### Scam and Fraud Risk
- Oil and gas tax shelters have historically been a hotbed for fraudulent schemes
- **Red flags:** Guaranteed returns, unrealistic production projections, pressure to
  invest before year-end, operators with no track record, no independent geological
  assessment
- **Due diligence requirements:** Verify operator's track record, review independent
  reserve reports, confirm SEC filings (if applicable), check with state regulatory
  commission for permits and compliance history

### Suitable Investors
- High-income taxpayers in the 35-37% federal bracket (and high state bracket)
- Taxpayers with significant W-2 or active business income who need deductions against
  non-passive income
- Investors who can absorb a total economic loss on the investment
- Taxpayers who understand that the tax deduction subsidizes the risk but does not
  eliminate it
- NOT suitable for: taxpayers who cannot afford to lose the investment, those seeking
  passive income, risk-averse investors

## Worked Example: $100,000 Drilling Program Investment

### Year 1: The IDC Deduction

**Investment:** $100,000 in a drilling program (working interest, held directly)

**Cost breakdown:**
- Intangible Drilling Costs: $70,000 (70% of investment)
- Tangible equipment: $20,000
- Land/lease costs: $10,000

**Year 1 deductions:**
- IDC deduction (Section 263(c)): $70,000
- Bonus depreciation on tangible equipment (80% in 2025): $16,000
- Total Year 1 deduction: $86,000

**Tax savings at 37% federal + 3.8% NIIT + 10% state:**
- Federal: $86,000 x 37% = $31,820
- NIIT: may not apply to working interest income (active)
- State (assume 10%): $86,000 x 10% = $8,600
- **Total Year 1 tax savings: approximately $40,420**

On a $100,000 investment, the taxpayer recovers ~40% through tax savings in Year 1.

### Years 2-10: Production Phase (Assuming Productive Well)

**Assumptions:**
- Well produces 5,000 barrels/year initially, declining 20% annually
- Oil price: $70/barrel
- Operating costs: $25/barrel
- Taxpayer's 15% percentage depletion applies

**Year 2:**
- Gross revenue: 5,000 barrels x $70 = $350,000 (taxpayer's share, say 5% working
  interest = $17,500)
- Operating costs: 5,000 x $25 x 5% = $6,250
- Net income from property: $11,250
- Percentage depletion: $17,500 x 15% = $2,625 (limited to $11,250 net income = OK)
- Remaining tangible depreciation: $4,000 (20% of $20,000 remaining after Year 1)
- Taxable income from well: $11,250 - $2,625 - $4,000 = $4,625

**Year 2 tax on income (37% + 10% state):** $4,625 x 47% = $2,174

**Year 2 cash flow:**
- Revenue: $17,500
- Operating costs: -$6,250
- Tax: -$2,174
- **Net cash flow: $9,076**

### Cumulative Analysis (10-Year Projection)

| Year | Revenue | Costs | Deductions | Tax Saved/(Paid) | Net Cash Flow |
|------|---------|-------|------------|------------------|---------------|
| 1 | $0 | $100,000 | $86,000 | +$40,420 | -$59,580 |
| 2 | $17,500 | $6,250 | $6,625 | -$2,174 | +$9,076 |
| 3 | $14,000 | $5,000 | $3,100 | -$2,773 | +$6,227 |
| 4 | $11,200 | $4,000 | $1,680 | -$2,594 | +$4,606 |
| 5 | $8,960 | $3,200 | $1,344 | -$2,075 | +$3,685 |
| 6-10 | ~$22,000 | ~$10,000 | ~$3,300 | -$4,089 | +$7,911 |
| **Total** | **$73,660** | **$128,450** | **$102,049** | **+$26,715** | **-$28,075** |

**Economic result:** The well returns approximately $72,000 of the $100,000 investment
through cash flow over 10 years. After accounting for the $40,420 Year 1 tax savings
(net of subsequent tax payments), the total return is approximately $72,000.

**Key insight:** Without the tax benefits, this investment would lose $28,000. WITH the
tax benefits, the investor approximately breaks even. The tax savings transform a losing
economic proposition into a break-even or marginally profitable investment. If the well
is more productive, the returns improve significantly. If it is a dry hole, the $40,420
tax savings partially offsets the $100,000 loss.

### Dry Hole Scenario

If the well is non-productive:
- **Total deduction:** $100,000 (all costs deductible as loss in the year of abandonment)
- **Tax savings:** $100,000 x 47% = $47,000
- **Net economic loss:** $100,000 - $47,000 = $53,000

Even in a complete failure, the government absorbs ~47% of the loss through tax deductions.

## Interaction with Other Tax Provisions

### Net Operating Loss (NOL)
Large IDC deductions can create a net operating loss. Post-TCJA:
- NOLs can offset up to 80% of taxable income in carryforward years
- NOLs carry forward indefinitely (no carryback except for certain farming losses)

### AMT Planning
- If IDC deduction creates large AMT preference, consider Section 59(e) election
- Monitor the 65% net income test (Section 57(a)(2)(E)) — if IDCs are below 65%
  of net oil/gas income, no AMT preference applies
- AMT credits from prior years may offset current-year regular tax

### Qualified Business Income (QBI) Deduction
- Oil and gas income may qualify for the Section 199A QBI deduction (20% deduction)
- Oil and gas extraction is NOT a Specified Service Trade or Business (SSTB)
- The deduction is limited to the greater of: 50% of W-2 wages, or 25% of W-2 wages
  plus 2.5% of the unadjusted basis of qualified property
- For investors without W-2 employees, the QBI deduction may be limited

### Estate Planning
- Oil and gas interests may qualify for valuation discounts (minority interest,
  marketability) for gift and estate tax purposes
- Depletion carryover: excess depletion that was not usable by the decedent is generally
  lost at death (it does not pass to heirs)
- Heirs receive stepped-up basis in the mineral interest under Section 1014
- Future production income is taxed to the heirs at their rates

## Reporting Requirements

**Schedule E or Schedule C:** Royalty income on Schedule E (Part I). Working interest
income on Schedule C if the taxpayer is in the trade or business of oil and gas
extraction (otherwise Schedule E).

**Form 6198:** At-risk limitations — required if losses exceed the at-risk amount.

**Form 8582:** Passive activity limitations — required if MLP or limited partnership
losses are involved (does NOT apply to qualifying working interests).

**Form 4562:** Depreciation and amortization — for tangible equipment depreciation.

**K-1 (Form 1065):** If investment is through a partnership/MLP.

**State returns:** Mineral interest income is typically sourced to the state where the
minerals are located, requiring non-resident state returns for out-of-state investments.
