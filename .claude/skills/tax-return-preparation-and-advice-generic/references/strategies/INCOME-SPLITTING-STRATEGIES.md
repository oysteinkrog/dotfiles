# INCOME SPLITTING STRATEGIES: LEGAL METHODS TO SHIFT INCOME AMONG FAMILY MEMBERS

## Overview and Constitutional Framework

Income splitting involves redistributing income from higher-bracket taxpayers to
lower-bracket family members to reduce the family's aggregate tax burden. The fundamental
constraint is the **assignment of income doctrine** established in *Lucas v. Earl*, 281
U.S. 111 (1930): income is taxed to the person who earns it, and a taxpayer cannot avoid
tax by directing earned income to another person.

However, the tax code provides numerous legitimate mechanisms to shift investment income,
business income, and economic benefits to lower-bracket family members. The key is
creating genuine economic relationships — not paper transactions.

---

## Strategy 1: Employing Family Members

### Employing Your Spouse

**Benefits:**
- Legitimate salary shifts income from self-employment to W-2 wages
- Spouse becomes eligible for employer-provided fringe benefits (health insurance,
  retirement plans, educational assistance, dependent care)
- If sole proprietorship employs spouse, the business can provide spouse with family
  health coverage that also covers the owner (the employee's spouse)
- Spouse earns income for Social Security benefit calculations
- Spouse's earned income supports IRA/Roth IRA contributions

**Requirements:**
- Spouse must perform legitimate services (not a sham employment arrangement)
- Compensation must be reasonable for services performed
- Proper payroll documentation: W-4, W-2, payroll taxes withheld and remitted
- IRS scrutinizes spouse employment arrangements — keep time records and job descriptions

**Retirement plan power play:** Employing a spouse opens access to an employer-sponsored
retirement plan. If the business establishes a Solo 401(k) or SEP-IRA, the spouse can
make their own contributions:
- Solo 401(k): $23,500 employee deferral + 25% employer contribution on spouse's wages
- If spouse is age 50+: additional $7,500 catch-up
- Combined with business owner's contributions, a married couple can shelter $69,000+
  each in retirement accounts per year

### Employing Children Under 18 — The FICA Exemption

**Section 3121(b)(3)(A): Wages paid to a child under age 18 employed by a parent's
sole proprietorship or a partnership where both partners are the child's parents are
EXEMPT from Social Security and Medicare taxes (FICA).**

This is one of the most powerful income-splitting tools available:

1. **Income shifting:** Child's earned income is taxed at the child's rate (potentially 0%
   if under the standard deduction of $14,600 for 2025)
2. **FICA exemption:** No Social Security or Medicare tax for the business OR the child
3. **Business deduction:** Wages are deductible as ordinary business expense under Section 162
4. **Child's earned income funds Roth IRA:** See Strategy 5 below
5. **FUTA exempt** for children under 21 (Section 3306(c)(5))

**Age rules for FICA exemption:**
- Under 18: exempt from FICA and FUTA (sole prop or spouse-only partnership)
- 18-20: subject to FICA, but still FUTA exempt
- 21+: fully subject to FICA and FUTA (same as any employee)

**CRITICAL: Entity limitation.** The FICA exemption applies ONLY to sole proprietorships
and partnerships where both partners are parents. It does NOT apply to:
- S corporations
- C corporations
- LLCs taxed as corporations
- Partnerships that include non-parent partners

**Workaround for S-Corps:** Some practitioners structure a separate sole proprietorship
(management company) that employs the children and provides services to the S-Corp.
This is aggressive and must have economic substance — the management company must be
a genuine business, not a shell.

**Legitimate work for children:**
- Office cleaning and maintenance
- Filing, organizing, shredding documents
- Social media management, website updates
- Photography for marketing materials
- Inventory management (counting, organizing)
- Answering phones, greeting customers
- Data entry
- Modeling for advertising (family business marketing)

**Documentation requirements:**
- Written job description with specific duties
- Time sheets documenting hours worked
- Compensation at market rate for the work performed (not $50/hour for filing)
- W-4 on file; W-2 issued annually
- Payments made to the child's own bank account (not parent's account)

### Employing Parents

**Benefits:**
- Shifts income to potentially lower-bracket parent
- Parent earns Social Security credits (if under maximum benefit)
- Parent can participate in employer retirement plan

**Household employee exception:** If employing a parent for domestic services (housekeeping,
childcare), wages are exempt from FUTA if your child under 18 lives in the household
(Section 3306(c)(5)). However, FICA applies unless the parent is age 65+ and performing
non-business domestic work.

## Strategy 2: Family LLC or Partnership

Forming a family LLC (taxed as a partnership) allows allocation of business income to
family members who are legitimate equity owners.

**Structure:**
- Parents and children (or trusts for children) are members of an LLC
- LLC operates a business or holds investment assets
- Income, deductions, gains, and losses are allocated per the operating agreement
- Allocations must have "substantial economic effect" under Section 704(b)

**Requirements for validity:**
1. **Genuine ownership:** Family members must have a real economic interest — not just
   a paper allocation. Capital must be a material income-producing factor, or the family
   member must provide services. (*Commissioner v. Culbertson*, 337 U.S. 733 (1949))
2. **Substantial economic effect:** Allocations must reflect actual economic arrangements.
   Special allocations solely to shift income to lower-bracket members without economic
   substance will be disregarded (Section 704(b) regulations).
3. **Capital is a material income-producing factor:** If the business relies primarily
   on personal services (like a law firm or consulting practice), merely gifting a
   partnership interest to a family member does NOT shift the service income. The
   assignment of income doctrine applies. (*Lucas v. Earl*)
4. **Gift tax implications:** Gifting LLC interests may use annual exclusion ($19,000/person
  in 2025) or lifetime exemption. Interests may qualify for valuation discounts (lack of
   marketability, minority interest).

**Example — Family Real Estate LLC:**
- Parents contribute a $1,000,000 rental property to an LLC
- Over time, parents gift 40% of LLC interests to two adult children (20% each)
- LLC generates $100,000 of rental income annually
- $60,000 allocated to parents (24% bracket = $14,400 tax)
- $20,000 allocated to each child (12% bracket = $2,400 each, $4,800 total)
- Family tax savings: approximately $5,000-$10,000 per year vs. parents reporting 100%

**Kiddie Tax warning:** If children are under 19 (or under 24 if full-time students),
unearned income over $2,700 may be taxed at the parents' marginal rate. See Strategy 6.

## Strategy 3: Gifting Appreciated Assets

Gift appreciated stock or other assets to a family member in a lower tax bracket, who
then sells at their lower capital gains rate.

**How it works:**
1. Parent holds stock with $100,000 of unrealized long-term capital gain
2. Parent gifts stock to adult child (not subject to Kiddie Tax)
3. Child receives parent's carryover basis (Section 1015)
4. Child sells stock, recognizes $100,000 LTCG
5. If child is in the 0% LTCG bracket (taxable income below $48,350 single in 2025),
   the gain is taxed at 0%

**Tax savings:** Parent would have paid 20% + 3.8% NIIT = $23,800. Child pays $0.
Family saves $23,800.

**Gift tax:** The FMV of the stock at the time of gift counts against the $19,000 annual
exclusion (per donee) and, if exceeding that, against the lifetime exemption.

**Basis rules for gifts (Section 1015):**
- If FMV at gift > donor's basis: donee's basis = donor's basis (carryover)
- If FMV at gift < donor's basis: donee's basis for LOSS = FMV at gift date
  (prevents gifting losses to higher-bracket family members)
- Holding period tacks: donor's holding period carries over to donee

**CRITICAL LIMITATION — Kiddie Tax (Section 1(g)):**
This strategy does NOT work for:
- Children under age 19
- Children age 19-23 who are full-time students and do not provide more than half their
  own support

For these children, unearned income (including capital gains) above $2,700 is taxed at
the parents' marginal rate. See Strategy 6 for details.

**Best candidates:** Adult children age 24+ who are in low income years (graduate school,
starting a career, between jobs, early retirement).

## Strategy 4: Section 529 Plan Contributions

529 education savings plans shift future income to tax-free status:

- Contributions grow tax-free
- Withdrawals for qualified education expenses are completely tax-free (federal)
- Many states offer income tax deductions for contributions (varies by state)
- Up to $19,000/year per beneficiary without gift tax (2025)
- 5-year superfunding: up to $95,000 in a single year per beneficiary using 5-year
  gift tax averaging (Section 529(c)(2)(B))

**Income shifting mechanism:** Instead of investing in a taxable account where gains are
taxed annually at the parent's rate, investing in a 529 generates tax-free growth. For
a family in the 37% + 3.8% NIIT bracket investing for 18 years, the tax-free compounding
can add 30-50% to the ending balance compared to taxable investing.

**529 to Roth IRA rollover (SECURE 2.0):** Starting in 2024, excess 529 funds can be
rolled over to a Roth IRA for the beneficiary, subject to:
- 529 account must have been open for 15+ years
- Contributions and earnings from the last 5 years are ineligible
- Annual limit: Roth IRA contribution limit ($7,000 in 2025)
- Lifetime limit: $35,000
- Beneficiary must have earned income ≥ the rollover amount

## Strategy 5: Roth IRA for Children with Earned Income

**One of the most powerful long-term wealth-building strategies in the tax code.**

**Requirements:**
- Child must have legitimate earned income (W-2 or self-employment income)
- Roth IRA contribution limited to lesser of earned income or $7,000 (2025)
- No minimum age for Roth IRA contributions

**The parent gift strategy:**
1. Child earns $7,000 from family business (see Strategy 1 — employing children)
2. Child's earned income is under the standard deduction = $0 income tax
3. FICA exempt if under 18 in sole prop = $0 payroll tax
4. Parent gifts child $7,000 separately (annual exclusion, no gift tax)
5. Child contributes $7,000 to Roth IRA
6. Child uses the gifted $7,000 for spending money (the contribution is funded
   by the earned wages, the gift replaces the spending money)

**Note:** The Roth contribution does not have to come from the same dollars as the
earned income — it just cannot exceed earned income. The parent's gift is a separate
transaction.

**Long-term impact:** $7,000 contributed at age 14, growing at 8% annually:
- Age 30 (16 years): $24,000
- Age 50 (36 years): $107,000
- Age 65 (51 years): $370,000
- Age 80 (66 years): $1,278,000

All growth is 100% tax-free. Starting contributions at a young age creates extraordinary
wealth due to decades of compounding.

## Strategy 6: UGMA/UTMA Accounts and the Kiddie Tax

### Uniform Gifts to Minors Act (UGMA) / Uniform Transfers to Minors Act (UTMA)

Custodial accounts that allow parents to gift assets to children, with income taxed at
the child's rate (subject to Kiddie Tax limitations).

**2025 Kiddie Tax thresholds (Section 1(g)):**
- First $1,350 of child's unearned income: TAX-FREE (standard deduction for dependents)
- Next $1,350 ($1,351-$2,700): taxed at child's rate (10%)
- Above $2,700: taxed at **parents' marginal rate** (Kiddie Tax)

**Kiddie Tax applies to:**
- Children under age 19
- Children age 19-23 who are full-time students and do not provide more than half
  of their own support with earned income

**Kiddie Tax does NOT apply to:**
- Children age 19+ who are not full-time students
- Children age 24+ regardless of student status
- Children under 19 whose earned income exceeds half their total support
- Married children filing joint returns

**Strategy within Kiddie Tax constraints:**
- Invest UGMA/UTMA funds in growth stocks (no dividends = no current Kiddie Tax)
- Sell appreciated assets after child turns 19/24 (exits Kiddie Tax)
- Generate up to $2,700/year in unearned income with minimal tax impact
  ($0 on first $1,350, ~$135 on next $1,350 at 10%)

**UGMA/UTMA drawbacks:**
- Irrevocable gift to the child
- Child gains full control at age of majority (18 or 21 depending on state)
- Assets count as student's asset for FAFSA (assessed at 20% vs. 5.64% for parent assets)
- Cannot be taken back if child makes poor financial decisions

## Strategy 7: Trust Income Distribution

Trusts (other than grantor trusts) are separate taxpayers with highly compressed
tax brackets:

**2025 Trust Tax Brackets:**
| Taxable Income | Rate |
|---------------|------|
| $0 - $3,150 | 10% |
| $3,151 - $11,450 | 24% |
| $11,451 - $15,650 | 35% |
| Over $15,650 | 37% |

A trust reaches the 37% bracket at just $15,650 — compared to $609,350 for a single
individual. This makes retaining income inside a trust extremely tax-inefficient.

**Distributable Net Income (DNI) strategy:**
- Trust distributes income to lower-bracket beneficiaries
- Trust receives a deduction for distributions (limited to DNI)
- Beneficiaries include the distributed income on their personal returns
- If beneficiaries are in lower brackets, net family tax decreases

**Example:**
- Irrevocable trust earns $50,000 of interest and dividend income
- If retained in trust: tax = approximately $16,400 (37% on most of it)
- If distributed to adult child with no other income:
  - Standard deduction: $14,600
  - Taxable: $35,400
  - Tax: approximately $4,000 (10% and 12% brackets)
  - Savings: approximately $12,400

**Timing distributions:** The trust can make distributions within 65 days after year-end
and elect (under Section 663(b)) to treat them as made on December 31 of the prior year.
This gives the trustee time to evaluate the tax situation before deciding on distributions.

**Kiddie Tax interaction:** If the trust beneficiary is a child subject to Kiddie Tax,
trust distributions of unearned income are taxed at the parents' rate — negating the
income-splitting benefit.

## Strategy 8: Spousal IRA

**Section 219(c): A non-working spouse can contribute to an IRA based on the working
spouse's earned income.**

**Requirements:**
- Must file married filing jointly
- Working spouse must have earned income at least equal to both spouses' IRA contributions
- Non-working spouse (or low-earning spouse) can contribute up to $7,000 (2025)
- Age 50+ catch-up: additional $1,000 ($8,000 total)

**Both Traditional and Roth are available:**
- Traditional spousal IRA: deductible if couple meets income limits (based on working
  spouse's retirement plan participation)
- Roth spousal IRA: available if MAGI below $236,000 (MFJ, 2025 phase-out start)

**Income splitting benefit:** Creates a separate retirement account for the non-working
spouse, diversifying the family's retirement assets across two accounts with potentially
different beneficiary designations and withdrawal strategies.

## Strategy 9: Family Management Company

A family management company centralizes business services and employs family members.

**Structure:**
1. Form an LLC (taxed as sole prop if single-member, or partnership if family-owned)
2. Management company provides genuine services to the family's businesses/investments
   (bookkeeping, property management, marketing, administrative support)
3. Family members are employed by the management company
4. Management company pays reasonable wages, provides benefits, and makes retirement
   plan contributions

**Advantages:**
- Centralizes payroll and benefits administration
- Provides legitimate employment for family members
- Enables retirement plan contributions for all employed family members
- If taxed as a partnership or sole prop (not S-Corp or C-Corp), the FICA exemption
  for children under 18 applies

**IRS scrutiny:** The management company must provide genuine services at arm's-length
rates. It must have economic substance beyond tax avoidance. Keep detailed records of
services provided, time worked, and fees charged.

## Strategy 10: Assignment of Income Doctrine — Boundaries

### What You CANNOT Do
- Assign salary or wages to family members (*Lucas v. Earl*)
- Direct clients to pay a family member for services you performed
- Assign income from a personal services contract to a family trust
- Create a sham partnership that allocates service income to non-participating family
  members (*Commissioner v. Culbertson*)

### What You CAN Do
- Create genuine employment relationships (family members perform real work)
- Gift income-producing assets to family members (they own the asset, they earn the income)
- Form partnerships where family members contribute capital AND/OR services
- Establish trusts that own income-producing assets (trust or beneficiaries taxed)
- Hire family members through a legitimate business entity

### Key Court Cases
- **Lucas v. Earl (1930):** Husband cannot assign half his salary to wife by contract.
  Income is taxed to the earner.
- **Helvering v. Horst (1940):** Cannot assign interest income by detaching bond coupons
  and giving them away. Income is taxed to the owner of the income-producing property.
- **Commissioner v. Culbertson (1949):** Family partnership must be bona fide — partners
  must actually contribute capital or services.
- **Foglesong v. Commissioner (1988):** Payments to children for farm work were legitimate
  deductions where children performed genuine services.

## State Community Property Considerations

In community property states (AZ, CA, ID, LA, NV, NM, TX, WA, WI), earned income
during marriage is automatically owned 50/50 by both spouses, regardless of who earned it.

**Impact on income splitting:**
- Less need for artificial income-splitting strategies between spouses — income is
  already split by operation of law
- Community property rules apply to federal tax only if both spouses are alive and
  the couple is not legally separated
- Upon death of one spouse, the surviving spouse receives a full stepped-up basis on
  the ENTIRE community property (both halves) — Section 1014(b)(6)

**Planning consideration:** In non-community-property states, spouses may need to use
trusts, joint ownership, or gifting strategies to achieve similar income splitting. The
community property step-up at death is a significant estate planning advantage.

## Comprehensive Example: Family Income Splitting in Action

**The Martinez Family:**
- Carlos (age 48): sole proprietor consultant, $500,000 net self-employment income
- Maria (age 46): stay-at-home parent, no earned income
- Sophia (age 16): high school student
- Diego (age 22): college senior, full-time student, no other income

**Strategy implementation:**

1. **Employ Maria in the sole proprietorship:** $50,000 salary for legitimate bookkeeping,
   client coordination, and office management.
   - Tax effect: $50,000 shifted from 35%+ bracket to 12-22% bracket
   - Enables Solo 401(k) contributions for Maria

2. **Employ Sophia (age 16):** $14,600 salary for social media management, filing, and
   office cleaning. FICA exempt (sole prop, child under 18).
   - Tax effect: $14,600 business deduction, $0 tax to Sophia (under standard deduction)
   - FICA savings: $14,600 x 15.3% = $2,234

3. **Fund Sophia's Roth IRA:** $7,000 contribution from Sophia's earnings. Carlos gifts
   Sophia $7,000 separately for spending money.
   - 50+ years of tax-free growth

4. **Gift appreciated stock to Diego (age 22, full-time student):**
   - CAUTION: Diego is subject to Kiddie Tax until age 24 as a student
   - Wait until Diego graduates (age 22-23) and is no longer a full-time student,
     OR wait until age 24
   - Alternatively, gift stock NOW and Diego holds until graduation for sale

5. **Spousal IRA for Maria:** In addition to Solo 401(k), Maria can contribute to a
   traditional or Roth IRA ($7,000).

6. **Family LLC for rental property:** Carlos and Maria transfer a rental property to
   an LLC. Over time, gift 10% interests to each child.
   - Rental income allocated proportionally
   - Valuation discounts on gifted interests reduce gift tax exposure

**Annual tax savings estimate:**
- Income shifting to Maria: ~$8,000-$12,000
- Sophia employment: ~$3,500-$5,500 (income tax + FICA)
- Gift appreciated stock (when Diego exits Kiddie Tax): varies, potentially $5,000-$15,000
- Retirement plan contributions (Maria): $10,000-$15,000 in deferred tax
- **Total family tax reduction: $27,000-$47,000+ per year**

## Compliance and Documentation Checklist

For every income-splitting strategy, maintain:

1. **Written employment agreements** with job descriptions for all family employees
2. **Time records** documenting hours worked and tasks performed
3. **Payroll records** with W-4s, W-2s, and timely payroll tax deposits
4. **LLC/partnership operating agreements** with legitimate economic terms
5. **Gift documentation** (Form 709 if annual exclusion exceeded)
6. **Fair market value appraisals** for gifted interests (LLC interests, real property)
7. **Trust documents** with clear distribution provisions
8. **Business purpose documentation** — written explanation of why each arrangement
   exists beyond tax savings
9. **Arm's-length evidence** — comparable salary surveys, third-party management fee
   benchmarks, market-rate analysis for services provided
