# Entity Selection Strategies for US Taxpayers

## Overview

Choosing the right business entity is one of the most consequential tax decisions a self-employed individual or business owner can make. The wrong entity wastes money on unnecessary compliance costs; the right one can save thousands annually in self-employment tax, enable SALT workarounds, and unlock retirement plan options.

This guide covers the 2025 tax year, including OBBBA changes effective for tax years beginning after January 19, 2025.

## Sole Proprietorship

**What it is:** The default entity for any individual conducting business without forming a separate legal entity. Reported on Schedule C (Form 1040).

**Advantages:**
- Zero formation cost or paperwork
- Simplest tax filing (Schedule C)
- No separate tax return required
- Full QBI deduction eligibility (subject to income limits)
- Net operating losses flow directly to Form 1040

**Disadvantages:**
- No liability protection
- All net income subject to self-employment tax (15.3% on first $176,100 of combined wages + SE income for 2025, 2.9% Medicare on remainder, plus 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax on earned income above $200K/$250K MFJ)
- Cannot elect PTET
- Less credible to clients/partners than LLC or corporation

**Best for:** Side hustles, freelancers earning under $50K, businesses with minimal liability risk.

---

## Single-Member LLC (Disregarded Entity)

**What it is:** An LLC with one owner, treated as a disregarded entity for Federal tax purposes (same as sole proprietorship) unless it elects corporate taxation.

**Tax treatment:** Identical to sole proprietorship. Reported on Schedule C. The LLC is "disregarded" -- the IRS looks through it to the owner.

**Advantages:**
- Liability protection (asset separation)
- Same simple tax treatment as sole proprietorship
- Can elect S-Corp or C-Corp taxation later without changing legal structure
- Professional appearance

**Disadvantages:**
- State filing fees (annual or biennial)
- Cannot elect PTET as a single-member entity in most states
- Still subject to full self-employment tax unless S-Corp election made
- Some states impose additional taxes (e.g., NY LLC filing fee, CA $800 franchise tax)

**NY LLC Filing Fee Example (important state-specific cost):**

**CA Franchise Tax:** Minimum $800/year for all LLCs (waived for first year in some circumstances).

**Best for:** Solo business owners wanting liability protection without S-Corp complexity.

### Single-Member LLC Traps

#### Husband-Wife Qualified Joint Ventures (Section 761(f))

A married couple who co-own an unincorporated business can elect to treat it as a "qualified joint venture" instead of a partnership, but ONLY if:

1. Both spouses **materially participate** in the business
2. The business is **not held in the name of a state-law entity** (LLC, LP, etc.) -- EXCEPT in community property states
3. Both spouses must file a joint return
4. The couple must elect the treatment by dividing income/expenses on two Schedule Cs

**Community property state exception:** In the nine community property states (AZ, CA, ID, LA, NV, NM, TX, WA, WI), a husband-wife LLC can be treated as a disregarded entity (Rev. Proc. 2002-69). This avoids the requirement to file Form 1065 while maintaining LLC liability protection.

**The trap:** In a non-community-property state, if a married couple forms a multi-member LLC, they MUST file Form 1065 as a partnership. They cannot use the qualified joint venture election because the business is held in an LLC. This adds $2,000-$4,000 in annual compliance costs and creates late-filing penalty risk ($220/month per partner for 2025).

**Planning point:** In non-community-property states, couples should either:
- Each form their own SMLLC (separate Schedule Cs)
- Elect S-Corp treatment for the MMLLC
- Accept partnership filing obligations

#### Employment Tax Identity Confusion

A disregarded entity is treated as a separate entity for Federal employment tax and certain excise tax purposes (Reg. 301.7701-2(c)(2)(iv)). The SMLLC (not the owner) is the employer of record. Payroll returns (Forms 941, 940, W-2) must be filed under the LLC's EIN, not the owner's SSN. Getting this wrong causes IRS matching failures, penalty notices, and delayed Social Security credits.

## Multi-Member LLC (Partnership)

**What it is:** An LLC with two or more members, taxed as a partnership by default. Files Form 1065 and issues Schedule K-1 to each member.

**Tax treatment:** Pass-through. Each member reports their share of income, deductions, and credits on their personal return.

**Advantages:**
- Flexible profit/loss allocation (unlike S-Corp, which must be proportional to ownership)
- PTET eligible in states that offer it
- Pass-through taxation avoids double taxation
- Can have different classes of membership interests

**Disadvantages:**
- More complex tax filing (Form 1065 + K-1s)
- General partners subject to self-employment tax on their share
- Guaranteed payments are always subject to SE tax
- Partnership tax rules are among the most complex in the Code
- Late filing penalty: $220/month per partner (2025)

**Best for:** Businesses with multiple owners who want flexibility in allocations, or married couples wanting PTET eligibility.

## Check-the-Box Regulations (Section 301.7701-3)

### Default Entity Classifications

The check-the-box regulations determine how business entities are classified for Federal tax purposes absent an affirmative election:

| Entity Type | Default Classification |
|---|---|
| Domestic entity, one owner | Disregarded entity (Schedule C) |
| Domestic entity, two+ owners | Partnership (Form 1065) |
| Foreign entity, one owner, limited liability | Corporation |
| Foreign entity, two+ owners, limited liability | Corporation |
| Foreign entity, one owner, no limited liability | Disregarded entity |
| Foreign entity, two+ owners, no limited liability | Partnership |

### Making an Election (Form 8832)

- **When to file:** The election can be effective up to 75 days prior to the filing date and up to 12 months after the filing date
- **Effective date:** Cannot be more than 75 days before the date Form 8832 is filed
- **60-month rule:** Once an election is made, the entity generally cannot change its classification again for 60 months unless there is a change in more than 50% of ownership
- **Late election relief:** Reg. 301.7701-3(b)(3)(ii) -- if the entity had reasonable cause and filed within 3 years and 75 days

### Common Election Scenarios

1. **SMLLC electing C-Corp:** File Form 8832 checking "corporation." Used when QSBS benefits are desired.
2. **SMLLC electing S-Corp:** File Form 2553 (which simultaneously treats the LLC as a corporation and elects S status). No need to file Form 8832 separately.
3. **Partnership converting to C-Corp:** File Form 8832. The partnership is treated as contributing all assets and liabilities to the new corporation in exchange for stock, then distributing stock to the partners (Reg. 301.7701-3(g)(1)(i)). Generally tax-free under Section 351.
4. **C-Corp electing disregarded entity status:** File Form 8832 to check the "disregarded" box. This is treated as a complete liquidation of the corporation -- potentially taxable at both corporate and shareholder levels. Extremely dangerous without careful planning.

## S-Corporation

### Overview

An S-Corp is not a separate entity type -- it is a tax election made by an existing corporation or LLC. The entity files Form 1120-S and issues K-1s. The key tax benefit is splitting income between salary (subject to FICA) and distributions (not subject to FICA).

### How to Elect S-Corp Status

1. **Form 2553** -- Election by a Small Business Corporation
2. **Deadline:** By March 15 of the tax year for which the election is effective (or within 75 days of formation)
3. **Late election relief:** Available if filed within 3 years and 75 days of the intended effective date, with reasonable cause
4. **All shareholders must consent** by signing Form 2553

### Late S-Corp Election Relief (Rev. Proc. 2013-30)

If the Form 2553 deadline is missed, Rev. Proc. 2013-30 provides a streamlined path for late elections:

**Eligibility requirements (ALL must be met):**

1. The entity intended to be classified as an S-Corp as of the intended effective date
2. The entity has reasonable cause for failing to file timely
3. The entity has filed all required S-Corp returns (Form 1120-S) consistent with S-Corp status for all tax years affected
4. Less than 3 years and 75 days have elapsed since the intended effective date
5. No shareholders have reported income inconsistently with S-Corp status

**How to file:**
- Write "FILED PURSUANT TO REV. PROC. 2013-30" at the top of Form 2553
- Attach a reasonable cause statement explaining why the filing was late
- Common acceptable reasons: relied on accountant who failed to file, did not know about the requirement, corporate formation service oversight
- File the late Form 2553 with the IRS service center where the entity files its Form 1120-S

**What does NOT qualify as reasonable cause:**
- "We wanted to wait and see if S-Corp was beneficial"
- Intentional delay to game the election timing
- Knowledge of the deadline combined with inaction

**If Rev. Proc. 2013-30 does not apply:** The taxpayer must request a Private Letter Ruling (PLR) from the IRS. Cost: $3,000-$10,000+ user fee. These are granted liberally when the taxpayer acted in good faith.

### S-Corp Requirements

- Domestic corporation or eligible entity
- Maximum 100 shareholders
- Only one class of stock (voting rights can differ)
- Shareholders must be individuals, certain trusts, or estates (no partnerships, corporations, or nonresident aliens)
- Cannot be an ineligible corporation (certain financial institutions, insurance companies, DISCs)

### The S-Corp Tax Math

The core benefit: distributions from an S-Corp are not subject to self-employment tax / FICA. Only W-2 salary triggers payroll taxes.

**Self-employment tax rate:** 15.3% (12.4% Social Security + 2.9% Medicare) on first $176,100 of combined wages + SE income for 2025. 2.9% Medicare on amounts above that. Additional 0.9% Medicare Tax on earned income above $200K single / $250K MFJ.

### Reasonable Salary Requirements

The IRS requires S-Corp shareholder-employees to pay themselves a "reasonable" salary before taking distributions. Factors:

- Industry norms for the role performed
- Training, education, and experience
- Time devoted to the business
- Comparable wages in the geographic area
- Revenue and profitability of the business

**Rule of thumb:** Salary should be 40-60% of net business income, but never below what a third party would be paid for the same work. The IRS has won cases where salaries were set too low.

**Resources:** BLS Occupational Employment Statistics, Salary.com, Glassdoor for comparable salary data.

#### Key Case Law on Reasonable Salary

**Watson v. Commissioner (2012, 8th Circuit)**
- Facts: CPA earned $200K+ through his S-Corp but paid himself only $24,000 in salary for multiple years
- Holding: The Tax Court determined $91,044 was reasonable compensation. The 8th Circuit affirmed.
- Key takeaway: The court looked at what an employee performing the same services would be paid on the open market. The fact that Watson was the sole revenue-generator for the firm was critical.
- Reclassified distributions as wages, plus imposed back employment taxes + penalties

**David E. Watson, P.C. v. United States (related):**
- The court emphasized that "reasonable" does not mean the minimum a replacement could be hired for. It means fair market value of the actual services rendered by the shareholder.

**Hale Murfeesboro Medical Clinic v. Commissioner (Tax Court)**
- An S-Corp medical clinic paid shareholder-physicians below-market salaries while distributing significant profits
- The court recharacterized distributions as wages, finding the salaries were unreasonably low given the physicians' specialties, experience, and the revenue they generated
- Reinforced: medical professionals, attorneys, consultants, and other professionals whose personal services drive the revenue face heightened scrutiny

**IRS S-Corp Audit Guide -- Job Aid Factors:**

The IRS instructs revenue agents to evaluate these specific factors when auditing reasonable compensation:

1. Training and experience of the employee
2. Duties and responsibilities
3. Time and effort devoted to the business
4. Dividend history
5. Payments to non-shareholder employees
6. Timing and manner of paying bonuses to key people
7. What comparable businesses pay for similar services
8. Compensation agreements
9. The use of a formula to determine compensation (e.g., percentage of revenue)
10. Whether the company has ever paid reasonable compensation

**Practical guidance:**
- A shareholder who works full-time and is the primary revenue generator should receive at least 40-50% of net income as salary (before the S-Corp's own payroll tax deduction)
- A shareholder who provides only oversight/management with employees doing the work might justify 25-35%
- Zero salary is never acceptable if the shareholder performs ANY services (even the IRS won *Radtke v. United States* on this point)
- Document the salary determination annually with a corporate resolution referencing market data

### S-Corp Basis Tracking

S-Corp shareholders must track two types of basis to determine the tax consequences of distributions and deductibility of losses.

#### Stock Basis

Stock basis begins with the shareholder's initial investment (cash + FMV of property contributed) and is adjusted annually:

**Increases to stock basis:**
- Separately stated income items (capital gains, Section 1231 gains, tax-exempt income, etc.)
- Nonseparately stated income (ordinary business income from K-1 line 1)
- Excess depletion over property basis

**Decreases to stock basis (in this order):**
1. Non-dividend distributions (but not below zero)
2. Non-deductible expenses (e.g., 50% meals disallowance, penalties, related-party losses)
3. Depletion deductions
4. Losses and deductions (separately and nonseparately stated)

**Critical ordering rule:** Distributions reduce basis BEFORE losses. This means a shareholder who receives a large distribution may not have enough remaining basis to deduct losses.

**Stock basis cannot go below zero.** If losses exceed stock basis (after debt basis), the excess is suspended and carries forward indefinitely until the shareholder creates additional basis.

#### Debt Basis

If a shareholder makes loans DIRECTLY to the S-Corp (not third-party loans guaranteed by the shareholder), the shareholder gets additional basis for loss deduction purposes.

**What creates debt basis:**
- Direct loans from shareholder to S-Corp (promissory note, open account, etc.)
- Cash advances documented as loans

**What does NOT create debt basis:**
- Shareholder guarantee of corporate debt (back-to-back loans may work; see below)
- Third-party loans to the S-Corp, even if the shareholder is a co-signer or guarantor
- Capital contributions (these increase stock basis, not debt basis)

**Back-to-back loan structure:** Shareholder borrows from the bank personally, then re-lends the proceeds to the S-Corp. This creates legitimate debt basis because the shareholder has made a direct economic outlay. The IRS has challenged arrangements where the shareholder merely guarantees but never actually funds the loan (see *Oren v. Commissioner*, *Bolding v. Commissioner*).

**Debt basis restoration:** When the S-Corp repays the shareholder loan, the shareholder must recognize gain to the extent that debt basis was previously reduced by losses below the face amount of the loan. This is a trap -- the shareholder gets an unexpected taxable event upon loan repayment.

#### Loss Limitation Ordering (Section 1366(d))

An S-Corp shareholder can deduct their pro-rata share of losses ONLY to the extent of their combined stock + debt basis. The ordering is:

1. **Stock basis first:** Losses reduce stock basis (but not below zero)
2. **Debt basis second:** If losses exceed stock basis, the excess reduces debt basis (but not below zero)
3. **Suspended losses:** Any remaining losses are suspended under Section 1366(d)(2) and carry forward indefinitely

When future income or basis increases occur:
- **Debt basis is restored first** (before stock basis) from pass-through income
- **Suspended losses** then become deductible to the extent of the restored basis

**Additional limitation layers (applied after basis):**
- At-risk rules (Section 465) -- generally mirrors basis for S-Corps since guaranteed debt does not create basis
- Passive activity rules (Section 469) -- losses may still be limited even if the shareholder has sufficient basis
- Excess business loss limitation (Section 461(l)) -- $313,000 single / $626,000 MFJ for 2025

#### AAA vs. OAA and Distribution Ordering Rules (Section 1368)

The Accumulated Adjustments Account (AAA) tracks the S-Corp's post-election undistributed net income that has already been taxed to shareholders. It is a corporate-level account (not per-shareholder) used to determine the tax treatment of distributions.

**AAA adjustments (similar to stock basis but different):**
- Increased by: income items (same as stock basis)
- Decreased by: losses, deductions, non-deductible expenses (EXCEPT tax-exempt income -- this is the key difference from stock basis)
- AAA CAN go below zero (unlike stock basis)
- Tax-exempt income does NOT increase AAA (it increases the Other Adjustments Account, OAA)
- Expenses related to tax-exempt income decrease OAA, not AAA

**Other Adjustments Account (OAA):**
- Tracks tax-exempt income and related expenses
- Federal and state tax-exempt interest, life insurance proceeds payable to the corporation
- Distributions from OAA are tax-free

**Distribution ordering for S-Corps WITH NO C-Corp E&P (Section 1368(b)):**
1. Tax-free return of basis (reduces stock basis)
2. After stock basis is exhausted: capital gain

**Distribution ordering for S-Corps WITH accumulated C-Corp E&P (Section 1368(c)):**
1. Tax-free to the extent of AAA (reduces AAA and stock basis)
2. Dividend to the extent of accumulated E&P (does NOT reduce stock basis)
3. Tax-free return of basis (reduces stock basis)
4. Capital gain after basis exhausted

**Why this matters:** When an S-Corp has legacy C-Corp E&P (from years when it was a C-Corp, or from an acquisition), distributions can become taxable dividends after AAA is exhausted. The corporation can elect to distribute E&P first ("bypass the AAA") under Section 1368(e)(3) if it wants to purge the E&P.

### Built-in Gains Tax (Section 1374)

#### What It Is

When a C-Corp converts to S-Corp status, the IRS imposes a corporate-level tax on any built-in gains that existed at the time of conversion, IF those gains are recognized within a recognition period.

#### The Recognition Period

- **5-year recognition period:** Applies to conversions after December 31, 2011 (made permanent by PATH Act of 2015)
- The period starts on the first day of the first tax year as an S-Corp
- If the S-Corp sells an asset that had a built-in gain at the conversion date, AND the sale occurs within the 5-year window, the built-in gains tax applies

#### Computation of Net Recognized Built-in Gain (NRBIG)

For each year within the recognition period:

1. **Recognized built-in gain:** Gain on sale of assets held at conversion, limited to the built-in gain existing at the conversion date. Gain in excess of the built-in amount is not subject to BIG tax.
2. **Recognized built-in loss:** Loss on disposition of assets that had built-in losses at conversion. These offset recognized built-in gains.
3. **NRBIG ceiling:** The cumulative NRBIG over the entire recognition period cannot exceed the net unrealized built-in gain (NUBIG) at the conversion date
4. **Tax rate:** The highest corporate rate in effect (currently 21%)
5. **Carryovers:** Net operating losses and capital loss carryforwards from C-Corp years can offset NRBIG

#### Worked Example

```
Acme Corp converts from C-Corp to S-Corp on January 1, 2025.

Assets at conversion:
  Building: FMV $800K, basis $400K -> built-in gain $400K
  Equipment: FMV $50K, basis $80K -> built-in loss ($30K)
  Inventory: FMV $200K, basis $150K -> built-in gain $50K
  Goodwill: FMV $500K, basis $0 -> built-in gain $500K

NUBIG at conversion: $400K + $50K + $500K - $30K = $920K

Year 2 (2026): S-Corp sells the building for $900K.
  Total gain: $900K - $400K = $500K
  But built-in gain at conversion was only $400K
  Recognized built-in gain = $400K (capped at conversion-date built-in gain)
  Remaining $100K of gain is S-Corp pass-through gain (no BIG tax)

BIG tax on $400K: $400K x 21% = $84,000
  (paid at the corporate level, reduces shareholder pass-through income)

Cumulative NRBIG tracker: $400K of $920K ceiling used.
```

#### Planning to Avoid BIG Tax

1. **Wait out the recognition period:** Do not sell appreciated assets for 5 years after conversion
2. **Installment sales:** Structure sales so gain recognition falls outside the recognition period (but the IRS may accelerate under anti-abuse rules)
3. **Appraise assets at conversion:** Establish FMV at the conversion date with a professional appraisal. Without this, the IRS can argue higher built-in gains.
4. **Use C-Corp NOL carryovers:** NOLs from C-Corp years can offset NRBIG dollar-for-dollar
5. **Consider whether S-Corp conversion is worth it:** If the entity holds substantial appreciated assets, the BIG tax may outweigh the benefits of S-Corp status for the first 5 years

### S-Corp Compliance Requirements

1. **Payroll:** Must run payroll (W-2), withhold Federal/state income tax, FICA
2. **Quarterly payroll filings:** Form 941 (quarterly), state equivalents
3. **Annual payroll filings:** Form 940 (FUTA), W-2/W-3
4. **Corporate tax return:** Form 1120-S (due March 15)
5. **K-1 issuance:** To each shareholder by March 15
6. **State filings:** State corporate return, state payroll filings
7. **Payroll service:** Gusto, ADP, Paychex, or accountant-managed ($50-$200/month)
8. **Annual meeting minutes** (good practice for corporate veil)

### When NOT to Elect S-Corp

- **Income below $80K-$100K:** Compliance costs eat the savings
- **Highly variable income:** Hard to set reasonable salary; may overpay FICA in low years
- **Planning to raise outside investment:** S-Corp restrictions on shareholders and stock classes are limiting
- **Non-US shareholders:** S-Corps cannot have nonresident alien shareholders
- **Retirement plan complications:** SEP-IRA employer contributions are based on W-2 salary (lower base), though Solo 401(k) employer contributions are also salary-based. However, the employee elective deferral ($23,500 for 2025) is available regardless of salary level (as long as salary >= deferral amount)
- **State-level disadvantages:** Some states impose separate S-Corp taxes (e.g., CA 1.5% net income tax, minimum $800)
- **Single year of high income:** Formation and compliance costs may not be recovered in one year

## C-Corporation

### Overview

A C-Corp is taxed at the entity level at a flat 21% rate. Distributions to shareholders are taxed again as qualified dividends (0%/15%/20%). This "double taxation" is generally unfavorable, but there are specific scenarios where C-Corp makes sense.

### When C-Corp Makes Sense

1. **Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS) -- Section 1202:**
   - Exclude up to $10M (or 10x basis) of gain on sale of QSBS
   - Stock must be held 5+ years
   - Corporation must be a C-Corp at issuance
   - Aggregate gross assets must not exceed $50M
   - Active business requirement (excludes professional services, finance, hospitality)
   - This is one of the most powerful tax exclusions in the Code

2. **Retained earnings strategy:**
   - If the business needs to retain significant earnings for growth
   - 21% corporate rate vs. up to 37% individual rate
   - Beware: Accumulated Earnings Tax (penalty for retaining earnings beyond reasonable business needs, $250K threshold for most businesses)

3. **Fringe benefits:**
   - C-Corp shareholder-employees can deduct health insurance, group term life, and other fringe benefits that are not deductible for >2% S-Corp shareholders

4. **Venture capital / IPO path:**
   - VCs typically require C-Corp structure
   - Preferred stock, multiple classes, institutional investors all require C-Corp

### QSBS Disqualified Businesses (Section 1202(e)(3))

The following business types are EXCLUDED from QSBS eligibility. Any corporation whose principal activity falls into these categories cannot issue qualifying QSBS:

1. **Health** -- any trade or business involving the performance of services in the field of health (doctors, dentists, veterinarians, physical therapists, psychologists, etc.)
2. **Law** -- services in the field of law
3. **Engineering** -- services in the field of engineering
4. **Architecture** -- services in the field of architecture
5. **Accounting** -- services in the field of accounting
6. **Actuarial science** -- services in the field of actuarial science
7. **Performing arts** -- services in the field of performing arts
8. **Consulting** -- services in the field of consulting
9. **Athletics** -- services in the field of athletics
10. **Financial services** -- any banking, insurance, financing, leasing, investing, or similar business
11. **Brokerage services** -- any brokerage business
12. **Farming** -- any farming business (including the raising or harvesting of trees)
13. **Mining and natural resources** -- any business involving the production or extraction of products for which percentage depletion is allowable
14. **Hospitality** -- any hotel, motel, restaurant, or similar business

**Additional QSBS requirements often missed:**
- The corporation must use at least 80% (by value) of its assets in the active conduct of one or more qualified businesses during "substantially all" of the shareholder's holding period
- Stock must be acquired at original issuance (in exchange for money, property other than stock, or as compensation for services). Secondary market purchases do NOT qualify.
- The $50M gross asset test is measured at the time of issuance AND at all times after August 10, 1993 and before the issuance
- Stock in an S-Corp does NOT qualify for QSBS (the corporation must be a C-Corp when the stock is issued)
- Section 1202 stacking: each shareholder gets their own $10M / 10x basis exclusion. A married couple filing jointly each have separate limits if they each own stock directly.

### Accumulated Earnings Tax (Section 531)

#### What It Is

The accumulated earnings tax (AET) is a penalty tax imposed on C-Corps that accumulate earnings beyond the reasonable needs of the business for the purpose of avoiding shareholder-level income tax on dividends.

#### Tax Rate and Computation

- **Rate:** 20% of accumulated taxable income (Section 531)
- **Applies in addition to** the regular 21% corporate income tax
- **Accumulated earnings credit (Section 535):**
  - $250,000 for most corporations
  - $150,000 for personal service corporations (accounting, law, engineering, health, etc.)
  - This is a cumulative lifetime credit, not annual. Once accumulated E&P exceeds the threshold, every additional dollar of retained earnings is subject to scrutiny.

#### Section 535 Computation

```
Taxable income
+ Dividends-received deduction add-back
- Federal income tax accrued
- Charitable contributions in excess of 10% limit
- Net capital loss adjustment
= Adjusted taxable income

Adjusted taxable income
- Dividends paid deduction (dividends actually distributed)
- Accumulated earnings credit (greater of $250K/$150K threshold or reasonable business needs)
= Accumulated taxable income

Accumulated taxable income x 20% = Accumulated Earnings Tax
```

#### What Constitutes "Reasonable Business Needs"

The IRS allows retention for specific, definite, and feasible plans:
- Business expansion or acquisition
- Working capital (using the Bardahl formula: one operating cycle of costs)
- Debt retirement
- Product liability or self-insurance reserves
- Realistic contingency plans

**What does NOT justify retention:**
- Vague or indefinite expansion plans
- Investment portfolio accumulation
- Loans to shareholders (treated as constructive dividends)
- Personal investments held inside the corporation

#### Planning

- Document the business purpose for retained earnings annually in board resolutions
- Pay reasonable dividends to reduce accumulated E&P
- Consider making S-Corp election to eliminate the issue entirely (S-Corps are not subject to AET)
- Monitor the $250K/$150K thresholds annually

### Personal Service Corporation Trap (Sections 269A and 448)

#### What Is a Personal Service Corporation?

A C-Corp is a personal service corporation (PSC) if:

1. **The principal activity** is the performance of personal services, AND
2. **Such services are substantially performed** by employee-owners, AND
3. **Employee-owners own more than 10%** of the FMV of outstanding stock

#### Tax Consequences

1. **Flat 21% rate with no benefit of lower brackets:** Before the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act, PSCs were taxed at a flat 35% while other C-Corps had graduated rates. Now the rate is a flat 21% for all C-Corps, so this distinction is less punitive. However, the forced calendar year requirement remains important.

2. **Required calendar year-end (Section 441(i)):** PSCs must use a calendar year unless they establish a business purpose for a fiscal year (very hard to do) or make a Section 444 election (which requires a deposit under Section 7519 roughly equivalent to the deferral benefit).

3. **Section 269A assignment of income:** If a PSC is formed or used to avoid or evade income tax by assigning income to the corporation that would otherwise be earned by the employee-owner, the IRS can allocate income, deductions, credits, exclusions, and other allowances between the corporation and the employee-owner.

4. **Cash method restriction relaxation:** Under Section 448, PSCs can use the cash method of accounting regardless of gross receipts. This is actually a benefit compared to other C-Corps that exceed the $30M gross receipts test.

#### Who Falls Into This Trap

- Solo consultants who incorporate as a C-Corp to retain earnings at 21%
- Physicians, attorneys, engineers, and accountants operating through a C-Corp
- The IRS specifically targets these structures when the PSC pays below-market salaries and retains earnings

**Planning:** For most personal service businesses, an S-Corp is superior to a C-Corp PSC. The S-Corp avoids the AET risk, the PSC classification issues, and allows tax-efficient distributions. The only reason to use a C-Corp for a personal service business is QSBS -- and personal services businesses are disqualified from QSBS anyway.

### C-Corp Disadvantages

- Double taxation on distributions
- 21% entity-level tax even on income distributed as salary (salary is deductible, but distributions are not)
- No pass-through of losses to shareholders
- More complex and expensive compliance
- Personal Holding Company tax risk if primarily investment income
- Accumulated Earnings Tax risk

## PTET Eligibility by Entity Type

Pass-Through Entity Tax (PTET) is a state-level workaround to the personal Federal SALT cap, which is generally $40,000 for 2025 ($20,000 MFS, subject to MAGI-based reduction). It allows the entity to pay state income tax at the entity level, generating a Federal deduction not subject to the personal SALT cap.

| Entity Type | PTET Eligible? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Sole Proprietorship | No | Not an entity |
| Single-Member LLC | Generally No | Most states require 2+ members; some exceptions exist |
| Multi-Member LLC (Partnership) | Yes | In states offering PTET |
| S-Corporation | Yes | In states offering PTET |
| C-Corporation | No | Already pays entity-level tax |

**Key point:** If PTET is a significant benefit (high state income tax + high income), it may justify forming a multi-member entity (e.g., husband-wife LLC) even if a single-member LLC would otherwise suffice.

## Multi-Entity Optimization Strategies

### Why Use Multiple Entities?

Sophisticated taxpayers often separate business activities into multiple entities to achieve specific tax, liability, or operational goals. This is not merely about complexity -- it solves real problems.

### Strategy 1: Separating Activities for PTET Optimization

**Problem:** A high-income taxpayer in a high-tax state has a single S-Corp. The PTET saves them from the SALT cap, but they also have consulting income on Schedule C that does not qualify for PTET.

**Solution:** Form a separate LLC (taxed as partnership or S-Corp) for the consulting activity. Both entities now elect PTET. ALL business income receives the state-level deduction bypass.

### Strategy 2: Isolating Activities for QBI Purposes

**Problem:** A taxpayer has a qualifying business (software development) and a specified service trade or business (consulting). If combined in one entity, the SSTB taint may reduce or eliminate the QBI deduction on all income at higher income levels.

**Solution:** Separate the qualifying business and the SSTB into different entities. The software development entity's QBI is not tainted by the consulting entity's SSTB status. (Note: the IRS has anti-abuse rules under Reg. 1.199A-5(c)(2) for artificially separating activities that are substantially the same, so the businesses must be genuinely distinct.)

### Strategy 3: Liability Isolation

**Problem:** A real estate investor has 10 rental properties and an active consulting business. A lawsuit on one property could theoretically reach others.

**Solution:**
- Each property (or group of properties) in its own LLC
- A holding LLC owns the property LLCs
- The consulting business is in a separate S-Corp
- This "series LLC" or "holding company" structure isolates liability

### Strategy 4: Self-Rental Strategy (With Tax Awareness)

**Problem:** A business owner wants to own the building separately from the operating company for liability protection.

**Solution:** Owner holds the building in an LLC, which rents to the owner's S-Corp. The rent is a deductible expense to the S-Corp and rental income to the LLC. BUT see the self-rental recharacterization rule in REAL-ESTATE.md (Section 469(c)(2) and Reg. 1.469-2(f)(6)) -- the rental income is recharacterized as non-passive, which eliminates passive loss benefits on the rental activity.

### Strategy 5: Separating for State Tax Arbitrage

**Problem:** A taxpayer operates in multiple states. Some states have favorable entity-level taxes or credits that only apply to specific entity types.

**Solution:** Use separate entities for activities in different states. For example, a Wyoming LLC for royalty income (no state income tax) while the operating business is in the taxpayer's home state.

**Caution:** State nexus rules, throwback rules, and the economic substance doctrine can override aggressive multi-entity structures. Each entity must have a genuine business purpose beyond tax reduction.

## Entity Formation Checklist

### Step-by-Step Process

1. **Choose entity type** based on income level, liability needs, and tax goals
2. **Choose state of formation** (usually state of residence/business activity)
3. **File Articles of Organization (LLC) or Articles of Incorporation (Corp)**
   - File with Secretary of State
   - Cost: $50-$500 depending on state
   - Can use registered agent service if needed ($50-$300/year)
4. **Obtain EIN** (Employer Identification Number)
   - Free from IRS (Form SS-4 or online at irs.gov)
   - Required for any entity with employees, or any partnership/corporation
   - Takes minutes online
5. **Draft Operating Agreement (LLC) or Bylaws (Corp)**
   - Not filed with the state but essential for governance
   - Defines ownership, profit/loss allocation, management, dissolution
   - Critical for multi-member entities
6. **Open business bank account**
   - Requires EIN + formation documents
   - Maintain strict separation from personal accounts
7. **File S-Corp election (if applicable)**
   - Form 2553 to IRS
   - Due by March 15 of the first tax year (or within 75 days of formation)
   - All shareholders must sign
8. **Set up payroll (if S-Corp)**
   - Choose provider (Gusto, ADP, Paychex, QuickBooks Payroll)
   - Set salary amount
   - Register for state payroll tax accounts
9. **Register for state/local taxes**
   - State income tax withholding
   - State unemployment insurance
   - Local business licenses/permits as required
10. **Obtain business insurance** as appropriate
    - General liability, professional liability/E&O, etc.
11. **File PTET election (if applicable)**
    - Typically due March 15 of the tax year
    - Check state-specific deadlines and requirements

## Decision Framework

### Quick Decision Tree

```
Is your net business income consistently above $80K-$100K?
+-- NO -> Stay as Sole Prop or SMLLC (Schedule C)
|        +-- Need liability protection? -> Form SMLLC
|        +-- No liability concerns? -> Sole Prop is fine
|
+-- YES -> Consider S-Corp election
         +-- Income $100K-$150K -> S-Corp likely beneficial; run the math
         +-- Income $150K+ -> S-Corp strongly recommended
         +-- Want PTET? -> Need multi-member entity (MMLLC or S-Corp)
         +-- Planning VC funding? -> Consider C-Corp (QSBS benefits)
         +-- Multiple owners with different allocation needs? -> MMLLC (partnership)
         +-- Retaining earnings for growth? -> C-Corp if non-SSTB and QSBS eligible
         +-- Converting from C-Corp? -> Evaluate BIG tax exposure first
```

### Annual Review

Entity selection is not permanent. Review annually based on:
- Changes in income level
- Changes in state of residence (different PTET rules, state taxes)
- Changes in business plans (investors, partners, expansion)
- Changes in tax law
- Life events (marriage, divorce, retirement planning needs)
- Accumulated C-Corp E&P approaching $250K/$150K threshold

## Common Mistakes

1. **Electing S-Corp too early** -- when income does not justify compliance costs
2. **Setting salary too low** -- IRS audit risk; they can reclassify distributions as wages (*Watson v. Commissioner*)
3. **Setting salary too high** -- defeats the purpose of S-Corp election
4. **Ignoring state taxes** -- CA $800 franchise tax, NY LLC fees can erode savings
5. **Missing Form 2553 deadline** -- late elections require reasonable cause statement (Rev. Proc. 2013-30)
6. **Not running actual payroll** -- paying yourself by check without withholding is not compliant
7. **Forgetting PTET elections** -- annual election with strict deadlines
8. **Mixing personal and business funds** -- pierces the corporate veil and invites audit scrutiny
9. **Not tracking S-Corp basis** -- leads to unexpected taxable distributions or disallowed losses
10. **Ignoring BIG tax** when converting C-Corp to S-Corp with appreciated assets
11. **Husband-wife LLC in non-community-property state** without filing Form 1065
12. **C-Corp accumulating earnings past $250K** without documented business purpose
13. **Forming a C-Corp for a personal service business** -- loses QSBS eligibility AND triggers PSC rules
14. **Guaranteeing S-Corp debt and assuming it creates basis** -- it does not; only direct loans count
15. **Taking distributions exceeding AAA** from an S-Corp with legacy C-Corp E&P -- creates unexpected dividend income
