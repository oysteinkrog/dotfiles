# LIKE-KIND EXCHANGE (SECTION 1031): COMPREHENSIVE GUIDE

## Overview

IRC Section 1031 permits taxpayers to defer recognition of capital gains and depreciation
recapture when they exchange real property held for productive use in a trade or business
or for investment for like-kind real property. The gain is not forgiven — it is deferred
by reducing the basis of the replacement property.

**What is deferred:**
- Long-term capital gain (Section 1231 gain)
- Short-term capital gain (if applicable)
- Unrecaptured Section 1250 gain (25% depreciation recapture on real property)
- Net Investment Income Tax (3.8% NIIT) that would apply to recognized gain

**What is NOT deferred:**
- Boot received (cash, non-like-kind property, debt relief) triggers immediate recognition

---

## Like-Kind Definition

**Post-TCJA (2018+): Only real property qualifies for Section 1031.**

"Like-kind" for real property is extremely broad:
- Residential rental for commercial office building: YES
- Raw land for apartment complex: YES
- Fee simple interest for 30-year lease: YES (leases of 30+ years are real property)
- Domestic for domestic: YES
- US property for foreign property: NO (Section 1031(h))

**Qualifying real property includes:**
- Land (improved or unimproved)
- Commercial buildings
- Residential rental property (but NOT primary residence or second home used primarily
  for personal use)
- Industrial facilities
- Mineral rights and water rights (if treated as real property under state law)
- Certain intangibles related to real property (easements, land use permits)
- Delaware Statutory Trust interests (Revenue Ruling 2004-86)

### NOT Eligible for Section 1031

**Post-TCJA, the following are explicitly excluded:**
- Personal property of any kind (machinery, equipment, vehicles, art, collectibles)
- Inventory or property held primarily for sale (dealer property)
- Stocks, bonds, notes, and other securities
- Partnership interests (Section 1031(a)(2)(D))
- Certificates of trust or beneficial interests
- Cryptocurrency / digital assets
- Foreign real property exchanged for US real property (or vice versa)

**Primary residence / vacation home:** Generally not eligible because not held for
investment or business use. However, a taxpayer who converts a primary residence to
a rental property and holds it as investment for a sufficient period may then exchange
it under Section 1031. See Revenue Procedure 2008-16 for safe harbor on dwelling units.

## Qualified Intermediary (QI) Requirement

The taxpayer CANNOT touch the sale proceeds. If the taxpayer has actual or constructive
receipt of funds at any point, the exchange fails.

**QI structure:**
1. Taxpayer enters into exchange agreement with QI BEFORE closing on relinquished property
2. QI is assigned into the purchase/sale agreements
3. Proceeds from sale of relinquished property go directly to QI's escrow account
4. QI holds funds until replacement property closing
5. QI uses funds to acquire replacement property on taxpayer's behalf

**QI disqualified persons:** The QI cannot be the taxpayer's agent — this includes:
- The taxpayer's employee, attorney, accountant, investment banker, or real estate agent
  who acted in those capacities within the prior 2 years (Section 1031(a) regulations)
- Family members or controlled entities of the taxpayer

**QI risk:** Funds held by the QI are NOT FDIC insured (they are in the QI's escrow).
If the QI goes bankrupt, the taxpayer may lose the exchange funds. Use a reputable,
bonded QI with fidelity insurance and segregated accounts.

---

## Timeline Requirements — The Two Clocks

### Clock 1: 45-Day Identification Period
Starting from the date the relinquished property is transferred (closing), the taxpayer
has exactly 45 calendar days to identify potential replacement properties in writing
to the QI.

- The identification must be in writing, signed by the taxpayer
- Must unambiguously describe the property (legal description or street address)
- Must be delivered to the QI (or other party to the exchange, not the taxpayer's agent)
- The 45th day is absolute — no extensions for weekends, holidays, or disasters
  (unless IRS grants formal relief)

### Clock 2: 180-Day Exchange Period
The taxpayer must receive the replacement property by the earlier of:
- 180 calendar days after the transfer of the relinquished property, OR
- The due date (including extensions) of the taxpayer's tax return for the year of transfer

**Critical filing tip:** If the relinquished property is transferred in October-December
and the return is due April 15, the 180-day period may extend past April 15. The taxpayer
MUST file an extension (Form 4868) to preserve the full 180 days.

**Example:** Relinquished property closes November 15, 2025.
- 45-day ID deadline: December 30, 2025
- 180-day close deadline: May 14, 2026
- Tax return due: April 15, 2026 (without extension, taxpayer loses ~29 days)
- Solution: File Form 4868 to extend return to October 15, 2026

## Identification Rules

### Three-Property Rule (most common)
The taxpayer may identify up to 3 replacement properties regardless of their fair market
value. The taxpayer need only acquire one (or more) of the identified properties.

### 200% Rule
The taxpayer may identify any number of replacement properties, provided their aggregate
fair market value does not exceed 200% of the fair market value of the relinquished property.

**Example:** Relinquished property sold for $500,000. Taxpayer can identify unlimited
properties as long as their combined FMV does not exceed $1,000,000.

### 95% Rule
If the taxpayer identifies more than 3 properties AND their aggregate FMV exceeds 200%
of the relinquished property, the taxpayer must actually acquire at least 95% of the
aggregate FMV of all identified properties.

**Practical note:** The 95% rule is extremely difficult to satisfy. Most taxpayers use the
3-property rule or 200% rule.

**Strategy:** Under the 3-property rule, identify 3 carefully selected replacement
properties ranked by preference. If the primary target falls through, you have two
backups. There is no requirement that the identified properties be of equal value.

## Boot — Taxable Portion of the Exchange

"Boot" is anything received in the exchange that is NOT like-kind real property.

### Types of Boot

**Cash boot:** Cash received from the exchange (excess proceeds not reinvested).

**Mortgage boot (debt relief boot):** If the taxpayer's debt on the replacement property
is less than the debt on the relinquished property, the difference is treated as boot
received by the taxpayer.

**Personal property boot:** Any non-real-property received (appliances, furniture,
equipment that is not a structural component of the building).

### Boot Netting Rules

- Cash paid offsets cash received
- Mortgage assumed on replacement offsets mortgage relief on relinquished
- Cash paid CAN offset mortgage boot
- Mortgage assumed CANNOT offset cash boot

### Gain Recognition on Boot

Gain recognized = the LESSER of:
- Boot received (net), OR
- Realized gain on the exchange

You can NEVER recognize a loss in a 1031 exchange.

### Worked Example: Exchange with Mortgage Boot

**Relinquished property:**
- FMV: $800,000
- Adjusted basis: $300,000
- Mortgage: $500,000
- Realized gain: $500,000

**Replacement property:**
- FMV: $700,000
- New mortgage: $400,000
- Cash from exchange: $0

**Boot calculation:**
- Mortgage relief: $500,000 (old) - $400,000 (new) = $100,000 mortgage boot
- Cash boot: $0
- Net boot received: $100,000

**Gain recognized:** Lesser of $100,000 (boot) or $500,000 (realized gain) = $100,000

**Strategy to avoid mortgage boot:** Add $100,000 cash to the exchange. Cash paid offsets
mortgage boot dollar-for-dollar. Borrow the $100,000 if necessary — the interest cost
is almost certainly less than the tax on $100,000 of recognized gain.

---

## Basis Calculation

**Replacement property basis = Adjusted basis of relinquished property
  - Boot received
  + Boot paid
  + Gain recognized
  + Exchange expenses (commissions, legal fees)**

**Using the example above (with $100,000 mortgage boot recognized):**
- Old basis: $300,000
- Less boot received: -$100,000
- Plus gain recognized: +$100,000
- Plus exchange expenses (assume $40,000): +$40,000
- Replacement property basis: $340,000

The deferred gain ($400,000) is embedded in the lower basis of the replacement property.
When the replacement is eventually sold (without another 1031 exchange), the deferred
gain is recognized.

## Depreciation After a 1031 Exchange

The carryover basis from the relinquished property retains its original depreciation
schedule and method. New basis from boot paid or improvements gets a fresh depreciation
schedule.

**Practical approach (two-layer depreciation):**
1. **Layer 1 (carryover):** Continue depreciating the old basis using the remaining useful
   life and method from the relinquished property
2. **Layer 2 (new):** Begin depreciating any additional basis (from boot paid, exchange
   expenses, or improvements) as new property placed in service on the exchange date

**Example:** Relinquished was a 27.5-year residential rental with 10 years remaining on
original basis. Replacement is also residential rental:
- Carryover basis: depreciate over remaining 10 years (same method, same convention)
- Additional basis: depreciate over new 27.5-year life starting from exchange date

**Cost segregation:** A cost segregation study on the replacement property can accelerate
depreciation by reclassifying components to 5-year, 7-year, or 15-year property. This
applies to the NEW basis layer and potentially to components not previously segregated
in the carryover basis.

## Reverse Exchange (Revenue Procedure 2000-37)

In a reverse exchange, the taxpayer acquires the replacement property BEFORE selling
the relinquished property. This is useful in competitive markets where the replacement
must be purchased immediately.

**Structure:**
1. An Exchange Accommodation Titleholder (EAT) — typically a single-purpose LLC
   controlled by the QI — takes title to the "parked" property
2. The EAT can park either the replacement property (most common) or the relinquished property
3. The taxpayer has 45 days from the EAT's acquisition of the parked property to identify
   the relinquished property
4. The entire transaction must be completed within 180 days

**Revenue Procedure 2000-37 safe harbor requirements:**
- Qualified Exchange Accommodation Agreement (QEAA) in writing before EAT takes title
- EAT holds property as beneficial owner (on its own balance sheet)
- EAT cannot hold property for more than 180 days
- Taxpayer cannot have owned the parked property within 180 days before the EAT acquires it

**Cost:** Reverse exchanges are significantly more expensive than forward exchanges due to
the EAT structure, additional legal work, and potential financing costs ($15,000-$50,000+
in fees). However, this cost is usually trivial compared to the tax deferral achieved.

---

## Improvement (Build-to-Suit) Exchange

Allows the taxpayer to use exchange funds to improve the replacement property before
taking title, effectively exchanging into a customized property.

**Structure:**
1. QI (or EAT in a reverse context) acquires the replacement property
2. While QI/EAT holds title, improvements are made using exchange funds
3. Improved property is transferred to taxpayer within the 180-day period
4. Taxpayer receives the property with improvements already in place

**Requirements:**
- All improvements must be completed before the taxpayer takes title
- Any improvements made AFTER the taxpayer takes title are NOT part of the exchange
- The property, as improved, must be identified within the 45-day window

**Common use:** Taxpayer sells a $2 million property, wants to exchange into a $1.5 million
property that needs $500,000 in renovations. The EAT buys the property, makes improvements,
and transfers the $2 million improved property to the taxpayer.

## Delaware Statutory Trust (DST) as Replacement Property

A DST is a legal entity created under Delaware law that holds title to real estate.
Revenue Ruling 2004-86 confirmed that a beneficial interest in a qualifying DST is
treated as a direct interest in real property for Section 1031 purposes.

**Advantages:**
- Passive investment: no management responsibilities
- Monthly income distributions
- Professional management by DST sponsor
- Fractional interests available (invest exact exchange amount)
- Ideal for retiring landlords who want to defer gain without active management
- Can serve as "backup" identified property if primary replacement falls through

**Limitations (the "7 Deadly Sins" from Revenue Ruling 2004-86):**
1. No new capital contributions after closing
2. No renegotiation of existing loans
3. No new loans (except for normal maintenance)
4. No reinvestment of sale proceeds from individual assets
5. No new leases or renegotiation of existing leases (except in defined circumstances)
6. No structural modifications (except for normal maintenance/repairs)
7. Hold period: must hold until DST liquidation or sale (typically 5-10 year horizon)

**Tax reporting:** DST investors receive Form 1099 or K-1 depending on structure.
Depreciation pass-through may generate taxable losses that offset other income.

---

## Tenant-in-Common (TIC) as Replacement Property

Revenue Procedure 2002-22 provides a safe harbor for TIC interests to qualify as direct
real property interests for Section 1031.

**Key requirements:**
- Maximum 35 co-owners
- Each co-owner holds title as tenants-in-common (not as partnership)
- Each co-owner must have the right to transfer, partition, and encumber their own interest
- No requirement to vote or approve other owners' actions
- Management must be limited to customary landlord functions

**TIC vs. DST:**
- TIC: more control, can refinance, make improvements, and re-lease
- DST: purely passive, lower minimums, simpler management
- TIC: risk of co-owner disputes
- DST: risk of illiquidity and limited exit options

## Related Party Rules — Section 1031(f)

Exchanges between related parties (as defined in Sections 267(b) and 707(b)) are subject
to a 2-year holding period requirement.

**Rule:** If either the taxpayer or the related party disposes of the exchanged property
within 2 years, the deferred gain is recognized in the year of the subsequent disposition.

**Related parties include:**
- Family members: siblings, spouse, ancestors, lineal descendants
- Controlled entities: >50% ownership (directly or constructively)
- Certain trusts and beneficiaries

**Exceptions to the 2-year rule:**
- Death of either party
- Involuntary conversion (Section 1033)
- Disposition not for tax avoidance purposes (must demonstrate)

**Practical risk:** The IRS scrutinizes related-party exchanges closely. A related party
exchange followed by a sale within 2 years is presumed to be a disguised sale. Document
the business purpose thoroughly.

## Death and Section 1031: The Ultimate Tax Strategy

**If the taxpayer dies while holding 1031 replacement property, the deferred gain
is NEVER recognized.**

Under Section 1014, heirs receive a stepped-up basis equal to the fair market value at
the date of death. The gain that was deferred through potentially decades of 1031
exchanges is permanently eliminated.

**Example — The "Swap 'til You Drop" Strategy:**
1. Taxpayer buys property for $200,000 in 1990
2. 1031 exchange into $500,000 property in 2000 (deferred gain: $300,000)
3. 1031 exchange into $1,000,000 property in 2010 (deferred gain: $800,000)
4. 1031 exchange into $2,000,000 property in 2020 (deferred gain: $1,800,000)
5. Taxpayer dies in 2025, property worth $2,500,000
6. Heirs receive stepped-up basis of $2,500,000
7. Total deferred gain permanently eliminated: $2,300,000
8. Federal tax saved at 20% LTCG + 3.8% NIIT + 25% unrecaptured §1250: potentially $500,000+

This is one of the most powerful wealth transfer strategies in the tax code.

## Partnership and LLC Considerations

**Fundamental rule:** Partnership interests are NOT eligible for 1031 exchange
(Section 1031(a)(2)(D)). However, the underlying real property owned by a partnership
CAN be exchanged.

### Entity-Level Exchange
The partnership/LLC sells the relinquished property and acquires replacement property.
All partners continue with their same ownership interests. Simplest approach.

### Drop-and-Swap Strategy
For situations where some partners want to exchange and others want cash:

1. Partnership distributes the relinquished property to partners (or to an LLC owned
   by the partners who want to exchange) — this is a Section 731 distribution
2. The exchanging partners (now holding direct ownership) execute a 1031 exchange
3. Non-exchanging partners receive their share as cash and recognize gain

**Timing concern:** The IRS may challenge a drop-and-swap if the distribution and
exchange occur too quickly. Courts have found no specific holding period requirement
(*Magneson v. Commissioner*, 753 F.2d 1490), but practitioners generally recommend
holding the distributed property for at least 12 months if possible to establish
investment intent.

### Swap-and-Drop Strategy
1. Partnership executes the 1031 exchange
2. After the exchange, the partnership distributes interests in the replacement property
   to partners who want to go their separate ways

**Risk:** IRS may argue the exchange was a disguised sale. Document investment intent.

## State Tax Considerations

**Most states conform to federal Section 1031.** However:

### California (Form 3840)
California requires filing Form 3840 (California Like-Kind Exchange) EVERY YEAR that
the taxpayer holds replacement property from a 1031 exchange. This is a tracking
mechanism — California wants to ensure it collects tax if the taxpayer later moves
out of state and sells the property.

**Claw-back risk:** If a California taxpayer does a 1031 exchange and then moves to a
no-income-tax state before selling the replacement, California will still tax the
deferred gain attributable to the period the property was held in California.

### Other State Issues
- Some states do not conform to Section 1031 (verify for each state)
- Multi-state exchanges: gain may be sourced to the state where the relinquished property
  was located, regardless of where the taxpayer resides
- PTET implications: if gain flows through a pass-through entity, state PTET may apply

## Depreciation Recapture Through Exchanges

**Unrecaptured Section 1250 gain is NOT eliminated by a 1031 exchange.** It carries
through to the replacement property.

When the replacement property is eventually sold (without another 1031 exchange), the
accumulated unrecaptured Section 1250 gain from ALL prior exchanges is recognized at
the 25% rate.

**Example:**
- Property 1: $100,000 depreciation taken
- 1031 into Property 2: $100,000 unrecaptured Section 1250 gain carries through
- Property 2: $80,000 additional depreciation taken
- Sale of Property 2: $180,000 total unrecaptured Section 1250 gain taxed at 25%

**The only way to eliminate carried-over depreciation recapture:** Hold the property
until death (stepped-up basis under Section 1014 eliminates ALL deferred gain, including
depreciation recapture).

## Reporting Requirements

**Form 8824: Like-Kind Exchanges** — filed for the year in which the exchange occurs.

Required information:
- Description of both properties
- Dates of identification and transfer
- Relationship between parties (if related)
- FMV of like-kind and non-like-kind property received
- Adjusted basis and realized gain/loss calculation
- Recognized gain and deferred gain

**Subsequent years:** No annual federal filing required (unlike California's Form 3840).
However, the taxpayer must maintain records of the exchange indefinitely to support the
basis of the replacement property.

## Worked Examples

### Example 1: Simple Exchange (No Boot)
- Sell rental house: FMV $600,000, basis $250,000, no mortgage
- Buy replacement: FMV $650,000, pay $50,000 cash plus $600,000 from exchange
- Boot: $0 (taxpayer paid more than received)
- Gain recognized: $0
- New basis: $250,000 (old basis) + $50,000 (cash paid) = $300,000
- Deferred gain: $350,000

### Example 2: Exchange with Cash Boot
- Sell commercial building: FMV $1,000,000, basis $400,000, mortgage $300,000
- Buy replacement: FMV $800,000, new mortgage $200,000, receive $100,000 cash
- Mortgage boot: $300,000 - $200,000 = $100,000
- Cash boot: $100,000
- Total boot: $200,000
- Realized gain: $600,000
- Gain recognized: $200,000 (lesser of boot and realized gain)
- Tax on recognized gain (assume 20% LTCG + 3.8% NIIT): $47,600
- New basis: $400,000 - $200,000 + $200,000 = $400,000
- Deferred gain: $400,000

### Example 3: Reverse Exchange
- Taxpayer finds ideal replacement property: $900,000
- EAT acquires replacement and parks it
- 30 days later: taxpayer identifies relinquished property (their current rental, FMV $750,000)
- 90 days later: relinquished property sells for $750,000
- QI transfers $750,000 to EAT
- Taxpayer pays $150,000 additional cash
- EAT transfers replacement to taxpayer
- Total time: 120 days (within 180-day limit)
- Result: successful reverse exchange with $150,000 cash "boot paid" increasing basis

### Example 4: DST Replacement
- Retired landlord sells apartment building: FMV $2,000,000, basis $500,000
- Does not want management burden
- Uses exchange proceeds to purchase interests in 2 DSTs:
  - DST A (multifamily, Southeast): $1,200,000
  - DST B (net-lease retail, Midwest): $800,000
- Monthly distributions: approximately $8,000-$10,000 combined
- No management responsibility
- Gain deferred: $1,500,000
- Tax deferred (20% + 3.8% + 25% depreciation recap): approximately $400,000

## Common Mistakes and Audit Issues

1. **Missing the 45-day deadline:** NO extensions. Mark the calendar immediately upon closing.
2. **Constructive receipt of funds:** Never let proceeds flow through your bank account.
3. **Failing to file extension when exchange spans year-end:** The 180-day period can be
   shortened if return due date arrives first.
4. **Inadequate identification:** Be specific — "a property in Phoenix" is insufficient.
5. **Personal use property:** Vacation homes used primarily for personal enjoyment do not qualify.
6. **Related party dispositions within 2 years:** Triggers deferred gain.
7. **Not accounting for mortgage boot:** Taxpayers focus on cash but forget debt relief is boot.
8. **Dealer property:** If IRS classifies you as a dealer (frequent buying/selling), inventory
   is NOT eligible for 1031.
9. **Mixed-use property:** Must allocate between qualifying (business/investment) and
   non-qualifying (personal use) portions.
10. **QI bankruptcy risk:** Use established, bonded intermediaries with segregated accounts.
