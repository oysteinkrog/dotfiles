# Charitable Giving Tax Strategies

## Overview

Charitable giving provides both personal fulfillment and tax benefits. With the standard deduction at $31,500 (MFJ, 2025 under OBBBA), strategic timing and vehicle selection are critical to maximizing the tax benefit of charitable contributions.

---

## Cash Donations

### Deduction Rules

- Deductible on Schedule A as an itemized deduction
- **AGI limit: 60%** of AGI for cash contributions to public charities (50% for organizations that are not 50% limit organizations but are still public charities)
- **AGI limit: 30%** for cash contributions to private foundations
- Excess contributions carry forward for 5 years
- Must itemize to benefit (standard deduction includes no charitable component for 2025)

### Documentation and Substantiation Requirements by Amount

| Donation Amount | Required Documentation | Key Details |
|---|---|---|
| Under $250 | Bank record, receipt, or written acknowledgment from charity | Canceled check, bank/credit card statement, or charity receipt showing name, date, amount |
| $250 - $499 | Contemporaneous written acknowledgment (CWA) from charity | Must state: (1) amount of cash, (2) description of non-cash property, (3) whether charity provided goods/services in return, (4) good faith estimate of value of goods/services. Must be obtained BEFORE filing the return. |
| $500 - $4,999 | CWA + Form 8283 Section A | Form 8283 Section A describes the property, date acquired, donor's cost basis, FMV, and method of valuation |
| $5,000 - $499,999 (non-cash) | CWA + Form 8283 Section B + Qualified Appraisal | Appraisal must be conducted no earlier than 60 days before donation and no later than the due date (including extensions) of the return. Appraiser must be a qualified appraiser (not the donor, donee, or party to the transaction). Appraisal fee cannot be based on a percentage of appraised value. |
| $500,000+ (non-cash) | All of the above + attach appraisal to the return | The actual appraisal document must be attached to the tax return (not just Form 8283) |

**Critical: The CWA for $250+ donations must be obtained BEFORE the return is filed.** A post-filing acknowledgment does not satisfy the requirement. In Durden v. Commissioner (T.C. Memo 2012-140), the Tax Court denied a $22,517 charitable deduction because the taxpayers did not obtain a contemporaneous written acknowledgment before filing.

### Cash Donation Timing

- Donations made by credit card in December are deductible in that year (even if the credit card bill is paid in January)
- Checks mailed by December 31 are deductible in that year (postmark date controls)
- Pledges are NOT deductible until actually paid
- Electronic transfers/ACH: deductible when the transfer is initiated (debited from account)

## Property Donations

### Appreciated Property (Held Over 1 Year)

- **Deduction: Fair Market Value** (not cost basis)
- **AGI limit: 30%** of AGI for appreciated property to public charities (20% for private foundations)
- **No capital gains tax** on the appreciation
- 5-year carryforward for excess contributions

### The Section 170(e)(1)(A) Reduction Rule

This rule limits the deduction for donations of ordinary income property and short-term capital gain property:

**General rule:** If the donated property would produce ordinary income or short-term capital gain if sold, the charitable deduction is reduced by the amount of gain that would have been ordinary income or short-term capital gain.

**Practical effect:** The deduction equals the donor's BASIS, not FMV, for:
- Property held 1 year or less (short-term capital gain property)
- Inventory
- Property subject to depreciation recapture
- Self-created works (art, manuscripts, compositions by the creator)
- S-Corp stock that has appreciated -- but see exception below

**Key exception: publicly traded stock held over 1 year** -- the full FMV deduction is available regardless of the gain character. This is why donating appreciated publicly traded stock is so powerful.

**S-Corp and partnership pass-through:**
- For S-Corps: charitable contributions pass through to shareholders on Schedule K-1 and are deducted at the individual level subject to 170(b) AGI limits. The contribution reduces the shareholder's stock basis.
- For partnerships: same pass-through treatment. The charitable contribution is a separately stated item on Schedule K-1.
- The 170(e)(1)(A) reduction applies at the entity level when determining the deduction amount.

### Example: Appreciated Stock

```
Stock purchased for $5,000, now worth $25,000 (held over 1 year)

If sold and cash donated:
  Capital gains tax: ($25,000 - $5,000) x 20% = $4,000
  NIIT (if applicable): ($25,000 - $5,000) x 3.8% = $760
  Cash available to donate: $20,240
  Tax deduction: $20,240

If stock donated directly:
  Capital gains tax: $0
  NIIT: $0
  Deduction: $25,000 (full FMV)
  Tax benefit vs. selling first: saves $4,760 in taxes + extra $4,760 deduction
```

### Tangible Personal Property

- If the charity uses the property for its exempt purpose ("related use"): deduction at FMV
- If the charity sells the property ("unrelated use"): deduction limited to basis
- Items over $5,000 require a qualified appraisal
- If claimed deduction >$500 and charity disposes within 3 years, charity must file Form 8282 and donor's deduction may be recaptured

### Vehicle Donations

- If the charity sells the vehicle: deduction is limited to the gross sale proceeds (Form 1098-C)
- If the charity uses or materially improves the vehicle: deduction at FMV
- Must file Form 1098-C with the return for vehicles worth over $500

### Bargain Sale to Charity

A bargain sale is a transfer of property to a charity for less than FMV -- part sale, part gift.

**Basis allocation:**
The donor's basis must be allocated between the sale portion and the gift portion:

```
Basis allocated to sale = Total basis x (Amount realized / FMV)
Basis allocated to gift = Total basis x (Gift portion / FMV)

Example:
Property FMV: $100,000
Donor's basis: $20,000
Sale price to charity: $40,000

Basis allocated to sale: $20,000 x ($40,000 / $100,000) = $8,000
Capital gain on sale: $40,000 - $8,000 = $32,000
Charitable deduction: $100,000 - $40,000 = $60,000 (subject to AGI limits)
Basis allocated to gift: $20,000 x ($60,000 / $100,000) = $12,000
```

The donor recognizes gain on the sale portion and claims a charitable deduction on the gift portion. This is sometimes used when the donor needs partial liquidity from the property.

## Donor-Advised Fund (DAF) Strategy

### What It Is

A DAF is a charitable giving account that provides an immediate tax deduction when funded, with grants to charities distributed over time at the donor's recommendation.

### Bunching Strategy

The core concept: instead of giving $10,000/year (which may not push total itemized deductions above the standard deduction), contribute 3-5 years of giving at once.

```
Strategy: Bunch 3 years of $10,000 annual giving into one year

Year 1 (Bunch): Contribute $30,000 to DAF
  Itemized deductions: $20,000 SALT + $8,000 mortgage + $30,000 charitable = $58,000
  Benefit over standard deduction ($31,500): $26,500

Year 2: Take standard deduction ($31,500)
Year 3: Take standard deduction ($31,500)

Total deductions over 3 years: $58,000 + $31,500 + $31,500 = $121,000
Without bunching: max($38,000, $31,500) x 3 = $114,000 (if itemizing) or $94,500 (if standard)
Additional deductions from bunching: $7,000 to $26,500 depending on other deductions
```

### Funding a DAF with Appreciated Stock

- Transfer appreciated stock directly to the DAF
- Receive immediate deduction at FMV
- DAF sells the stock with no capital gains tax (it is a charity)
- Double benefit: FMV deduction + capital gains avoidance

### DAF Providers and Minimums

| Provider | Minimum Initial Contribution | Minimum Grant |
|---|---|---|
| Fidelity Charitable | $5,000 | $50 |
| Schwab Charitable | $5,000 | $50 |
| Vanguard Charitable | $25,000 | $500 |
| National Philanthropic Trust | $5,000 | $250 |
| Community Foundations | Varies | Varies |

### DAF Rules

- Contributions are irrevocable (cannot be returned to the donor)
- Grants go to IRS-recognized 501(c)(3) organizations only
- No direct benefit to the donor from grants (no event tickets, memberships with benefits, etc.)
- Investments within the DAF grow tax-free
- No minimum distribution requirement (unlike private foundations)
- Grants can be made anonymously

## Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCD)

### What It Is

A direct transfer from an IRA to a qualified charity. The distribution is excluded from gross income.

### Requirements

- Taxpayer must be age 70 1/2 or older
- Maximum: $108,000 per person per year (2025; indexed for inflation)
- Must be paid directly from the IRA to the charity (not to the taxpayer first)
- Applies to Traditional IRAs (not SEP or SIMPLE IRAs if employer contributions were made in the same year)
- Charity must be a 501(c)(3) (NOT a DAF, private foundation, or supporting organization)

### Tax Benefits

- QCD is excluded from gross income (does not appear on AGI)
- Reduces AGI (which affects many other tax calculations: Medicare premiums, Social Security taxation, ACA subsidies, etc.)
- Satisfies Required Minimum Distribution (RMD) for the year
- Benefits even taxpayers who take the standard deduction (since it reduces AGI rather than being an itemized deduction)

### QCD + RMD Strategy: The Triple Benefit

The QCD is one of the most powerful tools for retirees because it simultaneously:

1. **Satisfies the RMD** -- the QCD counts toward the year's RMD requirement
2. **Excludes income from AGI** -- unlike a normal RMD (which is taxable) and a charitable deduction (which only reduces taxable income if itemizing)
3. **Cascading AGI reduction** -- lower AGI reduces:
   - Taxable Social Security benefits (up to 85% of SS is taxable based on combined income)
   - Medicare IRMAA surcharges ($1,000-$6,000+/year per person for high-income retirees)
   - ACA premium credit clawback (for early retirees on marketplace plans)
   - Net Investment Income Tax threshold ($250,000 MFJ)
   - Capital gains tax bracket thresholds
   - State income tax (in states that use federal AGI as starting point)

### QCD vs. Itemized Charitable Deduction

| Feature | QCD | Itemized Deduction |
|---|---|---|
| Reduces AGI | Yes | No (below-the-line) |
| Requires itemizing | No | Yes |
| Available age | 70.5+ | Any age |
| Maximum | $108,000 | 60% of AGI (cash) |
| Satisfies RMD | Yes | No |
| Affects Medicare premiums (IRMAA) | Yes (reduces MAGI) | No |
| Affects SS taxation | Yes (reduces combined income) | No |
| Affects ACA subsidies | Yes (reduces MAGI) | No |

For taxpayers age 70.5+ who do not itemize, the QCD is strictly superior to a regular charitable deduction. Even for taxpayers who DO itemize, the QCD is often superior because of the AGI-related cascading benefits.

## Charitable Remainder Trusts (CRT)

### Types

**Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust (CRAT):**
- Fixed annuity amount paid to income beneficiary each year
- Annuity must be at least 5% and no more than 50% of initial trust value
- No additional contributions after funding
- Trust terminates when annuity exceeds trust value (or at specified date/beneficiary death)

**Charitable Remainder Unitrust (CRUT):**
- Fixed percentage of trust value (revalued annually) paid to income beneficiary
- Percentage must be at least 5% and no more than 50%
- Additional contributions permitted
- NIMCRUT variation: pays lesser of unitrust amount or net income, with make-up provisions

### Tax Benefits

- Partial charitable deduction in the year funded (present value of the remainder interest)
- Trust is exempt from capital gains tax on asset sales within the trust
- Income distributions are taxable to the beneficiary under a 4-tier system: (1) ordinary income, (2) capital gains, (3) tax-exempt income, (4) return of corpus
- Estate tax charitable deduction for the remainder interest

### CRT for Concentrated Stock Positions

One of the most powerful applications:

```
Scenario: Founder holds $5M of company stock with $100K basis

Direct sale:
  Capital gain: $4,900,000
  Federal LTCG tax (20%): $980,000
  NIIT (3.8%): $186,200
  State tax (CA 13.3%): $651,700
  Total tax: ~$1,817,900
  Net after tax: ~$3,182,100

CRT strategy:
  1. Contribute $5M stock to CRUT (5% payout)
  2. Partial charitable deduction: ~$1,200,000 (depends on term, rate, age)
  3. CRUT sells stock: $0 capital gains tax (trust is tax-exempt entity)
  4. CRUT reinvests $5,000,000 (fully diversified)
  5. Annual income: $250,000/year (5% of value, fluctuates)
  6. Income is taxable as distributed (but spread over many years)
  7. At death/term end: remainder goes to charity

Benefits:
  - Avoid $1.8M in immediate capital gains tax
  - Charitable deduction saves ~$444,000 (at 37% rate)
  - Income stream of $250K+/year
  - Diversified portfolio instead of concentrated position
  - Estate tax charitable deduction for remainder
```

### Limitations

- Irrevocable -- assets cannot be recovered
- The 10% remainder test: the present value of the remainder interest must be at least 10% of the initial contribution
- Complex administration, annual filings (Form 5227)
- Income is eventually taxable (deferred, not eliminated)

## Charitable Lead Trusts (CLT)

### What It Is

The inverse of a CRT: the charity receives income for a term of years, and the remainder passes to heirs (typically children/grandchildren) at a reduced transfer tax cost.

### Types

**Charitable Lead Annuity Trust (CLAT):**
- Fixed annuity paid to charity each year
- If trust growth exceeds the IRS 7520 rate, excess passes to heirs gift/estate-tax-free
- "Zeroed-out" CLAT: annuity stream sized so present value of charitable interest = FMV of assets transferred, resulting in $0 gift tax

**Charitable Lead Unitrust (CLUT):**
- Fixed percentage of annually-revalued trust value paid to charity
- Less commonly used than CLAT

### Grantor vs. Non-Grantor

**Grantor CLT:**
- Donor gets income tax deduction in year trust is funded (present value of charity's income stream)
- Donor is taxed on trust income each year (even though charity receives it)
- Best when: donor has a very high-income year and wants a large upfront deduction

**Non-Grantor CLT:**
- No income tax deduction to donor
- Trust pays its own income taxes (and gets charitable deduction for amounts paid to charity)
- Transfer tax benefit: assets pass to heirs at reduced gift/estate tax value
- Best when: primary goal is wealth transfer, not income tax reduction

### Worked Example (CLAT)

```
Grantor transfers $5M to a 20-year CLAT, 7520 rate = 5.4%
Annual annuity to charity: $400,000/year
Present value of annuity stream (at 7520 rate): ~$4,800,000
Taxable gift: $5,000,000 - $4,800,000 = $200,000

If trust assets grow at 8%/year:
  After 20 years, trust value = ~$5,800,000
  Charity received: $8,000,000 total
  Heirs receive: ~$5,800,000 with only $200,000 of gift tax cost
  Transfer tax savings: ($5,800,000 - $200,000) x 40% = ~$2,240,000
```

## Pooled Income Funds

### What It Is

A pooled income fund is a trust maintained by a public charity into which multiple donors contribute and receive a proportional share of the fund's income for life. Upon the donor's death, the donor's share of the fund goes to the charity.

### How It Works

1. Donor contributes cash or property to the charity's pooled income fund (irrevocable)
2. Donor (and/or other income beneficiary) receives a proportional share of the fund's net investment income for life
3. At the death of the last income beneficiary, the donor's portion goes to the charity
4. Donor receives a charitable income tax deduction in the year of contribution equal to the present value of the remainder interest

### Tax Treatment

- Charitable deduction: present value of remainder interest (depends on age and fund's historical return rate)
- Income received: taxable as ordinary income (the fund cannot allocate capital gains to beneficiaries)
- No tax-exempt income can be earned by the fund (unlike a CRT)
- Capital gains on contributed property: fund sells tax-free (similar to CRT)

### When It Makes Sense

- Donor wants lifetime income + charitable deduction but contribution is too small for a CRT
- No minimum investment at some charities (vs. $100K+ typical for CRT)
- Simpler administration than a standalone CRT
- Downside: income fluctuates with fund performance, and returns have historically been lower than CRTs

## Charitable Gift Annuity (CGA)

### What It Is

A contract between a donor and a charity where the donor transfers assets and the charity agrees to pay a fixed annuity for the donor's life (or joint lives). Part of the annuity is a tax-free return of basis; the remainder is taxable.

### How It Works

1. Donor transfers cash or property to charity
2. Charity pays fixed annuity for donor's life (rates set by American Council on Gift Annuities)
3. Donor receives partial charitable deduction in year of gift
4. Each payment is split: part tax-free return of basis, part ordinary income, part capital gain (if funded with appreciated property)

### Sample Rates (ACGA suggested rates, 2025)

| Age | Single-Life Rate |
|---|---|
| 65 | 5.4% |
| 70 | 5.8% |
| 75 | 6.4% |
| 80 | 7.4% |
| 85 | 8.6% |
| 90+ | 9.8%+ |

### Tax Treatment of Each Payment

If funded with appreciated property, each annuity payment during the donor's life expectancy is split:
- **Tax-free return of basis portion:** (Basis / Expected return) of each payment
- **Capital gain portion:** (Unrealized appreciation / Expected return) of each payment
- **Ordinary income portion:** remainder

After the donor's life expectancy is exceeded, the entire payment becomes ordinary income.

### Deferred Gift Annuity

Payments start at a future date (e.g., retirement). Higher annuity rate because payments are deferred. Larger charitable deduction because the remainder interest is worth more.

## Private Foundations

### AGI Limitations

Private foundations have LOWER AGI deduction limits than public charities:

| Contribution Type | Public Charity | Private Foundation |
|---|---|---|
| Cash | 60% of AGI | 30% of AGI |
| Appreciated property (LTCG) | 30% of AGI | 20% of AGI |
| Publicly traded stock (LTCG) | 30% of AGI (FMV deduction) | 20% of AGI (FMV deduction) |
| Other appreciated property | 30% of AGI (FMV) | 20% of AGI (basis only, not FMV) |

### Excise Tax on Net Investment Income

Private foundations pay a **1.39%** excise tax on net investment income (IRC 4940). This includes:
- Interest, dividends, rents, royalties
- Net capital gains
- Calculated on Form 990-PF

### Minimum Distribution Requirement

Private foundations must distribute at least **5%** of the average FMV of non-charitable-use assets annually for charitable purposes. This includes:
- Grants to other charities
- Direct charitable activities
- Reasonable administrative expenses related to charitable activities

Failure to meet the 5% requirement triggers a 30% excise tax on the undistributed amount (IRC 4942).

### Self-Dealing Rules (IRC 4941)

Extremely strict rules prohibit virtually all transactions between the foundation and "disqualified persons" (founders, substantial contributors, family members, foundation managers):

- **Prohibited:** Sales/purchases, leases, loans, furnishing goods/services, compensation (except reasonable pay for personal services), transferring income/assets
- **Penalty:** 10% excise tax on the self-dealer (5% on foundation manager who knowingly participates)
- **Correction required:** The transaction must be undone

**Example:** Founder cannot rent office space to the foundation (even at below-market rate). Founder cannot purchase art from the foundation. Founder's children cannot receive grants from the foundation for educational purposes.

### When a Private Foundation Makes Sense

- Donor wants maximum control over charitable giving
- Family involvement in philanthropy (family members can serve as directors/officers)
- Endowment model: principal grows, 5% distributed annually
- Legacy/naming (the "Smith Family Foundation")
- Typically requires $1M+ in initial funding to justify administrative costs ($5K-$20K/year)

## Conservation Easements

### What They Are

A legal agreement where a landowner permanently restricts development rights on their property, donating those rights to a qualified land trust or government entity.

### Requirements

1. **Qualified real property interest:** Must be a restriction on use (not a fee simple transfer)
2. **Qualified organization:** Land trust or government entity that will enforce the restriction
3. **Conservation purpose:** Must serve one of: preservation of land for outdoor recreation/education, protection of natural habitat, preservation of open space (including farmland), preservation of historically important land/structures
4. **Perpetuity requirement:** The restriction must be permanent and enforceable in perpetuity -- this is where many easements fail on audit

### Tax Benefit

- Deduction equals the reduction in property value caused by the easement
- Can be up to 50% of AGI (100% for qualified farmers/ranchers)
- 15-year carryforward for excess contributions

### IRS Scrutiny and Listed Transactions

**Syndicated conservation easement transactions are listed transactions (Notice 2017-10).** The IRS defines a syndicated easement as one where the deduction claimed is 2.5x or more the investor's investment.

- IRS has aggressively challenged inflated appraisals in these transactions
- Tax Court has ruled against taxpayers in numerous cases (e.g., TOT Property Holdings, Plateau Holdings)
- Penalties for overvaluation: 20% accuracy-related penalty (40% for gross valuation misstatement = value claimed is 200%+ of correct value)
- Participants must disclose listed transactions on Form 8886 or face $10,000+ penalties
- Material advisors must file Form 8918

### Legitimate vs. Abusive

**Legitimate:** Family farm/ranch with genuine conservation value, reasonable appraisal by qualified independent appraiser, donated to established land trust, property has genuine development potential that is being permanently restricted

**Abusive:** Syndicated deals where investors buy into a partnership, the partnership donates an easement with inflated appraisal, investors claim deductions 4-5x their investment. The IRS treats these as listed transactions and will challenge them.

## State-Specific Charitable Deduction Rules

### States with No Income Tax

AK, FL, NV, NH (limited), SD, TN, TX, WA, WY -- charitable deductions have no state income tax benefit.

### States That Conform to Federal

Most states that have an income tax follow Federal itemized deduction rules for charitable contributions. However:

### Notable State Differences

- **NY:** Allows charitable deduction for taxpayers with AGI over $1M (who are otherwise subject to limited itemized deductions)
- **CA:** Conforms to Federal charitable rules; 50%/30%/20% AGI limits apply
- **NJ:** Does not allow charitable deductions on the state return (no itemized deductions)
- **MA:** Does not allow a separate charitable deduction (part of a limited exemption system)
- **OR:** Offers a charitable deduction credit for non-itemizers (Oregon Cultural Trust credit, up to match of prior year cultural giving)
- **VA:** Neighborhood Assistance Program tax credits; Land Preservation Tax Credit (up to $50K/year, transferable)
- **MT:** Qualified endowment contribution credit (up to $10,000) on top of federal deduction

### State Tax Credit Charitable Programs

Several states offer tax credits (not just deductions) for contributions to designated organizations:
- **AZ:** Dollar-for-dollar credits for contributions to School Tuition Organizations ($1,459 single / $2,435 MFJ for 2025) and other qualifying charities ($470 single / $938 MFJ)
- **GA:** Dollar-for-dollar credits for Student Scholarship Organizations (up to $2,500 single / $5,000 MFJ)
- **VA:** Neighborhood Assistance Program tax credits
- **MT:** Qualified Endowment Tax Credit (10% of first $10,000 contributed)
- **Many others:** Check state-specific rules for credit-eligible charitable programs

These credits reduce state tax liability dollar-for-dollar, which is more valuable than a deduction. And the Federal charitable deduction still applies (though the state credit received may reduce the Federal deduction amount under some circumstances per IRS Notice 2019-12 safe harbor -- the donor may treat state credits of 15% or less of the contribution amount as having no effect on the Federal deduction; credits exceeding 15% reduce the Federal deduction by the credit amount).

## Common Mistakes

1. **Not getting contemporaneous written acknowledgment** for donations $250+ before filing -- this is a strict requirement and courts have denied large deductions for lack of documentation
2. **Claiming deduction for donations to individuals** (GoFundMe personal campaigns are NOT deductible)
3. **Overvaluing non-cash donations** without qualified appraisal -- items over $5,000 require a qualified appraisal; items over $500,000 require attaching the appraisal to the return
4. **Donating appreciated stock by selling first** -- donate directly to avoid capital gains
5. **Missing the bunching opportunity** -- giving the same amount every year when bunching would save money
6. **Using QCD funds for a DAF** -- QCDs cannot go to DAFs, private foundations, or supporting organizations
7. **Not checking charity status** -- verify 501(c)(3) status at IRS Tax Exempt Organization Search
8. **Deducting the full amount when receiving something in return** -- subtract the FMV of any benefit received (dinner, event tickets, etc.); the quid pro quo threshold is $75
9. **Ignoring the private foundation 30% AGI limit** -- private foundation cash donations are limited to 30% of AGI (not 60%)
10. **Failing to allocate basis in a bargain sale** -- the entire gain on the sale portion is taxable
11. **Donating ordinary income property at FMV** -- deduction is limited to basis under 170(e)(1)(A)
12. **Not filing Form 8283 for non-cash donations over $500** -- deduction will be disallowed
13. **Participating in syndicated conservation easements** -- listed transactions with severe penalty exposure
14. **Missing the CRT 10% remainder test** -- the trust is invalid if the remainder interest is less than 10% of the initial contribution
