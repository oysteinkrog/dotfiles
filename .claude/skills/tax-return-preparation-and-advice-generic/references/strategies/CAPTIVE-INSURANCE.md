# CAPTIVE INSURANCE (Section 831(b) Micro-Captives)

## Overview

- A captive insurance company is a wholly-owned subsidiary that insures risks of the parent business
- Section 831(b) election allows small captives with up to $2.8M in net premiums (2025, indexed) to be taxed only on investment income — premiums received are tax-free to the captive
- The parent business deducts premium payments as ordinary business expenses
- Net effect: income shifts from the operating business (taxed at full rate) to the captive (taxed only on investment income)

---

## How It Works

- Operating business identifies insurable risks not adequately covered by commercial insurance
- Captive insurance company is formed (often in favorable domiciles: Vermont, Delaware, Utah, Bermuda, Cayman Islands)
- Operating business pays premiums to the captive for coverage of these risks
- Captive must operate as a real insurance company: actuarial analysis, policy issuance, claims administration
- Captive invests premium reserves; investment income is taxable
- If captive has no claims, reserves can eventually be distributed to shareholders (as dividends, taxed at qualified dividend rates, or via liquidation at capital gain rates)

---

## IRS Requirements for Legitimate Captives

- **Risk shifting**: the insured entity must genuinely shift risk to the captive
- **Risk distribution**: the captive must have adequate risk distribution (Brother-sister arrangements or third-party policies help)
- **Insurance in its commonly accepted sense**: economic substance, not a tax avoidance vehicle
- **Arm's length premiums**: actuarially determined, not inflated
- **Claims history**: a captive that never pays claims raises red flags

---

## IRS Scrutiny and Enforcement

- **Listed Transaction (Notice 2016-66)**: Certain micro-captive transactions are listed transactions requiring disclosure on Form 8886
- **Avrahami v. Commissioner (2017)**: IRS won — premiums were not arm's length, captive lacked risk distribution
- **Reserve Mechanical Corp v. Commissioner (2018)**: IRS won — lack of risk shifting and distribution
- **Criminal prosecutions**: 9+ guilty pleas in promoter cases, sentences of 23-25 years
- **IRS settlement program**: offered to taxpayers willing to concede deductions
- **$36B+ in fraudulent deductions identified**

---

## When Captive Insurance IS Appropriate

- Business with genuine, quantifiable risks not fully covered commercially (employment practices liability, cyber risk, product recall, business interruption, key person)
- Annual premiums $500K-$2.8M based on actuarial analysis
- Business willing to operate the captive as a real insurance company
- Long-term commitment (not a one-year tax play)
- Professional management: captive manager, independent actuary, claims administrator

---

## When Captive Insurance IS NOT Appropriate

- No genuine insurance need (pure tax motivation)
- Inflated premiums divorced from actuarial reality
- Captive never pays claims
- Promoter-driven arrangements with cookie-cutter policies
- Premium amounts that "coincidentally" match desired tax deductions

---

## Risk Assessment

- **Audit risk: HIGH** — IRS has this as a priority compliance campaign
- Legitimate, well-structured captives with genuine insurance purpose: defensible
- Promoter-driven, tax-motivated arrangements: extremely risky, potential penalties + interest + criminal exposure
- Penalty exposure: 20% accuracy penalty (substantial understatement) or 40% penalty (listed transaction)

---

## KEY TAKEAWAYS

1. Properly structured captive insurance is legal and can provide genuine risk management + tax benefits
2. IRS enforcement is aggressive — this is NOT a casual strategy
3. Must have genuine insurable risks, arm's length premiums, and real claims activity
4. $2.8M premium cap for Section 831(b) election (2025, indexed)
5. Requires professional captive management, independent actuary, and experienced tax counsel
6. Listed transaction disclosure may be required (Form 8886)
7. The income shifting benefit is real: premiums deductible by parent, tax-free to captive
8. Exit strategies: dividends (qualified rate) or liquidation (capital gains rate)
9. Do NOT pursue without specialized captive insurance counsel
