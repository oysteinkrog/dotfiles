# PRIVATE PLACEMENT LIFE INSURANCE (PPLI) — Comprehensive Guide

## Overview

Private Placement Life Insurance (PPLI) wraps an investment portfolio inside a variable universal life insurance policy, converting taxable investment income into tax-free growth under IRC ss7702. All gains, dividends, interest, and trading profits inside the policy compound tax-free. At death, the entire value passes to beneficiaries income-tax-free under IRC ss101(a). Access during life is available via policy loans (tax-free) and withdrawals up to basis (tax-free).

PPLI is the premier tax shelter for ultra-high-net-worth investors willing to commit $1M+ in premiums. It is particularly powerful for tax-inefficient investments — hedge funds generating short-term gains, high-yield credit, actively traded portfolios — where annual tax drag on a taxable account can exceed 3-5% of assets. PPLI eliminates that drag entirely.

**OBBBA (One Big Beautiful Bill Act, signed July 4, 2025) did NOT curtail PPLI.** The insurance wrapper structure remains fully intact and available. No premium limits, no investment restrictions, and no new reporting requirements were imposed on PPLI policies.

## HOW PPLI WORKS — MECHANICS

### Basic Structure

1. **Policyholder** pays premiums into a PPLI policy issued by a specialty insurance carrier
2. Premiums fund a **separate investment account** (the "policy account" or "insurance dedicated fund" / IDF) managed by an independent investment manager
3. The investment manager invests the policy account per one or more pre-approved strategies — the policyholder **cannot directly control** specific investment decisions (the "investor control doctrine")
4. All gains, dividends, interest, and trading profits inside the policy account are **tax-deferred** (and effectively tax-free if accessed properly)
5. Policyholder accesses cash value via **policy loans** (tax-free, no repayment required during life) and **partial withdrawals up to basis** (tax-free under FIFO treatment for non-MEC policies)
6. At death, the **entire death benefit** (including all accumulated investment gains) passes to beneficiaries **income-tax-free** under ss101(a)

### Tax-Free Access During Life

| Access Method | Tax Treatment (Non-MEC) | Tax Treatment (MEC) |
|---|---|---|
| Withdrawal up to basis | Tax-free (FIFO — basis out first) | Taxable (LIFO — gain out first) |
| Withdrawal above basis | Ordinary income | Ordinary income |
| Policy loan | Tax-free (no limit) | Tax-free (no limit) |
| Full surrender | Gain taxed as ordinary income | Gain taxed as ordinary income |
| Death benefit | Income-tax-free (ss101) | Income-tax-free (ss101) |

**Critical insight**: Even if a policy is a MEC, policy LOANS remain tax-free. The MEC designation only affects withdrawals (which become LIFO — gain taxed first). For investors who intend to access funds primarily via loans and hold until death, MEC status may be acceptable.

### Carrier Selection

PPLI policies are issued by specialty carriers, typically domiciled in:
- **Bermuda**: Most common offshore domicile, strong regulatory framework, no premium tax
- **Cayman Islands**: Similar to Bermuda, favorable regulatory environment
- **Barbados**: US tax treaty benefits for certain structures
- **US jurisdictions**: South Dakota, Delaware, and Connecticut have domestic PPLI-friendly regulatory environments

**Offshore vs. domestic**: Offshore carriers (Bermuda, Cayman) typically offer more investment flexibility, lower costs, and no state premium taxes. Domestic carriers provide more familiarity and avoid any perception issues with offshore structures. Both are fully compliant with US tax law — the tax treatment under ss7702 and ss101 is identical regardless of carrier domicile.

## TAX REQUIREMENTS — IRC ss7702

### The Definitional Test

For a life insurance contract to qualify for tax-favored treatment (tax-deferred growth, tax-free loans, tax-free death benefit), it must satisfy the definition of "life insurance contract" under ss7702. The contract must meet EITHER:

**1. Cash Value Accumulation Test (CVAT) — ss7702(b)**

The cash surrender value at any time cannot exceed the net single premium that would be needed to fund future benefits under the contract. In practice, this means the death benefit must be high enough relative to the cash value — the insurer calculates the minimum death benefit corridor automatically.

**2. Guideline Premium Test (GPT) — ss7702(c)**

Two requirements:
- Premiums paid cannot exceed the **guideline premium limitation** (the greater of the guideline single premium or the sum of guideline level premiums)
- The cash surrender value cannot exceed the **cash value corridor** (minimum death benefit relative to cash value, varying by insured's age)

### GPT Cash Value Corridor (Post-2021 — Consolidated Appropriations Act, 2021 ss205)

| Attained Age of Insured | Minimum Death Benefit as % of Cash Value |
|---|---|
| 0-40 | 250% |
| 41-45 | 215% |
| 46-50 | 185% |
| 51-55 | 150% |
| 56-60 | 130% |
| 61-65 | 120% |
| 66-70 | 115% |
| 71-75 | 105% |
| 76+ | 100% |

**Practical impact**: A 50-year-old funding a PPLI policy with $5M must carry a minimum death benefit of ~$9.25M (185% of cash value) at funding. As the insured ages, the required corridor narrows, and more of the premium can be allocated to investment. Most PPLI policies use CVAT rather than GPT because CVAT generally allows higher cash value accumulation relative to death benefit.

### Which Test to Choose

| Factor | CVAT | GPT |
|---|---|---|
| Maximum cash value relative to death benefit | Higher | Lower |
| Premium flexibility | More restrictive | More flexible |
| Common PPLI choice | **Yes — most common** | Less common |
| Death benefit corridor | Wider | Narrower |

Most PPLI practitioners default to **CVAT** because it allows maximum investment accumulation relative to the death benefit, minimizing insurance costs (Cost of Insurance charges are based on the "net amount at risk" — the difference between the death benefit and the cash value).

## MODIFIED ENDOWMENT CONTRACT (MEC) — THE SEVEN-PAY TEST

### ss7702A — MEC Determination

A life insurance contract becomes a Modified Endowment Contract if cumulative premiums paid during the first 7 years exceed the "seven-pay test" limit — the level annual premium that would fund the contract's guaranteed death benefit over 7 years.

### MEC vs. Non-MEC Treatment

| Feature | Non-MEC | MEC |
|---|---|---|
| Withdrawals | FIFO (basis first — tax-free up to basis) | LIFO (gain first — taxable) |
| Policy loans | Tax-free | Tax-free |
| 10% penalty on gain (pre-59.5) | No | Yes (ss72(v)) |
| Death benefit | Income-tax-free | Income-tax-free |
| Tax-deferred growth | Yes | Yes |

### Strategic Decision: Accept MEC or Avoid It?

**Accept MEC when**:
- Primary access strategy is policy loans (not withdrawals) — loans are tax-free regardless
- Insured expects to hold the policy until death (death benefit is income-tax-free regardless)
- Maximizing upfront funding is more valuable than preserving non-MEC withdrawal treatment
- The premium commitment is large enough that spreading over 7 years is impractical

**Avoid MEC when**:
- Policyholder wants withdrawal flexibility (FIFO treatment for non-MEC)
- Policyholder is under 59.5 and may need withdrawals (10% penalty on MEC gain distributions)
- Estate planning calls for potential policy surrender or exchange before death

**In practice**, most large PPLI policies ARE MECs because the policyholder wants to fund heavily upfront. The trade-off is acceptable because access is primarily via tax-free loans, and the death benefit is income-tax-free regardless.

## DIVERSIFICATION REQUIREMENTS — IRC ss817(h)

### The Safe Harbor (Reg. ss1.817-5(b)(1))

The investments in the separate account underlying a variable life insurance policy must satisfy diversification requirements. The IRS safe harbor provides:

| Requirement | Limit |
|---|---|
| No more than **55%** in any one issuer | Maximum single-issuer concentration |
| No more than **70%** in any two issuers | Maximum two-issuer concentration |
| No more than **80%** in any three issuers | Maximum three-issuer concentration |
| No more than **90%** in any four issuers | Maximum four-issuer concentration |

US government securities are treated as issued by a SINGLE issuer for this purpose — so a portfolio of 100% Treasury bonds would FAIL the diversification test.

### Look-Through Rules

Partnerships, LLCs, and other pass-through entities held inside the policy account are generally subject to **look-through** treatment — the underlying assets of the entity are tested for diversification, not the entity itself as a single holding.

**Exception**: A partnership interest may be treated as a single asset (not looked through) if:
- The partnership qualifies as a "regulated investment company" equivalent
- The partnership interest is publicly traded
- The partnership itself satisfies the diversification requirements at the entity level

### Consequence of Failure

If the separate account fails the diversification requirements at ANY time, the policyholder is treated as the **owner of the underlying assets** under ss817(h)(2). This means:
- ALL income, gains, and losses are taxable to the policyholder currently
- The insurance wrapper provides NO tax benefit
- This is **retroactive** — not just prospective

**This is catastrophic and irrecoverable.** The investment manager and policy administrator must monitor diversification continuously.

### Practical Compliance

Most PPLI carriers use **Insurance Dedicated Funds (IDFs)** — commingled investment vehicles specifically structured to satisfy ss817(h) diversification at the fund level. The IDF pools assets from multiple PPLI policies, and the fund manager ensures continuous compliance. Individual policyholders invest in IDF units rather than directly holding securities, which simplifies diversification compliance.

## INVESTOR CONTROL DOCTRINE

### The Core Principle

The policyholder of a PPLI policy **cannot directly control** the specific investments in the policy account. If the policyholder is treated as having "incidents of ownership" over the underlying investments, the IRS will disregard the insurance wrapper and tax the policyholder as the direct owner of the assets.

### Key Authorities

| Authority | Holding |
|---|---|
| **Christoffersen v. United States**, 749 F.2d 513 (8th Cir. 1984) | Policyholder who selected specific stocks to be held in policy treated as owner — wrapper disregarded |
| **Rev. Rul. 2003-91** (IRS) | Safe harbor: policyholder may choose among pre-approved investment strategies/managers offered by the insurance company without violating investor control |
| **Rev. Rul. 2003-92** (IRS) | Policyholder who directs specific investments violates investor control — even if the policy terms technically prohibit it |
| **TAM 200244001** | Pre-existing relationship between policyholder and investment manager, combined with control over strategy, caused wrapper to be disregarded |

### What IS Permitted

- Selecting from a **menu of pre-approved investment strategies** offered by the carrier (e.g., "Global Equity Strategy," "Private Credit Strategy," "Multi-Manager Hedge Fund Strategy")
- Selecting from a **list of pre-approved investment managers** offered by the carrier
- Allocating premiums among multiple pre-approved strategies
- Reallocating among pre-approved strategies (subject to carrier limitations)
- Receiving periodic reports on policy account performance

### What is NOT Permitted

- Directing the purchase or sale of specific securities
- Communicating investment instructions to the manager through the carrier
- Having a **pre-existing advisory relationship** with the investment manager where the manager effectively takes instructions from the policyholder (this is the most aggressively litigated area)
- Structuring the IDF or separate account to hold only assets the policyholder previously owned or specifically selected
- Having de facto control through side agreements, winks, or informal understandings

### The Pre-Existing Relationship Trap

This is where most investor control problems arise. A UHNW individual who has a long-standing relationship with a hedge fund manager wants to move their investment inside a PPLI wrapper. If the same manager manages the IDF strategy and the policyholder is the only investor in that strategy, the IRS may argue the policyholder retains effective control.

**Best practice**:
- Use an IDF or strategy with **multiple policyholders** (not a single-investor dedicated strategy)
- If a dedicated strategy is used, ensure the investment manager has **full discretion** and the policyholder has **no communication channel** for investment instructions
- Document the independence of the manager from the policyholder thoroughly
- Use a manager the policyholder has **no prior relationship** with, or if using an existing manager, ensure the strategy is genuinely independent and offered to multiple policyholders

**This is the single most critical compliance area in PPLI.** Violation means the entire policy is disregarded for tax purposes — full current taxation of all gains, retroactively. Use specialized PPLI counsel.

## IDEAL CANDIDATES

### Financial Profile

| Factor | Minimum | Optimal |
|---|---|---|
| Net worth | $10M+ | $50M+ |
| Premium commitment | $1M+ | $5M-$25M+ |
| Time horizon | 10+ years | 20+ years / lifetime |
| Marginal tax rate | 37%+ federal | 37% federal + high-tax state (CA, NY, NJ) |
| Investment allocation to tax-inefficient assets | $1M+ | $5M+ |

### Best Use Cases

**1. Hedge fund investors**: Hedge funds generate short-term capital gains, ordinary income (interest, dividends), and frequent trading — all highly tax-inefficient. Moving hedge fund allocations inside PPLI eliminates 100% of the annual tax drag.

**2. Private equity and venture capital**: PE/VC investments generate carried interest and capital gains — PPLI defers and ultimately eliminates taxation on these returns.

**3. Concentrated stock positions**: A founder with a large position in a single stock can contribute shares to the PPLI policy (subject to diversification requirements and investor control compliance) and subsequently diversify inside the wrapper tax-free. No capital gains tax on the internal diversification.

**4. High-yield/private credit**: Fixed income generating ordinary income (taxed at up to 40.8% with NIIT) benefits enormously from the PPLI wrapper — converting 40.8% annual tax drag to 0%.

**5. Active traders**: Investors who trade frequently and generate short-term gains pay up to 40.8% annually. Inside PPLI, all trading gains are tax-free.

**6. Real estate (via partnerships/LLCs)**: Real estate partnerships can be held inside PPLI, but must meet diversification requirements. Rental income, 1231 gains, and depreciation recapture — all shielded.

## COST STRUCTURE

### Ongoing Costs

| Cost Component | Typical Range | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Cost of Insurance (COI) | 0.05%-0.50%/year | Based on net amount at risk (death benefit minus cash value); decreases as cash value grows |
| Policy administration | $2,000-$10,000/year | Flat fee or small basis-point charge |
| Investment management fees | 0.50%-2.00%/year | Charged by the IDF/investment manager, same as outside PPLI |
| Carrier wrap fee | 0.10%-0.30%/year | Insurance company's margin on the policy |
| Total insurance-specific costs (excluding investment management) | 0.20%-0.80%/year | This is the "cost" of the tax shelter |

### Setup Costs

| Component | Typical Range |
|---|---|
| Legal/tax structuring | $25,000-$100,000+ |
| Policy application and underwriting | $5,000-$15,000 |
| IDF setup (if dedicated fund) | $15,000-$50,000 |
| Total setup | $50,000-$150,000+ |

### Breakeven Analysis

The breakeven point is when the cumulative tax savings exceed the cumulative insurance-specific costs (COI + administration + wrap fee). Setup costs are sunk at inception.

**Worked Example — Breakeven Calculation**:
- Premium: $5,000,000
- Annual investment return: 8% ($400,000/year on initial premium)
- Tax rate on returns if held taxably: 40.8% (short-term gains + NIIT)
- Annual tax saved by PPLI: $400,000 x 40.8% = **$163,200/year**
- Annual insurance-specific costs: $5M x 0.40% = **$20,000/year**
- Net annual benefit: $163,200 - $20,000 = **$143,200/year**
- Setup costs: $75,000
- **Breakeven: ~6 months** from inception (setup costs recovered in first year)

For tax-efficient investments (long-term capital gains at 23.8%), breakeven is longer:
- Annual tax saved: $400,000 x 23.8% = $95,200
- Net annual benefit: $95,200 - $20,000 = $75,200
- **Breakeven: ~1 year** from inception

**For long-term capital gains with no annual realization (buy-and-hold)**: PPLI may not break even for 5-8+ years because the taxable account also defers gain until sale. The PPLI advantage in this case comes primarily from (a) eliminating gain at death via ss101 (vs. stepped-up basis under ss1014 for the taxable account — which may be modified by future legislation) and (b) tax-free loan access.

## RISKS AND LIMITATIONS

### Financial Risks

**1. Insurance carrier credit risk**: The policy account is a general account asset of the carrier in some structures, or a segregated separate account. If the carrier becomes insolvent, policyholders may have claims as general creditors (for general account policies) or may be protected (for separate account policies). Use only highly rated carriers with separate account structures.

**2. Illiquidity / early surrender charges**: Most PPLI policies impose surrender charges in the first 5-10 years (typically declining from 5-10% in Year 1 to 0% by Year 7-10). Early surrender can be costly. Policy loans provide liquidity without triggering surrender charges.

**3. Opportunity cost of insurance charges**: The 0.20-0.80%/year in insurance-specific costs is a permanent drag. For very long holding periods, this compounds — but it is almost always dwarfed by the tax savings.

### Legal/Compliance Risks

**4. Investor control doctrine violation**: If the IRS determines the policyholder had direct or indirect control over investments, the entire policy is disregarded. This is the **existential risk** of PPLI. Mitigation: specialized PPLI counsel, independent investment managers, multi-policyholder IDFs.

**5. Diversification failure (ss817(h))**: If the policy account fails diversification at any point, the policyholder is treated as owner of the assets. Continuous monitoring is required.

**6. MEC risk from over-funding**: If premiums exceed the seven-pay limit, the policy becomes a MEC. This is often intentional (see MEC section above), but an unintentional MEC designation removes FIFO withdrawal treatment.

### Regulatory/Political Risks

**7. Potential legislative changes**: Congress could modify ss7702, ss101, or ss817(h) to restrict PPLI. However, PPLI has survived multiple tax reform cycles (TEFRA 1982, DEFRA 1984, TAMRA 1988, TCJA 2017, OBBBA 2025) without material restriction. The life insurance lobby is powerful, and the ss101 exclusion has bipartisan support.

**8. State premium taxes**: Some states impose premium taxes on life insurance policies (typically 1-3% of premiums). Offshore carriers are often exempt. Domestic carriers may be subject to the tax in the policyholder's state of residence. At $5M+ premiums, this can be $50K-$150K — a material one-time cost.

### Asset Protection

Life insurance cash value enjoys strong creditor protection in most states, but the degree varies:

- **Florida**: Unlimited creditor protection for life insurance cash value (FL Const. Art. X, ss4; FL Stat. ss222.14)
- **Texas**: Unlimited creditor protection (Tex. Ins. Code ss1108.051)
- **South Dakota**: Unlimited creditor protection (SDCL ss58-12-4)
- **Nevada**: Unlimited for NV-issued policies (NRS ss687B.290)
- **California**: Limited — only "reasonably necessary" amount protected (Cal. CCP ss704.100)
- **New York**: $10,000/year of premiums protected for policies on the debtor's life (NY CPLR ss5205(i))

**Planning point**: For a UHNW individual in California or New York, placing a PPLI policy inside a South Dakota trust (with a South Dakota trustee) may provide both income-tax-free trust growth AND unlimited asset protection — a double benefit not available in the policyholder's home state.

### State Premium Taxes

State premium taxes on life insurance typically range from 0.5%-3.5% of premiums. For a $5M premium, this is $25,000-$175,000. Some states exempt policies issued by non-admitted (offshore) carriers from premium tax, while others impose a "surplus lines" tax. Planning the carrier domicile and policy issuance state can minimize this cost.

## PPLI + ESTATE PLANNING COMBINATIONS

### 1. PPLI Inside an Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT)

The most powerful combination. The ILIT owns the PPLI policy:
- **Income tax**: All growth inside the policy is tax-free (ss7702)
- **Estate tax**: The policy (and its entire cash value and death benefit) is **outside the insured's taxable estate** because the trust owns it (ss2042)
- **Income-tax-free death benefit**: ss101(a) exclusion applies regardless of trust ownership
- **Result**: Investment growth that is income-tax-free AND estate-tax-free AND passes to beneficiaries income-tax-free at death

**Worked Example**:
- Insured (age 50) creates ILIT and gifts $5M to it (uses $5M of $13.99M gift/estate exemption)
- ILIT purchases PPLI policy with $5M premium
- Investments earn 8% annually for 30 years
- Policy cash value at age 80: ~$50.3M
- Death benefit at age 80: ~$52M+ (depending on corridor)
- **Tax on $47M of growth: $0** (ss7702 + ss101)
- **Estate tax on $52M death benefit: $0** (trust owns policy, outside estate)
- If held in taxable account in estate: ~$47M gain x 23.8% = $11.2M income tax + ($50M - $13.99M exemption) x 40% = $14.6M estate tax = **$25.8M in taxes avoided**

### 2. PPLI + Intentionally Defective Grantor Trust (IDGT)

- Grantor creates IDGT and sells assets to it in exchange for an installment note (Rev. Rul. 85-13 — no gain recognized)
- IDGT uses proceeds to purchase PPLI policy
- Grantor trust status means ALL income is taxed to the grantor — but there IS no income because the PPLI wrapper eliminates it
- Grantor pays the installment note interest (at AFR) — further reducing estate
- At grantor's death: trust becomes non-grantor trust, holds PPLI policy outside estate, death benefit passes income-tax-free to beneficiaries

**Key advantage**: The IDGT eliminates the need for Crummey notices (which are required for annual exclusion gifts to an ILIT) because the trust is funded via an installment sale, not gifts.

### 3. Dynasty Trust + PPLI

- Fund a dynasty trust (sited in South Dakota, Nevada, or similar jurisdiction) with GST exemption allocation
- Trust purchases PPLI policy
- Policy compounds tax-free for 50-100+ years inside the dynasty trust
- No estate tax at any generational transfer (trust is GST-exempt)
- No income tax on growth (PPLI wrapper)
- Beneficiaries access funds via trust distributions funded by policy loans

**Worked Example — Multi-Generational Compounding**:
- $10M PPLI policy inside dynasty trust, 8% annual return, 100-year horizon
- Taxable account (after 23.8% annual tax on gains, 40% estate tax every 30 years): ~$18M after 100 years
- PPLI in dynasty trust (0% income tax, 0% estate tax): ~**$2.2 BILLION** after 100 years
- **Difference: $2.18 billion** — the compounding effect of eliminating both income and estate taxes

### 4. PPLI + Spousal Lifetime Access Trust (SLAT)

- One spouse creates a SLAT for the benefit of the other spouse (and descendants)
- SLAT purchases PPLI policy
- The beneficiary spouse can access funds via trust distributions (funded by policy loans)
- Policy is outside the grantor spouse's estate
- Provides both spouses access to the investment during life while removing it from the taxable estate

## FOREIGN REPORTING REQUIREMENTS

### Offshore Carrier Reporting

PPLI policies issued by offshore carriers (Bermuda, Cayman, etc.) trigger certain US reporting obligations:

| Form | Requirement | Penalty for Non-Filing |
|---|---|---|
| **Form 720** (IRS Excise Tax) | 1% excise tax on premiums paid to foreign insurers (ss4371-4374) | Substantial penalties + interest |
| **FBAR (FinCEN 114)** | If policy cash value qualifies as a "foreign financial account" exceeding $10,000 at any time during the year — **currently debated; IRS position unclear** | $10,000+ per account per year (civil); criminal penalties possible |
| **FATCA (Form 8938)** | If policy cash value exceeds FATCA thresholds ($50K-$600K depending on filing status and residence) — report as "other foreign financial asset" | $10,000 penalty + additional penalties |
| **Form 3520/3520-A** | Generally NOT required for a commercial PPLI policy (these forms apply to foreign trusts, not insurance) — but if the policy is held inside a foreign trust, the trust reporting applies |

### The 1% Excise Tax (ss4371)

Premiums paid to a foreign insurer for a life insurance policy on a US risk are subject to a 1% excise tax. For a $5M premium, this is $50,000. This is a one-time cost on each premium payment — factored into the overall cost-benefit analysis. The excise tax is paid via Form 720, filed quarterly.

**Note**: Some US-based PPLI carriers have offshore reinsurance arrangements that may trigger similar issues. Consult PPLI-specialist tax counsel.

## WORKED EXAMPLES

### Example 1: Hedge Fund Investor

- **Profile**: Investor, age 45, allocates $10M to hedge fund strategies generating 12% annual returns, 70% taxed as short-term gains (37% + 3.8% NIIT = 40.8%)
- **Without PPLI**: Annual return $1.2M, tax on 70% short-term ($840K x 40.8% = $342,720), tax on 30% long-term ($360K x 23.8% = $85,680), total annual tax: **$428,400** (~3.6% drag on $10M portfolio)
- **With PPLI**: Annual return $1.2M, insurance costs ~$40,000/year (0.40% of $10M), net benefit: **$388,400/year**
- Over 30 years (to age 75), assuming 12% gross return:
  - Taxable account (after annual taxes): ~$84M
  - PPLI account (after insurance costs, no taxes): ~$262M
  - **PPLI advantage: $178M**
- At death: PPLI death benefit passes income-tax-free (ss101). Taxable account gets stepped-up basis (ss1014) — but the $178M gap is already locked in from decades of tax-free compounding.

### Example 2: Concentrated Stock Position

- **Profile**: Tech executive, age 50, holds $15M in employer stock (basis $500K), wants to diversify
- **Without PPLI**: Sell $15M stock, pay $14.5M gain x 23.8% = **$3,451,000** tax, invest remaining $11.5M
- **With PPLI**: Contribute $15M of stock to PPLI policy (subject to diversification planning — may need to contribute cash + stock, or contribute stock and diversify internally over time within ss817(h) requirements). Internal diversification triggers **$0 tax**. Full $15M remains invested.
- After 20 years at 8% annual return:
  - Taxable (after selling and reinvesting $11.5M): ~$53.6M
  - PPLI (full $15M, minus ~0.4%/yr costs): ~$66.5M
  - **PPLI advantage: $12.9M** (plus tax-free death benefit)

### Example 3: PPLI + ILIT for Estate Planning

- **Profile**: Couple (both age 55), $100M net worth, $25M available for PPLI
- **Structure**: Each spouse creates an ILIT; each ILIT purchases a $12.5M PPLI policy (second-to-die structure also available as single policy in joint ILIT)
- **Funding**: Each spouse gifts $12.5M to their ILIT (total $25M of combined $27.22M exemption used)
- **Returns**: 8% annual, 25-year horizon (to age 80)
- **PPLI cash value at age 80**: ~$25M x (1.076)^25 (net of 0.4% insurance costs) = ~$155M
- **Death benefit**: ~$160M+ (corridor-adjusted)
- **Taxes owed: $0** — income tax eliminated by PPLI, estate tax eliminated by ILIT
- **Without planning**: $25M invested taxably in estate, after 25 years of taxes (~2% annual drag) = ~$88M. Estate tax on $88M (above combined exemption): ~$26M. Income tax on unrealized gains at death: $0 (stepped-up basis). Net to heirs: ~$62M.
- **PPLI + ILIT net to heirs: ~$160M vs. $62M = $98M more to heirs**

## KEY TAKEAWAYS

1. **PPLI is the premier tax shelter for UHNW investors** willing to commit $1M+ in premiums — all growth tax-free, access via tax-free loans, death benefit income-tax-free
2. **OBBBA (July 4, 2025) did NOT restrict PPLI** — the structure remains fully available with no new limitations
3. **Investor control doctrine compliance is non-negotiable** — the policyholder cannot direct specific investments; violation causes the entire wrapper to be disregarded. Use specialized PPLI counsel and independent investment managers
4. **Diversification requirements (ss817(h)) must be met at all times** — failure causes retroactive taxation of all policy gains. Use Insurance Dedicated Funds (IDFs) with continuous compliance monitoring
5. **Best for tax-inefficient investments** — hedge funds, private credit, active trading strategies — where annual tax drag exceeds 3-5% of assets. PPLI eliminates this drag entirely
6. **Breakeven vs. taxable investing is typically under 2 years** for tax-inefficient strategies, and 5-8 years for tax-efficient buy-and-hold
7. **Pairs powerfully with irrevocable trusts** — PPLI inside an ILIT or dynasty trust achieves the trifecta: income-tax-free growth, income-tax-free death benefit, AND estate-tax-free transfer
8. **Cost structure is modest** — insurance-specific costs of 0.20-0.80%/year are almost always dwarfed by the tax savings for appropriate candidates
9. **Always use specialized PPLI counsel** (not general estate planning attorneys), a **reputable carrier** (Bermuda or US-domiciled), and an **independent investment manager** with no pre-existing relationship to the policyholder
