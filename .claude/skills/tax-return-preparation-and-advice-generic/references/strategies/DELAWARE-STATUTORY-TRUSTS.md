# DELAWARE STATUTORY TRUSTS (DSTs) — 1031 Exchange Replacement Property

## Overview

- A DST is a legal entity established under Delaware law that holds title to investment real estate
- Investors hold beneficial interests (not direct ownership) in the trust
- IRS Revenue Ruling 2004-86 confirmed that DST interests qualify as "like-kind" replacement property for Section 1031 exchanges
- Primary use case: passive real estate investors completing 1031 exchanges who want institutional-grade property without active management

---

## How DSTs Work

- A DST sponsor (typically an institutional real estate firm) acquires and structures a property
- The property is placed in a Delaware statutory trust
- Investors purchase beneficial interests in the trust, typically with 1031 exchange proceeds
- The DST structure is designed to comply with Rev. Rul. 2004-86 and the "Seven Deadly Sins" (restrictions that preserve 1031 qualification)
- Investors receive their proportionate share of rental income and depreciation
- At the end of the hold period (typically 5-10 years), the property is sold or refinanced

---

## The "Seven Deadly Sins" (Prohibited Activities)

To maintain 1031 qualification, a DST CANNOT:

1. Accept additional capital contributions from investors after closing
2. Renegotiate existing loans or borrow new funds
3. Reinvest sale proceeds from the sale of the trust's property
4. Make new investments (no property acquisitions)
5. Renegotiate tenant leases (except with master tenant in master-lease structures)
6. Make more than minor, non-structural modifications to the property
7. Distribute cash to investors except from the trust's income

---

## Tax Treatment

- **1031 exchange**: investor defers capital gains on the property sold by reinvesting in DST interests within the 45-day identification / 180-day closing window
- **Depreciation**: DST investors receive their proportionate share of depreciation deductions (straight-line, typically 27.5 or 39 years depending on property type)
- **Rental income**: flows through to investors as passive income on Schedule E
- **At disposition**: investor recognizes deferred gain unless they do another 1031 exchange
- **At death**: beneficiaries receive stepped-up basis (eliminates deferred gain entirely)

---

## Ideal Candidates

- Real estate investors age 55+ tired of active management
- Investors completing a 1031 exchange who need passive replacement property
- Investors seeking diversification across property types and geographies
- Investors whose current property has appreciated significantly and would face large tax liability on sale
- Estate planning: hold DST until death for stepped-up basis

---

## Cost and Fee Structure

- **Upfront fees**: typically 10-15% of investment (sales commissions, offering costs, sponsor fees)
- **Ongoing asset management fees**: 0.5-1.0%
- **Typical minimum investment**: $100,000-$250,000
- **Hold period**: 5-10 years (illiquid during this period)

---

## Risks and Limitations

- **Illiquidity**: no secondary market for DST interests
- **No management control**: the Seven Deadly Sins restrict all active management
- **Sponsor risk**: quality and alignment of interests varies significantly
- **High fees** reduce net returns relative to direct ownership
- **Cannot add leverage or capital** after closing
- **Cannot roll into another 1031 exchange easily** (some sponsors offer "UPREITs" as an alternative exit)
- **Property-specific risks** (vacancy, market decline, natural disaster)

---

## DST vs. Alternative 1031 Structures

| Feature | DST | TIC (Tenants-in-Common) | Direct Purchase | UPREIT |
|---------|-----|--------------------------|-----------------|--------|
| 1031 Qualified | Yes | Yes | Yes | No (but tax-deferred exchange for OP units) |
| Passive/No Management | Yes | Semi-passive | No | Yes |
| Minimum Investment | $100K+ | $250K+ | Property-specific | Varies |
| Investor Limit | Unlimited | 35 | N/A | N/A |
| Financing Flexibility | None (no new debt) | Limited | Full | N/A |
| Stepped-Up Basis at Death | Yes | Yes | Yes | Complex |

---

## Due Diligence Checklist

- Sponsor track record (minimum 10-year history, multiple full-cycle deals)
- Property quality and location (institutional grade vs. secondary markets)
- Debt structure (non-recourse, reasonable LTV, fixed rate preferred)
- Master lease vs. multi-tenant (different risk profiles)
- Fee transparency
- Projected cash-on-cash return vs. peer DSTs
- Exit strategy: will sponsor facilitate a follow-on 1031 exchange?

---

## KEY TAKEAWAYS

1. DSTs allow passive 1031 exchanges into institutional-grade real estate
2. Rev. Rul. 2004-86 confirmed 1031 qualification
3. "Seven Deadly Sins" restrictions are the price of 1031 qualification
4. Best for investors age 55+ seeking passive income and eventual stepped-up basis at death
5. High upfront fees (10-15%) — must be weighed against the 1031 tax deferral value
6. Illiquid investment: plan for the full hold period
7. Sponsor quality is the single most important due diligence factor
8. Can combine with estate planning for complete tax elimination (defer → step up at death)
9. Not suitable for investors who want active management or portfolio flexibility
