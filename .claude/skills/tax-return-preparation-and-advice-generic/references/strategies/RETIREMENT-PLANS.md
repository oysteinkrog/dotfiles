# Retirement Plan Strategies for US Taxpayers

## Overview

Retirement plan contributions are the single most powerful tax reduction tool for most taxpayers. They reduce taxable income dollar-for-dollar (traditional/pre-tax) or provide tax-free growth (Roth). For self-employed individuals, the combination of multiple plans can shelter $60,000-$300,000+ annually.

All limits below are for the 2025 tax year.

## Section 415(c) Aggregation Rules -- Critical

### The Rule

All employer contributions to defined contribution plans aggregate across ALL plans of ALL related employers (controlled group/affiliated service group under IRC 414(b), (c), (m), (o)). The total employee elective deferral limit ($23,500 for 2025) also applies across ALL employers, related or not.

### Why This Matters

A person with a W-2 job AND a side business faces aggregation traps:

**Scenario: W-2 employee + Schedule C side business**
- W-2 employer 401(k): $23,500 employee deferral + $10,000 employer match = $33,500
- Side business Solo 401(k): CANNOT make another $23,500 employee deferral (already used the limit)
- Side business Solo 401(k): CAN make employer profit-sharing contribution of 20% of net SE income
- Total 415(c) limit ($70,000) applies to each employer SEPARATELY (not aggregated) unless the employers are part of a controlled group

**Scenario: Two businesses owned by same person (controlled group)**
- The $70,000 415(c) limit applies ONCE across both businesses
- The $23,500 employee deferral limit applies ONCE across both businesses
- Must coordinate contributions carefully to avoid excess

### Controlled Group Rules

Businesses are a controlled group if:
- **Parent-subsidiary:** One business owns 80%+ of the other
- **Brother-sister:** Five or fewer individuals own 80%+ of each business (same owners)
- **Combined group:** Parent-subsidiary + brother-sister overlap

**Practical implication:** If you own 100% of two businesses, they are a brother-sister controlled group and the 415(c) limit is shared. If you own one business and are a W-2 employee at an unrelated company, the limits are separate (except the elective deferral limit, which is always shared).

### Common Mistake

A W-2 employee who defers $23,500 at their day job and then tries to defer another $23,500 in their side-business Solo 401(k) has made an excess deferral. The excess must be corrected by April 15 of the following year or the amount is taxed twice (once when contributed, once when distributed).

## Solo 401(k)

### What It Is

A one-participant 401(k) plan for self-employed individuals with no full-time employees (other than a spouse). Also called an individual 401(k) or uni-k.

### Contribution Components

1. **Employee elective deferral:** Up to $23,500 (2025)
   - Age 50+ catch-up: additional $7,500 (total $31,000)
   - Ages 60-63 super catch-up: additional $11,250 instead of $7,500 (total $34,750)
2. **Employer profit-sharing contribution:**
   - Self-employed (Schedule C): 20% of net self-employment income (after deducting 1/2 SE tax)
   - S-Corp shareholder-employee: 25% of W-2 wages
3. **Overall limit:** $70,000 (2025) excluding catch-up; $77,500 with standard catch-up; $81,250 with super catch-up

### Key Advantages

- Highest total contribution potential among simple plans
- Employee deferral available immediately (no income threshold needed for employer match)
- Roth 401(k) option available -- contribute post-tax for tax-free growth
- Loan provision available (up to $50,000 or 50% of vested balance)
- No annual filing until plan assets exceed $250,000 (Form 5500-EZ)

### S-Corp Interaction

- Employee elective deferral is based on W-2 salary (salary must be >= deferral amount)
- Employer contribution is 25% of W-2 salary
- Example: $100K W-2 salary -> $23,500 employee + $25,000 employer = $48,500 total
- Example: $60K W-2 salary -> $23,500 employee + $15,000 employer = $38,500 total

### Spousal Solo 401(k)

If both spouses have self-employment income from the business (even if one spouse is an employee of the other's sole proprietorship), EACH spouse can participate in the Solo 401(k). This doubles the household shelter:

| Component | Spouse A | Spouse B | Total |
|---|---|---|---|
| Employee deferral | $23,500 | $23,500 | $47,000 |
| Employer contribution (20% of $100K each) | $20,000 | $20,000 | $40,000 |
| Catch-up (both age 50+) | $7,500 | $7,500 | $15,000 |
| **Total** | **$51,000** | **$51,000** | **$102,000** |

Requirements: the non-owner spouse must receive W-2 compensation from the business and must actually perform services.

### Deadlines

- **Establish plan:** December 31 of the tax year
- **Employee deferrals:** December 31 of the tax year (for calendar year filers)
- **Employer contributions:** Tax filing deadline including extensions (October 15 for individual filers who extend)

### Providers

- Fidelity, Schwab, Vanguard (no account fees, wide investment options)
- E*Trade, TD Ameritrade (Schwab)
- For Roth option and loan features, verify with provider

## SEP-IRA (Simplified Employee Pension)

### What It Is

An employer-funded retirement plan where contributions are made solely by the employer (or self-employed individual). There are no employee elective deferrals.

### Contribution Limits

- **Self-employed:** 20% of net self-employment income (NOT 25% -- the 25% figure applies to W-2 employees; the self-employed calculation reduces to approximately 20% after the SE tax deduction adjustment)
- **W-2 employees of the business:** 25% of compensation
- **Maximum:** $70,000 (2025)

### The 20% vs 25% Clarification

This is a common source of confusion:
- For W-2 employees: the limit is 25% of W-2 compensation
- For self-employed individuals: the limit is effectively 20% of net self-employment income
- The reduction from 25% to ~20% occurs because self-employed individuals must subtract the SEP contribution itself and 1/2 of SE tax from net earnings before calculating the percentage
- The IRS worksheet in Publication 560 walks through this calculation

### Key Advantages

- Can be established and funded up to the tax filing deadline including extensions
- Extremely simple to set up (one-page form at most brokerages)
- No annual filing requirements regardless of balance
- No employee deferral administration

### Key Disadvantages

- No employee elective deferral component (employer-only contributions)
- No catch-up contributions
- If the business has employees, must contribute the same percentage for all eligible employees (coverage rules)
- SECURE 2.0 now permits Roth SEP-IRA contributions starting in 2023 (though many providers have been slow to implement)

### When to Choose SEP-IRA Over Solo 401(k)

- When a verified deadline analysis shows the taxpayer can no longer make the desired Solo 401(k)
  contribution for that year
- Very simple administration needed
- No desire for loan provision
- No W-2 employees (coverage rules make SEP expensive with employees)

## SIMPLE IRA

### What It Is

A plan designed for small employers (100 or fewer employees). Allows both employee salary deferrals and employer contributions with minimal administrative requirements.

### Contribution Limits (2025)

- **Employee deferral:** $16,500
- **Age 50+ catch-up:** additional $3,500 (total $20,000)
- **Ages 60-63 super catch-up:** additional $5,250 (total $21,750)
- **Employer contribution:** Either 3% match (dollar for dollar up to 3% of compensation) or 2% nonelective contribution for all eligible employees

### The Two-Year Penalty Trap

**This is one of the most dangerous traps in retirement plan taxation.**

During the first 2 years of SIMPLE IRA participation, the early withdrawal penalty is **25%** (not the standard 10%). This applies to:
- Withdrawals taken within 2 years of first participation
- Rollovers to non-SIMPLE plans within 2 years of first participation

The 2-year clock starts on the date the employee first participates in the SIMPLE IRA, NOT the date of contribution.

**Example:**
- Employee first participates January 1, 2024
- Employee withdraws $20,000 on December 15, 2025 (before the 2-year mark of January 1, 2026)
- Penalty: $20,000 x 25% = $5,000 (vs. $2,000 at the normal 10% rate)
- The employee also owes ordinary income tax on the $20,000

**Safe rollover timing:** Wait until after the 2-year anniversary, then roll to a Traditional IRA, 401(k), or other qualified plan at the normal 10% early withdrawal penalty (or 0% if an exception applies).

### Key Features

- Must be established by October 1 of the tax year
- Cannot maintain alongside a 401(k) or SEP for the same year
- SECURE 2.0: Roth SIMPLE IRA contributions now permitted (starting 2023)

### When SIMPLE IRA Makes Sense

- Small business with employees where 401(k) administration is too costly
- Business owner income is moderate and SEP alone is insufficient
- October 1 deadline is met

## Traditional IRA

### Contribution Limits (2025)

- **Under age 50:** $7,000
- **Age 50+:** $8,000

### Deductibility Phase-Outs (2025)

If the taxpayer OR spouse is covered by a workplace retirement plan:

| Filing Status | Full Deduction | Partial Deduction | No Deduction |
|---|---|---|---|
| Single / HoH (covered by plan) | MAGI <= $79,000 | $79,000 - $89,000 | > $89,000 |
| MFJ (contributor covered by plan) | MAGI <= $126,000 | $126,000 - $146,000 | > $146,000 |
| MFJ (spouse covered, contributor NOT) | MAGI <= $236,000 | $236,000 - $246,000 | > $246,000 |
| MFS (covered by plan) | N/A | $0 - $10,000 | > $10,000 |

If neither spouse is covered by a workplace plan: **fully deductible at any income level.**

### Key Point

Even if not deductible, a Traditional IRA contribution may still be worthwhile as a stepping stone for a backdoor Roth conversion.

## Roth IRA

### Contribution Limits (2025)

Same as Traditional IRA: $7,000 ($8,000 age 50+). Combined limit across Traditional and Roth IRAs.

### Income Phase-Outs for Direct Contributions

| Filing Status | Full Contribution | Partial | No Direct Contribution |
|---|---|---|---|
| Single / HoH | MAGI <= $150,000 | $150,000 - $165,000 | > $165,000 |
| MFJ | MAGI <= $236,000 | $236,000 - $246,000 | > $246,000 |
| MFS | N/A | $0 - $10,000 | > $10,000 |

### Backdoor Roth IRA

For taxpayers above the income limits:

1. Contribute to a Traditional IRA (nondeductible -- report on Form 8606)
2. Convert the Traditional IRA to a Roth IRA (ideally immediately or shortly after)
3. Pay tax on any gains between contribution and conversion (minimal if done quickly)

**Critical: The Pro-Rata Rule**

If the taxpayer has ANY pre-tax IRA balances (Traditional, SEP, SIMPLE, rollover IRAs), the conversion is taxed proportionally based on the ratio of pre-tax to total IRA balances. This can make the backdoor Roth partially or fully taxable.

**Solution ("Reverse Rollover"):** Roll pre-tax IRA balances into a 401(k) plan (Solo 401(k) or employer plan) before December 31 of the conversion year. This zeros out the pre-tax IRA balance, making the backdoor Roth conversion tax-free. The balance is measured as of December 31, regardless of when during the year the conversion occurred.

**Pro-rata calculation example:**
```
Pre-tax IRA balance: $93,000 (from prior SEP-IRA contributions)
Nondeductible IRA contribution: $7,000
Total IRA balance: $100,000

Pro-rata taxable percentage: $93,000 / $100,000 = 93%
Convert $7,000 to Roth: $7,000 x 93% = $6,510 taxable

Fix: Roll $93,000 into employer 401(k) BEFORE Dec 31
New pro-rata: $0 / $7,000 = 0% taxable
Convert $7,000 to Roth: $0 taxable
```

### Mega Backdoor Roth

Available if the employer plan (or Solo 401(k)) allows BOTH after-tax contributions AND in-service withdrawals or in-plan Roth conversions:

1. Max out employee elective deferrals ($23,500)
2. Max out employer contributions (25% of W-2)
3. Make additional after-tax (non-Roth) contributions up to the $70,000 overall 415(c) limit
4. Convert those after-tax contributions to Roth (in-plan conversion or rollover to Roth IRA)

**Potential additional Roth contribution:** $70,000 - $23,500 - employer contributions = remaining amount

**Both conditions must be met:**
1. The plan document must permit after-tax (non-Roth, non-elective-deferral) contributions
2. The plan must permit in-service distributions or in-plan Roth conversions of after-tax money

**Worked example (Solo 401(k), S-Corp, $150K W-2):**
```
Employee deferral:                    $23,500
Employer profit-sharing (25%):        $37,500
Subtotal:                             $61,000
415(c) limit:                         $70,000
Room for after-tax contributions:     $9,000
Convert $9,000 after-tax to Roth:     $9,000 additional Roth
```

**Worked example (large employer 401(k), $200K W-2, 4% match):**
```
Employee deferral:                    $23,500
Employer match (4% of $200K):         $8,000
Subtotal:                             $31,500
415(c) limit:                         $70,000
Room for after-tax contributions:     $38,500
Convert $38,500 after-tax to Roth:    $38,500 additional Roth
```

Not all plans support this. Verify with plan provider. Many large tech companies (Google, Meta, Amazon) do support it.

## Roth 401(k) vs Traditional 401(k) Decision Framework

### The Core Question

Should pre-tax (Traditional) or after-tax (Roth) contributions go into the 401(k)?

### Decision Factors

| Factor | Favors Traditional 401(k) | Favors Roth 401(k) |
|---|---|---|
| Current vs. future tax rate | Currently in high bracket, expect lower in retirement | Currently in lower bracket, expect higher in retirement |
| State tax | Live in high-tax state now, plan to retire in low/no-tax state | Live in low-tax state now, may move to higher-tax state |
| Time horizon | Close to retirement (fewer years of tax-free growth) | Young, decades of tax-free growth ahead |
| Tax diversification | Already have substantial Roth assets | Most retirement assets are pre-tax |
| RMD avoidance | N/A | Roth 401(k) now has no RMDs (SECURE 2.0, starting 2024) |
| Current liquidity | Need the tax savings now for cash flow | Can afford the higher after-tax cost |

### SECURE 2.0: Roth 401(k) RMD Elimination

Starting in 2024, Roth 401(k) accounts are NO LONGER subject to RMDs during the owner's lifetime. Previously, Roth 401(k) participants had to take RMDs (or roll to a Roth IRA to avoid them). This change makes Roth 401(k) more attractive for wealth transfer since the account can grow tax-free indefinitely during the owner's life.

### Practical Guidance

- **High earner in CA/NY, retiring to FL/TX:** Traditional 401(k) strongly favored (37%+ combined marginal rate now vs. potentially 22-24% federal-only in retirement)
- **Mid-career engineer in TX at $150K, expecting $300K+ later:** Roth 401(k) may be favored (24% now vs. potentially 32-35% later)
- **When in doubt:** Split contributions 50/50 for tax diversification
- **Near retirement with large pre-tax balances:** Consider Roth 401(k) to build tax-free bucket for Roth conversion ladder strategy

## SEPP / 72(t) -- Substantially Equal Periodic Payments

### What It Is

An exception to the 10% early withdrawal penalty (IRC 72(t)(2)(A)(iv)) that allows penalty-free distributions from IRAs and qualified plans before age 59 1/2, provided the distributions follow a schedule of "substantially equal periodic payments" based on the taxpayer's life expectancy.

### The Three Calculation Methods

**1. Required Minimum Distribution (RMD) Method**
- Annual payment = Account balance / Life expectancy factor (from IRS tables)
- Payment recalculated each year based on current balance and age
- Produces the LOWEST annual payment
- Payment amount fluctuates year to year
- Uses the Uniform Lifetime Table, Single Life Table, or Joint Life Table

**2. Fixed Amortization Method**
- Annual payment = Amortize the account balance over life expectancy at a reasonable interest rate
- Payment is FIXED once calculated (does not change)
- Produces a HIGHER payment than RMD method
- Interest rate must not exceed 120% of the federal mid-term rate for either of the two months preceding the election

**3. Fixed Annuitization Method**
- Annual payment = Account balance / Annuity factor (from IRS mortality table, currently based on Table 2000CM)
- Payment is FIXED once calculated
- Produces a payment SIMILAR to fixed amortization
- Interest rate limitation same as amortization method

### IRS-Approved Interest Rates

- Must not exceed 120% of the federal mid-term rate (published monthly by IRS in Revenue Rulings)
- As of early 2025, this rate is approximately 4.5-5.0% (check current rate before calculating)
- A higher interest rate produces larger payments
- Once selected, the interest rate is locked for the duration (amortization and annuitization methods)

### Duration and Modification Rules

**Duration:** Payments must continue for the LONGER of:
- 5 years, OR
- Until the taxpayer reaches age 59 1/2

| Start Age | End Requirement |
|---|---|
| 40 | Age 59 1/2 (19.5 years -- age 59.5 is later than age 45) |
| 50 | Age 59 1/2 (9.5 years -- age 59.5 is later than age 55) |
| 55 | Age 60 (5 years from age 55 is later than age 59.5) |
| 57 | Age 62 (5 years from age 57 is later than age 59.5) |

### Penalty for Breaking the Schedule

If the taxpayer modifies the payment schedule before the end of the required period (other than switching from amortization/annuitization to RMD method, which is a one-time permitted change per Rev. Rul. 2002-62):
- The 10% early withdrawal penalty is applied RETROACTIVELY to ALL distributions taken since the SEPP began
- PLUS interest on the penalty amounts from the date each distribution was taken
- This can amount to thousands or tens of thousands of dollars

**Example of breaking penalty:**
```
Started SEPP at age 50, taking $40,000/year
At age 54 (4 years in), modifies the schedule
Retroactive penalty: $40,000 x 4 years x 10% = $16,000
Plus interest on each year's penalty from the date it was due
Total penalty could exceed $18,000-$20,000
```

### One-Time Switch Permitted

Rev. Rul. 2002-62 allows a one-time irrevocable switch from the fixed amortization or fixed annuitization method to the RMD method. This is useful if:
- The account balance has dropped and the fixed payment is too large relative to the remaining balance
- The taxpayer wants to reduce distributions (RMD method produces lower amounts)
- This does NOT count as a modification and does NOT trigger the retroactive penalty

### Strategy Considerations

- Best for early retirees (ages 40-55) who need regular income before 59 1/2
- Can split IRA into multiple IRAs and apply SEPP to only one (controlling the distribution amount)
- Plan carefully because the commitment is long and breaking is expensive
- Consider consulting an actuary or CPA before starting

## Defined Benefit / Cash Balance Plans

### What They Are

Traditional pension-style plans where the annual contribution is actuarially determined based on the participant's age, compensation, and the promised benefit at retirement.

### When to Consider

- Consistent income of $200,000+ annually
- Age 40+ (contributions increase significantly with age due to shorter accumulation period)
- Desire to shelter $100,000-$350,000+ per year
- Stable business with predictable cash flow (contributions are mandatory once established)

### Contribution Potential by Age (Approximate, 2025)

| Age | Approximate Maximum Annual Contribution |
|---|---|
| 30 | $40,000 - $60,000 |
| 35 | $60,000 - $90,000 |
| 40 | $100,000 - $150,000 |
| 45 | $130,000 - $180,000 |
| 50 | $170,000 - $230,000 |
| 55 | $220,000 - $290,000 |
| 60 | $280,000 - $350,000+ |

Exact amounts depend on actuarial calculations, interest rate assumptions, and plan design. The maximum benefit at retirement age is $280,000/year (2025, indexed for inflation), and the contribution is whatever the actuary determines is necessary to fund that benefit.

### Defined Benefit Plan Mechanics

**Minimum Funding Rules:**
- IRC 412 requires minimum contributions each year based on actuarial calculations
- Failure to meet minimum funding results in a 10% excise tax on the funding shortfall (25% if not corrected)
- The plan actuary certifies the minimum required contribution annually
- For plans covering only the business owner, the actuary has flexibility in assumptions, but minimum funding is still mandatory

**PBGC Insurance (Multi-Participant Plans):**
- The Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation insures benefits in defined benefit plans with more than one participant
- Annual PBGC premiums apply: flat-rate premium ($101/participant in 2025) + variable-rate premium based on underfunding
- Solo/owner-only plans are generally exempt from PBGC coverage
- If the plan covers employees beyond the owner, PBGC insurance and premiums apply

**Plan Termination:**
- Plan must be maintained for a "reasonable period" (IRS guidance suggests 3+ years minimum to avoid being treated as a sham)
- Standard termination: all benefits are fully funded, assets distributed to participants via lump sum or annuity purchase
- Distress termination: employer demonstrates financial distress (rare for small plans)
- Upon termination, participants receive their accrued benefit, which can be rolled to an IRA

### Cash Balance Plan vs. Traditional DB

| Feature | Traditional DB | Cash Balance |
|---|---|---|
| Benefit formula | Monthly pension at retirement | Hypothetical account balance |
| Investment risk | Employer bears all risk | Employer bears risk, but benefit is stated as a balance |
| Portability | Difficult to transfer | Easier to roll over upon departure |
| Employee communication | Complex (pension formula) | Simpler (participants see a "balance") |
| Typical user | Professional firms, physicians, attorneys | Same, but easier to explain to employees |

### Costs

- Actuarial administration: $2,000-$5,000/year
- TPA (Third Party Administrator) fees
- Must fund minimum contributions each year (IRS penalty for underfunding)
- Plan must be maintained for a reasonable period (general guidance: 3+ years)

### Key Point

A defined benefit plan can be layered ON TOP of a 401(k) or SEP-IRA. The DB plan contribution is in addition to the 401(k)/SEP limits.

## RMD Aggregation Rules

### The Rules Are Different for Each Plan Type

**Traditional IRAs: AGGREGATE**
- Calculate RMD for EACH Traditional IRA separately
- Add up all RMD amounts into a single total
- Withdraw the total from ANY one or combination of Traditional IRAs
- Example: Three IRAs with RMDs of $5,000, $3,000, $2,000 = total RMD of $10,000. Can take all $10,000 from one IRA.

**401(k) Plans: DO NOT AGGREGATE**
- Calculate RMD for EACH 401(k) separately
- Must withdraw the RMD from EACH specific 401(k)
- Cannot satisfy one plan's RMD with a withdrawal from another plan
- This is why consolidating old 401(k)s into IRAs before RMD age simplifies administration

**403(b) Plans: AGGREGATE SEPARATELY**
- Calculate RMD for each 403(b) separately
- Can aggregate and withdraw from any combination of 403(b) accounts
- BUT: 403(b) RMDs cannot be satisfied by IRA withdrawals (and vice versa)
- 403(b) aggregation is separate from IRA aggregation

**Roth IRAs: NO RMDs**
- No RMDs during the owner's lifetime (Roth IRAs are exempt)

**Roth 401(k): NO RMDs (starting 2024)**
- SECURE 2.0 eliminated RMDs for Roth 401(k) accounts starting in 2024
- Previously, Roth 401(k) had RMDs unless rolled to a Roth IRA

### Practical Implications

| Situation | Action |
|---|---|
| Multiple old 401(k)s from prior employers | Consider rolling all into a single IRA to simplify RMD administration |
| Large IRA + small IRA | Calculate total RMD, withdraw entirely from the IRA you want to draw down |
| 403(b) + IRA | Must take separate RMDs from each system |
| Inherited IRA | Has its own RMD schedule, cannot aggregate with owned IRAs |

## Pro-Rata Rule for Roth Conversions -- Deep Dive

### The Problem

Any pre-tax IRA balance (Traditional, SEP, SIMPLE, rollover) taints ALL Roth conversions. You cannot selectively convert "just the nondeductible portion."

### The Calculation

The IRS treats ALL of a taxpayer's Traditional IRAs (including SEP and SIMPLE IRAs) as ONE combined pool for conversion purposes. The taxable percentage of any conversion equals:

```
Taxable % = Total pre-tax IRA balance / Total IRA balance (all Traditional, SEP, SIMPLE, rollover)
```

This is calculated as of December 31 of the year of conversion, using Form 8606.

### The Fix: Reverse Rollover

To eliminate the pro-rata problem:

1. Roll ALL pre-tax IRA balances into an employer 401(k) or Solo 401(k) -- most plans accept incoming rollovers
2. This leaves only the nondeductible (after-tax) IRA balance
3. Convert the after-tax balance to Roth -- 0% taxable
4. The rollover must be completed before December 31 of the conversion year

**What can be rolled into a 401(k):**
- Traditional IRA pre-tax balances
- SEP-IRA balances
- Rollover IRA balances

**What CANNOT be rolled into a 401(k):**
- Nondeductible (after-tax) Traditional IRA basis
- SIMPLE IRA balances within the first 2 years

### Common Mistake

Doing a backdoor Roth while having a $200,000 rollover IRA from a prior employer. The conversion will be mostly taxable. Fix: roll the $200,000 into current employer's 401(k) first.

## HSA as a Retirement Vehicle

### Triple Tax Benefit

1. **Tax-deductible contributions** (above-the-line, reduces AGI)
2. **Tax-free growth** (no tax on investment gains)
3. **Tax-free withdrawals** for qualified medical expenses (at any time, no age requirement)

### 2025 Limits

| Coverage Type | Contribution Limit | Catch-Up (Age 55+) |
|---|---|---|
| Self-only | $4,300 | +$1,000 |
| Family | $8,550 | +$1,000 per eligible spouse |

### Retirement Strategy

- Pay current medical expenses out-of-pocket (if affordable)
- Let HSA balance grow and invest for decades
- After age 65, withdraw for any purpose (taxed as ordinary income, like Traditional IRA -- but no 20% penalty)
- Withdraw tax-free at any age for qualified medical expenses (keep receipts indefinitely)
- No RMDs (unlike Traditional IRA/401(k))

### Eligibility Requirements

- Must be enrolled in a qualifying High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP)
- 2025 HDHP minimums: $1,650 individual / $3,300 family deductible
- 2025 HDHP OOP maximums: $8,300 individual / $16,600 family
- Cannot be enrolled in Medicare
- Cannot be claimed as a dependent
- Cannot have non-HDHP coverage (except permitted insurance like dental, vision, specific disease)

## SECURE 2.0 Act Changes (Effective Dates Vary)

### Auto-Enrollment Requirement (2025+)

New 401(k) and 403(b) plans established AFTER December 29, 2022 must automatically enroll employees at a contribution rate between 3% and 10% of pay, with annual automatic escalation of 1% per year up to at least 10% (maximum 15%). Employees can opt out. Exceptions for small businesses (10 or fewer employees), businesses less than 3 years old, church plans, and governmental plans.

### Student Loan Matching (2024+)

Employers may treat qualified student loan payments as elective deferrals for purposes of matching contributions. Employee pays student loans instead of contributing to 401(k), employer still provides a match as if the employee had contributed. This helps young employees who cannot afford both student loan payments and retirement contributions.

### Emergency Savings Accounts (2024+)

Employers may offer plan-linked emergency savings accounts for non-highly-compensated employees. Contributions are Roth (after-tax), limited to $2,500 balance. First 4 withdrawals per year are penalty-free and tax-free. Designed to reduce hardship withdrawals from retirement accounts.

### Roth SIMPLE and SEP Contributions (2023+)

Employers may now offer Roth contribution options for SIMPLE IRA and SEP-IRA plans. Previously, these plans were pre-tax only. Many providers have been slow to implement this option.

### Part-Time Worker Eligibility (2025+)

Long-term part-time employees who work 500+ hours per year for 2 consecutive years (reduced from 3 years under original SECURE Act) must be allowed to make elective deferrals to 401(k) plans. Does not require employer matching for these employees.

### Other Key Provisions

- **Catch-up contribution Roth requirement (2026):** For employees earning $145,000+, catch-up contributions to 401(k) plans MUST be Roth (after-tax). Delayed from original 2024 effective date.
- **RMD age increase:** Age 73 (2023-2032), age 75 (2033+)
- **Reduced penalty for missed RMDs:** 25% (down from 50%), further reduced to 10% if corrected within 2 years
- **529 to Roth IRA rollover (2024+):** Up to $35,000 lifetime, subject to annual Roth IRA contribution limits, 529 must have been open 15+ years

## State Tax on Retirement Plan Distributions

### States That Fully Exempt Pension/Retirement Income

The following states do not tax pension or retirement plan distributions at all (either no income tax or full exemption):

**No income tax states:** AK, FL, NV, NH (interest/dividends only), SD, TN (interest/dividends only until 2021, now fully no income tax), TX, WA, WY

**States with income tax that fully exempt pension income:** AL, HI (public pensions), IL, MS, PA

### States That Tax Social Security Benefits

Nine states tax Social Security benefits to some degree (though most offer partial exemptions based on income):

| State | Treatment |
|---|---|
| CO | Fully exempt if age 65+; partial exemption for ages 55-64 |
| CT | Exempt if AGI below threshold ($75K single / $100K MFJ) |
| KS | Exempt if AGI below $75,000 |
| MN | Partial exemption based on income |
| MO | Exempt if AGI below $85,000 (single) / $100,000 (MFJ) |
| MT | Partial exemption based on income |
| NE | Phasing out taxation by 2025 |
| NM | Exempt if income below $100,000 (single) / $150,000 (MFJ) |
| UT | Tax credit effectively exempts lower-income retirees |

### Strategic Implications

- Relocating to a no-income-tax state before taking large retirement distributions or Roth conversions saves significant state tax
- Some states (PA, IL, MS) exempt retirement income but tax earned income -- ideal for retirees with pension income
- California and New York tax all retirement income and do not exempt Social Security -- the least favorable states for retirees
- State residency changes must be genuine (domicile change, not just mailing address) -- CA and NY aggressively audit departures

## Layer Strategy: Maximum Deferral

For a high-income self-employed individual (age 50+, S-Corp, $300K+ income):

| Layer | Contribution | Tax Benefit |
|---|---|---|
| 1. Solo 401(k) employee deferral | $31,000 | Reduces taxable income |
| 2. Solo 401(k) employer contribution | 25% of W-2 salary | Reduces taxable income |
| 3. Defined Benefit Plan | $150,000-$300,000+ (age-dependent) | Reduces taxable income |
| 4. HSA (family) | $9,550 | Above-the-line deduction |
| 5. Backdoor Roth IRA (both spouses) | $16,000 | Tax-free growth (no deduction) |
| **Total potential deferral** | **$230,000 - $380,000+** | |

### Ordering Priority

1. **401(k) employee deferral** -- always max this first (guaranteed dollar-for-dollar deduction)
2. **HSA** -- triple tax benefit makes this second priority
3. **401(k) employer contribution** -- maximize employer match/profit sharing
4. **Backdoor Roth IRA** -- tax-free growth even without deduction
5. **Defined Benefit Plan** -- if income supports it and cash flow is stable
6. **Mega Backdoor Roth** -- if plan allows and room remains under 415(c) limit

## Common Mistakes

1. **Using 25% instead of 20%** for self-employed SEP-IRA calculation -- leads to excess contributions and penalties
2. **Using an oversimplified Solo 401(k) deadline rule** -- verify adoption and contribution
   timing based on entity type before concluding that only a SEP-IRA is available
3. **Ignoring the pro-rata rule** when doing backdoor Roth -- unexpected tax bill
4. **Over-contributing** -- 6% excise tax on excess IRA contributions each year until corrected
5. **Not considering the spouse** -- a non-working spouse can contribute to an IRA based on the working spouse's earned income (spousal IRA); each spouse with SE income can have own Solo 401(k)
6. **Choosing SIMPLE IRA when Solo 401(k) is available** -- Solo 401(k) has higher limits and more flexibility
7. **Funding a defined benefit plan without stable income** -- mandatory minimum contributions create cash flow risk
8. **Missing the April 15 deadline** for IRA/Roth IRA contributions (no extension)
9. **Exceeding the single elective deferral limit across multiple employers** -- $23,500 total, not per employer
10. **Rolling out of a SIMPLE IRA within the first 2 years** -- triggers 25% penalty instead of 10%
11. **Breaking a SEPP/72(t) schedule** -- retroactive 10% penalty on ALL prior distributions plus interest
12. **Not rolling pre-tax IRAs into 401(k) before backdoor Roth conversion** -- pro-rata rule makes conversion mostly taxable
13. **Assuming Roth 401(k) has RMDs** -- SECURE 2.0 eliminated Roth 401(k) RMDs starting 2024
14. **Not aggregating 415(c) limits for controlled group employers** -- excess contributions and plan disqualification risk
