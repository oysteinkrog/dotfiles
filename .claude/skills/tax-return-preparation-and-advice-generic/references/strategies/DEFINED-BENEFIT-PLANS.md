# DEFINED BENEFIT AND CASH BALANCE PLANS — Maximum Tax-Deferred Shelter

## Overview

Defined benefit (DB) plans and cash balance plans allow far larger tax-deductible contributions than any defined contribution plan. Self-employed and small business owners age 50+ can shelter $200,000-$350,000+ per year. Contributions are deductible by the business; growth is tax-deferred. The single most powerful retirement tax shelter available — often overlooked by taxpayers focused on 401(k) limits.

All figures are for the 2025 tax year.

---

## Plan Types

### Traditional Defined Benefit Plan

- Employer promises a fixed monthly benefit at retirement (e.g., $265,000/year — the IRC Section 415(b) limit for 2025)
- Annual required contributions are determined actuarially based on participant's age, target benefit, assumed rate of return, and years to retirement
- Older participants = larger required contributions (more years of compounding needed in fewer years)
- For a 55-year-old planning to retire at 62, the annual contribution required to fund $265,000/year benefit can exceed $300,000

### Cash Balance Plan

- Hybrid: defined benefit structure but with an individual account balance
- Each participant has a "hypothetical account" credited with pay credits (e.g., percentage of salary) and interest credits (e.g., 5% fixed rate)
- More predictable annual contributions than traditional DB plans
- Easier for participants to understand (looks like a 401(k) with a guaranteed return)
- Can be "stacked" with a 401(k) for maximum total shelter

## The DB + 401(k) Stack

- This is the maximum-shelter approach for self-employed
- Establish BOTH a defined benefit plan AND a Solo 401(k)
- Solo 401(k) contributions ($23,500 employee + up to $46,500 employer = $70,000 max for 2025)
- DB plan contributions ON TOP of the 401(k) contributions
- Combined: potentially $300,000-$400,000+ in annual deductible contributions for older high-earners

---

## S-Corp Salary Linkage (Critical)

- For S-Corp owners, the reasonable salary determination directly controls the DB plan contribution ceiling
- DB plan contributions are based on W-2 compensation (salary), NOT distributions
- A higher reasonable salary = larger DB plan deduction but also higher payroll tax
- **The optimization**: Find the salary level where marginal DB plan tax savings exceed marginal payroll tax cost
- Example: At $200K salary, increasing to $250K adds ~$7,650 in payroll tax (employer + employee FICA on $50K above SS wage base is just 2.9% Medicare = $1,450 each side) but may increase DB deduction by $50K+ at 37% marginal rate = $18,500+ tax savings. Net benefit: ~$10,850+
- For high-income owners age 50+, the DB plan math almost always favors a higher salary

---

## Funding Commitment and Risks

- DB plans have MANDATORY minimum annual contributions (unlike 401(k)s where contributions are discretionary)
- If business income drops, you still must fund the plan or face excise taxes
- Must continue for at least 3-5 years to avoid IRS scrutiny for plan termination
- Actuarial volatility: if investment returns are below assumed rate, required contributions increase
- If investment returns are above assumed rate, required contributions decrease (good problem)
- Plan termination: allowed but triggers accelerated vesting and potential excise taxes if within first few years

---

## Administration Requirements

- Annual actuarial valuation required (cost: $2,000-$5,000/year)
- Form 5500 filing requirement
- PBGC premiums may apply (Pension Benefit Guaranty Corporation) for traditional DB plans with employees
- Cash balance plans may have lower PBGC requirements
- Need a third-party administrator (TPA) and enrolled actuary

---

## Ideal Candidates

- Self-employed or small business owner with consistent high income ($250K+/year)
- Age 45+ (contribution limits increase with age)
- Few or no employees (employees must be covered, increasing costs)
- S-Corp or sole proprietorship/LLC structure
- Stable or growing income (can handle mandatory funding)
- Already maxing out 401(k)/SEP-IRA and wants additional shelter

---

## When NOT to Use

- Volatile income that may not support mandatory contributions
- Business with many employees (plan must cover them too — costly)
- Young taxpayers (contribution limits are low when retirement is far away)
- Short time horizon (plan should run 5+ years minimum)
- Business likely to be sold soon (plan termination complexity)

---

## Setup Timeline

- Plan must be established by the end of the tax year (December 31 for calendar year)
- Contributions can be made until the business's tax return due date (including extensions)
- Actuarial study must be completed before plan establishment
- Allow 60-90 days for plan design, actuarial study, and document preparation

## Key Takeaways

1. DB and cash balance plans are the maximum tax shelter for high-income self-employed — up to $350K+/year
2. Contribution limits INCREASE with age — the opposite of most retirement plans
3. Stack DB + 401(k) for maximum combined shelter ($300K-$400K+ for older owners)
4. S-Corp reasonable salary directly controls DB plan deduction ceiling — optimize the tradeoff
5. Mandatory annual funding — only for businesses with stable, predictable income
6. Few/no employees is ideal; more employees = higher costs
7. Must be established by December 31; contributions can be made until tax filing deadline
8. Requires enrolled actuary and TPA — budget $2K-$5K/year administration
9. Minimum 3-5 year commitment to avoid IRS scrutiny
10. Often the single highest-ROI tax strategy for qualifying taxpayers
