# ESTATE PLANNING — Comprehensive Tax Strategies

## Overview

Federal estate tax applies a 40% rate to taxable estates exceeding the applicable exclusion amount ($13.99M per individual in 2025, $27.98M per married couple). With the TCJA sunset scheduled for January 1, 2026, these exemptions are expected to drop to approximately $7M per individual (inflation-adjusted from the pre-TCJA $5.49M base). Planning NOW is not optional for estates above $7M.

---

## FEDERAL ESTATE TAX FUNDAMENTALS

### Current Framework (2025)

| Parameter | Amount |
|---|---|
| Exemption (individual) | $13,990,000 |
| Exemption (married, with portability) | $27,980,000 |
| Top rate | 40% |
| Annual gift exclusion | $19,000 per donee |
| Annual gift exclusion (married, split gifts) | $38,000 per donee |
| Generation-skipping transfer (GST) exemption | $13,990,000 |
| ss7520 rate (current) | ~5.4% |

### TCJA Sunset — The $7M Cliff

The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 (P.L. 115-97) doubled the estate/gift tax exemption from $5.49M to $11.18M (2018), indexed for inflation. This doubling SUNSETS on December 31, 2025, per ss11061 of the TCJA.

**Post-sunset projection (2026)**: Exemption drops to approximately $7,000,000 (the inflation-adjusted amount as if the TCJA had never been enacted).

**Anti-clawback rule**: Reg. ss20.2010-1(c) confirms that gifts made using the higher exemption before sunset will NOT be "clawed back" — the IRS will use the HIGHER of the exemption at death or at the time of gift. This is the regulatory green light for accelerated gifting.

**Critical planning window**: For individuals with estates between $7M and $13.99M, using the full exemption before 2026 is essentially a "use it or lose it" proposition. A married couple could transfer up to $27.98M before sunset, even though post-sunset they would only have ~$14M in exemption.

## PORTABILITY — DECEASED SPOUSAL UNUSED EXCLUSION AMOUNT (DSUEA)

### Mechanics (ss2010(c))

When the first spouse dies, any unused estate tax exemption can be "ported" to the surviving spouse. The surviving spouse then has their own exemption PLUS the DSUEA.

### MUST File Form 706

Portability is NOT automatic. The executor must file Form 706 (Federal Estate Tax Return) to elect portability, even if:
- No estate tax is due
- The estate is well below the exemption
- The only reason to file is portability

**Revenue Procedure 2022-32**: Provides a simplified late portability election for estates of decedents dying after 2010. Must file Form 706 within 5 years of the decedent's date of death (extended from 2 years by Rev. Proc. 2022-32).

### Portability vs Credit Shelter Trust

| Factor | Portability | Credit Shelter (Bypass) Trust |
|---|---|---|
| Must file Form 706 | YES | Trust must be funded |
| Protects future appreciation | NO — DSUEA is fixed at first death | YES — growth in trust is outside estate |
| Asset protection | NO — surviving spouse owns outright | YES — trust assets protected from creditors |
| GST exemption | NOT portable | Can allocate GST exemption to trust |
| State estate tax | Varies — many states don't recognize | Can shelter state exemption amount |
| Step-up at second death | YES (ss1014) | NO — trust assets don't get second step-up |

**Best practice**: For estates above $20M, use BOTH — credit shelter trust funded to the first spouse's exemption amount, PLUS portability election for any unused remainder. For estates under $20M, portability alone may suffice if asset protection is not a concern.

## ANNUAL EXCLUSION GIFTS

### Basic Mechanics (ss2503(b))

$19,000 per donee per year (2025). Married couples can "split" gifts (ss2513) to give $38,000 per donee even if only one spouse funds the gift. Requires Form 709 filing to elect gift splitting.

### Power of Consistency

Annual exclusion gifts to a family of 4 children and 4 spouses:
- Single grandparent: 8 donees x $19,000 = $152,000/year
- Married grandparents: 8 donees x $38,000 = $304,000/year
- Over 20 years: $6,080,000 transferred completely tax-free (not counting growth)

### Direct Tuition and Medical Payments (ss2503(e))

Payments made DIRECTLY to educational institutions for tuition or to medical providers for medical expenses are EXEMPT from gift tax — they do not count against the annual exclusion or lifetime exemption. Must be paid directly (not reimbursed to the student/patient).

**Strategy**: Grandparent pays $80,000 tuition directly to university, PLUS gives $19,000 annual exclusion gift to the same grandchild. Total: $99,000 transferred with zero gift tax consequence.

## 529 SUPERFUNDING

### Five-Year Election (ss529(c)(2)(B))

A donor can contribute up to 5 years' worth of annual exclusion gifts to a 529 plan in a single year:
- Individual: $19,000 x 5 = $95,000
- Married couple (split gifts): $38,000 x 5 = $190,000

Must file Form 709 for the year of contribution AND the following 4 years, reporting 1/5 of the gift each year. If the donor dies within the 5-year period, the pro-rata unused portion is included in the estate.

**SECURE 2.0 addition**: Starting 2024, 529 plan assets can be rolled over to a Roth IRA for the beneficiary (lifetime limit $35,000, subject to annual Roth contribution limits, 529 must have been open 15+ years).

## GRATs — GRANTOR RETAINED ANNUITY TRUSTS

### Mechanics (ss2702)

1. Grantor transfers assets to irrevocable trust
2. Grantor retains annuity payments for a fixed term
3. At end of term, remainder passes to beneficiaries
4. Gift value = FMV of assets transferred MINUS present value of retained annuity (using ss7520 rate)

### The Zeroed-Out GRAT

Structure the annuity so that the present value of retained payments equals the full FMV of the transferred assets — gift value = $0 (or technically, a few dollars due to the Walton case requirement).

Per Walton v. Commissioner, 115 T.C. 589 (2000): the IRS must value the retained annuity interest using the same ss7520 assumptions, even if the asset is likely to outperform.

### Rolling GRATs

Instead of one long-term GRAT, create a series of short-term (2-year) GRATs:
- If a GRAT's assets outperform the ss7520 rate, the excess passes tax-free
- If they underperform, the GRAT "zeros out" — you lose nothing (except transaction costs)
- Each maturing GRAT's remainder is contributed to the next GRAT
- This "reloads" the strategy and captures short-term spikes

**Worked Example — 2-Year Zeroed-Out GRAT**:
- Transfer $5M in growth stock to GRAT
- ss7520 rate: 5.4%
- Annuity: ~$2,640,000/year for 2 years (structured to zero out)
- Stock doubles to $10M in Year 1
- Year 1 annuity: $2.64M paid in stock (at current FMV)
- Year 2 annuity: $2.64M paid in stock
- Remainder to beneficiaries: ~$4.72M — transferred gift-tax-free
- If stock declines: annuity payments return most/all assets to grantor — no harm done

### GRAT Mortality Risk

If the grantor dies during the GRAT term, the annuity stream terminates and the full FMV of GRAT assets is included in the estate (ss2036). Mitigation:
- Short GRAT terms (2 years) minimize mortality risk
- Purchase term life insurance
- Use rolling GRATs to limit exposure

## SLATs — SPOUSAL LIFETIME ACCESS TRUSTS

### The Problem SLATs Solve

Many wealthy couples want to use their exemptions before the TCJA sunset but are reluctant to give away assets they might need. A SLAT allows one spouse to create an irrevocable trust for the benefit of the OTHER spouse, using the donor spouse's exemption while retaining indirect access to the funds through the beneficiary spouse.

### Structure

1. Spouse A creates irrevocable trust for the benefit of Spouse B (and descendants)
2. Spouse A gifts up to $13.99M to the trust
3. Spouse B can receive discretionary distributions from the trustee
4. Trust assets are outside Spouse A's estate
5. If Spouse B needs funds, the trustee distributes to Spouse B — who shares with Spouse A during the marriage

### Reciprocal Trust Doctrine Risk

If BOTH spouses create substantially identical SLATs for each other, the IRS may invoke the reciprocal trust doctrine (United States v. Estate of Grace, 395 U.S. 316 (1969)) to "uncross" the trusts, treating each spouse as the grantor of the trust benefiting themselves — negating the estate tax benefit.

**Mitigation strategies**:
- Create trusts at different times (6+ months apart)
- Different assets in each trust
- Different trustees
- Different distribution standards (one HEMS, one broader discretion)
- Different beneficiary classes
- Bittker & Lokken suggest structural differences must be "more than cosmetic"

## FAMILY LIMITED PARTNERSHIPS (FLPs)

### Valuation Discounts

FLP interests are entitled to valuation discounts for:
- **Lack of marketability**: 15-35% (cannot sell partnership interests on open market)
- **Minority/lack of control**: 15-40% (limited partners cannot control distributions, liquidation, management)
- **Combined discount**: 25-45% is typical range

### Worked Example

- Family transfers $20M of investment assets to FLP
- Parents are 1% general partners, children receive 99% limited partnership interests
- Appraiser values limited partnership interests at 35% discount
- Gift value of 99% LP interest: $20M x 99% x (1 - 35%) = $12,870,000
- Uses $12.87M of exemption to transfer $19.8M of underlying assets

### IRS Attack Vectors

The IRS aggressively challenges FLPs. Key cases:

- **Estate of Strangi v. Commissioner**, T.C. Memo 2003-145: FLP included in estate under ss2036(a)(1) because decedent retained enjoyment of transferred assets (FLP was mere recycling of assets with no legitimate non-tax purpose)
- **Estate of Bongard v. Commissioner**, 124 T.C. 95 (2005): Legitimate non-tax purposes (asset protection, centralized management, educating heirs) can save the FLP
- **Estate of Turner v. Commissioner**, T.C. Memo 2011-209: FLP respected where there were legitimate business activities and the decedent did not retain control

**Best practices to survive audit**:
1. Transfer operating businesses or actively managed investments (not personal-use assets)
2. Respect the entity: separate accounts, partnership tax returns, formal meetings
3. Do NOT commingle personal and partnership funds
4. Do NOT fund the FLP on the deathbed (see Estate of Powell, 148 T.C. 392 (2017))
5. Retain sufficient assets outside the FLP for personal living expenses
6. Obtain a qualified appraisal from a credentialed appraiser (ASA, MAI)

## IRREVOCABLE LIFE INSURANCE TRUST (ILIT)

### Why Use an ILIT

Life insurance proceeds are income-tax-free (ss101(a)), but they ARE included in the insured's gross estate if the insured holds "incidents of ownership" (ss2042) — power to change beneficiaries, borrow against the policy, surrender/cancel, etc.

An ILIT removes the policy from the estate:
- Trust owns the policy
- Trust is beneficiary of the policy
- Insured has NO incidents of ownership
- Death benefit passes estate-tax-free AND income-tax-free

### Three-Year Lookback (ss2035)

If the insured transfers an EXISTING policy to an ILIT and dies within 3 years, the policy is pulled back into the estate. Mitigation: have the ILIT purchase a NEW policy (the insured never owned it) or survive the 3-year period.

### Crummey Notices (Crummey v. Commissioner, 397 F.2d 82 (9th Cir. 1968))

To qualify premium payments as annual exclusion gifts (not taxable gifts), the trust must give beneficiaries a temporary withdrawal right:
- Typically 30-60 days to withdraw their share of the contribution
- Must provide written notice to each beneficiary
- Beneficiaries must have real economic interest (not just "naked" Crummey powers)
- Cristofani v. Commissioner, 97 T.C. 74 (1991): Even contingent beneficiaries can have Crummey powers, but IRS continues to challenge this

### Second-to-Die (Survivorship) Policies

For married couples, a second-to-die policy in an ILIT is ideal:
- No estate tax until second death (marital deduction covers first death)
- Second-to-die premiums are much lower (insures two lives)
- Provides exactly the liquidity needed at exactly the time estate tax is due

## GENERATION-SKIPPING TRANSFER TAX (GSTT)

### Three Types of GST Events

1. **Direct skip**: Transfer directly to a skip person (grandchild when parent is alive)
2. **Taxable distribution**: Distribution from a trust to a skip person
3. **Taxable termination**: Trust interest terminates and skip persons are the only remaining beneficiaries

### Exemption Allocation Strategies

- **Allocate to trusts expected to grow the most**: GST exemption is most efficient when allocated to assets with high growth potential
- **Reverse QTIP election** (ss2652(a)(3)): Allows the deceased spouse's executor to allocate the deceased spouse's GST exemption to a QTIP trust, even though the QTIP causes the trust to be included in the surviving spouse's estate for estate tax purposes
- **Late allocation**: Possible but assessed at FMV at time of late allocation (not at time of transfer), often much more expensive

### Predeceased Ancestor Exception (ss2651(e))

If a grandchild's parent (the transferor's child) is deceased at the time of the transfer, the grandchild is treated as the transferor's child (NOT a skip person). This means no GSTT applies. Important for transfers to grandchildren whose parent has died.

## BASIS AT DEATH VS BASIS OF GIFTS

### Stepped-Up Basis at Death (ss1014)

Assets included in the decedent's gross estate receive a basis equal to their fair market value at date of death (or alternate valuation date under ss2032, if elected).

**Worked Example**:
- Decedent purchased stock for $100,000 (basis)
- FMV at death: $1,000,000
- Heir's basis: $1,000,000
- If heir sells for $1,000,000: $0 capital gain
- Avoided tax: $900,000 gain x 23.8% = $214,200

### Step-Down Risk

Section 1014 also means losses can be lost:
- Decedent purchased stock for $500,000
- FMV at death: $200,000
- Heir's basis: $200,000 — the $300,000 loss is gone forever

**Planning**: Sell loss assets BEFORE death to harvest the capital loss, then leave cash to heirs.

### Carryover Basis for Gifts (ss1015)

Gifts during life carry the donor's basis (plus adjustment for gift tax paid on appreciation, under ss1015(d)):
- Donor's basis: $100,000
- FMV at gift: $1,000,000
- Donee's basis: $100,000
- If donee sells for $1,000,000: $900,000 capital gain

### Strategic Basis Selection

| Scenario | Best Approach | Why |
|---|---|---|
| Highly appreciated asset, donor near death | HOLD until death | Step-up eliminates gain |
| Loss asset, donor near death | SELL before death | Harvest loss on donor's return |
| Appreciated asset, donor young and healthy | Consider gifting if using exemption | Estate tax savings may exceed capital gains cost |
| Low-basis asset, no estate tax concern | HOLD until death | Step-up is free; gifting creates carryover basis |

### Community Property Step-Up (ss1014(b)(6))

In community property states (AZ, CA, ID, LA, NM, NV, TX, WA, WI), BOTH halves of community property receive a step-up at the first spouse's death — not just the decedent's half. This is an enormous advantage over common law states where only the decedent's half gets stepped up.

## QUALIFIED DOMESTIC TRUST (QDOT) — ss2056A

### The Problem

The unlimited marital deduction (ss2056) does NOT apply to transfers to a surviving spouse who is NOT a U.S. citizen. Without a QDOT, the entire transfer is subject to estate tax at the first death.

### QDOT Requirements

1. At least one trustee must be a U.S. citizen or domestic corporation
2. Trust must satisfy requirements set by Treasury regulations to ensure estate tax collection
3. If trust corpus exceeds $2M: must have a U.S. bank trustee or furnish a bond/letter of credit for 65% of FMV
4. Executor must make the QDOT election on Form 706

### Tax Treatment

- Estate tax is DEFERRED (not eliminated) until the surviving spouse receives a distribution of corpus or dies
- Income distributions are NOT subject to estate tax
- Corpus distributions are subject to estate tax at the rate that would have applied at the first spouse's death

### Alternative: Increased Annual Exclusion

For non-citizen spouses, the annual gift exclusion is increased to $190,000 (2025) instead of the standard $19,000. This allows significant lifetime transfers without the QDOT structure.

### New York's Estate Tax Cliff

New York imposes a devastating "cliff": if the taxable estate exceeds 105% of the exemption ($7,287,000 in 2025), the ENTIRE exemption is lost — not just the excess. An estate of $7.3M could face ~$585,000 MORE in NY estate tax than an estate of $6.94M.

**Mitigation**: Gifts to reduce the NY taxable estate to just under the exemption. Note: NY does NOT have a gift tax (except for gifts made within 3 years of death, which are "clawed back" under NY EPTL ss3-3.4(b)).

### State Decoupling Strategies

For residents of states with low exemptions:
1. **Bypass trust** funded to the state exemption amount (e.g., $2M for MA) — shelters state exemption
2. **QTIP trust** for the balance between state and federal exemptions
3. **Partial QTIP election** — include enough in the surviving spouse's estate to use the federal exemption, but not more than necessary for state purposes
4. **Domicile change**: Moving to a state with no estate tax (FL, TX, NV, etc.) eliminates state estate tax entirely. Must establish true domicile (driver's license, voter registration, homestead exemption, etc.)

## CHARITABLE PLANNING AT DEATH

### Estate Tax Charitable Deduction (ss2055)

Unlimited deduction for bequests to qualified charities. Unlike the income tax charitable deduction, there is no AGI limitation.

### Charitable Strategies at Death

1. **Direct bequest**: Simple, fully deductible, no income tax benefit during life
2. **CRT at death**: Provides income stream to non-charitable beneficiaries, then remainder to charity. Estate gets deduction for present value of charitable remainder.
3. **Private foundation**: Family retains control over charitable giving, perpetual existence, 5% annual distribution requirement
4. **Donor Advised Fund**: Simpler than foundation, no excise taxes, but donor advisory committee has less control
5. **Charitable Lead Trust at death**: Reduces estate tax by the present value of the charitable annuity/unitrust stream, remainder passes to heirs at reduced transfer tax cost

### IRD and Charity — The Double Tax Problem

Income in Respect of a Decedent (IRD) — such as traditional IRA/401(k) balances — is subject to BOTH estate tax and income tax. Naming charity as beneficiary of IRD assets eliminates both taxes (estate tax deduction offsets estate inclusion, and charity pays no income tax on distribution). Meanwhile, leave non-IRD assets (which get a step-up in basis) to heirs.

**Worked Example**:
- Estate: $5M IRA (IRD) + $5M stock portfolio (basis $2M)
- Leave IRA to charity: $5M estate tax deduction, $0 income tax
- Leave stock to heirs: $5M stepped-up basis, $0 capital gain
- Total tax savings vs leaving IRA to heirs: ~$2M (estate tax) + ~$1.19M (income tax) = ~$3.19M

## ORDER OF OPERATIONS — ESTATE PLANNING CHECKLIST

1. **Calculate current estate value** including life insurance, retirement accounts, real property, business interests
2. **Determine exposure**: Is the estate above $7M (post-sunset risk) or $13.99M (current risk)?
3. **File Form 706 for first-to-die**: Elect portability (DSUEA) — always
4. **Use annual exclusion gifts**: $19K per donee, $190K to non-citizen spouse
5. **Direct payments**: Tuition and medical directly to providers (ss2503(e))
6. **529 superfunding**: $95K/$190K lump sum if appropriate
7. **GRATs for appreciated assets**: Zeroed-out rolling 2-year GRATs
8. **SLATs before sunset**: Use remaining exemption while retaining indirect access
9. **FLP/LLC for family business/investments**: Obtain valuation discount
10. **ILIT for life insurance**: Remove from estate
11. **Dynasty trust**: For long-term multi-generational planning
12. **Review beneficiary designations**: Ensure IRD assets go to charity, stepped-up basis assets go to heirs
13. **State planning**: Address state estate tax exposure with bypass trusts, domicile change
14. **Document everything**: Qualified appraisals, timely gift tax returns, Crummey notices
