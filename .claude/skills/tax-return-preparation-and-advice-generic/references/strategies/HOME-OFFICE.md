# Home Office Deduction Guide

## Overview

The home office deduction (Section 280A) allows taxpayers who use part of their home regularly and exclusively for business to deduct a portion of home expenses. This is one of the most commonly misunderstood and under-claimed deductions.

**Important:** W-2 employees generally cannot claim a home office deduction for Federal tax purposes (the TCJA suspended the unreimbursed employee expense deduction through 2025). This deduction is available to self-employed individuals (Schedule C), partners, and through certain S-Corp arrangements.

---

## IRS Requirements (Section 280A)

### Two Mandatory Tests

1. **Exclusive Use:** The space must be used exclusively for business. A desk in a bedroom that is also used for personal purposes does NOT qualify. Exception: storage of inventory/product samples and daycare facilities.

2. **Regular Use:** The space must be used on a regular basis for business (not occasional or incidental).

### Additional Requirement (Meet One)

The home office must be:
- **Principal place of business** -- where you conduct substantial administrative or management activities and have no other fixed location for those activities, OR
- **A place where you meet clients/customers** in the normal course of business, OR
- **A separate structure** not attached to the home (e.g., detached garage, studio)

### What "Exclusive Use" Means in Practice

- A dedicated room with a door is ideal
- A clearly defined area within a room can qualify (e.g., a partitioned section of a basement)
- A room that doubles as a guest bedroom does NOT qualify
- A room used as both an office and a playroom does NOT qualify
- The IRS does not require a door, but the space must be identifiable and dedicated

## Simplified Method

### How It Works

- **$5 per square foot** of dedicated home office space
- **Maximum 300 square feet** (maximum deduction: $1,500)
- No depreciation, no Form 8829 required
- Reported directly on Schedule C, line 30

### Advantages

- Extremely simple -- no need to track actual home expenses
- No depreciation recapture when home is sold
- Reduces audit risk

### Disadvantages

- Maximum deduction is only $1,500
- Cannot carry forward unused deduction (no loss carryforward)
- Usually results in a smaller deduction than the regular method for offices over 100 sq ft in moderate-to-high-cost homes

### When to Use Simplified Method

- Office is small (under 150 sq ft)
- Home expenses are low (low-cost housing, no mortgage)
- Taxpayer wants minimal recordkeeping
- Marginal tax rate is low (small deduction matters less)

## Regular Method (Form 8829)

### How It Works

1. **Calculate business percentage** of the home
2. **Apply that percentage** to allowable home expenses
3. **Add direct expenses** (expenses solely for the office, like painting the office)
4. **Apply ordering rules** and income limitation

### Calculating Business Percentage

**Square footage method (preferred by IRS):**
```
Business % = Office square footage / Total home square footage
```

**Room count method (acceptable if rooms are approximately equal size):**
```
Business % = Number of rooms used for business / Total rooms in home
```

**Time-based method (for daycare facilities only):**
```
Business % = (Square footage % ) x (Hours used for daycare / Total hours in year)
```

### Allowable Expenses on Form 8829

**Direct expenses (100% deductible):**
- Painting the office
- Repairs specific to the office
- Built-in shelving for the office

**Indirect expenses (deductible at business percentage):**
- Mortgage interest (or rent)
- Real estate taxes
- Homeowner's insurance
- Utilities (electric, gas, water, internet)
- Repairs and maintenance (whole house)
- HOA fees
- Pest control
- Security system
- Depreciation of the home (not land)

**NOT deductible:**
- Lawn care (unless home office has separate entrance used by clients)
- First phone line (personal)
- Capital improvements (added to basis for depreciation instead)

### Depreciation Component

- The home (excluding land) is depreciated over 39 years (nonresidential real property rules for home office portion)
- Land is NOT depreciable -- must allocate purchase price between land and building
- Typical land allocation: 15-30% of total value (varies by area; use property tax assessment ratio or appraisal)
- Depreciation must be claimed (even if you choose not to, the IRS treats it as "allowed or allowable" for recapture purposes)

### Income Limitation

Home office expenses cannot create a loss on Schedule C. The ordering rules are:

1. **First:** Business percentage of mortgage interest and real estate taxes (deductible regardless -- goes to Schedule A if not used here)
2. **Second:** Operating expenses (utilities, insurance, repairs) at business percentage
3. **Third:** Depreciation

If income is insufficient, unused expenses carry forward to the next year (except items in group 1, which are deductible on Schedule A regardless).

### Example Calculation

```
Home: 2,000 sq ft total, 200 sq ft office = 10% business use

Annual expenses:
  Mortgage interest:     $18,000  x 10% = $1,800
  Real estate taxes:      $8,000  x 10% = $800
  Insurance:              $2,400  x 10% = $240
  Utilities:              $4,800  x 10% = $480
  Repairs (whole house):  $1,200  x 10% = $120
  Internet:               $1,800  x 10% = $180
  Depreciation:           $6,500* x 10% = $650

Total home office deduction: $4,270

* Depreciation = (Home cost - land) / 39 years
  Example: ($300,000 home - $47,000 land) / 39 = ~$6,487/year
```

## Mid-Year Move: Two Forms 8829

If the taxpayer moves during the year, they can claim home office deductions for BOTH homes:

### Form 8829 #1 (Old Home)

- Business percentage based on old home dimensions
- Expenses pro-rated for months lived there
- Example: Lived there Jan-Apr (4 months) -> 4/12 = 33.3% of annual expenses

### Form 8829 #2 (New Home)

- Business percentage based on new home dimensions (likely different square footage)
- Expenses pro-rated for months lived there
- Example: Lived there May-Dec (8 months) -> 8/12 = 66.7% of annual expenses

### Key Points for Mid-Year Move

- Each Form 8829 has its own business percentage
- Mortgage interest, taxes, insurance all pro-rated by occupancy period
- Depreciation calculated separately for each home
- Must actually have qualifying home office in BOTH homes (exclusive and regular use in each)
- Moving expenses are generally NOT deductible (TCJA suspended for non-military through 2025)

## Multiple Businesses in Same Home

If both spouses have separate businesses (or one person has multiple Schedule Cs), each can claim a home office deduction -- but NO double-dipping:

### Rules

- Each business must have its own dedicated space (or share space with allocation)
- The total business percentage across all businesses cannot exceed the actual percentage of the home used for business
- If Spouse A uses 10% for Business A and Spouse B uses 8% for Business B, total is 18%
- Each files their own Form 8829 on their own Schedule C
- Shared expenses (mortgage, utilities) are split according to each office's percentage
- Cannot claim the same square footage for two businesses simultaneously

### Example: Two-Spouse Household

```
Home: 2,500 sq ft
Spouse A office: 250 sq ft (10%)  -> Form 8829 on Spouse A's Schedule C
Spouse B office: 200 sq ft (8%)   -> Form 8829 on Spouse B's Schedule C
Total business use: 450 sq ft (18%)

Mortgage interest $24,000:
  Spouse A claims: $24,000 x 10% = $2,400
  Spouse B claims: $24,000 x 8% = $1,920
  Personal (Schedule A): $24,000 x 82% = $19,680
```

## S-Corp Interaction

S-Corp shareholder-employees cannot claim a home office deduction directly on their personal return (they are W-2 employees of the S-Corp, and employee home office deductions are suspended through 2025). Instead, two approaches:

### Option 1: Accountable Plan Reimbursement

1. S-Corp adopts an accountable plan (written policy)
2. Shareholder-employee submits expense report with documentation
3. S-Corp reimburses the home office expenses
4. Reimbursement is:
   - Deductible by the S-Corp (reduces corporate income)
   - Tax-free to the employee (not reported on W-2)
   - Not subject to payroll taxes

**Requirements for accountable plan:**
- Business connection (home office qualifies)
- Adequate accounting (expense reports with substantiation)
- Return of excess reimbursements within reasonable time

### Option 2: Rental Arrangement (Section 280A(c)(6))

1. Shareholder rents the home office space to the S-Corp
2. S-Corp pays rent (deductible business expense)
3. Shareholder reports rental income on Schedule E
4. Shareholder deducts allocable expenses against rental income
5. Net effect can be tax-favorable but more complex

**Caution:** The rental arrangement is more aggressive and less commonly used. The IRS may scrutinize above-market rents. The accountable plan approach is generally safer and simpler.

## Documentation Requirements

### What to Keep

1. **Floor plan or diagram** -- showing office area within the home, with dimensions
2. **Photos** -- of the dedicated office space (showing it is used exclusively for business)
3. **Usage log** -- especially for spaces that might be questioned (not strictly required but valuable in audit)
4. **Receipts and records** for all claimed expenses:
   - Mortgage statements (Form 1098)
   - Property tax bills
   - Insurance declarations page
   - Utility bills (12 months)
   - Repair invoices
   - Internet bills
5. **Home purchase/rental agreement** -- for establishing basis (depreciation) or rent amount
6. **Property tax assessment** -- for land vs. building allocation

### How Long to Keep

- Keep home office records for at least 3 years after the later of: (a) filing the return, or (b) selling the home
- Depreciation records must be kept for as long as you own the home PLUS 3 years after the return reporting the sale

## Common Audit Triggers

1. **Identical business percentages** across different homes (looks formulaic)
2. **Zeroing out Schedule C** -- home office deduction that exactly equals net income raises flags
3. **Round numbers** -- 25.0% or 50.0% business use looks estimated rather than measured
4. **Very high business percentage** -- claiming 50%+ of a home for business invites scrutiny
5. **Home office claimed on high-income W-2 returns** -- W-2 employees generally cannot claim this
6. **Inconsistency with other returns** -- claiming home office in a year but not in adjacent years without explanation
7. **Luxury homes** -- large dollar amounts from expensive homes draw attention

### Audit Defense Best Practices

- Use precise measurements (e.g., 12.7% not 13%)
- Take and date-stamp photos of the office annually
- Keep the space genuinely exclusive -- no personal items, no guest bed
- Document any changes (renovation, move, expansion of office area)
- Be consistent year to year unless circumstances genuinely change

## Depreciation Recapture on Home Sale

When a home with a claimed home office deduction is sold, depreciation recapture applies:

- Recapture is taxed at a maximum rate of 25% (Section 1250)
- Applies to depreciation actually claimed OR "allowed or allowable" (even if not claimed)
- The Section 121 exclusion ($250K single / $500K MFJ) does NOT exclude depreciation recapture
- This means even if the overall gain is excluded under Section 121, the depreciation portion is recaptured

### Example

```
Depreciation claimed over 8 years: $5,200
Home sale gain: $150,000 (within Section 121 exclusion)

Section 121 excludes: $150,000 - $5,200 = $144,800 excluded
Depreciation recapture: $5,200 x 25% = $1,300 tax owed
```

### Simplified Method Advantage

If the simplified method was used in all years, there is NO depreciation to recapture (because no depreciation was ever claimed or allowable under the simplified method).

## State Considerations

- Most states conform to Federal home office rules
- Some states have additional requirements or restrictions
- State income tax on home office deductions may differ from Federal treatment
- Renter vs. owner treatment is the same for Federal purposes (rent replaces mortgage interest)
- Some cities/counties require a home occupation permit or business license for home-based businesses
