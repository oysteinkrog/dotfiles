# YEAR-END TAX PLANNING CHECKLIST: MONTH-BY-MONTH GUIDE

## Philosophy

Effective tax planning is not a December 31 scramble. It requires year-round monitoring,
quarterly adjustments, and strategic timing of income, deductions, and transactions.
This checklist organizes critical deadlines and planning opportunities by month to
ensure nothing is missed.

All dollar figures are 2025 amounts unless otherwise noted. Verify current-year limits
annually as they are indexed for inflation.

---

## OCTOBER

### Review Year-to-Date Tax Position
- [ ] Pull year-to-date income summary: W-2 stub, business P&L, investment account
  statements, rental property income/expense
- [ ] Compare actual income to projections made in January — significant variance
  requires immediate strategy adjustment
- [ ] Run preliminary tax projection (Form 1040 estimate) using year-to-date figures
  plus reasonable Q4 estimates
- [ ] Determine if estimated tax payments are adequate — if underpaid, consider
  increasing W-4 withholding (withholding is treated as paid ratably throughout the
  year regardless of when actually withheld, per Section 3402 — this avoids
  underpayment penalties even if Q1-Q3 estimated payments were short)

### Tax-Loss Harvesting — First Pass
- [ ] Review investment portfolio for positions with unrealized losses
- [ ] Identify losses that can offset realized gains from earlier in the year
- [ ] Sell loss positions before October 31 to allow the 30-day wash sale window
  (Section 1091) to expire before any year-end rebalancing
- [ ] If losses exceed gains: up to $3,000 of net capital loss offsets ordinary income
  ($1,500 if MFS); excess carries forward indefinitely
- [ ] Replacement strategy: immediately purchase a similar (but NOT substantially
  identical) security to maintain market exposure during the 31-day wash sale window.
  Example: sell S&P 500 index fund, buy total market index fund

### Capital Gain Harvesting (Low-Bracket Taxpayers)
- [ ] If projected taxable income is below the 0% LTCG threshold ($96,700 MFJ,
  $48,350 Single in 2025), consider selling appreciated assets to recognize gains
  at 0% rate
- [ ] Calculate exactly how much gain can be recognized at 0% — do not overshoot
  into the 15% bracket
- [ ] Particularly valuable for retirees or taxpayers in a low-income year

### Extended Return Filing
- [ ] **October 15: Filing deadline for extended individual returns (Form 1040)**
- [ ] Verify all K-1s have been received from partnerships, S-Corps, estates, and trusts
- [ ] If still missing K-1s: file with best estimates, amend when received
- [ ] Ensure state extended returns are filed simultaneously (most states conform to
  October 15, but verify)

---

## NOVEMBER

### 401(k) / 403(b) / 457(b) Contribution Maximization
- [ ] Review year-to-date 401(k) contributions on most recent pay stub
- [ ] Calculate remaining payroll periods in the year
- [ ] Adjust deferral percentage to ensure you hit $23,500 limit by December 31
  (or $31,000 if age 50-59/64+, or $34,750 if age 60-63 under SECURE 2.0 super catch-up)
- [ ] Verify employer match is not affected by front-loading contributions (some
  plans match per-paycheck, not annually — may lose match by hitting limit early)
- [ ] If plan allows after-tax (non-Roth) contributions: consider mega backdoor Roth
  strategy up to the Section 415(c) limit of $70,000 total ($77,500 with catch-up)

### Open Enrollment (Benefits Election for Next Year)
- [ ] **Health Savings Account (HSA):** Elect maximum contribution for next year
  ($4,300 individual / $8,550 family for 2025; verify 2026 limits when published).
  Must be enrolled in HDHP.
- [ ] **Health FSA:** Elect up to $3,300 for 2025 (verify 2026 limit). Use-or-lose
  rule applies — estimate medical expenses carefully. Some plans offer $660 carryover
  OR 2.5-month grace period (not both).
- [ ] **Dependent Care FSA:** Elect up to $5,000 if applicable. Compare to Child and
  Dependent Care Credit — DCFSA usually wins because it saves FICA (7.65%) in addition
  to income tax.
- [ ] **Qualified transportation:** Elect pre-tax transit ($325/month) and/or parking
  ($325/month) for next year.

### Charitable Giving Strategy
- [ ] Evaluate bunching strategy: if standard deduction is close to itemized deductions,
  consider bunching 2+ years of charitable contributions into one year to exceed the
  standard deduction threshold ($15,750 Single / $31,500 MFJ in 2025)
- [ ] **Donor-Advised Fund (DAF):** Contribute a large amount to DAF this year for
  full tax deduction, then distribute to charities over multiple years
- [ ] **Contribute appreciated long-term stock directly to charity:** Deduct FMV,
  avoid recognizing capital gain entirely (Section 170(e); must be held >1 year)
- [ ] **Qualified Charitable Distribution (QCD):** If age 70.5+, direct up to $108,000
  from IRA to charity. Counts toward RMD but is excluded from AGI. Superior to
  taking the RMD and then donating (QCD avoids AGI increase that affects IRMAA,
  Social Security taxation, and other AGI-based thresholds).
- [ ] Charitable deduction limits: 60% of AGI for cash to public charities, 30% of
  AGI for appreciated property to public charities, 20% for private foundations.
  Excess carries forward 5 years.

### ISO Exercise Strategy (If Applicable)
- [ ] Review year-to-date ISO exercises and any disqualifying dispositions
- [ ] Calculate AMT exposure: ISO bargain element (spread at exercise) is an AMT
  preference item
- [ ] Model how many additional shares can be exercised before triggering AMT liability
- [ ] If already in AMT: exercising additional ISOs may be "free" if the additional
  spread does not increase total AMTI above what triggers additional AMT
- [ ] Consider exercising ISOs in low-income years to minimize AMT impact

### Roth Conversion Planning — Finalize Amount
- [ ] Review year-to-date income to determine how much "bracket space" remains
- [ ] **Strategy:** Convert enough traditional IRA/401(k) to fill up the current
  bracket without jumping to the next. Example: if $50,000 of room before the 32%
  bracket, convert $50,000.
- [ ] Consider long-term tax rate expectations: if future rates are expected to be
  higher (due to legislation, RMDs, or income changes), converting now is advantageous
- [ ] Factor in IRMAA: Medicare Part B/D surcharges begin at $106,000 (Single) /
  $212,000 (MFJ) MAGI. A Roth conversion that pushes MAGI above these thresholds
  triggers IRMAA surcharges 2 years later.
- [ ] **Roth conversions are IRREVOCABLE after December 31.** There is no recharacterization
  of conversions (eliminated by TCJA). Finalize the number with your tax projection.

## DECEMBER

### Section 179 and Bonus Depreciation — Equipment Purchases
- [ ] **Section 179:** Purchase AND place in service qualifying equipment by December 31.
  2025 limit: $2,500,000 (phase-out begins at $4,000,000 total equipment placed in service).
- [ ] **Bonus depreciation (Section 168(k)):** For eligible property acquired and placed in
  service after January 19, 2025, 100% bonus depreciation is back. Verify acquisition date,
  placed-in-service date, and any transitional election before assuming full expensing.
- [ ] "Placed in service" means ready and available for use — not merely ordered or
  paid for. A machine sitting in the factory crate on December 31 is NOT placed in service.
- [ ] Vehicle limitations: Section 280F caps first-year depreciation on passenger vehicles.
  2025 limit with bonus depreciation: $20,200 (verify current year).
  SUVs over 6,000 lbs GVWR: Section 179 limit of $31,300, plus any otherwise available depreciation.

### Solo 401(k) — Employee Contribution Deadline
- [ ] **Employee elective deferrals MUST be made by December 31** — no extension.
  $23,500 (or $31,000 if age 50-59/64+, $34,750 if age 60-63).
- [ ] Employer profit-sharing contributions generally have until the tax filing deadline
  (including extensions), but verify the current adoption rules for the taxpayer's entity type.
- [ ] **If you do not yet have a Solo 401(k): do not rely on a blanket December 31 rule.**
  Verify whether the taxpayer can still adopt the plan for employer contributions and whether any
  desired employee elective deferral is still available.

### Roth Conversion — Final Deadline
- [ ] **December 31 is the absolute deadline for Roth conversions.**
- [ ] Process conversion by mid-December to ensure it clears by year-end (custodians
  may have processing delays during the holiday period).
- [ ] Confirm the conversion amount with your tax advisor based on final year income.

### Tax-Loss Harvesting — Final Deadline
- [ ] Sell losing positions by the last trading day of the year (typically December 31
  or the last business day before).
- [ ] Trade date (not settlement date) determines the tax year for the transaction.
- [ ] Remember the wash sale rule: do NOT repurchase substantially identical securities
  within 30 days before or after the sale — this includes purchases in IRAs or 401(k)s,
  or by a spouse, per IRS guidance.

### Charitable Contributions — Final Deadline
- [ ] **Cash contributions:** Must be delivered (mailed, charged, or electronically
  transferred) by December 31.
- [ ] **Stock contributions:** Must be transferred to the charity's brokerage account
  by December 31. Initiate transfers by mid-December — DTC transfers can take 3-5
  business days.
- [ ] **Real property or other non-cash contributions over $5,000:** Qualified appraisal
  required (Section 170(f)(11)). Obtain appraisal before year-end.
- [ ] **Written acknowledgment:** Get contemporaneous written acknowledgment from the
  charity for any single contribution of $250 or more (Section 170(f)(8)).

### Required Minimum Distribution (RMD)
- [ ] **December 31 deadline** for all RMDs (except first-year RMDs, which can be
  delayed to April 1 of the following year — but then two RMDs in year 2).
- [ ] Applies to traditional IRAs, SEP-IRAs, SIMPLE IRAs, 401(k)s, 403(b)s, and
  other qualified plans for individuals who have reached their Required Beginning Date.
- [ ] SECURE 2.0 RMD age: 73 for those born 1951-1959, 75 for those born 1960+.
- [ ] **Roth IRAs: No RMD during owner's lifetime** (but inherited Roth IRAs may have
  distribution requirements).
- [ ] Penalty for missed RMD: 25% excise tax on the shortfall (reduced to 10% if
  corrected within 2 years under SECURE 2.0). Previously 50%.
- [ ] Consider QCD strategy (see November, above) for RMD amounts.

### 529 Contributions
- [ ] Contribute by December 31 for most state tax deductions.
- [ ] Some states (e.g., Georgia, Mississippi, Oklahoma, South Carolina, Wisconsin)
  allow contributions until April 15 for prior-year deduction — verify your state.
- [ ] 5-year superfunding: up to $95,000 per beneficiary ($19,000 x 5) using gift
  tax averaging, or $190,000 for a married couple using gift-splitting. Must file Form 709 and elect over 5 years.

### Annual Gift Exclusion
- [ ] Use the $19,000 per donee annual gift tax exclusion before year-end.
- [ ] Each spouse can give $19,000 = $38,000 per donee if gift-splitting (Form 709).
- [ ] Gifts to 529 plans count toward the annual exclusion.
- [ ] Gifts do NOT include: tuition paid directly to educational institutions or
  medical expenses paid directly to providers (Section 2503(e) — unlimited exclusion
  for these direct payments).

### W-4 Adjustment for Estimated Tax Shortfall
- [ ] If estimated tax payments are short, increase W-4 withholding for remaining
  December paychecks.
- [ ] **W-4 withholding is treated as paid evenly throughout the year** (Section 3402),
  so a large December withholding retroactively covers Q1-Q3 shortfalls.
- [ ] This is the single best way to avoid underpayment penalties when you realize
  late in the year that estimated payments were insufficient.
- [ ] If you have an employer AND are self-employed: increase W-4 withholding to cover
  both the employment and self-employment tax shortfall.

### Review AGI for Next-Year Implications
- [ ] **ACA Premium Tax Credit:** AGI thresholds affect marketplace insurance subsidies.
  There is no automatic 400% FPL cliff for 2025, but AGI still materially affects subsidies.
  If income is near a subsidy inflection point, run Form 8962 projections before accelerating or deferring income.
- [ ] **IRMAA (Medicare surcharges):** Based on MAGI from 2 years prior. If 2025 MAGI
  will trigger 2027 IRMAA surcharges, evaluate whether Roth conversions or other
  income recognition should be deferred.
- [ ] **Social Security taxation:** Up to 85% of Social Security benefits are taxable
  if combined income exceeds $44,000 (MFJ) or $34,000 (Single).
- [ ] **Student loan repayment:** Income-driven repayment (IDR) is based on AGI.
  Higher AGI means higher monthly payments.
- [ ] **Education credits:** American Opportunity and Lifetime Learning Credits phase
  out at higher MAGI levels.

### Prepay Deductible Expenses (Bunching Strategy)
- [ ] If bunching deductions into the current year, prepay:
  - State and local property taxes (subject to the personal SALT cap)
  - January mortgage payment (December interest is deductible in current year)
  - Charitable contributions (see above)
  - Medical expenses (if over 7.5% AGI floor and itemizing)
- [ ] Caution on state income tax prepayment: the personal SALT cap limits the benefit
  of prepaying state taxes. For 2025, the cap is generally $40,000 ($20,000 MFS,
  subject to MAGI-based reduction). Only prepay if you have remaining effective SALT cap room.

## JANUARY

### Q4 Estimated Tax Payment
- [ ] **January 15: Fourth quarter estimated tax payment due** (Form 1040-ES).
- [ ] Exception: if you file your completed return AND pay all remaining tax by
  January 31, you can skip the Q4 estimated payment entirely (Section 6654(d)(2)(C)).

### Information Return Filing (Employers and Businesses)
- [ ] **January 31: Issue Form W-2** to all employees (also file with SSA).
- [ ] **January 31: Issue Form 1099-NEC** to all independent contractors paid $600+.
- [ ] **January 31: Issue Form 1099-MISC** for rent, royalties, and other payments
  (some 1099-MISC categories have February 28 deadline for paper / March 31 for e-file).
- [ ] **January 31: Form 940** (annual FUTA return) filing and payment deadline.
- [ ] **January 31: Form 941** (Q4 payroll tax return) or Form 944 (annual, if eligible).
- [ ] Penalties for late or missing information returns: $60-$310 per return
  depending on how late (Section 6721/6722).

### Prior-Year IRA and HSA Contributions
- [ ] **You have until April 15 to contribute to an IRA for the prior tax year.**
  Traditional IRA: $7,000 ($8,000 if age 50+). Deductibility depends on AGI and
  retirement plan participation.
- [ ] **You have until April 15 to contribute to an HSA for the prior tax year.**
  $4,300 individual / $8,550 family (2025). Must have been covered by an HDHP.
- [ ] **Backdoor Roth IRA:** If income exceeds Roth IRA limits, contribute to
  non-deductible traditional IRA, then convert to Roth. Watch for pro-rata rule
  (Form 8606) if you have existing pre-tax IRA balances.
- [ ] **Roth IRA contributions** can also be made until April 15 for the prior year.
  2025 income phase-out: $236,000-$246,000 (MFJ), $150,000-$165,000 (Single).

### Prior-Year SEP-IRA Contribution
- [ ] SEP-IRA contributions can be made until the filing deadline INCLUDING extensions
  (October 15 if extended). The plan itself can also be established by the filing deadline.
- [ ] Contribution limit: 25% of W-2 wages (S-Corp/C-Corp) or ~20% of net
  self-employment income (sole prop/partnership). Maximum $70,000 for 2025.
- [ ] If you have a Solo 401(k), the EMPLOYER contribution portion has the same
  extended deadline. Only the EMPLOYEE deferral portion must be made by December 31.

## QUARTERLY RECURRING ITEMS

### Estimated Tax Payments (Form 1040-ES)
- [ ] **April 15:** Q1 payment (covers January 1 - March 31)
- [ ] **June 15:** Q2 payment (covers April 1 - May 31)
- [ ] **September 15:** Q3 payment (covers June 1 - August 31)
- [ ] **January 15 (following year):** Q4 payment (covers September 1 - December 31)

**Safe harbor rules (Section 6654):** No underpayment penalty if total payments
(withholding + estimated) equal or exceed:
- 90% of current year tax liability, OR
- 100% of prior year tax liability (110% if prior year AGI exceeded $150,000)

The 110% prior-year safe harbor is the safest approach for variable-income taxpayers.

### Quarterly Business Activities
- [ ] Review year-to-date income vs. projections — adjust estimated payments if needed
- [ ] Adjust W-4 withholding if income is tracking higher or lower than expected
- [ ] Track business expenses and mileage CONTEMPORANEOUSLY (Section 274(d) —
  retroactive reconstruction of records is insufficient for IRS audit purposes)
- [ ] Review accounts receivable: accelerate or defer invoicing based on income timing strategy
- [ ] Reconcile payroll: verify employer and employee tax deposits are current

### Quarterly Payroll Tax Deposits
- [ ] **Form 941** due April 30, July 31, October 31, January 31
- [ ] Deposit schedule: semi-weekly or monthly depending on total payroll tax liability
  (lookback period determines schedule)
- [ ] Trust fund recovery penalty (Section 6672): responsible persons are personally
  liable for unpaid employee withholding — this pierces corporate protection

## ANNUAL STRATEGIC ITEMS

### March 15 Deadlines
- [ ] **S-Corp election (Form 2553):** Must be filed by March 15 for the election to
  be effective for the current tax year. Late elections possible with reasonable cause.
- [ ] **Partnership and S-Corp tax returns:** Form 1065 and Form 1120-S due March 15
  (or request 6-month extension via Form 7004 by March 15).
- [ ] **PTET election:** Many states require Pass-Through Entity Tax election by
  March 15 for the current tax year. Some states allow election on the return (due
  March 15 or extended date). Check your specific state's requirements.

### Entity Structure Review (Annually)
- [ ] Evaluate S-Corp vs. LLC vs. sole proprietorship tax efficiency based on current
  and projected income levels
- [ ] S-Corp reasonable compensation analysis: is officer salary high enough to
  withstand IRS scrutiny? Too high and you lose FICA savings.
- [ ] Review qualified business income (QBI) deduction eligibility under Section 199A:
  are you within W-2 wage and UBIA limitations?
- [ ] Evaluate whether entity conversion would be beneficial for the coming year

### Estate Planning Review
- [ ] Update beneficiary designations on retirement accounts, life insurance, and
  transfer-on-death accounts after any life changes (marriage, divorce, birth, death)
- [ ] Review annual gifting strategy: $19,000 per donee, $38,000 with gift-splitting
- [ ] Consider Grantor Retained Annuity Trust (GRAT), Irrevocable Life Insurance
  Trust (ILIT), or other advanced strategies if estate exceeds federal exemption
  ($13,990,000 per person in 2025)
- [ ] Do not rely on pre-OBBBA automatic-sunset assumptions. Under current law, the
  basic exclusion amount rises to $15,000,000 for 2026. Review the current transfer-tax
  landscape annually and still consider lifetime gifting when the estate is likely to
  exceed projected future exemption levels.
- [ ] Review trust provisions and consider decanting or modification if terms no
  longer serve the family's needs

### Insurance and Benefits Review
- [ ] Verify adequate liability coverage (umbrella policy) as net worth grows
- [ ] Review life insurance needs — has income or family situation changed?
- [ ] Evaluate long-term care insurance, especially for ages 55-65
- [ ] Confirm disability insurance coverage is adequate for current income level

## SPECIAL SITUATION TRIGGERS

### If You Are Selling a Business This Year
- [ ] Structure as asset sale vs. stock sale — dramatically different tax consequences
- [ ] Installment sale (Section 453) to spread gain recognition over multiple years
- [ ] Qualified Small Business Stock (Section 1202): if eligible, up to 100% exclusion
  on gain from C-Corp stock held 5+ years
- [ ] Opportunity Zone reinvestment of eligible capital gains (180-day window)
- [ ] Charitable Remainder Trust to defer/reduce capital gains
- [ ] Negotiate allocation of purchase price (Section 1060) — buyer wants equipment
  and goodwill; seller wants capital gain treatment

### If You Are Retiring This Year
- [ ] Plan the transition from earned income to retirement income (different tax treatment)
- [ ] Evaluate Roth conversion opportunity in low-income years between retirement
  and Social Security/RMD start
- [ ] Determine optimal Social Security claiming strategy (delay to 70 for maximum
  benefit if life expectancy supports it)
- [ ] Plan health insurance coverage for gap between retirement and Medicare (age 65)
- [ ] Consider ACA marketplace coverage — manage AGI to maximize premium subsidies

### If You Are Getting Married or Divorced This Year
- [ ] Filing status is determined by December 31 — if married on December 31, you file
  as married for the entire year (MFJ or MFS)
- [ ] Divorce decree should address tax allocation: who claims children, how to split
  estimated tax payments, alimony treatment (pre-2019 agreements: deductible/included;
  post-2018: no deduction, no inclusion)
- [ ] Update W-4s immediately after marriage/divorce
- [ ] Review beneficiary designations on ALL accounts

### If You Are Moving to a New State
- [ ] Determine domicile change date — most states tax based on partial-year residency
- [ ] Review source income rules: some states tax income sourced to that state regardless
  of residency (e.g., California taxes wages earned while physically in CA)
- [ ] State-specific considerations: if moving from a high-tax state (CA, NY, NJ) to
  a no-tax state (TX, FL, WA, NV), establish clear domicile evidence (driver's license,
  voter registration, mail forwarding, professional license transfer)
- [ ] Some states have "clawback" rules for deferred gains (e.g., California Form 3840
  for 1031 exchanges, NY for installment sales)

## DOCUMENT RETENTION SCHEDULE

| Document | Retention Period |
|----------|-----------------|
| Tax returns | Permanently (at minimum 7 years) |
| W-2s | Permanently (needed for Social Security claims) |
| 1099s | 7 years |
| Investment purchase records (basis) | As long as you own the asset + 7 years after sale |
| Property records (purchase, improvements) | As long as you own the property + 7 years |
| Charitable donation receipts | 7 years |
| Business expense receipts | 7 years |
| Payroll records (employer) | 7 years |
| 1031 exchange documentation | Permanently (basis carries through indefinitely) |
| IRA contribution records (Form 8606) | Permanently (tracks non-deductible basis) |
| Estate/gift tax returns (Form 706/709) | Permanently |
