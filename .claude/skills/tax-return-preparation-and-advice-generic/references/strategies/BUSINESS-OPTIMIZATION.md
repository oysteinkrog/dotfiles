# Business Deduction Optimization Strategies

## Overview

Self-employed individuals and small business owners have access to a wide range of deductions that can dramatically reduce taxable income. The key is prioritizing high-impact deductions first and maintaining documentation to survive audit scrutiny.

All figures are for the 2025 tax year.

---

## Deduction Priority Order

For maximum tax savings, pursue deductions in this order:

1. **Retirement plan contributions** (SEP-IRA, Solo 401(k)) -- highest dollar impact
2. **Self-employed health insurance deduction** -- above-the-line, dollar-for-dollar
3. **Home office deduction** -- enables other deductions (mileage from home, etc.)
4. **Business expenses** (equipment, software, supplies, etc.)
5. **Vehicle deduction** -- mileage or actual expenses
6. **Traditional IRA** -- additional retirement savings if eligible
7. **HSA contributions** -- if HDHP-enrolled

---

## Retirement Plans for the Self-Employed

### SEP-IRA

- Contribution limit: 20% of net self-employment income (NOT 25%; the 25% figure applies to W-2 employees)
- Maximum: $70,000 (2025)
- Can be established and funded up to the tax filing deadline including extensions
- Simplest plan to administer
- See RETIREMENT-PLANS.md for full details

### Solo 401(k)

- Employee deferral: $23,500 (+$7,500 catch-up age 50+; +$11,250 super catch-up ages 60-63)
- Employer contribution: 20% of net SE income (Schedule C) or 25% of W-2 (S-Corp)
- Maximum: $70,000 + catch-up (2025)
- Adoption timing depends on entity type and contribution type; verify current IRS rules
- Roth option available
- See RETIREMENT-PLANS.md for full details

### Priority

If income supports it, the Solo 401(k) is often superior to the SEP-IRA due to the employee
deferral component. But do not use a simplistic December 31 cutoff. Verify the current IRS timing
rules for the taxpayer's entity type and contribution design before defaulting to a SEP-IRA.

## Self-Employed Health Insurance Deduction (Section 162(l))

### What It Is

Self-employed individuals can deduct 100% of health insurance premiums paid for themselves, their spouse, and dependents as an above-the-line deduction (reduces AGI).

### Requirements

- Must be self-employed with net profit from the business
- Cannot be eligible to participate in a subsidized health plan through an employer (own or spouse's)
- Deduction cannot exceed net self-employment income from the business under which the plan is established
- Applies to medical, dental, and qualified long-term care insurance

### S-Corp Shareholder Considerations

- For >2% S-Corp shareholders, health insurance premiums must be:
  1. Paid by the S-Corp or reimbursed to the shareholder
  2. Included on the shareholder's W-2 (Box 1, but NOT subject to FICA -- not in Boxes 3 or 5)
  3. Then deducted on Form 1040, Schedule 1, line 17

### What It Does NOT Cover

- This deduction does NOT reduce self-employment tax (it's an income tax deduction, not a SE tax deduction)
- Cannot be combined with Premium Tax Credit for the same months of coverage

## Home Office Deduction

### Dual Forms 8829

If both spouses have separate businesses operating from different dedicated spaces in the home, each can file their own Form 8829 on their respective Schedule C. See HOME-OFFICE.md for complete details.

### Why Home Office Matters Beyond the Deduction Itself

Establishing a qualified home office makes the home the "principal place of business," which:
- Allows **all business mileage** to be measured from home (not from a first commute destination)
- Makes the home the starting point for business travel (increasing deductible miles)
- Enables the home office deduction itself

## Business Expense Categories

### Software and Technology

- Business software subscriptions (accounting, project management, CRM, design tools)
- Cloud computing costs (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud)
- Domain names and hosting
- SaaS subscriptions used for business
- Computer equipment (Section 179 or depreciation)
- Phone: business-use percentage of cell phone bill (or second line)
- Internet: business-use percentage (allocated via home office or separately documented)

### Equipment and Supplies

- **Section 179 Expensing:** Immediate deduction up to $2,500,000 (2025) for qualifying property
  - Applies to tangible personal property (computers, furniture, equipment)
  - Phase-out begins at $4,000,000 in total property placed in service
  - Property must be used more than 50% for business
  - Cannot create a loss (limited to taxable business income)
- **Bonus Depreciation:** 100% for qualifying property placed in service after January 19, 2025 (OBBBA)
  - CAN create a loss (unlike Section 179)
  - Applies to new and used property (if new to the taxpayer)
- Office supplies, printer ink, paper, postage
- Business cards, marketing materials

### Vehicle Expenses

Two methods available (choose one per vehicle per year):

#### Standard Mileage Rate

- **2025 rate: $0.70 per mile**
- Track all business miles with a log (date, destination, purpose, miles)
- Add parking and tolls on top of mileage rate
- Cannot use mileage rate if: vehicle is depreciated using MACRS, claimed Section 179, used for hire, or fleet of 5+ vehicles

#### Actual Expense Method

- Track all vehicle expenses: gas, insurance, repairs, depreciation, registration, lease payments
- Multiply by business-use percentage
- Requires tracking total miles AND business miles
- May be better for expensive or high-maintenance vehicles

#### Which to Choose

| Scenario | Better Method |
|---|---|
| High miles, low-cost vehicle | Mileage rate |
| Low miles, expensive vehicle | Actual expenses |
| New vehicle (depreciation available) | Run both calculations |
| Lease vehicle | Actual expenses (usually) |

#### Heavy Vehicle Section 179 Strategy

- Vehicles over 6,000 lbs GVWR qualify for full Section 179 expensing (no luxury auto limits)
- Examples: Full-size SUVs (Suburban, Tahoe, Expedition, X5, GLE, etc.), trucks, vans
- Must be used more than 50% for business
- First-year deduction can be $50,000-$80,000+
- Common audit target -- document business use meticulously

### Meals and Entertainment

- **Business meals: 50% deductible** (2025; the temporary 100% restaurant meal deduction expired after 2022)
- Must be directly related to business or associated with business discussion
- Must document: date, amount, business purpose, attendees, business relationship
- Entertainment: NOT deductible (TCJA eliminated entertainment deductions)
- Team meals / office snacks provided for the convenience of the employer: 50%
- Client meals where business is discussed: 50%

### Travel

- Airfare, hotel, car rental for business travel
- 100% deductible if the primary purpose of the trip is business
- Meals while traveling: 50%
- Laundry/dry cleaning while traveling: 100%
- Tips: deductible (at same rate as underlying expense)
- Mixed business/personal trips: allocate based on business vs. personal days
  - Travel days count as business days
  - Weekend days between two business days count as business days
  - International travel has stricter allocation rules if trip is over 7 days and >25% personal

### Education and Professional Development

- Deductible if it maintains or improves skills in current business/profession
- NOT deductible if it qualifies you for a new trade or business
- Includes: courses, conferences, seminars, books, professional journals, certifications
- For self-employed: deducted on Schedule C
- Can include travel to conferences (subject to travel deduction rules)

### Insurance

- General liability insurance
- Professional liability / Errors & Omissions (E&O)
- Business property insurance
- Cyber liability insurance
- Workers' compensation (if applicable)
- Business interruption insurance
- NOT life insurance premiums (generally not deductible for business)

### Advertising and Marketing

- Website design and development
- Online advertising (Google Ads, social media ads)
- Print advertising
- Business cards and promotional materials
- Sponsorships (if directly related to business promotion)
- SEO services
- Email marketing platforms

### Legal and Professional Fees

- Accounting and tax preparation fees (for business returns)
- Legal fees for business matters
- Consulting fees
- Bookkeeping services
- Payroll service fees

### Office and Administrative

- Rent (if separate office space)
- Office supplies
- Postage and shipping
- Business phone line
- Coworking space membership
- Bank fees on business accounts
- Credit card processing fees
- Software subscriptions

## S-Corp Payroll Setup

For S-Corp shareholder-employees, running proper payroll is mandatory.

### Payroll Provider Options

| Provider | Monthly Cost | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Gusto | $40-$80/month | Popular, user-friendly, handles all filings |
| ADP Run | $59-$99+/month | Established, extensive features |
| Paychex | Varies | Good for multi-state |
| QuickBooks Payroll | $45-$125/month | Integrates with QuickBooks accounting |
| Wave Payroll | $40+/month | Budget option |
| Accountant-managed | Varies | CPA handles payroll as part of engagement |

### Payroll Deposit Schedules

- **Monthly depositor:** If total tax liability in the lookback period was $50,000 or less, deposits are due by the 15th of the following month
- **Semi-weekly depositor:** If total tax liability exceeded $50,000, deposits are due within 3 business days of payroll (Wednesday for Wed/Thu/Fri payrolls; Friday for Sat/Sun/Mon/Tue payrolls)
- **New employers:** Generally monthly depositors for the first year

### Quarterly Filings

- **Form 941:** Employer's Quarterly Federal Tax Return (income tax withheld + FICA)
  - Due: April 30, July 31, October 31, January 31
- **State equivalents:** Vary by state (quarterly unemployment, withholding)
- **Form 940:** Annual Federal Unemployment Tax (FUTA) -- due January 31

### Annual Filings

- **W-2 / W-3:** Due to employees by January 31; filed with SSA by January 31
- **Form 940:** FUTA annual return -- due January 31
- **State W-2 copies and reconciliations**

### Common Payroll Mistakes

1. **Not running payroll at all** -- distributing profits without W-2 salary is a red flag
2. **Running payroll too infrequently** -- salary should be paid on a regular schedule (monthly, semi-monthly, or biweekly)
3. **Late payroll tax deposits** -- penalties accumulate quickly (2%-15% depending on lateness)
4. **Misclassifying employees as contractors** -- significant penalties
5. **Not withholding state taxes** -- even if the business is in one state, remote employees may trigger withholding in their state

## Consulting / Expert Network Income

### Optimization Through Entity

Taxpayers who earn consulting income (via expert networks or direct engagements) should consider:

1. **Schedule C:** Default for sole proprietors. Subject to full SE tax.
2. **S-Corp:** If total consulting income exceeds $80K-$100K, S-Corp can reduce SE tax significantly.
3. **Entity should issue invoices** to the expert network (some networks pay only to individuals; others will pay to an LLC/S-Corp).
4. **Retirement plan contributions** through the entity -- Solo 401(k) or SEP-IRA against this income.

### Income Reporting

- Expert network payments reported on Form 1099-NEC (if $600+)
- If paid to the individual: reported on Schedule C
- If paid to an entity: reported on the entity's return (1120-S, 1065, or Schedule C for SMLLC)

## QBI Deduction (Section 199A)

### Overview

Qualified Business Income (QBI) deduction allows eligible taxpayers to deduct up to 20% of qualified business income from pass-through entities and sole proprietorships.

### Income Limits (2025)

| Filing Status | Full Deduction | Phase-Out Range | No Deduction (SSTB) |
|---|---|---|---|
| Single | Taxable income <= $197,300 | $197,300 - $247,300 | > $247,300 |
| MFJ | Taxable income <= $394,600 | $394,600 - $494,600 | > $494,600 |

### Specified Service Trades or Businesses (SSTBs)

SSTBs (health, law, accounting, actuarial, performing arts, consulting, athletics, financial services, investing, brokerage) are fully eligible below the threshold but phased out in the phase-out range and completely ineligible above it.

### Non-SSTB Businesses Above the Threshold

For non-SSTBs above the phase-out range, the deduction is limited to the GREATER of:
- 50% of W-2 wages paid by the business, OR
- 25% of W-2 wages + 2.5% of the unadjusted basis immediately after acquisition (UBIA) of qualified property

### Planning Implications

- S-Corp W-2 wages paid to shareholder-employee count toward the W-2 wage limit
- Property-intensive businesses (real estate, manufacturing) benefit from the UBIA component
- Taxpayers near the phase-out should consider strategies to reduce taxable income below the threshold (retirement contributions, charitable giving, timing of income)

## Common Mistakes in Business Deduction Optimization

1. **Not keeping receipts** -- the IRS can disallow any deduction without substantiation
2. **Mixing personal and business expenses** -- use a dedicated business bank account and credit card
3. **Missing retirement plan deadlines** -- IRA deadlines are fixed, but Solo 401(k) timing must
   be analyzed by entity type and contribution type rather than assumed from a blanket Dec 31 rule
4. **Using 25% instead of 20%** for self-employed SEP-IRA calculation
5. **Not claiming the home office** -- fear of audit causes taxpayers to leave legitimate money on the table
6. **Forgetting to track mileage** -- reconstructed logs are weak in audit
7. **Deducting personal meals** as business meals without documenting business purpose and attendees
8. **Not considering Section 179** for major equipment purchases
9. **Ignoring QBI deduction planning** -- strategies to stay below SSTB threshold
10. **Not reviewing entity structure annually** -- income changes may warrant S-Corp election or dissolution
