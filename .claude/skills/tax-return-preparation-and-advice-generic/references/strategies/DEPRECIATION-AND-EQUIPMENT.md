# Depreciation and Equipment -- Comprehensive Guide

## Overview

Depreciation is the mechanism for recovering the cost of business or investment property over its useful life. The Modified Accelerated Cost Recovery System (MACRS) is the primary depreciation method for most tangible property placed in service after 1986. Combined with section 179 immediate expensing and bonus depreciation under section 168(k), depreciation planning is one of the most powerful tools for controlling the timing of tax deductions.

All figures and rules reflect the 2025 tax year.

---

## MACRS Overview

### General Depreciation System (GDS) -- Default

GDS is the default system used by most taxpayers. It provides faster cost recovery through accelerated methods and shorter recovery periods.

### Alternative Depreciation System (ADS)

ADS uses straight-line depreciation over longer recovery periods. ADS is **required** for:
- Property used predominantly outside the United States
- Tax-exempt use property
- Tax-exempt bond-financed property
- Listed property used 50% or less for business
- Property for which the taxpayer elects ADS
- QIP for purposes of computing the section 163(j) ATI add-back (if elected)

ADS may be **elected** for any property. Once elected, the election is irrevocable for that property.

## Convention Rules

### Half-Year Convention (Default)

Property is treated as placed in service at the **midpoint** of the tax year, regardless of actual date. First-year depreciation is half a full year's amount.

### Mid-Quarter Convention

Triggered when **more than 40%** of the aggregate basis of all MACRS personal property placed in service during the year is placed in service in the **fourth quarter** (October-December for calendar-year taxpayers).

When triggered, ALL personal property placed in service during the year uses mid-quarter convention:
- Q1: treated as placed in service at midpoint of Q1 (10.5 months of depreciation)
- Q2: treated as placed in service at midpoint of Q2 (7.5 months)
- Q3: treated as placed in service at midpoint of Q3 (4.5 months)
- Q4: treated as placed in service at midpoint of Q4 (1.5 months)

**Trap:** If you place a large purchase in Q4, it can retroactively reduce depreciation on ALL property placed in service throughout the year.

### Mid-Month Convention

Used for real property (27.5-year and 39-year). Property is treated as placed in service at the midpoint of the month it is actually placed in service.

## Section 179 Immediate Expensing

### 2025 Limits

| Parameter | 2025 Amount |
|---|---|
| Maximum deduction | $2,500,000 |
| Phase-out threshold | $4,000,000 |
| Phase-out: dollar-for-dollar reduction | After $4,000,000 in total property |
| Complete phase-out | $6,500,000 |

### Eligible Property

- **Tangible personal property:** Machinery, equipment, furniture, fixtures
- **Off-the-shelf computer software:** Not custom-developed
- **Qualified Improvement Property (QIP):** Interior improvements to nonresidential buildings
- **Certain improvements to nonresidential real property:** Roofs, HVAC, fire protection, alarm systems, security systems (placed in service after the building was placed in service)
- **Single-purpose agricultural or horticultural structures**

### NOT Eligible for Section 179

- Buildings (the structure itself)
- Land
- Inventory / property held for sale
- Property used predominantly outside the United States
- Property acquired from related parties (section 179(d)(2))
- Air conditioning/heating units (but HVAC as QIP may qualify)
- Property used for lodging (certain exceptions for hotels)

### Income Limitation

**Section 179 deduction cannot exceed taxable income from the active conduct of any trade or business.** This includes:
- W-2 wages
- Net self-employment income
- S corporation/partnership pass-through income from active businesses

It does NOT include:
- Investment income (dividends, interest, capital gains)
- Rental income (unless taxpayer is a real estate professional)
- Passive activity income

**Excess carries forward indefinitely** to future years (subject to future-year income and section 179 limits).

### Worked Example: Section 179

```
Small business owner buys $200,000 of equipment in 2025:
  Taxable business income: $150,000

  Section 179 election: $150,000 (limited to taxable income)
  Carryforward: $50,000 (to 2026)

  Tax savings at 24% bracket: $150,000 x 24% = $36,000 in Year 1
```

## Bonus Depreciation (IRC Section 168(k))

### OBBBA Restoration (2025)

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) restored 100% bonus depreciation for qualified property acquired AND placed in service after January 19, 2025.

| Acquisition & Placement Date | Bonus Depreciation Rate |
|---|---|
| Before Jan 1, 2023 | 100% (original TCJA) |
| Jan 1, 2023 - Dec 31, 2023 | 80% (TCJA phasedown) |
| Jan 1, 2024 - Dec 31, 2024 | 60% (TCJA phasedown) |
| Jan 1, 2025 - Jan 19, 2025 | 40% (TCJA phasedown) |
| After Jan 19, 2025 | 100% (OBBBA restoration) |

### Key Differences: Bonus Depreciation vs. Section 179

| Feature | Section 179 | Bonus Depreciation |
|---|---|---|
| Maximum deduction | $2,500,000 (2025) | **Unlimited** |
| Investment limit | Phase-out begins at $4,000,000 | **None** |
| Income limitation | Cannot create/increase a loss | **CAN create a loss** |
| Eligible property | Listed above | New AND used property, recovery period 20 years or less |
| Election | Taxpayer elects IN | Default -- taxpayer must elect OUT |
| Applies to used property | Yes | Yes (since TCJA, maintained by OBBBA) |

### Property Eligible for Bonus Depreciation

- Property with MACRS recovery period of **20 years or less**
- Computer software (off-the-shelf or amortizable under section 167)
- Water utility property
- Qualified film, television, and live theatrical productions
- Qualified Improvement Property (15-year)

### Property NOT Eligible for Bonus Depreciation

- Property with recovery period >20 years (27.5-year, 39-year)
- Property required to use ADS
- Property used predominantly outside the US
- Certain property of regulated utilities
- Floor plan financing interest property

### Election Out

If bonus depreciation is not desired (e.g., to preserve NOLs, manage AMT, or spread deductions across years), the taxpayer can elect out on a **class-by-class basis** on the return. The election is made on Form 4562 and is irrevocable.

## Heavy Vehicle Strategies

### The GVWR >6,000 lbs Exemption

Vehicles with a Gross Vehicle Weight Rating (GVWR) exceeding 6,000 lbs are exempt from the "luxury automobile" depreciation limits under section 280F. However, SUVs are subject to a special section 179 cap.

### GVWR >6,000 lbs but <=14,000 lbs (SUVs/Trucks)

**Section 179 cap for SUVs: $31,300 (2025)**

The remaining basis qualifies for bonus depreciation.

**Worked Example:**
```
Purchase: 2025 Ford Expedition, $75,000
GVWR: 7,300 lbs (qualifies as heavy vehicle)
Business use: 90%

Business basis: $75,000 x 90% = $67,500

Section 179: $31,300
Remaining basis: $67,500 - $31,300 = $36,200
Bonus depreciation (100%): $36,200

Total first-year deduction: $31,300 + $36,200 = $67,500
Tax savings at 37%: $24,975
```

### GVWR >14,000 lbs (Heavy Trucks/Vans)

No SUV cap applies. Full section 179 limit ($2,500,000) is available.

**Examples:** Ford F-450/550, large commercial vans, box trucks, dump trucks.

### Qualifying Heavy Vehicles (>6,000 lbs GVWR)

| Vehicle | GVWR (approx.) | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Ford F-250/F-350 | 8,600-14,000 lbs | Pickup trucks |
| Chevrolet Silverado 2500/3500 | 8,600-14,000 lbs | Pickup trucks |
| Chevrolet Tahoe | 7,200 lbs | Full-size SUV |
| Chevrolet Suburban | 7,500 lbs | Full-size SUV |
| GMC Yukon XL | 7,500 lbs | Full-size SUV |
| Ford Expedition | 7,300 lbs | Full-size SUV |
| Ford Expedition MAX | 7,500 lbs | Extended SUV |
| Toyota Sequoia | 7,100 lbs | Full-size SUV |
| Jeep Grand Wagoneer | 7,200 lbs | Full-size SUV |
| Mercedes GLS | 6,600 lbs | Luxury SUV |
| BMW X7 | 6,300 lbs | Luxury SUV (borderline) |
| Range Rover | 6,200 lbs | Luxury SUV (borderline) |
| Tesla Model X | 5,390 lbs | Does NOT qualify (under 6,000) |
| Rivian R1S | 7,150 lbs | Electric SUV -- qualifies |
| GMC Hummer EV | 9,063 lbs | Electric truck -- qualifies |

**WARNING:** Always verify the GVWR from the manufacturer's door sticker, not the curb weight. GVWR includes the vehicle, passengers, and cargo capacity.

### Luxury Auto Limits (GVWR 6,000 lbs or Less) -- 2025

For passenger automobiles NOT exceeding 6,000 lbs GVWR:

| Year | With Bonus Depreciation | Without Bonus Depreciation |
|---|---|---|
| Year 1 | $20,200 | $12,400 |
| Year 2 | $19,800 | $19,800 |
| Year 3 | $11,900 | $11,900 |
| Year 4+ | $7,160 | $7,160 |

**Total recovery over ~8-9 years** for a luxury auto (vs. potentially 1 year for a heavy vehicle).

### Business Use Requirement

- **>50% business use** required for MACRS depreciation, section 179, and bonus depreciation
- Must maintain a **contemporaneous mileage log** documenting: date, destination, business purpose, miles driven
- If business use drops to 50% or below in any year, section 179 and bonus depreciation must be **recaptured** (added back to income)
- Commuting miles are NEVER business miles

## Qualified Improvement Property (QIP)

### Definition

Improvements to the **interior** portion of a nonresidential building placed in service **after** the building was first placed in service by any taxpayer.

### Specifically EXCLUDED from QIP

- Enlargement of the building
- Elevators and escalators
- Internal structural framework of the building

### Recovery Period and Method

- **MACRS:** 15-year, 150% declining balance
- **Bonus depreciation:** 100% eligible (OBBBA restored, retroactive to post-Jan 19, 2025 property)

### Common QIP Examples

- Tenant build-outs
- New flooring, ceilings, lighting
- Interior walls (non-structural)
- Restroom renovations
- Kitchen renovations in restaurants
- New electrical/plumbing (interior only)
- Interior painting and finishing

### The CARES Act Fix (Historical Context)

The TCJA originally assigned QIP a 39-year life due to a drafting error, making it ineligible for bonus depreciation. The CARES Act of 2020 retroactively corrected QIP to 15-year property, restoring bonus depreciation eligibility back to 2018.

## Cost Segregation

### What It Is

A cost segregation study reclassifies components of a building from 27.5-year (residential) or 39-year (commercial) property to shorter-lived asset classes (5-year, 7-year, 15-year), allowing accelerated depreciation and bonus depreciation.

### How It Works

A qualified engineer inspects the building and identifies components that qualify as personal property or land improvements rather than structural components:

| Component | Default Classification | Reclassified To |
|---|---|---|
| Decorative lighting | 39-year (building) | 5-year (personal property) |
| Specialty flooring | 39-year | 5-year |
| Specialized electrical | 39-year | 5-year or 7-year |
| Landscaping | 39-year | 15-year (land improvement) |
| Paving/sidewalks | 39-year | 15-year |
| Fencing | 39-year | 15-year |
| Carpeting | 39-year | 5-year |
| Kitchen cabinetry (restaurant) | 39-year | 5 or 7-year |
| Signage | 39-year | 7-year |

### Typical Results

For a commercial building:
- **20-40%** of total building cost can often be reclassified to shorter-lived categories
- Combined with 100% bonus depreciation, this generates massive first-year deductions

### ROI on Cost Segregation Studies

| Building Value | Study Cost | Typical Tax Benefit | ROI |
|---|---|---|---|
| $500,000 | $5,000-8,000 | $30,000-60,000 | 4-12x |
| $1,000,000 | $7,000-12,000 | $60,000-120,000 | 6-17x |
| $5,000,000 | $15,000-25,000 | $300,000-600,000 | 12-40x |
| $10,000,000+ | $20,000-40,000 | $600,000-1,200,000 | 15-60x |

### Lookback Studies

For buildings placed in service in prior years where no cost segregation was done, a **lookback study** can be performed and the catch-up depreciation claimed via Form 3115 (change in accounting method) -- no amended returns needed. This creates a one-time "catch-up" deduction in the current year.

## Partial Asset Disposition (Reg. Section 1.168(i)-8)

### The Hidden Deduction

When you **replace** a component of a building (roof, HVAC, plumbing, windows), you can elect to "dispose" of the old component and claim the remaining undepreciated basis as a loss.

### Mechanics

1. Determine the original cost allocated to the replaced component
2. Calculate depreciation taken on that component
3. Claim the remaining undepreciated basis as a loss (ordinary loss for section 1245 property, section 1231 loss for real property)
4. Capitalize the new replacement and begin depreciating it

### Worked Example

```
Building purchased in 2015 for $1,000,000 (39-year MACRS)
Original roof allocation: $80,000
Depreciation taken on roof (2015-2025): ~$20,512
Remaining basis: $80,000 - $20,512 = $59,488

2025: Replace roof for $120,000

Partial disposition election:
  Loss on old roof: $59,488 (deductible in 2025)
  New roof: $120,000 capitalized as QIP (15-year, 100% bonus)

Total 2025 deductions: $59,488 + $120,000 = $179,488
Without partial disposition: only $120,000 (new roof depreciation)
Extra deduction: $59,488
Tax savings at 37%: $22,011
```

### Filing Requirements

- File **Form 3115** (Application for Change in Accounting Method) for the year of disposition
- Category: DCN 206 (for partial dispositions)
- Automatic consent procedure -- no IRS approval needed
- Can be done retroactively for prior-year replacements via section 481(a) adjustment

## Safe Harbor Rules

### De Minimis Safe Harbor (Reg. Section 1.263(a)-1(f))

Allows immediate expensing of items below a threshold without capitalizing:

| Taxpayer Type | Threshold |
|---|---|
| With applicable financial statement (AFS) | $5,000 per invoice/item |
| Without AFS | $2,500 per invoice/item |

**Requirements:**
- Written accounting policy in place at beginning of year
- Applied consistently to all items
- Annual election statement attached to return (statement under Reg. section 1.263(a)-1(f)(5))

**Planning tip:** Purchase items separately when possible to keep individual costs below the threshold. A $4,800 computer qualifies; a $6,000 computer does not (for non-AFS taxpayers at $2,500 threshold).

### Routine Maintenance Safe Harbor (Reg. Section 1.263(a)-3(i))

Activities that keep property in ordinarily efficient operating condition can be expensed as repairs if:
- The taxpayer reasonably expects to perform the activity **more than once** during the property's class life
- The activity is recurring in nature

**Applies to:** Oil changes, filter replacements, HVAC maintenance, painting, minor repairs, cleaning.

**Does NOT apply to:** Betterments, restorations, or adaptations (see BRA test below).

---

## The BRA Test -- Capitalize or Expense?

### Betterment (Reg. Section 1.263(a)-3(j))

Must capitalize if the expenditure:
- Fixes a material condition or defect that existed **before** the taxpayer acquired the property
- Results in a material increase in capacity, productivity, efficiency, strength, or quality
- Is a material addition to the unit of property

### Restoration (Reg. Section 1.263(a)-3(k))

Must capitalize if the expenditure:
- Returns property to service after a casualty loss
- Replaces a major component or substantial structural part
- Returns property to "like new" condition after the end of its class life
- Results in previously deducted costs being restored (e.g., partial disposition)

### Adaptation (Reg. Section 1.263(a)-3(l))

Must capitalize if the expenditure adapts property to a new or different use (e.g., converting a warehouse to retail space).

### Practical Application

| Expenditure | BRA Result | Treatment |
|---|---|---|
| Patching a roof leak | Not BRA | Expense as repair |
| Replacing entire roof | Restoration | Capitalize (but claim partial disposition on old roof) |
| Adding new wing to building | Betterment | Capitalize |
| Repainting interior | Not BRA | Expense as repair |
| Upgrading HVAC to higher-capacity | Betterment | Capitalize |
| Replacing same-capacity HVAC | Restoration | Capitalize (partial disposition available) |
| Converting office to lab | Adaptation | Capitalize |
| Replacing carpet (same quality) | Not BRA | Expense as repair (if routine maintenance) |

## Listed Property Rules (IRC Section 280F)

### Definition

Property susceptible to personal use, including:
- Passenger automobiles
- Other transportation vehicles
- Entertainment/recreation/amusement property
- Computers (if not used exclusively at a regular business establishment -- though this rule has diminished significance since most computers are now primarily business tools)
- Cell phones (removed from listed property in 2010)

### >50% Business Use

If business use exceeds 50%:
- Full MACRS depreciation (200% DB)
- Section 179 expensing allowed
- Bonus depreciation allowed

### 50% or Less Business Use

If business use is 50% or less:
- Must use ADS (straight-line over longer recovery period)
- No section 179
- No bonus depreciation
- If business use drops below 50% after initial year, **recapture** of excess depreciation is required (section 280F(b)(2))

### Recapture Example

```
Year 1: Buy $60,000 vehicle (GVWR >6,000 lbs), 80% business use
        Section 179: $31,300 + Bonus on remainder
        Total deduction: ~$48,000

Year 2: Business use drops to 40%
        Must recapture excess depreciation over ADS straight-line
        Recapture = actual depreciation taken - ADS depreciation that would have been allowed
        This recapture is ordinary income reported on Form 4797
```

## Placed-in-Service Rules

### When Property Is "Placed in Service"

Property is placed in service when it is **ready and available for a specifically assigned function**, whether in a trade or business, production of income, or personal activity (Reg. section 1.167(a)-11(e)(1)(i)).

**Key distinctions:**
- Equipment purchased but still in the shipping box on December 31: **NOT** placed in service
- Equipment delivered, installed, and tested on December 31: **YES**, placed in service
- Building under construction: placed in service when **certificate of occupancy** is issued (or actually occupied, if earlier)
- Vehicle: placed in service when available for use on public roads

**Year-end planning:** To claim depreciation for the current year, ensure property is placed in service (not just purchased) before December 31.

## Recapture on Disposition

### Section 1245 -- Personal Property

When depreciable personal property is sold at a gain, the gain is treated as **ordinary income** to the extent of depreciation previously taken. Only the gain in excess of total depreciation is capital gain.

**Example:**
```
Equipment basis: $100,000
Depreciation taken: $100,000 (fully depreciated)
Sale price: $25,000

Section 1245 recapture: $25,000 (all ordinary income)
```

### Section 1250 -- Real Property

When real property is sold at a gain, "unrecaptured section 1250 gain" (the depreciation taken on the building) is taxed at a maximum rate of **25%** (not the 15%/20% LTCG rate).

**Example:**
```
Building basis: $500,000
Depreciation taken: $150,000
Adjusted basis: $350,000
Sale price: $600,000

Total gain: $250,000
  Unrecaptured 1250 gain (taxed at 25%): $150,000
  Remaining gain (taxed at 15/20% LTCG): $100,000
```

Most states conform to federal MACRS, but several do **not** allow bonus depreciation or have modified section 179 limits:

| State | Bonus Depreciation | Section 179 | Notes |
|---|---|---|---|
| California | NOT allowed | Conforms with $25,000 limit | Major divergence -- maintain separate schedules |
| New York | NOT allowed (for C corps, limited for others) | Conforms | Complex partial conformity |
| New Jersey | NOT allowed | Limited conformity | Significant state adjustments |
| Pennsylvania | NOT allowed | Limited conformity | GDS without bonus |
| Hawaii | NOT allowed | Conforms | ADS required for some property |
| Maryland | Partial conformity | Conforms | Decoupled from 100% bonus |
| Minnesota | NOT allowed | Conforms | Section 179 is added back then amortized |
| Connecticut | NOT allowed | Conforms | 5-year add-back then recovery |
| Illinois | Conforms | Conforms | Full federal conformity |

**Practical requirement:** For states that do not allow bonus depreciation, maintain a **separate depreciation schedule** tracking state-level depreciation alongside federal. Tax software typically handles this automatically.

## Comprehensive Worked Example: $50,000 Equipment Purchase

### Scenario

A sole proprietor (Schedule C) purchases a $50,000 piece of manufacturing equipment on July 1, 2025. Business income before depreciation: $200,000. Marginal federal tax rate: 32%. The equipment has a 7-year MACRS recovery period.

### Option A: Section 179 Expensing

```
Year 1 deduction: $50,000 (full expensing)
Tax savings: $50,000 x 32% = $16,000 (all in Year 1)
NPV of tax savings (at 5% discount): $16,000
```

### Option B: 100% Bonus Depreciation

```
Year 1 deduction: $50,000 (100% bonus)
Tax savings: $50,000 x 32% = $16,000 (all in Year 1)
NPV of tax savings (at 5% discount): $16,000
```

**Note:** For this amount, section 179 and bonus depreciation produce identical results. The difference matters when:
- You need to create a loss (bonus can, section 179 cannot)
- You are near the section 179 phase-out threshold
- State conformity differs (some states allow section 179 but not bonus)

### Option C: Regular MACRS (200% DB, 7-Year, Half-Year Convention)

```
Year 1: $50,000 x 14.29% = $7,145      Tax savings: $2,286
Year 2: $50,000 x 24.49% = $12,245     Tax savings: $3,918
Year 3: $50,000 x 17.49% = $8,745      Tax savings: $2,798
Year 4: $50,000 x 12.49% = $6,245      Tax savings: $1,998
Year 5: $50,000 x 8.93%  = $4,465      Tax savings: $1,429
Year 6: $50,000 x 8.92%  = $4,460      Tax savings: $1,427
Year 7: $50,000 x 8.93%  = $4,465      Tax savings: $1,429
Year 8: $50,000 x 4.46%  = $2,230      Tax savings: $714
Total:                      $50,000     Tax savings: $16,000

NPV of tax savings (at 5% discount): ~$13,200
```

**Conclusion:** Accelerated methods (section 179 or bonus) provide $16,000 in Year 1 vs. $13,200 NPV over 8 years -- a $2,800 time-value-of-money advantage.

### Option D: Combination (When Strategically Useful)

If section 179 is needed to avoid creating a loss, but you also want maximum deduction:
```
Section 179: $50,000 (up to taxable income limit)
Result: same as Options A/B for this example

But if taxable income is only $30,000:
  Section 179: $30,000 (limited to income)
  Remaining basis: $20,000
  Bonus depreciation on remainder: $20,000 (CAN create a loss)
  Total deduction: $50,000
  Net loss: ($20,000) -- carries forward as NOL
```

## Mid-Quarter Convention Trap -- Detailed Example

```
Taxpayer places the following in service during 2025:
  March: Computer equipment          $10,000 (Q1)
  May: Office furniture              $15,000 (Q2)
  November: Manufacturing equipment  $75,000 (Q4)

Total personal property: $100,000
Q4 property: $75,000 / $100,000 = 75% > 40%

RESULT: Mid-quarter convention applies to ALL property

Impact on March computer equipment:
  Half-year convention: 14.29% x $10,000 = $1,429
  Mid-quarter convention (Q1): 17.85% x $10,000 = $1,785 (actually better for Q1!)

Impact on November equipment:
  Half-year convention: 14.29% x $75,000 = $10,718
  Mid-quarter convention (Q4): 3.57% x $75,000 = $2,678 (much worse!)

Net effect: Q4 placement severely reduced, even though Q1/Q2 items improved slightly
```

**Strategy:** If making a large Q4 purchase, consider:
1. Delaying until January 1 of the next year
2. Making enough purchases in Q1-Q3 to keep Q4 below 40%
3. Using section 179 or bonus depreciation instead (which bypass convention rules entirely)
