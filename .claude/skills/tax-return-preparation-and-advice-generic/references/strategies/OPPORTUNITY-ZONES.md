# OPPORTUNITY ZONES — ss1400Z-2 Strategies

## Overview

Qualified Opportunity Zones (QOZs) were created by the Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 (ss1400Z-1 and ss1400Z-2) to incentivize investment in economically distressed communities. The **One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA)**, signed July 4, 2025, made the QOZ program **permanent** and introduced significant enhancements. The program provides three tax benefits for investing capital gains in Qualified Opportunity Funds (QOFs):

1. **Deferral**: Capital gains invested in a QOF are deferred until the QOF investment is sold or otherwise disposed of. **OBBBA removed the 12/31/2026 deferral cliff** — deferral is now open-ended for as long as the investor holds the QOF interest.
2. **Step-up to FMV**: If the QOF investment is held for 10+ years, the investor's basis in the QOF interest is stepped up to fair market value — meaning ALL appreciation during the hold period is tax-free. **This benefit is now permanent under OBBBA.**
3. ~~Basis increases~~: The 5-year (10%) and 7-year (15%) basis increases have EXPIRED — these only applied to investments made before 12/31/2021 and 12/31/2019, respectively

The 10-year hold benefit remains the crown jewel, the deferral benefit is now unlimited in duration, and OBBBA introduced new enhanced benefits for rural investments (see Qualified Rural Opportunity Funds below).

## OBBBA CHANGES — PERMANENT FRAMEWORK (Effective July 4, 2025)

### Deferral Is Now Permanent

The One Big Beautiful Bill Act (OBBBA) **eliminated the December 31, 2026 deferral cliff**. Under prior law, all deferred gains would have been forced into income on 12/31/2026 regardless of whether the QOF investment was sold. That forced recognition date no longer applies. Deferred gains are now recognized only upon actual sale or disposition of the QOF interest.

**Practical impact**: An investor who deferred a $2M capital gain by investing in a QOF in 2020 will NOT owe tax on that gain on their 2026 return. The gain remains deferred until the investor actually sells or disposes of the QOF investment.

### New QOZ Designation Cycle

1. **Existing QOZ designations extended** — tracts designated under the original TCJA process remain qualified
2. **New nomination window**: States may nominate new QOZ tracts during the **July-September 2026** window
3. **Stricter threshold for new tracts**: Post-2026 designations require a **70% median family income threshold** (tighter than the original 80% threshold), targeting investment toward more deeply distressed communities
4. Treasury will issue guidance on the new designation process

### Qualified Rural Opportunity Funds (QROFs)

OBBBA created a new enhanced tier — **Qualified Rural Opportunity Funds** — with additional incentives for investments in rural QOZs:

1. **Enhanced basis step-up**: QROFs receive additional basis step-up incentives beyond the standard 10-year FMV step-up, rewarding patient capital in underserved rural areas
2. **Eligibility**: The fund must invest substantially all assets in rural-designated QOZ tracts (Treasury to define "rural" via rulemaking)
3. **Planning opportunity**: Investors with flexibility on project location should evaluate rural QOZ tracts for the enhanced QROF benefits

### Anti-Abuse Provisions Strengthened

OBBBA tightened compliance requirements:

1. **Enhanced reporting**: Additional disclosure requirements for QOF investments and QOZ Business activities
2. **Stricter qualification standards**: Heightened scrutiny on whether QOZ Business Property genuinely serves the designated community
3. **Documentation is more critical than ever** — maintain robust records of QOZ compliance (see Common Pitfalls below)

### Strategic Planning Under the Permanent Framework

1. **No more deadline pressure**: Investors can take time to find the RIGHT QOZ investment rather than rushing to beat a sunset
2. **Longer deferral = more compounding**: With indefinite deferral, the time-value benefit of deferring the original gain is substantially greater
3. **State tax planning**: Some states don't conform to ss1400Z-2 — the deferred gain may have already been taxed at the state level. Confirm state conformity to OBBBA amendments.
4. **New investments are more attractive**: The permanent 10-year FMV step-up plus permanent deferral makes new QOF investments strictly better than under prior law
5. **Evaluate QROF eligibility**: For investors considering real estate or business investments in rural areas, QROF benefits add a meaningful additional incentive layer
6. **Do NOT sell the QOF prematurely**: With permanent deferral, there is no forced recognition event — selling before 10 years sacrifices both deferral AND the FMV step-up

### Timeline Decision Matrix (Post-OBBBA)

| Investment Date | Deferral Ends | 10-Year Hold Date | Strategy |
|---|---|---|---|
| 2019 or earlier | On sale/disposition | 2029+ | Hold through 10 years for FMV step-up; deferral continues until sale |
| 2020 | On sale/disposition | 2030 | Same — hold for 10-year FMV step-up |
| 2023 | On sale/disposition | 2033 | Continue holding; deferral and appreciation both benefit from time |
| 2025 (new) | On sale/disposition | 2035 | Full deferral + 10-year hold; no artificial deadline |
| 2026+ (new tracts) | On sale/disposition | 2036+ | New designation cycle; evaluate rural QOZs for QROF benefits |

## ELIGIBLE CAPITAL GAINS

### What Qualifies

ANY capital gain that would be recognized for federal income tax purposes can be deferred into a QOF, including:

- Sale of stocks, bonds, mutual funds
- Sale of real estate
- Sale of a business
- Sale of cryptocurrency (treated as property)
- Capital gain distributions from mutual funds or partnerships
- ss1231 gains (treated as capital gains to the extent they exceed ss1231 losses)
- Short-term AND long-term capital gains

### What Does NOT Qualify

- Ordinary income (even if from the sale of property)
- Gains from sale to a related party (ss1400Z-2(a)(2) — related party defined under ss267 and ss707(b))
- Gains that are already excluded (e.g., ss121 home sale exclusion — the excluded portion cannot be invested; the taxable portion CAN be)

### The 180-Day Investment Window

The capital gain must be invested in a QOF within 180 days of the date the gain would be recognized. Key nuances:

- **For direct sales**: 180 days from the sale date
- **For partnership/S-Corp gains**: The partner/shareholder can choose either (a) 180 days from the last day of the entity's tax year, OR (b) 180 days from the due date of the entity's return (without extensions). Per Reg. ss1.1400Z2(a)-1(b)(4).
- **For installment sales**: 180 days from each installment payment received (can invest each installment separately)
- **Only the gain**: You only need to invest the GAIN amount, not the entire sale proceeds

## QUALIFIED OPPORTUNITY FUND (QOF) REQUIREMENTS

### Formation and Self-Certification

A QOF is any corporation or partnership (including an LLC taxed as either) that self-certifies as a QOF by filing **Form 8996** (Qualified Opportunity Fund) with its tax return. There is NO application process, no IRS approval required.

### The 90% Asset Test

At least 90% of the QOF's assets must be Qualified Opportunity Zone Property (QOZP), tested semi-annually (on the last day of the 6th month and the last day of the tax year). Penalty for failure: calculated under ss1400Z-2(f)(1) — essentially an interest charge on the underpayment attributable to the non-qualifying assets.

### Qualified Opportunity Zone Property (QOZP) — Three Types

1. **QOZ Stock**: Stock in a domestic corporation that is a QOZ Business (QOZ-B)
2. **QOZ Partnership Interest**: Capital or profits interest in a domestic partnership that is a QOZ-B
3. **QOZ Business Property (QOZBP)**: Tangible property used in a trade or business in a QOZ, either (a) originally placed in service by the QOF in the QOZ, or (b) substantially improved by the QOF

### Substantially All Requirement (70% of 90%)

A QOZ Business must hold "substantially all" of its tangible property in a QOZ. Treasury regulations define "substantially all" as 70% for this purpose (Reg. ss1.1400Z2(d)-1(d)(3)). Combined with the 90% test:
- QOF must hold 90% of assets in QOZP
- QOZP businesses must hold 70% of tangible property in a QOZ
- **Effective threshold**: 63% (90% x 70%) of total assets must be tangible property in a QOZ

### Substantial Improvement Test

For EXISTING buildings (not original use), the QOF must "substantially improve" the property within 30 months. This means additions to basis must EXCEED the basis of the building (not land) at acquisition.

**Key point**: Land is NEVER required to be substantially improved (Reg. ss1.1400Z2(d)-2(b)(4)(iv)). Only the building must be doubled. If you buy a property for $1M where land = $700K and building = $300K, you need only $300,001 in improvements, not $1M.

## WORKING CAPITAL SAFE HARBOR

### 31-Month Safe Harbor (Reg. ss1.1400Z2(d)-1(d)(3)(v))

A QOZ Business can hold working capital (cash, cash equivalents) without violating the tangible property requirements if:

1. The amounts are designated in writing for acquisition, construction, or substantial improvement of tangible property in the QOZ
2. There is a written schedule for expenditure within 31 months
3. The working capital is actually used consistently with the schedule

### COVID Extension

Notice 2021-10 provided an additional 24 months for working capital safe harbors that were in effect on or after January 20, 2020 — effectively a 55-month window for pandemic-affected projects.

### Practical Application

A QOF raises $10M for a real estate development in a QOZ:
- Month 1-6: $10M in cash (safe harbor: written plan for construction)
- Month 7-12: $5M spent on land and initial construction, $5M in cash
- Month 13-24: Remaining $5M deployed into construction
- Month 25-31: Project completed and placed in service
- Result: QOF passes 90% test throughout due to safe harbor

## CRT + QOZ COMBINATION STRATEGY

### The Play

One of the most sophisticated tax strategies available:

1. **Taxpayer sells appreciated asset** (e.g., $5M stock, $500K basis, $4.5M gain)
2. **Contribute to CRT** before sale — CRT sells tax-free (ss664(c))
3. **CRT distributes capital gains tier** to taxpayer over time
4. **Taxpayer invests each gain distribution** into a QOF within 180 days
5. **QOF gain is deferred** indefinitely (until QOF interest is sold), then held for 10 years for FMV step-up

### Result

- CRT: tax-free sale, charitable deduction, income stream
- QOF: deferral of gain recognized from CRT distributions, plus 10-year exclusion of QOF appreciation
- Combined: taxpayer converts highly appreciated asset into diversified QOZ real estate portfolio with minimal current tax

### Timing Complexity

The 180-day clock for QOF investment starts when the CRT distributes capital gains to the taxpayer (which is when the gain is "recognized" for purposes of ss1400Z-2). Careful coordination with CRT distribution timing is essential.

## EXIT STRATEGIES

### 10-Year Hold (The Goal)

After holding a QOF investment for at least 10 years, the taxpayer may elect to receive a basis step-up to FMV under ss1400Z-2(c):
- ALL appreciation in the QOF investment from the date of investment becomes tax-free
- The election is made on the return for the year of sale
- There is no requirement to sell at the 10-year mark — holding longer continues the benefit

**Worked Example** (post-OBBBA):
- 2020: Invest $1M gain into QOF (basis = $0 — the deferred gain reduces basis)
- 2030: QOF investment worth $3M. Sell and elect FMV step-up under ss1400Z-2(c)
- Deferred gain of $1M becomes taxable upon sale (pay ~$238K at 23.8%)
- Basis step-up: FMV of $3M elected as basis on the QOF interest appreciation
- Capital gain on $2M of post-investment appreciation: $0 (excluded by 10-year FMV step-up)
- Tax saved on appreciation: $2M x 23.8% = $476,000
- Net: paid $238K on original deferred gain, saved $476K on appreciation — net benefit $238K plus years of tax-free compounding on the deferred amount

### Early Disposition (Before 10 Years)

If sold before 10 years:
- The deferred gain is recognized upon sale (it was deferred, not eliminated)
- Appreciation in QOF is taxed as capital gain
- No FMV step-up benefit
- The investor received only a deferral benefit, not the permanent exclusion of appreciation

### ss1031 Exchange OUT of QOZ

Controversial: Can a QOF sell QOZ real property and do a 1031 exchange into non-QOZ property? The regulations do not explicitly address this. Most practitioners believe the QOF itself can do 1031 exchanges, but the replacement property must still be in a QOZ to maintain the 90% asset test. An exchange OUT of a QOZ would likely cause a 90% test failure.

## STATE CONFORMITY

### Full Conformity (Most States)

Most states automatically conform to ss1400Z-2 because they conform to the IRC for their starting point of taxable income. States with full or substantial conformity include: AZ, CO, FL (no income tax), GA, ID, IL, IN, MD, MI, MO, NJ, NY, OH, PA, SC, TX (no income tax), VA, WA (no income tax).

### Partial or Non-Conformity

| State | Treatment |
|---|---|
| California | Partially conforms — allows deferral but DID NOT conform to the 5/7-year basis increases (now moot). Does conform to the 10-year FMV step-up. |
| Mississippi | Does NOT conform — gain is taxable in the year realized regardless of QOF investment |
| North Carolina | Does NOT conform — no deferral, no step-up benefit |
| Hawaii | Does NOT conform |
| Massachusetts | Partial — conforms to deferral but may not conform to FMV step-up |

### Planning Implications

For a California investor deferring a $2M gain into a QOF:
- Federal: $2M gain deferred until sale of QOF interest (permanent under OBBBA)
- California: $2M gain ALSO deferred (CA conforms to deferral). **Verify CA conformity to OBBBA amendments** — CA may lag in conforming to the permanent deferral change.
- After 10 years: Federal FMV step-up applies AND California FMV step-up applies
- Net California benefit: 13.3% x appreciation = significant savings

For a North Carolina investor:
- Must pay NC state tax on the gain in the year of the original sale
- Federal benefits still apply
- Net benefit: federal only

## COMMON PITFALLS

### 1. Missing the 180-Day Window
The deadline is absolute. Even one day late and the entire gain is ineligible. For partnership/S-Corp K-1 gains, the flexible start dates (entity year-end or return due date) provide some relief.

### 2. Failing the 90% Asset Test
Penalties accrue semi-annually. A QOF that holds too much cash without the working capital safe harbor, or invests in non-QOZ assets, can fail the test and owe penalties.

### 3. Not Meeting Substantial Improvement
For existing buildings, the basis-doubling requirement within 30 months is strict. Cost overruns or delays that push improvements past the 30-month window can disqualify the property.

### 4. Sin Businesses (ss144(c)(6)(B))
A QOZ Business cannot derive more than 5% of gross income from: golf courses, country clubs, massage parlors, hot tub facilities, suntan facilities, racetracks, gambling, or liquor stores.

### 5. Lack of Documentation
Self-certification means there's no upfront IRS review — but audits can come later. Maintain:
- Form 8996 filed with each QOF tax return
- Form 8949 and Form 8997 on individual returns
- Written working capital plans
- Semi-annual 90% test calculations
- Appraisals of QOZ property

### 6. Related Party Sales
Gains from sales to related parties (ss267/ss707(b)) cannot be deferred into a QOF. This includes sales between family members, controlled entities, and trusts where the seller is a beneficiary.

### 7. Forgetting State Non-Conformity
Investing in a QOF thinking the state tax is also deferred, only to discover the state doesn't conform — results in unexpected state tax liability in the year of the original gain.

## KEY TAKEAWAYS

1. **OBBBA made QOZs permanent** — deferral is now open-ended (no 12/31/2026 cliff) and the 10-year FMV step-up is permanent
2. **The 10-year hold remains the crown jewel** — 100% exclusion of post-investment appreciation, now with permanent deferral of the original gain until sale
3. **New QOZ tracts available** — states may nominate new tracts in the July-September 2026 window under a stricter 70% median income threshold
4. **Qualified Rural Opportunity Funds (QROFs)** — new enhanced benefits for rural QOZ investments; evaluate for additional basis step-up incentives
5. **Only capital gains qualify** — structure transactions to generate capital (not ordinary) gain
6. **Self-certification via Form 8996** — no application needed, but documentation is more critical than ever under OBBBA's strengthened anti-abuse provisions
7. **Land doesn't need improvement** — only the building basis must be doubled
8. **Working capital safe harbor** provides flexibility for development projects
9. **State conformity varies** — check state conformity to OBBBA amendments before assuming state benefits
10. **CRT + QOZ** is the most powerful combination for large appreciated assets
11. **Do not sell early** — with permanent deferral, there is no forced recognition event; selling before 10 years sacrifices both deferral AND the FMV step-up
