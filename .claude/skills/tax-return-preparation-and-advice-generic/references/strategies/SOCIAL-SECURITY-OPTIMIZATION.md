# SOCIAL SECURITY OPTIMIZATION — Claiming Strategies and Tax Coordination

## Overview

Social Security is the largest source of retirement income for most Americans, yet the claiming decision is often made with minimal analysis. The difference between optimal and suboptimal claiming strategies can exceed $200,000 in lifetime benefits for a married couple. When combined with tax optimization — particularly Roth conversions, withdrawal sequencing, and state tax planning — the total financial impact is even greater.

## CLAIMING AGE STRATEGIES

### Benefit Amounts by Claiming Age

For someone born in 1960 or later (Full Retirement Age = 67):

| Claiming Age | Benefit as % of FRA Amount | Monthly Benefit (if FRA = $3,000) | Annual Benefit |
|---|---|---|---|
| 62 | 70.0% | $2,100 | $25,200 |
| 63 | 75.0% | $2,250 | $27,000 |
| 64 | 80.0% | $2,400 | $28,800 |
| 65 | 86.7% | $2,601 | $31,212 |
| 66 | 93.3% | $2,799 | $33,588 |
| **67 (FRA)** | **100.0%** | **$3,000** | **$36,000** |
| 68 | 108.0% | $3,240 | $38,880 |
| 69 | 116.0% | $3,480 | $41,760 |
| **70** | **124.0%** | **$3,720** | **$44,640** |

### Reduction and Delayed Credits

- **Before FRA**: Benefits reduced by 5/9 of 1% per month for the first 36 months early, plus 5/12 of 1% per month for additional months (up to 60 months early for FRA of 67)
- **After FRA**: Delayed Retirement Credits (DRCs) of 8% per year (2/3 of 1% per month) for each month of delay up to age 70
- **After 70**: No additional credits — no reason to delay past 70

### Break-Even Analysis

The "break-even" point is when cumulative benefits from delayed claiming exceed cumulative benefits from early claiming:

**62 vs 67 (FRA)**:
- Claiming at 62: 5 years of extra benefits = $25,200 x 5 = $126,000 head start
- At 67: benefit is $36,000 vs $25,200 = $10,800/year more
- Break-even: $126,000 / $10,800 = 11.7 years → age **73.7**
- After 73.7, every year of life means the delay strategy wins by $10,800/year

**67 (FRA) vs 70**:
- Not claiming 67-70: 3 years of foregone benefits = $36,000 x 3 = $108,000
- At 70: benefit is $44,640 vs $36,000 = $8,640/year more
- Break-even: $108,000 / $8,640 = 12.5 years → age **82.5**
- After 82.5, the delay wins by $8,640/year for life

### When to Claim Early (62-66)

- **Health issues**: Life expectancy significantly below average (break-even age)
- **Immediate financial need**: No other income sources to bridge to 67/70
- **Spousal planning**: Lower earner claims early to provide household income while higher earner delays to 70
- **Investment opportunity**: If the early benefit would be invested and expected to earn returns exceeding the 8%/year delayed credit (rare in risk-adjusted terms)

### When to Delay to 70

- **Good health**: Average life expectancy or better
- **Higher earner in a couple**: The survivor will receive the higher benefit — maximizing this protects the surviving spouse
- **Longevity insurance**: SS is an inflation-adjusted annuity — no market risk, no sequence-of-returns risk
- **High earners**: The delayed credits represent an 8% guaranteed real return — difficult to replicate

## SPOUSAL BENEFITS

### Spousal Benefit Rules

- A spouse can receive up to **50% of the higher earner's FRA benefit** (the "Primary Insurance Amount" or PIA)
- The spousal benefit is reduced if claimed before the lower earner's FRA
- The higher earner MUST have filed for benefits (or the lower earner must have been married for 1+ year and the higher earner is 62+)
- If the lower earner's OWN benefit exceeds 50% of the higher earner's PIA, the spousal benefit is irrelevant (you get the higher of your own or spousal, not both)

### Worked Example — Spousal Strategy

- Spouse A (higher earner): PIA = $3,500/month at FRA (67)
- Spouse B (lower earner): PIA = $1,200/month at FRA (67)
- Spouse B's spousal benefit: 50% x $3,500 = $1,750 (higher than own $1,200)

**Optimal strategy**:
1. Spouse B claims at 62: Receives reduced own benefit (~$840/month)
2. Spouse A delays to 70: Receives $4,340/month (124% of $3,500)
3. When Spouse A claims at 70: Spouse B's benefit automatically steps up to spousal benefit (50% of Spouse A's PIA of $3,500 = $1,750, reduced for early filing)
4. Household income at 70: $4,340 + ~$1,550 = ~$5,890/month

**Alternative**: Both claim at 67: $3,500 + $1,750 = $5,250/month — $640/month less

## SURVIVOR BENEFITS

### Rules

- Surviving spouse receives **100% of the deceased spouse's benefit** (if higher than their own)
- Can claim survivor benefits as early as age 60 (50 if disabled)
- Survivor benefit is reduced if claimed before the survivor's FRA
- The survivor CAN switch: claim a reduced survivor benefit at 60, then switch to their own delayed benefit at 70 (or vice versa)

### Critical Planning Implication

The higher earner's decision to delay to 70 is also a decision about the SURVIVOR benefit. If Spouse A delays to 70 and dies at 80, Spouse B receives $4,340/month for the rest of their life — potentially 10-20+ more years.

**Worked Example — Survivor Impact**:
- Spouse A claims at 62 ($2,450/month) and dies at 78
- Spouse B survivor benefit: $2,450/month (locked in at A's reduced amount)
- If Spouse A had delayed to 70 ($4,340/month): Spouse B's survivor benefit would be $4,340/month
- Difference over 15 years of survivorship: ($4,340 - $2,450) x 12 x 15 = **$340,200**

This is why the higher earner should almost always delay to 70 — it's longevity insurance for the surviving spouse.

## LEGACY STRATEGIES (GRANDFATHERED)

### File and Suspend (Eliminated 4/30/2016)

Previously, the higher earner could file for SS at FRA and immediately suspend benefits — allowing the lower earner to claim spousal benefits while the higher earner continued earning delayed credits. This was eliminated by the Bipartisan Budget Act of 2015 for anyone who didn't file and suspend by April 29, 2016.

### Restricted Application (Grandfathered)

For those born BEFORE January 2, 1954, a restricted application allows:
- File for SPOUSAL benefits only at FRA
- Continue earning delayed credits on their OWN record
- Switch to their own (higher, delayed) benefit at 70

This is still available for the shrinking population born before 1954 (age 71+ in 2025). If a client qualifies, it should be utilized.

## TAXATION OF SOCIAL SECURITY BENEFITS

### The Provisional Income Formula

**Provisional Income = AGI + tax-exempt interest + 50% of Social Security benefits**

| Filing Status | Provisional Income | % of SS Benefits Taxable |
|---|---|---|
| Single | < $25,000 | 0% |
| Single | $25,000 - $34,000 | Up to 50% |
| Single | > $34,000 | Up to 85% |
| MFJ | < $32,000 | 0% |
| MFJ | $32,000 - $44,000 | Up to 50% |
| MFJ | > $44,000 | Up to 85% |

### The Tax Torpedo — Detailed Analysis

In the "torpedo zone" (between the 50% and 85% thresholds), each additional dollar of income causes up to $0.85 of Social Security to become newly taxable. This creates a phantom marginal rate on top of the statutory rate.

**MFJ Couple, $32K SS benefits, other income of $28,000**:
- Provisional income: $28,000 + $16,000 (50% of SS) = $44,000
- Right at the 85% threshold
- An additional $1,000 of IRA distribution:
  - Adds $1,000 to AGI
  - Adds $1,000 to provisional income
  - Causes $850 of previously untaxed SS to become taxable
  - Total new taxable income: $1,850
  - At 12% bracket: $1,850 x 12% = $222 in additional tax
  - Effective marginal rate on the $1,000: **22.2%** (not the statutory 12%)

In the 22% bracket, the same torpedo effect creates:
- $1,000 x 22% = $220 on the distribution
- $850 x 22% = $187 on the newly taxable SS
- Total: $407 on $1,000 = **40.7% effective marginal rate**

### Strategies to Minimize SS Taxation

1. **Roth conversions BEFORE claiming SS**: Reduce Traditional IRA balance (and future RMDs that inflate provisional income) while provisional income is zero
2. **Roth withdrawals instead of Traditional IRA**: Roth distributions are NOT included in provisional income
3. **Municipal bonds within reason**: Interest is excluded from AGI (but IS included in provisional income — a trap)
4. **Tax-loss harvesting**: Offset capital gains to reduce AGI
5. **QCDs**: Reduce AGI by satisfying charitable intent through QCDs (which are excluded from income)

## WORKING WHILE COLLECTING — THE EARNINGS TEST

### Before FRA

If claiming SS before FRA and still working:
- **$22,320 earnings limit** (2025)
- Benefits reduced by **$1 for every $2** earned above the limit
- Only EARNED income counts (wages, self-employment). Investment income, pensions, and distributions are NOT counted.

### In the Year of FRA

- **$59,520 earnings limit** (2025, applies only to months BEFORE the month of FRA)
- Benefits reduced by **$1 for every $3** earned above the limit

### After FRA

NO earnings test. Can earn unlimited amounts with no reduction.

### The Earnings Test Is NOT a Tax

Critically: benefits withheld due to the earnings test are NOT lost. SSA recalculates the benefit at FRA to credit back the months of withheld benefits. The result is a HIGHER monthly benefit going forward.

**Worked Example**:
- Claim at 62, earn $82,320 (before FRA)
- Excess earnings: $82,320 - $22,320 = $60,000
- Benefits withheld: $60,000 / 2 = $30,000/year
- At FRA: SSA recalculates as if you claimed later (approximately 1 year later based on months withheld)
- Higher benefit for life — the earnings test effectively forced a delay

## WINDFALL ELIMINATION PROVISION (WEP) AND GOVERNMENT PENSION OFFSET (GPO)

The Social Security Fairness Act was signed on January 5, 2025. For benefits payable after
December 2023, both WEP and GPO were repealed.

### What That Means in Practice

- Workers with pensions from non-Social-Security-covered employment should no longer model an
  ongoing WEP haircut into current-law retirement projections.
- Spousal and survivor benefits should no longer be reduced by GPO under current law.
- Transition mechanics, retroactive adjustments, and SSA processing delays may still matter, so
  taxpayers with affected pensions should reconcile actual SSA notices and payment history.
- Historical planning memos, older calculators, and many online articles are now stale. Treat any
  WEP/GPO discussion not updated after January 5, 2025 as suspect until verified.

## COORDINATION WITH ROTH CONVERSIONS

### The Master Strategy

The synergy between SS optimization and Roth conversions is the most impactful planning area for retirees:

**Phase 1 — Pre-SS (ages 60-69)**:
- No SS benefits → provisional income is zero
- Aggressively convert Traditional IRA to Roth
- Fill up to the 22% or 24% bracket (depending on projected future rates)
- Zero concern about SS taxation or IRMAA (pre-Medicare)

**Phase 2 — Early SS (ages 70-72)**:
- SS benefits begin
- Roth conversions continue but must account for SS taxation interaction
- Convert only to the point where provisional income stays below $44K (MFJ) to avoid the tax torpedo
- OR: convert aggressively past the torpedo zone to permanently reduce future RMDs

**Phase 3 — RMD Years (ages 73/75+)**:
- RMDs begin — forced IRA distributions inflate provisional income
- Roth conversions may still be beneficial if RMDs don't fill the desired bracket
- QCDs offset RMDs for charitable retirees

### Worked Example — Full Coordination

**Couple, both age 62, just retired**:
- Traditional IRA: $2,000,000
- Taxable accounts: $800,000
- SS benefits (Spouse A at FRA): $3,000/month; (Spouse B at FRA): $1,500/month
- No pension

**Strategy**:
1. Ages 62-69: Live off taxable accounts. Convert $97K/year of Traditional IRA to Roth (top of 12% bracket, ~$11,634/year in conversion tax)
2. Total converted over 8 years: $776,000. Tax paid: $93,072.
3. Age 70: Spouse A claims SS ($3,720/month = $44,640/year). Spouse B claims spousal.
4. Remaining Traditional IRA (after conversions and growth): ~$1,900,000
5. Age 73: RMD from $1.9M = ~$71,700 → combined with SS stays in 22% bracket
6. Without conversions: Traditional IRA would be ~$3.4M, RMD = $128,300 → pushes into 24%+ bracket, maximizes SS taxation, triggers IRMAA

**Lifetime tax savings from coordination: ~$250,000-$350,000**

## STATE TAXATION OF SOCIAL SECURITY

### States That Tax Social Security (2025)

Most states exempt SS benefits entirely. The following states still tax some or all SS income:

| State | Treatment |
|---|---|
| **Colorado** | Fully exempt if age 65+; partial exemption ($20,000) if under 65 |
| **Connecticut** | Exempt if AGI below $75K (single) / $100K (MFJ); partial taxation above |
| **Minnesota** | Follows federal taxation formula with state-level subtraction for lower incomes |
| **Montana** | Follows federal taxation formula; partial exemption for lower incomes |
| **New Mexico** | Exempt if AGI below $100K (MFJ); above, follows federal |
| **Rhode Island** | Exempt if AGI below $101,000 (single) / $126,250 (MFJ) and reached FRA |
| **Utah** | Tax credit that effectively exempts lower/middle income; phases out at higher income |
| **Vermont** | Exempt if AGI below $50K (single) / $65K (MFJ); taxable above |
| **West Virginia** | Phasing out SS taxation by 2026 (100% exempt by 2026) |

### Domicile Change Strategy

For retirees with significant SS benefits living in a state that taxes SS:
- Moving to a state with no income tax (FL, TX, NV, WA, WY, AK, SD, TN, NH) eliminates ALL state income tax — on SS, pensions, IRA distributions, and investment income
- Moving to a state that specifically exempts SS (e.g., PA exempts all retirement income) provides targeted relief
- The domicile change must be genuine — maintain driver's license, voter registration, spend majority of time in new state

## SOCIAL SECURITY AND DIVORCE

### Divorced Spouse Benefits

A divorced person can claim spousal benefits based on an ex-spouse's record if:
- Marriage lasted 10+ years
- Divorced for 2+ years (waived if ex-spouse is 62+ and eligible)
- Claimant is unmarried
- Claimant is 62+
- The ex-spouse does NOT need to have filed for benefits (if divorced 2+ years and ex is 62+)

The ex-spouse is NOT notified and their benefits are NOT reduced.

### Strategy for Divorced Individuals

If the ex-spouse had significantly higher earnings:
- Divorced spouse benefit: 50% of ex's PIA (at claimant's FRA)
- If claimant's own PIA < 50% of ex's PIA → claim divorced spouse benefit
- Can claim divorced spouse benefit while delaying own benefit to 70 (if born before 1/2/1954 — restricted application)
- For those born 1/2/1954 or later: deemed filing applies — cannot pick and choose

## KEY TAKEAWAYS

1. **Higher earner should almost always delay to 70** — it maximizes the survivor benefit, providing lifelong insurance
2. **Lower earner claiming early** can bridge household income while higher earner delays
3. **Break-even for delay is typically age 80-83** — most healthy retirees will live past this
4. **The tax torpedo** makes the effective marginal rate in the 12% bracket as high as 22.2%
5. **Roth conversions BEFORE claiming SS** are the single highest-ROI tax move for pre-retirees
6. **The earnings test is not a tax** — benefits are recalculated upward at FRA
7. **Old WEP/GPO assumptions are stale** — current projections should reflect the 2025 repeal and
   actual SSA notices rather than pre-repeal haircut estimates
8. **Divorced spouse benefits** are available after 10 years of marriage without affecting the ex-spouse
9. **State SS taxation** affects 9 states — domicile planning can eliminate this
10. **Coordinate SS claiming with Roth conversions, IRMAA, and withdrawal sequencing** for maximum lifetime benefit
