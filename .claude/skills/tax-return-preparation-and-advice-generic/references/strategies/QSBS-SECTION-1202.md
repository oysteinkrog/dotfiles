# QSBS — Section 1202 Qualified Small Business Stock Exclusion

## Overview

Section 1202 is one of the most powerful tax provisions in the Internal Revenue Code — potentially excluding 100% of capital gain on the sale of Qualified Small Business Stock (QSBS). For stock issued after July 4, 2025 (OBBBA enactment), the exclusion cap is the greater of **$15 million or 10x adjusted basis** (both inflation-indexed starting 2027). For pre-OBBBA stock, the legacy cap of $10M or 10x basis applies. OBBBA also made Section 1202 permanent — there is no sunset provision.

For a founder selling a startup for $50M, this can mean $0 federal tax on up to $15M+ of gain. Combined with trust stacking strategies (each non-grantor trust gets its own $15M cap), the exclusion can multiply to $75M-$150M+ for a well-planned family.

---

## THE EXCLUSION — ss1202(a)

### Exclusion Percentages (Based on Date of Stock Acquisition)

**Pre-OBBBA stock (issued on or before July 4, 2025):**

| Acquisition Date | Exclusion % | IRC Authority |
|---|---|---|
| After 9/27/2010 | **100%** (at 5 years) | ss1202(a)(4), as amended by SBJA 2010 and made permanent by PATH Act 2015 |
| 2/18/2009 - 9/27/2010 | 75% | ss1202(a)(3) |
| Before 2/18/2009 | 50% | ss1202(a)(1) |

For pre-OBBBA stock acquired after September 27, 2010, the exclusion is 100% at the 5-year mark — NO federal income tax on qualifying gain. The single 5-year holding period threshold applies.

**Post-OBBBA stock (issued after July 4, 2025) — New Tiered Holding Schedule:**

| Holding Period | Exclusion % |
|---|---|
| 3 years | **50%** |
| 4 years | **75%** |
| 5 years | **100%** |

The OBBBA (One Big Beautiful Bill Act, signed July 4, 2025) introduced a tiered holding schedule for stock issued after its enactment. This replaces the old all-or-nothing 5-year cliff for post-OBBBA stock. Founders who exit at 3 or 4 years now get partial exclusions rather than zero. Pre-OBBBA stock continues to follow the old rules (100% only at 5+ years, 0% below 5 years).

### Gain Exclusion Limits — Per Issuer

**Post-OBBBA stock (issued after July 4, 2025):**

The excluded gain cannot exceed the GREATER of:
- **$15,000,000** reduced by aggregate gain previously excluded for stock of the SAME issuer, OR
- **10 times the aggregate adjusted basis** of QSBS of that issuer disposed of during the tax year

Both the $15M figure and the 10x basis figure are **inflation-indexed starting in 2027** (adjusted annually for CPI).

**Pre-OBBBA stock (issued on or before July 4, 2025):**

The excluded gain cannot exceed the GREATER of:
- **$10,000,000** reduced by aggregate gain previously excluded for stock of the SAME issuer, OR
- **10 times the aggregate adjusted basis** of QSBS of that issuer disposed of during the tax year

(No inflation indexing for pre-OBBBA stock.)

This is a PER-ISSUER, PER-TAXPAYER limit. If a founder has $500K of basis (common for early-stage stock) in post-OBBBA stock, the limit is the GREATER of $15M or $5M (10 x $500K) = $15M.

But if the founder made an ss83(b) election and has $2M of basis, the limit is the GREATER of $15M or $20M = **$20M**.

## QUALIFIED SMALL BUSINESS REQUIREMENTS

### Five Core Requirements

**1. C-Corporation Status**

The stock must be issued by a domestic C-Corporation. Stock of S-Corps, partnerships, and foreign corporations does NOT qualify. However, an LLC that elects C-Corporation classification via Form 8832 (check-the-box) IS treated as a corporation for all federal tax purposes, and membership interests treated as stock CAN qualify as QSBS, provided all other requirements are met (domestic C-Corp status, $50M gross assets test, active business requirement, etc.). IRS PLRs confirm this treatment. See the "LLC/Partnership to C-Corp Conversion" section below for details on the deemed incorporation mechanics.

**Critical timing**: The entity must be a C-Corp at the time of stock issuance AND for substantially all of the holding period. A company that starts as an LLC and later converts to a C-Corp can have qualifying QSBS, but the 5-year holding period begins at the date of conversion (the deemed incorporation date under Rev. Rul. 2004-59) — only stock deemed issued at or after that conversion qualifies.

**2. $50M Gross Asset Test (ss1202(d)(1))**

The corporation's aggregate gross assets must not exceed $50,000,000:
- At any time BEFORE the stock issuance, AND
- Immediately AFTER the stock issuance (including the proceeds)

"Gross assets" = adjusted basis of all assets (cash plus tax basis of all property). This is NOT fair market value — a company with $30M in asset basis but $500M in FMV still qualifies.

**Trap**: Each funding round adds cash to gross assets. A Series C that brings total cash + asset basis above $50M disqualifies stock issued in that round AND all subsequent rounds. But stock issued in prior rounds (when assets were under $50M) remains qualified.

**3. Active Business Requirement (ss1202(e)(3))**

During substantially all of the taxpayer's holding period, at least 80% (by value) of the corporation's assets must be used in the active conduct of one or more qualified trades or businesses.

**Disqualified businesses** (ss1202(e)(3)):
- Professional services (health, law, engineering, architecture, accounting, actuarial science, performing arts, consulting, athletics, financial services, brokerage)
- Banking, insurance, financing, leasing, investing
- Farming (including raising/harvesting timber)
- Mining and oil/gas extraction
- Hospitality (hotel, motel, restaurant)
- Any business where the principal asset is the reputation or skill of employees

**The SaaS/tech sweet spot**: Software companies, technology companies, e-commerce, manufacturing, and similar businesses clearly qualify. The key question for borderline cases: is the company selling a product/service, or is it selling the expertise of its professionals?

**4. Original Issuance (ss1202(c)(1))**

The stock must be acquired at original issuance in exchange for:
- Money
- Property (other than stock)
- Services

Stock purchased on the secondary market does NOT qualify. Stock received in a merger or acquisition can qualify if it meets the successor rules.

**5. Holding Period (ss1202(a))**

For **pre-OBBBA stock** (issued on or before July 4, 2025): the taxpayer must hold the stock for more than 5 years for the 100% exclusion. There is no partial exclusion for shorter holding periods.

For **post-OBBBA stock** (issued after July 4, 2025): the tiered schedule applies — 50% exclusion at 3 years, 75% at 4 years, 100% at 5 years. This means a founder who exits at Year 3 or 4 now gets a meaningful partial exclusion rather than nothing.

The ss83(b) election remains critical — it starts the holding period clock at grant, not vesting.

## QSBS STACKING WITH TRUSTS

### The Multiplication Strategy — The Key Post-OBBBA Multiplier

The exclusion is per taxpayer per issuer. By gifting QSBS to multiple taxpayers (trusts, family members), each recipient gets their OWN exclusion cap. Post-OBBBA, each non-grantor trust gets a $15M cap, making trust stacking dramatically more powerful.

**Structure (post-OBBBA stock)**:
1. Founder holds QSBS with basis of $100K
2. Before sale, founder gifts QSBS to:
   - Spouse (if filing separately or in a community property state — married filing jointly SHARES the limit)
   - Irrevocable Trust #1 for benefit of Child A
   - Irrevocable Trust #2 for benefit of Child B
   - Irrevocable Trust #3 for benefit of Child C
   - Dynasty Trust
3. Each non-grantor trust is a SEPARATE taxpayer with its own **$15M exclusion**
4. Founder retains enough shares for their own $15M exclusion
5. Total exclusion: 6 taxpayers x $15M = **$90M**

**Even a simpler setup works**: A founder who creates just 3 non-grantor trusts that each hold qualifying post-OBBBA stock effectively has $45M+ in exclusion capacity (3 trusts x $15M, plus the founder's own $15M = $60M). This is the single most important planning technique post-OBBBA.

### Requirements for Trust Stacking

- Trusts must be **non-grantor trusts** — a grantor trust is disregarded and its exclusion is merged with the grantor's (Rev. Rul. 85-13 by implication; see also CCA 201016053)
- Each trust must be a separate, legitimate trust with independent terms
- Gift of QSBS is a completed gift for gift/estate tax purposes (uses exemption)
- The DONEE trust's holding period includes the DONOR's holding period (ss1202(h)(1) — tacking applies to gifts)
- The exclusion limit ($15M for post-OBBBA stock, $10M for pre-OBBBA) is reduced by any gain previously excluded by the DONOR for the same issuer — but each new trust starts fresh

### Practical Considerations

- **Timing**: Must gift BEFORE the sale (or before a binding contract to sell). Gifting after a sale agreement is too late.
- **Gift tax**: QSBS has a FMV that must be determined for gift tax purposes. Using exemption ($13.99M per person in 2025) covers most gifts.
- **Valuation**: Private company QSBS is valued at FMV, which may be significantly lower than eventual sale price (apply appropriate discounts for illiquidity, minority interest)
- **Grantor trust trap**: If the trust is an IDGT (intentionally defective grantor trust), the QSBS exclusion is the GRANTOR's, not the trust's — stacking fails. Must use non-grantor trusts. This is the most common mistake in QSBS trust planning and it completely defeats the purpose of the $15M-per-trust multiplier.

## ss1045 ROLLOVER

### Mechanics

If QSBS is held for at least 6 months (but less than 5 years), the taxpayer can defer gain by reinvesting in replacement QSBS within 60 days of the sale:

- Gain is deferred (not excluded)
- The replacement QSBS inherits the original QSBS's holding period for purposes of the 5-year requirement
- The replacement must meet all QSBS requirements independently
- The basis of the replacement stock is reduced by the deferred gain

### Strategic Use

A founder who has held QSBS for 3 years and receives an acquisition offer can:
1. Sell the QSBS
2. Reinvest the gain in new QSBS within 60 days
3. The new QSBS inherits the 3-year holding period
4. Hold the new QSBS for 2+ more years
5. Sell and claim the ss1202 100% exclusion

This is particularly useful when an early-stage company is acquired before the 5-year mark.

### Pass-Through Entities

For partnerships and S-Corps that sell QSBS: the ss1045 rollover can be made at the ENTITY level or the INDIVIDUAL partner/shareholder level (ss1045(a) and Reg. ss1.1045-1).

## AMT INTERACTION

### Post-9/27/2010 Stock: No AMT Preference

For stock acquired after September 27, 2010 (the 100% exclusion tier), ss1202(a)(4) provides that the excluded gain is NOT an AMT preference item. This means:
- 100% exclusion for regular tax
- 100% exclusion for AMT
- Effective tax rate: **0%**

### Pre-9/27/2010 Stock: 7% AMT Preference

For stock acquired before 9/28/2010 (the 50% or 75% exclusion tiers), 7% of the excluded gain is an AMT preference item under ss57(a)(7). This means:
- 50% exclusion: 7% x excluded gain is AMT preference → effective AMT rate of ~1.96% on total gain
- 75% exclusion: similar calculation

For all practical purposes with post-2010 stock, AMT is irrelevant.

## LLC/PARTNERSHIP TO C-CORP CONVERSION

### The Problem

Most startups begin as LLCs (taxed as partnerships). QSBS requires C-Corp status. Converting an LLC to a C-Corp is common but raises QSBS timing issues.

### Methods of Conversion

**1. Formative C-Corp + Asset Transfer**
- Form new C-Corp
- LLC contributes assets to C-Corp in exchange for stock
- LLC distributes stock to members and dissolves
- QSBS clock starts at the date of stock issuance

**2. Check-the-Box Election (Form 8832)**
- LLC elects to be classified as a corporation for tax purposes
- Treated as if LLC transferred all assets to a new corporation in exchange for stock (Rev. Rul. 2004-59)
- Stock is treated as issued at the date of the deemed incorporation
- QSBS clock starts at the deemed incorporation date

**3. Statutory Conversion**
- Some states allow direct conversion of LLC to C-Corp
- Tax treatment follows the check-the-box deemed incorporation rules

### Key Issue: Built-In Gains

If the LLC has appreciated assets at the time of conversion, the C-Corp takes a basis equal to the LLC's basis (ss351). The ss1202 exclusion applies only to gain AFTER issuance — but the stock's initial basis includes the FMV of contributed assets. If assets were contributed at basis (not FMV), the built-in gain may not be excludable under ss1202 if the IRS argues the stock was issued for property with a basis below FMV.

**Best practice**: Convert to C-Corp as EARLY as possible (when asset values and built-in gains are minimal) to maximize the gain eligible for ss1202 exclusion.

## ss83(b) ELECTION FOR FOUNDERS

### Why It Matters for QSBS

When a founder receives restricted stock subject to vesting:
- **Without ss83(b)**: Taxable event occurs at each vesting date (ordinary income on FMV minus amount paid). QSBS 5-year clock starts at each vesting date — different tranches have different holding periods.
- **With ss83(b)**: Taxable event occurs at grant date (ordinary income on FMV at grant minus amount paid, which is typically negligible for founders). QSBS 5-year clock starts at GRANT date for ALL shares.

### Worked Example

Founder receives 1,000,000 shares at $0.001/share, subject to 4-year vesting:
- FMV at grant: $0.001/share → $1,000 total FMV
- ss83(b) election: pay ordinary income tax on $1,000 (negligible)
- 5-year QSBS clock starts Day 1 for ALL 1,000,000 shares
- Company is acquired in Year 6 at $20/share = $20M
- Entire $20M is QSBS gain (5+ year holding period for all shares)
- ss1202 exclusion: $15M excluded for post-OBBBA stock ($10M for pre-OBBBA), plus 10x basis exclusion if basis supports it

**Without ss83(b)**: Shares vesting in Year 4 would not reach the 5-year mark until Year 9. At the Year 6 acquisition, those shares (25% of total) would NOT qualify for ss1202.

### The 30-Day Deadline

The ss83(b) election must be filed with the IRS within 30 days of the stock grant. There are NO extensions, NO exceptions, NO relief for missed elections. File the election even if the tax due is $0.

## STATE TREATMENT

### States That DO NOT Fully Conform

| State | Treatment |
|---|---|
| **California** | Caps the gain exclusion at $10M (below the post-OBBBA federal $15M cap; matched federal only under pre-OBBBA rules) and taxes gain above the exclusion at normal rates (up to 13.3%). CA also has a special rule: the exclusion percentage is 50% (not 100%) for CA purposes unless the QSBS issuer had at least 80% of payroll and assets in CA. (Cal. Rev. & Tax. Code ss18152.5) |
| **Pennsylvania** | Does not conform to ss1202 — QSBS gain is fully taxable at PA's 3.07% flat rate |
| **Mississippi** | Does not conform — full taxation |
| **Alabama** | Does not conform — full taxation |
| **New Jersey** | Conforms to federal treatment |
| **New York** | Conforms to federal treatment |

### California's Trap

Even with the CA exclusion, the remaining gain is taxed. For a founder with $50M in QSBS gain:
- Federal: $10M excluded, $40M taxed at 20% + 3.8% NIIT = $9.52M federal tax
- California (100% exclusion case): $10M excluded, $40M taxed at 13.3% = $5.32M CA tax
- California (50% exclusion case): $25M excluded, $25M taxed at 13.3% = $3.325M CA tax on the above-exclusion portion, PLUS 13.3% on the $25M not excluded = total ~$6.65M

**Planning**: Establish residency in a no-income-tax state (NV, TX, FL, WA, WY, SD, AK, TN, NH) before the QSBS sale. Must be a genuine domicile change — CA aggressively audits departures (FTB Publication 1031, "Guidelines for Determining Resident Status").

## COMMON TRAPS AND PITFALLS

### 1. Exceeding the $50M Threshold

Each funding round adds to gross assets. Track cumulative asset basis meticulously. Once exceeded, stock issued in that round and future rounds is permanently disqualified. Stock from earlier rounds remains qualified.

### 2. Disqualified Businesses

Professional services firms are disqualified. The IRS has provided limited guidance on where the line falls. A technology consulting firm that primarily sells software products may qualify; one that primarily sells consulting hours may not. See PLR 201436001 (engineering firm that developed proprietary software qualified).

### 3. Redemption Rules (ss1202(c)(3))

Significant stock redemptions by the corporation within certain windows can disqualify QSBS:
- **2-year window**: If the corporation redeems stock with aggregate value exceeding 5% of the aggregate value of ALL stock within the 2-year period beginning 1 year before issuance — the newly issued stock may be disqualified
- **4-year window**: If the corporation redeems stock from the taxpayer or related person within the 4-year period beginning 2 years before issuance

This can be triggered by common founder buybacks, stock repurchases, or even treasury stock acquisitions.

### 4. Holding Period Interruption

Converting from C-Corp to S-Corp and back may reset the holding period or disqualify the stock entirely. The corporation must be a C-Corp for "substantially all" of the holding period.

### 5. Stock Options vs Restricted Stock

- **ISO exercise**: Stock received on ISO exercise can be QSBS if all other requirements are met. The holding period starts at exercise (not grant). The $50M test is applied at the time of exercise.
- **NSO exercise**: Same as ISO — stock received on exercise can be QSBS. Holding period starts at exercise.
- **Stock options themselves are NOT QSBS** — only the stock received upon exercise qualifies.

### 6. Preferred Stock

QSBS must be "stock" — preferred stock generally qualifies unless it is treated as ss306 stock (preferred stock received as a non-taxable stock dividend) or has debt-like characteristics that cause it to be reclassified. Standard venture preferred stock with participation rights, liquidation preferences, and anti-dilution provisions generally qualifies.

### 7. Section 1045 + Section 1202 Interaction

If you use ss1045 to roll gain from one QSBS investment into another, the replacement QSBS inherits the holding period. But the exclusion limit ($15M for post-OBBBA stock, $10M for pre-OBBBA) for the replacement QSBS is REDUCED by any gain previously excluded on the original QSBS from the same issuer (ss1202(b)(2)).

## WORKED EXAMPLES

### Example 1: Founder Exit (Pre-OBBBA Stock)

- 2019: Founder incorporates C-Corp, receives 5M shares at $0.001/share. Files ss83(b). Basis: $5,000.
- 2020-2024: Company grows, raises Series A ($10M at $20M pre-money) and Series B ($30M at $100M pre-money). Gross assets after Series B: ~$45M basis (under $50M).
- 2025: Company acquired for $200M. Founder's shares worth $80M.
- Holding period: 6 years (>5 years) -- QUALIFIES
- Gain: $80M - $5K = ~$80M
- ss1202 exclusion (pre-OBBBA stock): Greater of $10M or 10x $5K basis ($50K) = **$10M excluded**
- Taxable gain: $70M at 20% + 3.8% NIIT = **$16,660,000**

### Example 2: Founder Exit (Post-OBBBA Stock)

- 2026: Founder incorporates C-Corp, receives 5M shares at $0.001/share. Files ss83(b). Basis: $5,000.
- 2027-2030: Company grows, raises Series A and B. Gross assets remain under $50M.
- 2031: Company acquired for $200M. Founder's shares worth $80M.
- Holding period: 5 years -- QUALIFIES for 100% exclusion under tiered schedule
- Gain: $80M - $5K = ~$80M
- ss1202 exclusion (post-OBBBA stock): Greater of **$15M** (inflation-adjusted) or 10x $5K basis ($50K) = **$15M excluded**
- Taxable gain: $65M at 20% + 3.8% NIIT = **$15,470,000**
- **$5M more excluded** than under pre-OBBBA rules

### Example 3: Trust Stacking with Post-OBBBA Stock

Founder with post-OBBBA stock gifts QSBS to 4 non-grantor trusts:
- Founder retains shares worth $20M at exit → $15M exclusion
- Trust 1: shares worth $20M → $15M exclusion
- Trust 2: shares worth $20M → $15M exclusion
- Trust 3: shares worth $20M → $15M exclusion
- Trust 4: shares worth $20M → $15M exclusion
- **Total exclusion: $75M** (5 taxpayers x $15M each)
- Total gain: $100M. Taxable gain: $25M x 23.8% = **$5,950,000**
- Without trust stacking: only $15M excluded, $85M taxable = $20,230,000 tax
- **Savings from trust stacking: $14,280,000**

### Example 4: 10x Basis Advantage

- Founder contributes $3M of property to C-Corp for post-OBBBA stock in 2026
- Basis in QSBS: $3M
- Company acquired in 2031 for $40M; founder's shares worth $35M
- ss1202 limit: Greater of $15M or 10x $3M ($30M) = **$30M excluded**
- Taxable gain: $5M x 23.8% = $1,190,000
- Without the 10x basis rule: $20M would be taxable = $4,760,000 tax
- **10x basis benefit: $3,570,000 in tax savings**

## KEY TAKEAWAYS

1. **ss1202 is the most valuable tax provision for startup founders** — 100% exclusion on up to $15M+ of gain (post-OBBBA stock), now permanent with no sunset
2. **OBBBA (July 4, 2025) raised the cap to $15M** (from $10M), inflation-indexed starting 2027, and introduced tiered exclusions at 3/4/5 years for post-OBBBA stock
3. **File the ss83(b) election within 30 days** — starts the holding period clock, locks in low basis
4. **Monitor the $50M gross asset threshold** at every funding round
5. **Convert to C-Corp early** — QSBS clock only runs while entity is a C-Corp
6. **Trust stacking is the key post-OBBBA multiplier** — each non-grantor trust gets its own $15M cap. Three trusts + the founder = $60M+ in exclusion capacity
7. **ss1045 rollover** preserves the holding period for pre-5-year exits (and the OBBBA tiered schedule means even a 3-year exit now gets a 50% exclusion)
8. **California does not fully conform** — plan residency accordingly
9. **Watch redemptions** — buybacks can disqualify newly issued QSBS
10. **This is not aggressive tax planning** — ss1202 is explicit Congressional policy to incentivize small business investment, now made permanent by OBBBA
