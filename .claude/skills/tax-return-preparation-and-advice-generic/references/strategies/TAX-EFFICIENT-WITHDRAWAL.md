# TAX-EFFICIENT WITHDRAWAL — Retirement Distribution Sequencing

## Overview

The conventional wisdom — "withdraw from taxable accounts first, then tax-deferred, then Roth last" — is a dangerous oversimplification that costs retirees hundreds of thousands of dollars over their lifetime. Optimal withdrawal sequencing requires integrating federal tax brackets, Medicare IRMAA surcharges, Social Security taxation, Required Minimum Distributions, Roth conversion windows, and state tax exposure into a holistic plan that minimizes lifetime taxes, not just current-year taxes.

---

## THE CONVENTIONAL WISDOM (AND WHY IT'S WRONG)

### The Standard Advice

1. Taxable accounts first (use up basis, realize capital gains)
2. Tax-deferred accounts second (Traditional IRA, 401(k), 403(b))
3. Roth accounts last (tax-free growth as long as possible)

### Why This Fails

This approach ignores:
- **Roth conversion opportunities** in low-income early retirement years (before SS and RMDs kick in)
- **RMD time bomb**: Large tax-deferred balances force massive RMDs at 73+, pushing retirees into high brackets
- **IRMAA surcharges**: Medicare Part B/D premiums jump at specific MAGI thresholds — a cliff effect
- **Social Security taxation**: The "tax torpedo" where an extra $1 of income can cause $0.85 of SS to become taxable
- **Capital gains brackets**: In some years, the 0% LTCG bracket can absorb significant gains with zero tax
- **ACA subsidies**: Pre-Medicare retirees may lose thousands in ACA premium tax credits by withdrawing from the wrong account

---

## THE OPTIMAL APPROACH: "FILL THE BRACKET"

### Core Philosophy

Instead of sequencing by account type, analyze each year's tax situation and fill the lowest brackets with the most tax-efficient source:

1. **Identify the target bracket**: What is the highest marginal rate you're willing to pay this year?
2. **Fill with the best source**: Use whichever account type produces the best after-tax result at that bracket level
3. **Consider multi-year impact**: A $10K Roth conversion at 12% now saves $3,700 if the same $10K would have been withdrawn at 37% later

### Bracket-Filling Strategy (2025 MFJ Rates)

| Taxable Income Band | Rate | Strategy |
|---|---|---|
| $0 - $23,850 | 10% | Fill with Roth conversions or Traditional IRA distributions — this income is nearly free |
| $23,851 - $96,950 | 12% | Aggressive Roth conversion zone — convert as much as possible at 12% |
| $96,951 - $206,700 | 22% | Continue Roth conversions if future brackets expected higher; otherwise, mix |
| $206,701 - $394,600 | 24% | Evaluate IRMAA thresholds before crossing |
| Above $394,600 | 32%+ | Only cross if RMDs or other mandatory income force it |

## MEDICARE IRMAA THRESHOLDS — THE HIDDEN TAX CLIFF

### What Is IRMAA?

Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amounts (IRMAA) are Medicare Part B and Part D premium surcharges based on Modified Adjusted Gross Income (MAGI) from 2 years prior.

### 2025 IRMAA Brackets (Based on 2023 MAGI)

| MAGI (Single) | MAGI (MFJ) | Monthly Part B Surcharge | Monthly Part D Surcharge | Annual Total Surcharge |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| <= $103,000 | <= $206,000 | $0 | $0 | $0 |
| $103,001 - $129,000 | $206,001 - $258,000 | $70.90 | $13.70 | **$2,030** per person |
| $129,001 - $161,000 | $258,001 - $322,000 | $177.00 | $35.50 | **$5,100** per person |
| $161,001 - $193,000 | $322,001 - $386,000 | $283.20 | $57.30 | **$8,165** per person |
| $193,001 - $500,000 | $386,001 - $750,000 | $389.30 | $79.00 | **$11,219** per person |
| > $500,000 | > $750,000 | $419.30 | $85.80 | **$12,122** per person |

### The 2-Year Lookback

IRMAA is based on the tax return from 2 years prior. This means:
- 2025 IRMAA is based on 2023 MAGI
- A large Roth conversion in 2023 triggers higher premiums in 2025
- Planning must account for this 2-year lag

### IRMAA as a Marginal Tax

Crossing from $206,000 to $206,001 (MFJ) triggers $2,030/person/year in surcharges. For a married couple, that is $4,060 in additional cost from $1 of extra income — an infinite marginal rate at the cliff.

**Planning**: Manage MAGI to stay BELOW the next IRMAA threshold. If you are at $204,000 MAGI, do NOT do a $5,000 Roth conversion — the $4,060 IRMAA surcharge exceeds the tax benefit. Instead, convert only up to $206,000.

### Life-Changing Event Exception

If MAGI was high due to a one-time event (retirement, divorce, death of spouse, work reduction), file SSA-44 (Medicare Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount Life-Changing Event) to request a recalculation using current-year income.

## SOCIAL SECURITY TAXATION INTERACTION

### The Provisional Income Formula

Provisional Income = AGI + tax-exempt interest + 50% of Social Security benefits

| Provisional Income (Single) | Provisional Income (MFJ) | % of SS Benefits Taxable |
|---|---|---|
| < $25,000 | < $32,000 | 0% |
| $25,000 - $34,000 | $32,000 - $44,000 | Up to 50% |
| > $34,000 | > $44,000 | Up to 85% |

### The "Tax Torpedo" Zone

Between 50% and 85% taxation, an extra $1 of income causes $0.50 or $0.85 of Social Security to become newly taxable. Combined with the regular marginal rate:

**Effective marginal rate in the torpedo zone**:
- At 12% bracket: 12% on the $1 + 12% on the $0.85 of newly taxable SS = 12% + 10.2% = **22.2% effective rate**
- At 22% bracket: 22% + 18.7% = **40.7% effective rate**
- At 24% bracket: 24% + 20.4% = **44.4% effective rate**

This means a retiree in the "12% bracket" actually faces a 22.2% marginal rate in the torpedo zone — and a retiree in the "22% bracket" faces 40.7%.

### Strategy: Manage Provisional Income

- **Roth withdrawals** do NOT count in provisional income — use Roth to supplement income without triggering SS taxation
- **Tax-exempt bond interest** DOES count in provisional income (a trap for retirees holding munis)
- **Roth conversions before claiming SS**: Convert Traditional to Roth in the years between retirement and SS claiming (age 62-70) when provisional income is low

## ROTH CONVERSION LADDER

### The Opportunity Window

The years between retirement and the start of Social Security benefits AND Required Minimum Distributions are the golden window for Roth conversions:

**Typical timeline**:
- Age 60-62: Retire. Income drops to near zero.
- Age 62-70: Low-income years. Convert Traditional IRA to Roth at 10%/12% bracket.
- Age 67: Social Security FRA (for those born 1960+)
- Age 70: Social Security delayed credits maximize
- Age 73: RMDs begin (under SECURE 2.0, age 75 for those born after 1960)

### How It Works

1. Retire at 60 with $2M Traditional IRA and $500K taxable accounts
2. Years 60-72: Live off taxable accounts and partial Roth conversions
3. Each year, convert ~$96,950 (top of 12% bracket for MFJ) from Traditional to Roth
4. Over 13 years: Convert ~$1.26M at 12% rate (tax cost: ~$151K)
5. At 73: Remaining Traditional IRA RMDs are much smaller
6. Without conversions: $2M growing at 6% for 13 years = $4.27M, RMD at 73 = ~$162K — forced into 24%+ bracket

### Conversion Tax Math

**Without Roth conversions** (assume 6% growth, MFJ):
- Age 73: Traditional IRA = ~$4.27M
- RMD at 73 (divisor 26.5): $161,000/year, growing each year
- Tax at 24%+: $38,640+/year

**With Roth conversions** ($96,950/year for 13 years):
- Conversion tax at 12%: $11,634/year x 13 = $151,242 total conversion tax
- Age 73: Traditional IRA reduced to ~$2.1M (conversions removed $1.26M)
- Roth IRA at 73: ~$2.0M (conversions + growth, tax-free)
- RMD at 73: $79,245/year — stays in the 12% bracket
- Annual tax savings: $14,000+/year for 20+ years
- **Net lifetime savings: $150,000-$300,000+**

## REQUIRED MINIMUM DISTRIBUTION STRATEGIES

### RMD Basics (SECURE 2.0)

| Birth Year | RMD Start Age |
|---|---|
| Before 1951 | 72 (already started) |
| 1951-1959 | 73 |
| 1960 or later | 75 |

### Aggregate RMDs

For TRADITIONAL IRAs: the RMD is calculated separately for each account, but can be taken from ANY Traditional IRA (aggregate method). This allows strategic selection of which account to liquidate.

For EMPLOYER PLANS (401(k), 403(b)): RMDs must be taken from EACH plan separately — no aggregation across plans (exception: 403(b) plans can be aggregated with other 403(b) plans).

### Qualified Charitable Distribution (QCD) — ss408(d)(8)

After age 70.5, a taxpayer can direct up to $108,000/year (2025, indexed) from an IRA directly to a qualified charity:
- Counts toward RMD satisfaction
- Excluded from gross income (not even included then deducted — it's as if it never existed)
- Reduces AGI, which reduces SS taxation, IRMAA exposure, and NIIT
- SECURE 2.0 also allows a one-time $54,000 QCD to a CRT or charitable gift annuity (indexed)

**Why QCD is superior to a charitable deduction**: The QCD reduces AGI (below-the-line). A regular charitable deduction only reduces taxable income (and only if itemizing). For retirees taking the standard deduction, QCD provides a benefit that a regular donation does not.

### Roth Conversion to Reduce Future RMDs

Every dollar converted from Traditional to Roth BEFORE RMDs begin is a dollar that will never generate an RMD. Since Roth IRAs have NO RMDs during the owner's lifetime (SECURE 2.0 also eliminated RMDs for Roth 401(k)s starting 2024), converting reduces the RMD base permanently.

## 72(t) / SEPP — SUBSTANTIALLY EQUAL PERIODIC PAYMENTS

### Purpose

Section 72(t)(2)(A)(iv) provides an exception to the 10% early withdrawal penalty for distributions that are part of a series of "substantially equal periodic payments" (SEPP) made at least annually for the longer of 5 years or until the taxpayer reaches age 59.5.

### The Three Calculation Methods

**1. Required Minimum Distribution Method**
- Annual payment = Account balance / Life expectancy factor (single life table)
- Payment recalculated annually
- Produces the LOWEST payment
- Most flexible (payment varies with account balance)

**2. Fixed Amortization Method**
- Annual payment = Account balance amortized over life expectancy at a reasonable interest rate
- Payment fixed at inception (not recalculated)
- Produces a MODERATE payment
- Interest rate: any rate up to 120% of the federal mid-term rate

**3. Fixed Annuitization Method**
- Annual payment = Account balance / Annuity factor (from mortality table and reasonable interest rate)
- Payment fixed at inception
- Produces the HIGHEST payment
- Uses the same interest rate limitation

### Critical Rules

- **Modification penalty**: If the SEPP series is modified (changed, stopped, or additional contributions made) before the later of 5 years or age 59.5, the 10% penalty is retroactively applied to ALL prior distributions, plus interest. This is devastating — it's as if the SEPP never existed.
- **One-time switch**: Rev. Rul. 2002-62 allows a one-time irrevocable switch from the amortization or annuitization method to the RMD method (to reduce payments if the account has declined).
- **Account isolation**: Segregate the SEPP IRA from other IRAs. Any contribution to or distribution from the SEPP IRA outside the scheduled payments is a modification.

### Worked Example

- Age 52, IRA balance: $1,000,000
- Want penalty-free access for 8 years (until age 59.5, which exceeds the 5-year minimum)
- Fixed amortization, life expectancy 33.3 years, 5.0% interest rate
- Annual SEPP: ~$58,000
- Income tax on $58K at 22%: $12,760/year
- Avoids 10% penalty: $5,800/year savings
- Over 8 years: $46,400 in penalties avoided

## NET UNREALIZED APPRECIATION (NUA) — ss402(e)(4)

### The Strategy

When employer stock is held inside a 401(k), NUA allows the participant to:
1. Take a lump-sum distribution of the ENTIRE account balance (ALL in the same tax year)
2. Roll non-stock assets to an IRA
3. Take the employer stock IN KIND (transfer to a taxable brokerage account)
4. Pay ORDINARY income tax only on the COST BASIS of the stock (not FMV)
5. ALL appreciation (the NUA) is taxed at LTCG rates when the stock is eventually sold

### Worked Example

- 401(k) balance: $1,500,000
  - $500,000 in company stock (cost basis: $100,000, FMV: $500,000, NUA: $400,000)
  - $1,000,000 in mutual funds
- **NUA approach**:
  - Roll $1M mutual funds to IRA (no current tax)
  - Transfer $500K of stock to brokerage account
  - Pay ordinary tax on cost basis: $100,000 x 37% = $37,000
  - NUA of $400K is taxed at LTCG rate when sold: $400,000 x 20% = $80,000
  - Total tax on stock: $117,000
- **Without NUA (roll everything to IRA)**:
  - $500K stock distributed from IRA = ordinary income
  - Tax: $500,000 x 37% = $185,000
- **NUA savings: $68,000**

### NUA Requirements

1. Must be a **lump-sum distribution** — entire balance of ALL plans of the same type (all 401(k)s) in a single tax year
2. Must be triggered by one of: separation from service, reaching age 59.5, disability, or death
3. Stock must be EMPLOYER stock (not mutual funds)
4. Cost basis is determined by average cost of all shares

### NUA + 10-Year Forward Averaging (for Pre-1936 Births)

For participants born before January 2, 1936 (now age 89+), special 10-year forward averaging on Form 4972 may further reduce the tax on the cost basis portion. Increasingly rare but still available.

## QUALIFIED LONGEVITY ANNUITY CONTRACT (QLAC)

### Mechanics (Reg. ss1.401(a)(9)-6, Q&A-17)

A QLAC is a deferred income annuity purchased within an IRA or employer plan that:
- Begins payments no later than age 85
- Excludes up to $200,000 (SECURE 2.0 increased from $145,000) from the RMD calculation
- Provides guaranteed income in late retirement (longevity insurance)

### Tax Benefits

- The QLAC amount is NOT included in the IRA balance for RMD calculation purposes
- This effectively reduces RMDs from age 73 until the QLAC payments begin (up to age 85)
- For a $2M IRA with $200K in a QLAC: RMD base is $1.8M instead of $2M — saving ~$7,547/year in RMDs (at the age 73 divisor of 26.5)

### When QLACs Make Sense

- Retiree with large Traditional IRA who doesn't need all the RMD income
- Concerned about longevity risk (living past 90)
- Willing to trade liquidity for guaranteed income
- Want to reduce IRMAA exposure by lowering RMD-driven MAGI

## PUTTING IT ALL TOGETHER — WITHDRAWAL SEQUENCING FRAMEWORK

### Phase 1: Early Retirement (55-62)

- **Primary income**: Taxable accounts (harvest capital gains in 0% bracket if possible)
- **Supplemental**: 72(t)/SEPP if needed (penalty-free early access from IRA)
- **Roth conversions**: Aggressive — fill up to top of 22% bracket (or even 24% if large Traditional balance)
- **HSA**: Do NOT withdraw — let it grow tax-free for medical expenses in later years
- **IRMAA**: Not yet applicable (pre-Medicare)
- **ACA**: Keep MAGI low enough to preserve premium tax credits; for 2025 there is no
  automatic 400% FPL cliff, but Form 8962 still makes subsidy size highly income-sensitive

### Phase 2: Social Security Bridge (62-70)

- **Delay SS if possible**: Each year of delay = 8% increase (after FRA). Guaranteed real return.
- **Bridge with taxable + Roth conversions**: Use taxable account drawdowns and continue Roth conversions
- **Monitor provisional income**: Once SS starts, Roth withdrawals supplement without increasing provisional income
- **Medicare starts at 65**: IRMAA planning begins — manage MAGI around thresholds

### Phase 3: RMD Years (73/75+)

- **RMDs are mandatory**: Take from Traditional IRA/401(k) as required
- **QCD for charitable giving**: Direct $105K/year from IRA to charity (satisfies RMD, reduces AGI)
- **Roth for excess needs**: Roth withdrawals do not increase MAGI, SS taxation, or IRMAA
- **Taxable accounts**: Use for large one-time expenses to avoid MAGI spikes
- **NUA**: If applicable, should have been executed earlier

### Phase 4: Late Retirement (85+)

- **QLAC payments begin**: Guaranteed income stream
- **Roth IRA**: Primary legacy asset (no RMDs, passes tax-free to heirs)
- **Stepped-up basis**: Taxable account assets pass to heirs with stepped-up basis at death
- **Traditional IRA**: Least desirable to pass to heirs (heirs must withdraw within 10 years under SECURE Act, fully taxable as ordinary income)

## KEY TAKEAWAYS

1. **The conventional withdrawal order is a costly oversimplification** — optimize annually, not by rule of thumb
2. **Roth conversions in low-income years** (between retirement and SS/RMDs) are the highest-ROI tax move available
3. **IRMAA thresholds are cliff taxes** — $1 over the line costs $2,000-$4,000/year per person
4. **The SS tax torpedo** makes the effective marginal rate in the 12% bracket as high as 22.2%
5. **QCDs are strictly superior** to regular charitable donations for retirees over 70.5
6. **NUA can save 17%+ on employer stock** vs rolling to IRA — but requires a lump-sum distribution
7. **72(t)/SEPP provides penalty-free early access** but the modification penalty is severe — plan carefully
8. **Coordinate across all accounts and all years** — the goal is minimum LIFETIME tax, not minimum THIS YEAR tax
9. **HSAs are the best retirement account** — triple tax benefit, no RMDs, inheritable (spousal step-in)
10. **QLACs reduce RMDs by up to $200K** and provide longevity insurance
