# Health Savings and Medical Tax Strategies

## Overview

Health-related tax provisions span multiple areas: HSAs, FSAs, HRAs, the self-employed health insurance deduction, the ACA Premium Tax Credit, and the medical expense itemized deduction. Proper coordination of these provisions can save thousands in taxes while optimizing healthcare spending.

All figures are for the 2025 tax year.

---

## Health Savings Account (HSA)

### Eligibility Requirements

- Must be enrolled in a qualifying **High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP)**
- 2025 HDHP minimum deductible: $1,650 (self-only) / $3,300 (family)
- 2025 HDHP maximum out-of-pocket: $8,300 (self-only) / $16,600 (family)
- Cannot be enrolled in Medicare (any part)
- Cannot be claimed as a dependent on another person's return
- Cannot have other non-HDHP health coverage (exceptions: dental, vision, specific disease, accident, disability, long-term care insurance, workers' comp)

### 2025 Contribution Limits

| Coverage Type | Under Age 55 | Age 55+ (Catch-Up) |
|---|---|---|
| Self-only | $4,300 | $5,300 |
| Family | $8,550 | $9,550* |

*The $1,000 catch-up is per individual, not per family. If both spouses are 55+, each opens their own HSA to get $1,000 catch-up each (total $10,550 for the family).

### Triple Tax Benefit

1. **Tax-deductible contributions:** Above-the-line deduction (reduces AGI). If contributed through employer payroll, also avoids FICA.
2. **Tax-free growth:** Investment gains within the HSA are not taxed
3. **Tax-free withdrawals:** For qualified medical expenses at any time, at any age

### HSA as a Retirement Vehicle

**Strategy:** Pay current medical expenses out-of-pocket (if affordable) and let the HSA balance grow and compound tax-free.

**Key features supporting retirement use:**
- No Required Minimum Distributions (unlike Traditional IRA/401(k))
- After age 65, withdrawals for ANY purpose are taxed as ordinary income (like a Traditional IRA) but with no 10% penalty
- Withdrawals for medical expenses remain tax-free at any age
- Keep receipts for ALL medical expenses paid out-of-pocket -- you can reimburse yourself from the HSA at any future date (no time limit)

### HSA Investment Strategy

- Most HSA providers offer investment options once the balance exceeds a threshold (typically $1,000-$2,000)
- Invest in low-cost index funds for long-term growth
- Keep a cash reserve for near-term medical expenses (e.g., $2,000-$3,000 in cash, invest the rest)
- Providers with good investment options: Fidelity (no minimum to invest, no fees), Lively, HSA Bank

### Pro-Rated Contributions

If you are HSA-eligible for only part of the year, contributions are pro-rated by the number of months of eligibility (divided by 12). Exception: the "last-month rule" allows a full-year contribution if you are eligible on December 1, but you must remain eligible through December 31 of the following year or face taxes and penalties on the excess.

### Deadline

HSA contributions for a tax year can be made up to **April 15** of the following year (no extensions). Same deadline as IRA contributions.

## Flexible Spending Accounts (FSA)

### Healthcare FSA

- **2025 limit: $3,300** per employer plan
- Employee-funded (pre-tax payroll deduction)
- Reduces Federal income tax, FICA, and state income tax (in most states)
- Qualified expenses: medical, dental, vision expenses not covered by insurance; OTC medications (since 2020); menstrual care products
- **Use-it-or-lose-it:** Unused funds are forfeited, UNLESS the employer offers:
  - Grace period: 2.5 months into the following year to use remaining funds, OR
  - Carryover: up to $640 (2025) can carry to the next year
  - Employer chooses one or neither; cannot offer both
- **Cannot have both an HSA and a general-purpose Healthcare FSA** (a Limited Purpose FSA for dental/vision only is compatible with an HSA)

### Dependent Care FSA (DCFSA)

- **2025 limit: $5,000** per household ($2,500 MFS)
- For care of children under 13 or disabled dependents to enable the taxpayer to work
- Qualified expenses: daycare, preschool, after-school care, summer day camp, nanny/au pair
- NOT qualified: overnight camp, kindergarten tuition (educational component), babysitting for non-work purposes
- Reduces Federal income tax AND FICA (unlike the Child and Dependent Care Credit, which does not reduce FICA)
- Use-it-or-lose-it (no carryover provision for DCFSA)
- **Interaction with Child and Dependent Care Credit:** Expenses used for DCFSA cannot also be used for the credit. At higher incomes, the DCFSA exclusion is generally more valuable.

## HRA / ICHRA / QSEHRA

### Health Reimbursement Arrangement (HRA)

- Employer-funded account that reimburses employees for medical expenses
- Employer determines the amount and which expenses qualify
- Reimbursements are tax-free to the employee, deductible by the employer
- Multiple types: traditional HRA, ICHRA, QSEHRA, Excepted Benefit HRA

### ICHRA (Individual Coverage HRA)

- Employer funds an HRA that reimburses employees for individual health insurance premiums
- No minimum or maximum contribution limit (employer sets amount)
- Available to businesses of any size
- Employees must have individual health insurance coverage (marketplace or off-marketplace)
- Can be offered alongside a group plan to different employee classes
- Employees who are offered an ICHRA that is considered "affordable" are NOT eligible for ACA Premium Tax Credit

### QSEHRA (Qualified Small Employer HRA)

- For employers with **fewer than 50 employees** that do NOT offer a group health plan
- 2025 limits: **$6,350** (self-only) / **$12,800** (family)
- Employee must have minimum essential coverage
- Reimbursements are tax-free (up to the extent of qualified expenses)
- Reduces the employee's Premium Tax Credit dollar-for-dollar

### S-Corp Shareholder Restrictions

**Critical:** Shareholders owning more than 2% of an S-Corporation are treated as self-employed for fringe benefit purposes. This means:
- They CANNOT participate in an HRA, FSA, or other employer-provided health benefit on a tax-free basis
- Health insurance premiums paid by the S-Corp for >2% shareholders must be:
  1. Included on the shareholder's W-2 (Box 1, but NOT in Boxes 3/5 -- not subject to FICA)
  2. Deducted by the shareholder as the self-employed health insurance deduction on Form 1040
- The net tax effect is similar to self-employed status: deduction reduces income tax but NOT SE tax

## Self-Employed Health Insurance Deduction (Section 162(l))

### What It Is

Self-employed individuals can deduct **100%** of health insurance premiums for themselves, their spouse, and dependents as an above-the-line deduction.

### Requirements

- Must have net self-employment income (deduction cannot exceed net SE income from the business under which the plan is established)
- Cannot be eligible to participate in a subsidized employer health plan (own or spouse's)
- Applies to: medical, dental, vision, and qualified long-term care insurance premiums

### What It Covers

- Health insurance premiums (marketplace, individual, COBRA)
- Dental insurance premiums
- Vision insurance premiums
- Qualified long-term care insurance premiums (age-based limits apply)
- Medicare premiums (Parts A, B, C, D, and Medigap) -- if the taxpayer is self-employed

### What It Does NOT Do

- Does NOT reduce self-employment tax (it is an income tax deduction only)
- Cannot be combined with the Premium Tax Credit for the same months of coverage
- Cannot exceed net self-employment income from the business

### Interaction with Premium Tax Credit

If the taxpayer receives ACA Premium Tax Credit AND claims the self-employed health insurance deduction, an iterative calculation is required because:
- The SE health insurance deduction reduces AGI
- Reduced AGI increases the Premium Tax Credit
- Increased PTC reduces the deductible premium amount
- Which changes the deduction... (circular)

Tax software handles this iterative calculation. The result is a balanced figure for both the deduction and the credit.

## ACA Premium Tax Credit (Form 8962)

### What It Is

A refundable tax credit to help pay for health insurance purchased through the Health Insurance Marketplace (exchange).

### Eligibility

- Household income generally starts at 100% of the Federal Poverty Level (FPL), but for 2025
  taxpayers above 400% of FPL may still qualify based on the benchmark-premium percentage cap
  on Form 8962
  - 2025 FPL guidelines: ~$15,060 for individual; ~$31,000 for family of 4 (updated annually)
  - 400% FPL: ~$60,240 individual; ~$124,000 family of 4
- Cannot be eligible for employer-sponsored coverage that is affordable and provides minimum value
- Cannot be eligible for Medicare, Medicaid, or CHIP
- Must file a tax return (even if not otherwise required)

### 2025 PTC Rule

For 2025 there is **no automatic 400% FPL subsidy cliff**. Taxpayers above 400% of FPL can
still receive a Premium Tax Credit if the benchmark plan exceeds the allowed percentage of
household income under Form 8962. MAGI still matters materially, but the planning target is
the actual Form 8962 premium cap, not a hard 400% cutoff.

### Advance Payments vs. Year-End Reconciliation

- Most taxpayers elect advance payment of the PTC (paid directly to the insurance company monthly)
- At year-end, reconcile on **Form 8962**: compare advance payments to the actual PTC based on actual income
- If actual income is higher than estimated: repay excess advance PTC (subject to repayment caps for income under 400% FPL)
- If actual income is lower than estimated: claim additional PTC on the return (refundable)

### Strategies

- **Self-employed taxpayers:** Retirement plan contributions (SEP-IRA, Solo 401(k)) reduce AGI, potentially increasing PTC
- **HSA contributions:** Reduce AGI, potentially increasing PTC
- **Capital gain management:** Realize gains in low-PTC-impact years
- **Roth conversions:** Increase AGI, may reduce or eliminate PTC -- time carefully

## COBRA and Marketplace Transition

### COBRA

- Allows continuation of employer group health coverage for 18-36 months after job loss or qualifying event
- Very expensive (100% of premium + 2% admin fee; no employer subsidy)
- Provides coverage continuity while transitioning

### Marketplace Enrollment

- Loss of employer coverage is a **qualifying life event** allowing Special Enrollment Period (60 days)
- Compare COBRA cost vs. marketplace plan + PTC
- If income will be lower (job loss, sabbatical, early retirement), marketplace + PTC may be dramatically cheaper
- COBRA can be elected retroactively (within 60 days), providing a safety net while marketplace coverage is arranged

## Medical Expense Deduction (Schedule A)

### 7.5% AGI Floor

Medical and dental expenses exceeding **7.5% of AGI** can be deducted as an itemized deduction on Schedule A.

### Qualified Medical Expenses

- Doctor visits, hospital bills, surgery, prescription drugs
- Dental work (fillings, crowns, orthodontics, implants)
- Vision care (exams, glasses, contacts, LASIK)
- Mental health care (therapy, psychiatry)
- Medical equipment (wheelchair, hearing aids, crutches)
- Transportation to medical care (mileage rate: $0.21/mile for 2025, or actual costs)
- Long-term care expenses and insurance premiums (age-based limits)
- Insurance premiums (if not deducted elsewhere -- not if using SE health insurance deduction or PTC)
- Home modifications for medical purposes (ramps, grab bars, widened doorways)

### NOT Qualified

- Cosmetic surgery (unless medically necessary for congenital abnormality, injury, or disease)
- Gym memberships (unless prescribed by a doctor for a specific condition)
- Over-the-counter medications (these ARE qualified for HSA/FSA, but NOT for the itemized deduction unless prescribed)
- Health insurance premiums already deducted as self-employed health insurance
- Expenses reimbursed by insurance

### When the Medical Expense Deduction Matters

- **Major medical event year:** Surgery, major dental work, or ongoing treatment that pushes expenses above 7.5% of AGI
- **Low-income year:** Lower AGI means lower 7.5% threshold
- **Planning opportunity:** Bunch elective medical expenses into a single year to exceed the threshold
  - Schedule elective procedures, dental work, vision correction in the same calendar year
  - Pay December bills before January 1

### Example

```
AGI: $100,000
7.5% threshold: $7,500
Total medical expenses: $15,000
Deductible amount: $15,000 - $7,500 = $7,500

AGI: $200,000
7.5% threshold: $15,000
Total medical expenses: $15,000
Deductible amount: $0 (does not exceed threshold)
```

## Summary: Choosing the Right Health Tax Strategy

| Situation | Best Strategy |
|---|---|
| Self-employed, HDHP eligible | HSA + Self-employed health insurance deduction |
| Self-employed, not HDHP | Self-employed health insurance deduction |
| W-2 employee, HDHP | HSA (ideally through payroll for FICA savings) |
| W-2 employee, not HDHP | Healthcare FSA ($3,300) |
| Small employer, no group plan | QSEHRA for employees |
| Any employer | ICHRA as alternative to group plan |
| Low income, marketplace | Premium Tax Credit (Form 8962) |
| >2% S-Corp shareholder | W-2 inclusion + SE health insurance deduction |
| Major medical expense year | Medical expense deduction (Schedule A) if > 7.5% AGI |
| Children in daycare | Dependent Care FSA ($5,000) |

## Common Mistakes

1. **Contributing to an HSA without HDHP coverage** -- excess contribution penalty (6% per year until corrected)
2. **Having both a general Healthcare FSA and an HSA** -- disqualifies HSA contributions (use Limited Purpose FSA instead)
3. **Not investing HSA funds** -- leaving money in a low-interest cash account instead of investing for growth
4. **Forgetting the catch-up contribution** -- each spouse age 55+ needs their own HSA for the extra $1,000
5. **Not coordinating SE health insurance deduction with PTC** -- can result in incorrect calculations
6. **Paying >2% S-Corp shareholder health insurance** without including on W-2
7. **Missing the DCFSA enrollment window** -- can only enroll during open enrollment or with a qualifying life event
8. **Bunching medical expenses but not exceeding the 7.5% floor** -- plan carefully before scheduling elective procedures
9. **Overlooking Medicare Part B/D premiums** as deductible for self-employed individuals
10. **Not keeping receipts for HSA reimbursement** -- you can reimburse years later, but only with documentation
