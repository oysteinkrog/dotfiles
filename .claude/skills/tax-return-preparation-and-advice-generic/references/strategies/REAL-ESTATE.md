# Real Estate Tax Strategies

## Overview

Real estate offers more tax benefits than any other asset class: depreciation deductions on appreciating property, passive loss sheltering, tax-deferred exchanges, and potentially tax-free gains on a primary residence. Proper structuring can save tens of thousands of dollars annually.

All figures are for the 2025 tax year including OBBBA changes.

---

## Cost Segregation Studies

### What It Is

A cost segregation study reclassifies components of a building from the default depreciation life (27.5 years residential / 39 years commercial) to shorter-lived asset classes:

| Asset Class | Depreciation Life | Examples |
|---|---|---|
| Personal property (5-year) | 5 years | Appliances, carpeting, decorative lighting, window treatments |
| Personal property (7-year) | 7 years | Office furniture, specialty fixtures |
| Land improvements (15-year) | 15 years | Landscaping, parking areas, fencing, sidewalks, drainage |
| Building (27.5 or 39 year) | 27.5 or 39 years | Structural components, walls, roof, HVAC, plumbing, electrical |

### Why It Matters

Shorter depreciation lives mean larger deductions in early years. Combined with bonus depreciation, components classified as 5, 7, or 15-year property can be fully deducted in the year of acquisition.

### Bonus Depreciation Interaction

Under OBBBA (effective for property placed in service after January 19, 2025):

- **100% bonus depreciation** is restored for qualifying property
- This applies to 5-year, 7-year, and 15-year property identified in a cost segregation study
- A $500K rental property might have $100K-$150K reclassified to shorter-lived assets
- With 100% bonus depreciation, that entire amount is deductible in Year 1

**Pre-OBBBA timeline (for reference):**
- 2023: 80% bonus depreciation
- 2024: 60% bonus depreciation
- Jan 1 - Jan 19, 2025: 40% bonus depreciation
- After Jan 19, 2025: 100% bonus depreciation (OBBBA)

### Cost vs. ROI

| Property Value | Typical Study Cost | Typical Year 1 Tax Benefit |
|---|---|---|
| $200K - $500K | $3,000 - $5,000 | $15,000 - $50,000 |
| $500K - $1M | $5,000 - $8,000 | $40,000 - $100,000+ |
| $1M+ | $8,000 - $15,000 | $80,000 - $200,000+ |

Tax benefit depends on marginal tax rate and ability to use passive losses.

### Providers

- Engineering firms specializing in cost segregation (CSSI, McGuire Sponsel, Capstan)
- Cost: may be deductible as a business expense in the year incurred
- Some firms offer "look-back" studies for properties placed in service in prior years (filed as Form 3115 change in accounting method)

### Cost Segregation Look-Back Studies

#### What It Is

A look-back study allows a taxpayer to retroactively apply cost segregation to a property placed in service in a prior year. Instead of amending prior returns, the catch-up depreciation is claimed as a Section 481(a) adjustment on the current year's return via Form 3115 (Application for Change in Accounting Method).

#### How It Works

1. **Engage a cost segregation firm** to perform the study on the existing property
2. **File Form 3115** with the current year's tax return, requesting a change from the general depreciation system to the reclassified asset lives
3. **Compute the Section 481(a) adjustment:** This is the difference between total depreciation that would have been claimed under the new method from the placed-in-service date through the beginning of the year of change, minus the depreciation actually claimed
4. **Deduct the entire catch-up amount** in the current tax year as a Section 481(a) negative adjustment (favorable to the taxpayer)

#### Worked Example

```
Property: Commercial building placed in service in 2018
Original cost: $1,000,000 (excluding land)
All depreciated over 39 years straight-line

Cost segregation study identifies:
  5-year property: $120,000
  15-year property: $80,000
  Remaining 39-year: $800,000

Depreciation actually claimed (2018-2024, 7 years):
  $1,000,000 / 39 = $25,641/year x 7 = $179,487

Depreciation that SHOULD have been claimed:
  5-year property: $120,000 (fully depreciated by 2023, or 100% bonus if OBBBA applies retroactively -- it does NOT for this asset; use MACRS)
  Actually for 2018 placed-in-service: 100% bonus was available in 2018 for 5-year and 15-year property
  5-year: $120,000 (Year 1 under 100% bonus)
  15-year: $80,000 (Year 1 under 100% bonus)
  39-year: $800,000 / 39 = $20,513/year x 7 = $143,590
  Total should-have-been: $120,000 + $80,000 + $143,590 = $343,590

Section 481(a) adjustment: $343,590 - $179,487 = $164,103 catch-up deduction

At 37% marginal rate: $164,103 x 0.37 = $60,718 tax savings IN THE CURRENT YEAR
```

**Key benefits:**
- No amended returns required
- Entire catch-up deduction taken in one year
- Automatic consent (Form 3115 designated change number 7) -- no IRS approval needed
- No statute of limitations issue (the change is prospective from an accounting method perspective)

### Caution

- Cost segregation is most beneficial for properties held long-term
- If the property is sold within a few years, depreciation recapture (ordinary income rates for personal property, 25% for real property) can offset the benefit
- The study must be performed by a qualified engineer or professional

## Qualified Improvement Property (QIP)

### Definition

Qualified Improvement Property (QIP) is any improvement to an interior portion of a nonresidential building placed in service after the building was placed in service. QIP does NOT include improvements attributable to:
- Enlargement of the building
- Any elevator or escalator
- The building's internal structural framework

### Depreciation Treatment

- **MACRS life:** 15 years (corrected by CARES Act from the erroneous 39-year life created by TCJA drafting error)
- **Bonus depreciation:** Eligible for 100% bonus depreciation under OBBBA (for property placed in service after January 19, 2025)
- **ADS life:** 20 years (relevant for real property trades or businesses that elected out of Section 163(j) interest limitation)

### Common Confusion: QIP vs. Structural Components

The distinction between QIP (15-year) and structural components of the building (39-year) is critical and frequently misapplied:

**QIP (15-year, eligible for bonus):**
- Interior walls (non-load-bearing)
- Interior ceilings
- Interior flooring
- Interior lighting
- Interior plumbing fixtures (sinks, toilets within the space)
- Interior HVAC components (ductwork, vents within the tenant space)
- Fire suppression, alarm, and security systems for the interior

**NOT QIP (39-year or other, NOT eligible for QIP treatment):**
- Roof replacements (but may qualify for partial disposition -- see below)
- Exterior improvements (facade, windows, parking lot)
- Building enlargements (adding square footage)
- HVAC systems that serve the entire building (rooftop units)
- Elevators and escalators
- Internal structural framework (load-bearing walls, columns, beams)

**Important:** QIP applies ONLY to nonresidential buildings. Improvements to residential rental property are depreciated over 27.5 years. This is a frequently missed distinction -- a taxpayer improving an apartment building does NOT get QIP treatment.

### Worked Example

```
Taxpayer leases commercial office space and makes $200,000 in improvements:
  New flooring: $40,000 (QIP - 15 year)
  Interior walls and paint: $30,000 (QIP - 15 year)
  Lighting upgrade: $25,000 (QIP - 15 year)
  Kitchen build-out: $35,000 (QIP - 15 year)
  HVAC for suite: $20,000 (QIP - 15 year)
  Bathroom renovation: $15,000 (QIP - 15 year)
  Structural column reinforcement: $20,000 (NOT QIP - 39 year)
  Exterior signage: $15,000 (NOT QIP - 15 year land improvement or 39 year)

Total QIP: $165,000 -> 100% bonus depreciation under OBBBA = $165,000 deduction in Year 1
Non-QIP: $35,000 -> depreciated over 39 years = $897/year

Without QIP classification, the entire $200,000 would be 39-year property.
Year 1 deduction: $200,000/39 = $5,128
With QIP: $165,000 + $897 = $165,897

Difference: $160,769 additional deduction in Year 1
At 37% rate: $59,485 tax savings
```

## Partial Asset Disposition (Reg. Section 1.168(i)-8)

### What It Is

The partial asset disposition election allows a taxpayer to dispose of a structural component of a building (roof, HVAC system, plumbing, electrical, etc.) and claim the remaining undepreciated basis of the OLD component as a loss in the year of disposition, while capitalizing the NEW replacement component and depreciating it over a fresh recovery period.

### Why This Is a Huge Hidden Deduction

Without this election, when a taxpayer replaces a roof on a building, the cost of the new roof is capitalized, but the OLD roof's remaining basis continues to be depreciated alongside the new one. The taxpayer is effectively depreciating two roofs. The partial disposition election allows the taxpayer to write off the old roof's remaining basis immediately.

### Requirements

1. The disposition must be of a portion of a MACRS asset (the building)
2. The taxpayer must make the partial disposition election
3. The election is made by reporting the disposed portion on Form 4797 or on the tax return
4. The election can be made on a timely filed return (including extensions) or on a late-filed Form 3115

### Worked Example

```
Taxpayer owns a commercial building purchased in 2010 for $1,000,000 (building only, excluding land).

Original roof cost allocation (from purchase): $150,000
Depreciation claimed on roof (2010-2025, 15 years of 39-year life):
  $150,000 / 39 = $3,846/year x 15 = $57,692
Remaining basis: $150,000 - $57,692 = $92,308

In 2025, the roof is replaced. New roof cost: $200,000.

WITHOUT partial disposition election:
  New roof: capitalized, depreciated over 39 years ($5,128/year)
  Old roof: continues depreciating at $3,846/year for 24 more years
  Year 1 total deduction: $5,128 + $3,846 = $8,974

WITH partial disposition election:
  Old roof: remaining basis of $92,308 recognized as a LOSS in 2025
  New roof: capitalized, depreciated over 39 years ($5,128/year)
  Year 1 total deduction: $92,308 + $5,128 = $97,436

Benefit of election: $97,436 - $8,974 = $88,462 additional deduction
At 37% rate: $32,731 in tax savings
```

### Common Applications

- **Roof replacement** (most common and highest value)
- **HVAC system replacement** (furnace, compressor, entire system)
- **Plumbing system replacement** (re-piping)
- **Electrical system replacement** (rewiring, panel upgrade)
- **Windows replacement** (commercial buildings)
- **Flooring replacement** (if original flooring was part of building basis)

### How to Determine the Old Component's Basis

If the original cost was not separately tracked:
1. **Use the cost segregation study** (if one was performed)
2. **Use a reasonable allocation method:** Get a contractor's estimate of what the component would cost to replace today, then deflate to the original placed-in-service date using cost indices
3. **Use the replacement cost method:** If the new component costs $200,000 and the building has appreciated 40% since acquisition, the old component's original cost was approximately $200,000 / 1.40 = $142,857

### Filing the Election

- **Current year:** Report the loss on Form 4797 Part II (ordinary gain or loss) by identifying the specific component disposed of
- **Prior years (missed elections):** File Form 3115 to change accounting method, claim the Section 481(a) catch-up adjustment for all prior-year missed dispositions

## Tangible Property Regulations

### De Minimis Safe Harbor

Taxpayers can elect to expense (deduct immediately rather than capitalize) amounts paid for tangible property if the cost per item or per invoice does not exceed:

- **$2,500** per item/invoice for taxpayers WITHOUT applicable financial statements (AFS)
- **$5,000** per item/invoice for taxpayers WITH an applicable financial statement (audited financial statements, filed with SEC, etc.)

**What qualifies:**
- Materials and supplies
- Tangible property acquisitions (appliances, tools, equipment, small improvements)
- Must be for property with an economic useful life of 12 months or less, OR cost does not exceed the applicable threshold

**Election:** Made annually by attaching a statement to the tax return. The election applies to ALL amounts within the threshold -- it is not item-by-item.

**Planning for rental property owners:** Every appliance, fixture, or small component replaced at a rental property that costs $2,500 or less can be expensed immediately. No more capitalizing a $1,200 dishwasher over 5 years.

### Routine Maintenance Safe Harbor

Amounts paid for routine maintenance are deductible (not capitalized) if the taxpayer reasonably expects to perform the activity:
- More than once during the 10-year period beginning when the property was placed in service (for buildings)
- More than once during the class life of the property (for non-building property)

**Examples:**
- Painting (interior and exterior)
- Caulking
- Cleaning/power washing
- Testing and inspecting
- Replacing minor components (filters, light bulbs, small parts)
- Seasonal HVAC maintenance

### Betterment, Restoration, and Adaptation Tests

If an expenditure does NOT qualify for the de minimis or routine maintenance safe harbors, it must be tested against the capitalization criteria:

**Betterment (must capitalize if):**
- Fixes a material condition or defect that existed before acquisition or arose during production
- Is a material addition (enlargement, expansion, extension)
- Is reasonably expected to materially increase the property's productivity, efficiency, strength, quality, or output

**Restoration (must capitalize if):**
- Returns the property to its ordinarily efficient operating condition after deterioration to a state of disrepair
- Rebuilds the property to a like-new condition after the end of its class life
- Replaces a major component or substantial structural part of the property
- Is for damage that the taxpayer properly deducted as a casualty loss

**Adaptation (must capitalize if):**
- Adapts the property to a new or different use inconsistent with the taxpayer's intended ordinary use at the time the property was placed in service

## Real Estate Professional Status (Section 469(c)(7))

### What It Is

Real Estate Professional (REP) status allows a taxpayer to treat rental real estate activities as non-passive, meaning rental losses can offset ordinary income without limitation.

### Requirements (BOTH must be met)

1. **750-hour test:** More than 750 hours of services in real property trades or businesses during the tax year
2. **50% test:** More than 50% of the taxpayer's total personal services (across all businesses) are performed in real property trades or businesses

### Qualifying Activities

- Real property development
- Real property construction
- Real property acquisition
- Real property management
- Real property rental
- Real property brokerage
- Real property operation

### Material Participation (Additional Requirement)

Even with REP status, each rental activity must meet material participation tests UNLESS the taxpayer elects to aggregate all rental activities (election under Reg. 1.469-9(g)):

**Election to aggregate:** File a statement with the tax return in the first year REP status is claimed, electing to treat all rental real estate activities as a single activity. This is generally advisable -- it allows hours across all properties to be combined for material participation.

### Material Participation Tests (Meet ANY One)

1. More than 500 hours of participation during the year
2. Participation constitutes substantially all participation by all individuals
3. More than 100 hours AND not less than any other individual's participation
4. Significant participation activity (>100 hours) AND aggregate of all such activities exceeds 500 hours
5. Material participation in any 5 of the prior 10 years
6. Personal service activity with material participation in any 3 prior years
7. Regular, continuous, and substantial participation based on all facts and circumstances

### Spousal Strategy

- Only ONE spouse needs to qualify as a REP
- The qualifying spouse's hours count toward both the 750-hour test and the 50% test
- **Key planning point:** If one spouse has a full-time W-2 job (2,000+ hours), they almost certainly fail the 50% test unless they also work 2,000+ hours in real estate
- The spouse WITHOUT a full-time non-RE job is the better candidate for REP status
- A spouse who manages rental properties, handles tenant relations, performs maintenance, and handles bookkeeping can accumulate 750+ hours across multiple properties

### Hour Tracking

- Must maintain a contemporaneous log (after-the-fact reconstruction is weak in audit)
- Record: date, hours, activity description, property
- Use a spreadsheet, app, or calendar
- IRS frequently challenges REP claims -- documentation is essential
- Court cases (e.g., Moss v. Commissioner) show taxpayers losing REP status due to inadequate records

## Short-Term vs. Long-Term Rental Tax Treatment

### Short-Term Rental (Average Stay < 7 Days)

- Reported on **Schedule C** (if substantial services provided) or **Schedule E**
- If Schedule C: subject to **self-employment tax**
- If average rental period is 7 days or less, the activity is NOT treated as a rental activity under Section 469 -- it is treated as a regular business
- This means passive activity loss limitations may not apply (if taxpayer materially participates)
- Short-term rental income does NOT count toward RE Professional hour requirements for the 50% test

### Long-Term Rental (Average Stay >= 7 Days)

- Reported on **Schedule E**
- NOT subject to self-employment tax
- Subject to **passive activity loss rules** unless taxpayer is a RE Professional
- $25,000 active participation exception available (see below)

### The 7-Day and 30-Day Rules (Reg. 1.469-1T(e)(3))

| Average Rental Period | Tax Treatment |
|---|---|
| 7 days or less | Not a rental activity; regular business rules apply |
| 8-30 days with significant personal services | Not a rental activity |
| More than 30 days (or 8-30 days without significant services) | Rental activity (passive unless REP) |

## Section 280A Vacation Home Rules

### The Personal Use Limitation

When a taxpayer uses a dwelling unit for personal purposes for more than the greater of 14 days or 10% of the days it is rented at fair rental value, the property is treated as a "vacation home" and deductions are limited.

### The Three Categories

**Category 1: Primarily Personal Use (rental < 15 days)**
- If rented for fewer than 15 days: ALL rental income is excluded from gross income (tax-free)
- No rental expenses are deductible (but mortgage interest and property taxes remain deductible on Schedule A as personal expenses)
- This is the "Augusta Rule" or "Masters Week" exception

**Category 2: Mixed Use (personal > 14 days or >10% of rental days)**
- Rental income is taxable
- Deductions are LIMITED to rental income (cannot create a rental loss)
- Expenses must be allocated between personal and rental days
- Order of deductions (Prop. Reg. 1.280A-3(d)):
  1. Taxes and interest (allocated by days)
  2. Operating expenses (utilities, insurance, maintenance -- allocated by days)
  3. Depreciation (allocated by days, taken last)
- If deductions exceed rental income after allocation, excess carries forward to future years (but only usable against future rental income from the same property)

**Category 3: Primarily Rental (personal use within limits)**
- Personal use is 14 days or less AND not more than 10% of rental days
- Full rental activity treatment applies
- Passive activity rules apply normally
- Can generate a rental loss (subject to passive activity limitations)

### Allocation Methods

**IRS Method (Bolton approach):** Allocate mortgage interest and property taxes based on rental days / total days used. More of these deductions go to rental (limited by rental income) and less to Schedule A.

**Tax Court Method (Bolton v. Commissioner):** Allocate mortgage interest and property taxes based on rental days / 365 (total days in the year). This is more favorable to the taxpayer because more mortgage interest and property tax goes to Schedule A (deductible without the rental income limitation) and less absorbs the rental income cap.

**The Tax Court method results in lower rental deductions (preserving room for operating expenses and depreciation against rental income) and higher personal deductions on Schedule A.**

### Worked Example

```
Vacation home rented 120 days, personal use 30 days (exceeds 14 days and 10% of 120 = 12 days)
Total expenses: Mortgage interest $12,000, property taxes $6,000, utilities $4,000,
  insurance $2,000, maintenance $3,000, depreciation $8,000
Rental income: $24,000

Tax Court method allocation ratio for interest/taxes: 120/365 = 32.88%
Allocation ratio for operating expenses: 120/150 = 80%

Step 1: Interest and taxes (allocated to rental)
  Interest: $12,000 x 32.88% = $3,946
  Taxes: $6,000 x 32.88% = $1,973
  Subtotal: $5,919
  Remaining rental income: $24,000 - $5,919 = $18,081

Step 2: Operating expenses (allocated to rental)
  Utilities: $4,000 x 80% = $3,200
  Insurance: $2,000 x 80% = $1,600
  Maintenance: $3,000 x 80% = $2,400
  Subtotal: $7,200
  Remaining rental income: $18,081 - $7,200 = $10,881

Step 3: Depreciation
  $8,000 x 80% = $6,400
  Limited to remaining rental income: $6,400 (fits within $10,881)

Total rental deductions: $5,919 + $7,200 + $6,400 = $19,519
Net rental income: $24,000 - $19,519 = $4,481

Personal portion goes to Schedule A:
  Interest: $12,000 - $3,946 = $8,054 (subject to $750K mortgage limit)
  Taxes: $6,000 - $1,973 = $4,027 (subject to SALT cap)
```

## Passive Activity Rules and the $25,000 Exception

### General Rule

Rental activities are passive by default. Passive losses can only offset passive income.

### $25,000 Active Participation Exception

- Available to taxpayers who **actively participate** in rental real estate (lower bar than material participation)
- Allows up to $25,000 of rental losses to offset non-passive income
- **Phase-out:** Begins at $100,000 MAGI, fully phased out at $150,000 MAGI
- Active participation requires: making management decisions (approving tenants, approving repairs, etc.)
- NOT available if ownership interest is less than 10%

### Unused Passive Losses

- Carry forward indefinitely
- Released in full upon taxable disposition of the activity (complete sale of the property)

### Tracking Suspended Passive Activity Losses (Section 469(g))

#### Why Tracking Matters

Every dollar of suspended passive loss from a rental property becomes fully deductible against ALL income (ordinary, capital, passive, non-passive) when the property is disposed of in a fully taxable transaction. Failing to track means losing potentially tens of thousands of dollars in deductions.

#### What Triggers Release

**Full release (Section 469(g)(1)):**
- Sale of the entire interest in the activity to an unrelated party in a fully taxable transaction
- ALL suspended passive losses from that activity are deductible in the year of disposition
- Deducted against income in this order: (1) passive income from the same activity, (2) net passive income from other activities, (3) any other income (ordinary, capital gains, etc.)

**Partial release or NO release:**
- Installment sale: suspended losses are released proportionally as payments are received
- Gift: suspended losses increase the donee's basis in the property, but are NOT deductible by the donor
- Death: suspended losses up to the amount of unrealized appreciation in the property are lost (step-up basis absorbs them); any excess is deductible on the decedent's final return
- Like-kind exchange (Section 1031): suspended losses carry over to the replacement property; NOT released
- Transfer to controlled entity: generally NOT a triggering disposition

#### Worked Example

```
Taxpayer has owned a rental property for 10 years.
Accumulated suspended passive losses: $85,000

Year 11: Property sold to unrelated buyer for $400,000.
  Adjusted basis: $250,000
  Gain on sale: $150,000

Tax treatment:
  Capital gain from sale: $150,000
  Suspended passive loss release: ($85,000)
  Net taxable: $150,000 - $85,000 = $65,000 (only the gain portion)
  
  But the $85,000 offsets income in this order:
  1. Gain from the activity itself ($150,000 available) -> absorbs $85,000
  2. Net result: $150,000 gain - $85,000 = $65,000 taxable gain

  Without tracking: Taxpayer reports $150,000 gain. Overpays tax on $85,000.
  At 24% rate: $20,400 in unnecessary tax.
```

## Self-Rental Recharacterization Rule

### Section 469(c)(2) and Reg. Section 1.469-2(f)(6)

#### What It Is

When a taxpayer rents property to a business in which the taxpayer materially participates, the rental income is recharacterized as NON-PASSIVE income -- but the rental expenses remain passive (or at best, reduce passive income). This is a one-way trap.

#### How It Works

1. Taxpayer owns a building in an LLC (passive rental activity)
2. Taxpayer's S-Corp (in which taxpayer materially participates) rents the building
3. The rental income from the LLC is recharacterized as non-passive
4. But if the LLC generates a rental loss (depreciation > rent), the loss remains passive

**The asymmetry is the trap:** Net rental income = non-passive (taxed immediately, cannot be offset by other passive losses). Net rental loss = passive (can only offset other passive income).

#### Worked Example

```
Taxpayer owns:
  Building LLC: rents to S-Corp for $36,000/year
  S-Corp: consulting business, taxpayer materially participates

Building LLC income/expenses:
  Rental income: $36,000
  Depreciation: ($20,000)
  Mortgage interest: ($18,000)
  Insurance, taxes, maintenance: ($8,000)
  Net rental loss: ($10,000)

Because taxpayer materially participates in the S-Corp tenant:
  The loss is PASSIVE -> can only offset other passive income
  If the rent were set higher and the LLC had net income, that income would be NON-PASSIVE

Strategy implications:
  - Set the rent at fair market value (arm's length) -- too high invites the recharacterization trap on income side
  - Too low creates issues with IRS challenging below-market rent
  - The self-rental rule applies regardless of the entity structure (LLC, S-Corp, sole prop)
  - This rule does NOT apply to short-term rentals that are not treated as rental activities under Section 469
```

#### Planning Around the Self-Rental Rule

1. **Ensure the property generates a loss (or at least breaks even):** If depreciation and expenses exceed rent, the loss is passive (not ideal, but not recharacterized as non-passive income)
2. **Qualify as a Real Estate Professional:** If the taxpayer is a REP who materially participates in the rental, the activity is non-passive on its own merits. The recharacterization rule becomes moot because the income and loss are both non-passive.
3. **Use the property for short-term rentals:** If the average rental period is 7 days or less, the activity is not a rental activity and the self-rental rule does not apply

## Property Tax Allocation Strategy

For taxpayers who own property used partly for rental, partly for home office, and partly personal use:

### Allocation Method

```
Total property taxes paid: $12,000

Rental property tax allocation:
  Rental property: 30% of property value -> $3,600 (Schedule E deduction, NOT subject to SALT cap)

Home office allocation:
  Home office: 10% of personal residence -> 10% x $8,400 remaining = $840 (Schedule C/Form 8829 deduction, NOT subject to SALT cap)

Personal allocation:
  Remaining: $12,000 - $3,600 - $840 = $7,560 (Schedule A, subject to SALT cap)
```

### SALT Cap Optimization

The SALT cap ($40,000 MFJ under OBBBA, with phase-down starting at $500K MAGI) applies ONLY to personal state and local taxes on Schedule A. By allocating property taxes to rental/business use, those amounts bypass the SALT cap entirely.

## Mortgage Interest Allocation

### $750,000 Limit

- Mortgage interest on acquisition debt up to $750,000 ($375,000 MFS) is deductible on Schedule A for the personal-use portion
- Applies to primary residence + one second home
- Pre-December 15, 2017 mortgages grandfathered at $1,000,000 limit

### Rental Property Mortgages

- Mortgage interest on rental property is deductible on Schedule E with NO dollar limit
- This is a business deduction, not an itemized deduction
- Not subject to the $750K limit or the SALT cap

### Home Office Mortgage Allocation

- Business percentage of mortgage interest is deducted on Form 8829 (not Schedule A)
- This portion is NOT subject to the $750K limit
- Reduces the amount going to Schedule A (which is subject to the limit)

## Points Deduction Rules

### Purchase Points

- Points paid to obtain a mortgage for the **purchase** of a primary residence: **fully deductible in the year paid**
- Must be a common practice in the area
- Must not exceed typical points charged
- Must be paid from taxpayer's own funds (not from seller proceeds)
- Reported on Form 1098

### Refinance Points

- Points paid on a refinance: **amortized over the life of the new loan**
- Example: $3,000 in points on a 30-year refinance = $100/year deduction
- If the refinanced loan is paid off early (sale, another refinance), the remaining unamortized points are deductible in that year

### Seller-Paid Points

- Buyer can deduct points paid by the seller (reduces buyer's basis in the home)

## Land Improvement Depreciation

### What Qualifies

Land improvements are depreciable assets that are distinct from both the land itself (never depreciable) and the building (27.5 or 39-year property):

| Improvement | MACRS Life | Bonus Eligible? |
|---|---|---|
| Fencing | 15 years | Yes (100% under OBBBA) |
| Paving (parking lots, driveways) | 15 years | Yes |
| Sidewalks | 15 years | Yes |
| Drainage systems | 15 years | Yes |
| Landscaping | 15 years | Yes |
| Retaining walls | 15 years | Yes |
| Swimming pools (rental property) | 15 years | Yes |
| Outdoor lighting | 15 years | Yes |
| Irrigation systems | 15 years | Yes |
| Septic systems | 15 years | Yes |

### Key Points

- Land improvements are 15-year MACRS property using 150% declining balance method, half-year convention
- Under OBBBA, 100% bonus depreciation applies to 15-year property, so the ENTIRE cost is deductible in Year 1
- This means a $50,000 parking lot replacement is fully deductible in the year placed in service
- The land itself is NEVER depreciable -- must allocate between land value and improvement cost
- A cost segregation study can identify land improvements that were incorrectly classified as building or land

### Worked Example

```
Rental property owner installs:
  New asphalt driveway and parking: $35,000
  Privacy fencing around property: $12,000
  Landscaping and drainage: $8,000
  Outdoor security lighting: $3,000
  Total land improvements: $58,000

With OBBBA 100% bonus depreciation:
  Year 1 deduction: $58,000

Without bonus (straight-line 15-year):
  Year 1 deduction: $58,000 / 15 x 0.5 (half-year) = $1,933

Bonus depreciation advantage: $56,067 accelerated into Year 1
At 37% rate: $20,745 in tax savings shifted to current year
```

## 1031 Like-Kind Exchange

### What It Is

Section 1031 allows deferral of capital gains on the sale of investment/business real property by exchanging it for "like-kind" replacement property.

### Requirements

- Both relinquished and replacement properties must be held for investment or business use
- Cannot exchange personal-use property (primary residence)
- Must be real property for real property (post-2017: only real estate qualifies)
- **45-day identification period:** Must identify replacement property within 45 days of closing
- **180-day exchange period:** Must close on replacement property within 180 days
- Must use a Qualified Intermediary (QI) -- cannot touch the proceeds
- Boot (cash or non-like-kind property received) is taxable

### Tax Benefit

- Defers ALL capital gains and depreciation recapture
- Basis carries over to replacement property (reduced by boot received)
- Can do serial 1031 exchanges indefinitely
- At death, heirs receive stepped-up basis, potentially eliminating ALL deferred gains

## Section 1033 Involuntary Conversion

### What It Is

Section 1033 allows a taxpayer to defer gain when property is involuntarily converted (destroyed, stolen, condemned, or disposed of under threat of condemnation) if the proceeds are reinvested in "similar or related in service or use" replacement property.

### Replacement Period

| Type of Involuntary Conversion | Replacement Period |
|---|---|
| Casualty, theft, or other destruction | 2 years from the close of the first tax year in which any gain is realized |
| Condemnation (or threat of condemnation) of real property held for business or investment | 3 years from the close of the first tax year in which any gain is realized |
| Federally declared disaster for business or investment real property | 4 years from the close of the first tax year in which any gain is realized |

### "Similar or Related in Service or Use" vs. "Like-Kind"

- For **owner-users** (the taxpayer uses the property in their own business): the replacement must be similar in physical characteristics and end use. A warehouse destroyed by fire should be replaced with a warehouse, not an office building.
- For **owner-investors** (the taxpayer holds property for investment, such as a landlord): the IRS applies a more relaxed "like-kind" standard similar to Section 1031. A destroyed apartment building could be replaced with an office building.
- **Condemnation of real property** held for business or investment: explicitly uses the "like-kind" standard (Section 1033(g)), the same standard as Section 1031.

### Computation

```
Insurance proceeds received: $500,000
Adjusted basis of destroyed property: $300,000
Gain realized: $200,000

Scenario A: Taxpayer reinvests $500,000+ in replacement property
  Gain recognized: $0 (fully deferred)
  Basis of replacement property: Cost - Deferred gain = $500,000 - $200,000 = $300,000

Scenario B: Taxpayer reinvests $450,000
  Amount not reinvested: $50,000
  Gain recognized: $50,000 (lesser of gain realized or amount not reinvested)
  Basis of replacement: $450,000 - ($200,000 - $50,000) = $300,000

Scenario C: Taxpayer does not reinvest
  Gain recognized: $200,000 (full gain)
```

### Planning Considerations

- The replacement period starts at the END of the tax year when gain is first realized, not the date of the event. A casualty on January 1, 2025 gives the taxpayer until December 31, 2027 for general casualties.
- Extensions of the replacement period can be requested from the IRS (letter ruling)
- Section 1033 and Section 1031 can sometimes overlap when property is condemned -- Section 1033(g) provides the more favorable like-kind standard for condemnations of business/investment real property
- Personal-use property (primary residence): Section 121 exclusion should be applied first, then Section 1033 for any remaining gain

## Section 121 + Section 1031 Combination

### The Strategy

When a taxpayer sells a property that was used partly as a personal residence and partly as a rental or business property, both Section 121 (exclusion) and Section 1031 (deferral) can potentially apply to different portions of the same transaction.

### How It Works

1. **Personal-use portion:** Qualifies for Section 121 exclusion ($250K/$500K) if ownership and use tests are met
2. **Rental/business portion:** Qualifies for Section 1031 exchange if replacement property is identified and acquired within deadlines
3. The allocation is based on the percentage of the property used for each purpose

### Worked Example

```
Taxpayer sells a duplex for $800,000 (adjusted basis $400,000).
Lived in Unit A (50%) for 6 years. Rented Unit B (50%) for 6 years.
Filing status: MFJ

Personal portion (50%):
  Sale price: $400,000
  Basis: $200,000
  Gain: $200,000
  Section 121 exclusion: ($200,000) -- fully excluded (under $500K MFJ limit)
  Depreciation recapture: $0 (no depreciation on personal-use portion)
  Tax: $0

Rental portion (50%):
  Sale price: $400,000
  Basis: $200,000 (original) - $40,000 (depreciation) = $160,000
  Gain: $240,000
  1031 exchange into $400,000+ replacement rental property:
    Gain deferred: $240,000 (including $40,000 depreciation recapture)
    Tax: $0

Total tax on $800,000 sale: $0
```

### Anti-Abuse Rule

Section 1031(a)(2) was amended to prevent taxpayers from doing a 1031 exchange into a replacement property and then immediately converting it to personal use to claim Section 121. Under the current rules:

- If property acquired via Section 1031 exchange is converted to personal use, the taxpayer must wait **5 years** from the acquisition date before selling and claiming the Section 121 exclusion (Section 121(d)(10))
- This prevents the "exchange and convert" strategy from working on a short timeline

### Mixed-Use Property Basis Tracking

When a property changes between personal and rental use, or is used for both simultaneously, maintaining dual basis records is essential:

**Conversion from personal to rental:**
- Depreciable basis = LESSER of (1) adjusted basis at conversion, or (2) FMV at conversion
- If FMV at conversion is less than basis, the "lost" basis can only be recognized as a personal loss (not deductible for tax purposes)
- Depreciation starts from the date of conversion

**Conversion from rental to personal:**
- Stop depreciation as of the conversion date
- Maintain the depreciated basis for future gain calculation
- Section 121 clock starts running from the conversion date (need 2 of last 5 years as personal use)

## Delaware Statutory Trust (DST) as 1031 Replacement

### What It Is

A Delaware Statutory Trust is a legal entity created under Delaware law that holds title to real property. Investors purchase beneficial interests in the trust. For tax purposes, each investor is treated as owning an undivided fractional interest in the underlying real property (Rev. Rul. 2004-86).

### Why DSTs Matter for 1031 Exchanges

- A DST interest qualifies as "like-kind" replacement property for Section 1031 purposes
- Allows a taxpayer to complete a 1031 exchange WITHOUT actively managing a replacement property
- Particularly useful for:
  - Aging landlords who want passive income without management burden
  - Taxpayers who cannot find suitable replacement property within the 45-day identification window
  - Investors who want diversification (invest in multiple DSTs across asset classes and geographies)

### Tax Treatment

- Pass-through of income, depreciation, and other items to beneficial owners
- Each owner reports their pro-rata share on Schedule E
- Depreciation deductions flow through, providing tax-sheltered cash flow
- At sale or liquidation, gain is recognized (unless another 1031 exchange is performed)

### Typical Structure

```
Investor sells rental property for $1,000,000 (basis $400,000, gain $600,000)
  |
  v
Qualified Intermediary holds proceeds
  |
  v
Investor identifies 3 DST interests within 45 days:
  DST A: $400,000 (multifamily apartment, Dallas)
  DST B: $350,000 (medical office, Atlanta)
  DST C: $250,000 (industrial warehouse, Phoenix)
  |
  v
All acquired within 180 days
  |
  v
$600,000 gain fully deferred
Basis in DST interests: $400,000 (carried over)

Annual cash flow: ~5-6% yield ($50,000-$60,000/year)
Much of the cash flow is tax-sheltered by depreciation
```

### Risks and Limitations

1. **Illiquidity:** DST interests cannot be easily sold on a secondary market. Typical hold period: 5-10 years.
2. **No management control:** The trustee makes all property decisions. Investors cannot vote on refinancing, capital improvements, or sale timing.
3. **Financing risk:** Many DSTs use leverage (55-65% LTV). If the property declines in value, the debt can exceed the property value.
4. **Sponsor risk:** DST sponsors charge fees (acquisition fees 1-3%, asset management 0.5-1%/year, disposition fees). Poor sponsors can destroy value.
5. **No additional capital contributions:** The DST trust agreement prohibits additional investor contributions. If the property needs capital expenditures, the sponsor must fund them from reserves or obtain new financing.
6. **1031 out of DST:** When the DST sells the property, investors can do another 1031 exchange, but identification and timing can be challenging because the sale date is controlled by the sponsor.

## Real Estate Syndication Tax Benefits

### What Is a Real Estate Syndication?

A syndication is a partnership (usually an LLC taxed as a partnership) where a sponsor/general partner (GP) manages the property and limited partners (LPs) provide capital. LPs receive K-1s with their share of income, deductions, and credits.

### Passive Loss Allocation

- LP interests in real estate syndications generate passive income and losses
- Depreciation (especially with cost segregation and bonus depreciation) often creates significant paper losses in early years
- These losses offset the LP's other passive income (other rentals, passive K-1 income)
- If the LP has no other passive income, losses are suspended until disposition

### At-Risk Rules (Section 465)

LPs can deduct losses only to the extent they are "at risk." For real estate:

**Amounts at risk:**
- Cash contributed to the partnership
- Adjusted basis of property contributed
- Recourse debt for which the partner is personally liable
- **Qualified non-recourse financing** (Section 465(b)(6)): non-recourse debt from a qualified lender (bank, government, regulated financial institution) secured by real property used in the activity. This is the key exception for real estate -- it allows partners to include their share of qualified non-recourse debt in their at-risk amount.

**Not at risk:**
- Non-recourse debt from the seller or a person who has an interest in the activity (related party non-recourse)
- Amounts protected by stop-loss agreements, guarantees, or similar arrangements

### Basis from Non-Recourse Debt

Under partnership tax rules (Section 752), a partner's share of partnership liabilities increases the partner's outside basis. For non-recourse debt, allocation depends on:

1. **Minimum gain:** Partners share non-recourse debt to the extent of their share of minimum gain (roughly, the extent to which debt exceeds the adjusted basis of the property securing it)
2. **Section 704(c) allocations:** If contributed property secures the debt, the contributing partner may bear a larger share
3. **Profit-sharing ratio:** Excess non-recourse debt is allocated per profit-sharing percentages

**Example:**
```
Real estate partnership with $10M property:
  Equity: $3M (30%), from 10 LPs at $300K each
  Non-recourse mortgage: $7M (70%), qualified lender

Each LP's basis:
  Cash contributed: $300,000
  Share of non-recourse debt (10% x $7M): $700,000
  Total outside basis: $1,000,000

If the partnership allocates $150,000 in losses to each LP:
  LP has $1,000,000 basis -> loss is deductible (subject to passive activity rules)
  LP's at-risk amount includes the qualified non-recourse debt -> loss passes at-risk test
```

## Depreciation Recapture

### Section 1250 Recapture (Real Property)

- Depreciation recapture on real property (buildings) is taxed at a maximum rate of **25%**
- Applies to "unrecaptured Section 1250 gain"
- Applies even if taxpayer used Section 121 exclusion for the rest of the gain
- Applies to depreciation "allowed or allowable" (must claim it or lose the deduction AND still owe recapture)

### Section 1245 Recapture (Personal Property from Cost Segregation)

- Depreciation on personal property (5-year, 7-year assets from cost segregation) is recaptured at **ordinary income rates** (up to 37%)
- This is more costly than Section 1250 recapture
- Planning point: if selling soon, cost segregation may not be beneficial after recapture

## Section 121 Exclusion (Primary Residence)

### Requirements

- Owned and used as primary residence for 2 of the last 5 years
- Exclusion: $250,000 (single) / $500,000 (MFJ)
- Can be used once every 2 years

### Partial Exclusion

If requirements not fully met (e.g., job relocation, health reasons, unforeseen circumstances), a partial exclusion is available based on the fraction of the 2-year requirement met.

### Interaction with Rental Use / 1031

- If property was previously used as a rental and converted to primary residence, the exclusion applies after 2 years of primary use
- However, gain allocable to depreciation claimed after 2008 is NOT excluded (depreciation recapture applies)
- Cannot do a 1031 exchange and then immediately convert to primary residence to claim Section 121 (must wait 5 years under anti-abuse rules)

## Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) and Real Estate

### The 3.8% NIIT

- Applies to net investment income when MAGI exceeds $200,000 (single) / $250,000 (MFJ)
- Rental income is generally investment income subject to NIIT
- **Exception:** If the taxpayer is a RE Professional who materially participates, rental income may not be subject to NIIT (it is not "passive" income)

### Strategy

- RE Professional status can save 3.8% NIIT on rental income
- Combined with SE tax avoidance on Schedule E income, the total tax savings on rental income can be significant

## Opportunity Zones (Section 1400Z)

### What They Are

Qualified Opportunity Zones (QOZs) are designated low-income census tracts where investment receives preferential tax treatment.

### Tax Benefits

1. **Deferral:** Capital gains invested in a Qualified Opportunity Fund (QOF) within 180 days are deferred until the earlier of: sale of the QOF investment or December 31, 2026
2. **Exclusion:** If the QOF investment is held 10+ years, ALL appreciation in the QOF investment is tax-free

### Current Status (2025)

- The original basis step-up provisions (10% at 5 years, 15% at 7 years) have expired for new investments
- The 10-year exclusion of appreciation remains valuable for investments made early enough to qualify
- New investments still benefit from deferral and the 10-year exclusion

## Installment Sale Treatment (Section 453)

### Overview

An installment sale spreads gain recognition over the payment period, potentially keeping the taxpayer in lower tax brackets and deferring tax.

### Mechanics

```
Selling price: $500,000
Adjusted basis: $200,000
Selling expenses: $30,000
Amount realized: $470,000
Gain: $270,000
Gross profit percentage: $270,000 / $500,000 = 54%

Year 1: Receive $100,000 payment
  Gain recognized: $100,000 x 54% = $54,000

Year 2: Receive $100,000 payment
  Gain recognized: $100,000 x 54% = $54,000

(Continues until fully paid)
```

### Interest Requirement

The seller must charge adequate interest (at least the applicable Federal rate, AFR) on deferred payments. If interest is below AFR, the IRS will impute interest under Section 1274 or 7872, recharacterizing part of each payment as interest income.

### Depreciation Recapture Exception

Section 1250 depreciation recapture is recognized in FULL in the year of sale, regardless of installment treatment. Only the capital gain portion (Section 1231 gain and Section 1250 gain above the recapture amount) can be spread over the installment period.

## Common Mistakes in Real Estate Tax

1. **Not depreciating rental property** -- depreciation is mandatory; failing to claim it results in recapture without the benefit
2. **Depreciating land** -- land is never depreciable; must allocate between land and building
3. **Missing cost segregation opportunity** -- especially with 100% bonus depreciation restored
4. **Claiming REP status without documentation** -- hour logs are essential
5. **Mixing personal use with rental** -- personal use days can limit deductions and disqualify the $25K active participation exception
6. **Missing the 45-day / 180-day deadlines** on a 1031 exchange
7. **Not tracking passive loss carryforwards** -- these are valuable and released on disposition
8. **Ignoring SALT optimization** through rental/business property tax allocation
9. **Overlooking depreciation recapture** when planning property sales
10. **Not considering installment sale treatment** (Section 453) to spread gain recognition over multiple years
11. **Missing partial asset disposition elections** -- the single most overlooked deduction for building owners replacing components
12. **Treating residential improvements as QIP** -- QIP applies ONLY to nonresidential (commercial) buildings
13. **Ignoring the self-rental recharacterization rule** when renting property to your own business
14. **Not performing cost segregation look-back studies** on properties placed in service in prior years
15. **Failing to apply the de minimis safe harbor** to small expenditures on rental properties
16. **Overlooking Section 1033** when insurance proceeds exceed basis on casualty losses
17. **Not tracking dual basis** when converting property between personal and rental use
