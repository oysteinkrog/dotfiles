# Frequently Missed Deductions and Credits (Tax Year 2025)

## Overview

This reference catalogs deductions and credits that taxpayers commonly fail to claim, organized by taxpayer situation. Collectively, these missed items cost American taxpayers billions of dollars annually in overpaid federal and state taxes. A thorough return review should check every applicable item in this list. Even experienced preparers miss items -- the most commonly overlooked are depreciation on rental property, foreign tax credits on 1099-DIV, capital loss carryforwards, and state-level deductions that have no federal equivalent.

## W-2 Employees

### Above-the-Line Deductions (No Itemization Required)

#### Educator Expenses -- Section 62(a)(2)(D)
- **$300** above-the-line deduction for K-12 teachers, instructors, counselors, principals, and aides
- Must work at least 900 hours during the school year
- Qualified expenses: books, supplies, computer equipment, software, supplementary materials, professional development courses
- Married educators filing jointly: $300 EACH ($600 combined) if both qualify
- Commonly missed because the amount seems small -- but $300 at 22% = $66 free money

#### HSA Contributions -- Section 223
- Contributions made directly (not through payroll) are above-the-line deductions
- Check if the full $4,300 (self) / $8,550 (family) limit was reached
- Catch-up contribution for age 55+: additional $1,000
- Often missed when employee changes jobs mid-year or has both payroll and direct contributions
- **Last-month rule**: if covered by HDHP on December 1, can contribute the full annual amount even if coverage began mid-year (must remain covered for 13 months)

#### Student Loan Interest -- Section 221
- Up to **$2,500** above-the-line deduction
- Applies to federal AND private student loans
- Phase-out: $80K-$95K (single) / $165K-$195K (MFJ)
- The person legally obligated on the loan claims it (not the parent making payments unless parent is the borrower)
- Often missed by recent graduates in their first few filing years

#### IRA Contributions -- Section 219
- Traditional IRA: up to $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+) above-the-line deduction
- Deductibility depends on whether covered by employer plan and income level
- If neither spouse is covered by an employer plan: FULLY deductible at any income level
- If one spouse is covered: non-covered spouse can deduct up to $236,000 MFJ MAGI
- Often missed by the spouse NOT covered by the employer's 401(k)

#### Moving Expenses (Military Only) -- Section 217
- Active-duty military: still deductible for PCS moves (above-the-line)
- Civilian moving expenses: NOT deductible 2018-2025 under TCJA
- Military members often forget this deduction exists because "moving expenses aren't deductible anymore" is the common wisdom

#### Jury Duty Pay Surrendered to Employer -- Section 62(a)(13)
- If your employer paid you during jury service but required you to surrender the jury duty pay to them: deduct the surrendered amount as an above-the-line deduction
- Report on Schedule 1, Line 24a
- Uncommon but technically an above-the-line deduction

### State/Local Tax Refund (1099-G)
- If you received a state/local tax refund and you **itemized deductions** in the prior year: the refund may be taxable
- But: many taxpayers who receive a 1099-G did NOT itemize (took the standard deduction) and do NOT need to include the refund as income
- **Commonly over-reported**: taxpayers include the 1099-G amount as income when they should not (because they did not benefit from the SALT deduction)
- Always check prior year: did the taxpayer itemize? Did they claim a SALT deduction? Was the standard deduction more beneficial?

## Self-Employed Taxpayers (Schedule C)

### Commonly Missed Above-the-Line Deductions

#### 50% Self-Employment Tax Deduction -- Section 164(f)
- Deduct the employer-equivalent portion (50%) of SE tax on Schedule 1
- Automatic calculation on Schedule SE, but some taxpayers using simple software miss it
- At $100,000 net SE income: ~$7,065 deduction

#### Self-Employed Health Insurance -- Section 162(l)
- Premiums for health, dental, vision, and qualified long-term care insurance
- Deductible above-the-line (not on Schedule C, not on Schedule A)
- Available for the self-employed individual, spouse, dependents, and children under 27
- Cannot exceed net SE income from the business
- Often missed by part-time self-employed who also have a W-2 job with employer coverage (if not eligible for employer plan, can still claim)
- **CRITICAL**: cannot claim for months you were eligible for employer-subsidized coverage (even if you declined it)

#### Retirement Plan Contributions
- **SEP-IRA**: up to 20% of net SE income (after SE tax deduction), max $70,000
- **Solo 401(k)**: $23,500 employee deferral + 20% of net SE income as employer contribution, max $70,000 total
- **Defined Benefit Plan**: potentially $200,000+ depending on age and design
- Solo 401(k) is often missed because self-employed think they "don't have a 401(k)"
- Deadline: SEP-IRA can be established and funded by the return filing deadline (including
  extensions). Solo 401(k) timing is more nuanced: verify adoption and contribution deadlines
  based on entity type and contribution type.

### Schedule C Business Expenses Commonly Missed

#### Home Office -- Section 280A
- Simplified method: $5/sq ft, max 300 sq ft = $1,500
- Regular method: actual expenses (mortgage interest, property tax, utilities, insurance, repairs, depreciation) x business use percentage
- Regular method often yields $3,000-$8,000+ deduction including depreciation
- **Depreciation on home office is frequently missed** -- even when claiming home office, many skip the depreciation component
- Must be regular and exclusive use (dedicated space used only for business)
- See HOME-OFFICE.md for deep dive

#### Internet and Phone -- Business Percentage
- Home internet: deduct business-use percentage (e.g., 50% if used equally for business and personal)
- Cell phone: deduct business-use percentage of monthly bill
- Business landline / dedicated business phone: 100% deductible
- Many self-employed forget to claim these or underestimate their business percentage

#### Vehicle Expenses -- Section 274
- Standard mileage rate: $0.70/mile (2025)
- Actual expense method: gas, insurance, repairs, registration, depreciation, lease payments x business use percentage
- Must maintain a mileage log (contemporaneous records required)
- Commonly missed: parking and tolls are deductible IN ADDITION to the standard mileage rate

#### Software and Digital Subscriptions
- Accounting software (QuickBooks, FreshBooks)
- Project management tools
- Cloud storage (Dropbox, Google Workspace)
- Design tools (Adobe Creative Suite, Canva Pro)
- Communication tools (Zoom, Slack)
- Website hosting, domain names
- SaaS tools specific to the business
- Often missed because they're auto-charged to a personal credit card

#### Professional Development
- Courses, certifications, workshops, conferences
- Books and publications related to the business
- Trade association dues and memberships
- Continuing education required for licensing

#### Financial and Processing Fees
- Business bank account fees (monthly fees, wire transfer fees)
- Merchant processing fees (Stripe, Square, PayPal fees)
- Invoicing software fees
- Bad debts from uncollectable invoices (must have been previously included in income for accrual basis; for cash basis, generally not deductible because it was never reported as income)

#### Marketing and Advertising
- Website design and maintenance
- Business cards, brochures, promotional materials
- Online advertising (Google Ads, Facebook Ads, LinkedIn)
- Sponsorships of local events (if commercial purpose)
- Photography for business use

#### Start-Up Costs -- Section 195
- Up to **$5,000** of start-up costs deductible in the first year (phase-out if total exceeds $50,000)
- Remainder amortized over 180 months (15 years)
- Includes: market research, advertising for opening, training, travel to establish suppliers

#### Organizational Costs -- Section 248
- Up to **$5,000** of organizational costs deductible in the first year
- Includes: state filing fees, legal fees for entity formation, accounting fees for initial setup
- Often missed by new LLCs and S-Corps

## Homeowners

### Mortgage Interest -- Schedule A
- **Check for TWO 1098s** if your loan was transferred mid-year (servicer change)
- Each servicer issues a 1098 for their portion -- both must be reported
- Commonly missed: the second 1098 from the new servicer, or points paid on a refinance (amortized over loan life)

### Points Paid in Purchase Year
- Points paid on a purchase mortgage: **fully deductible in the year of purchase**
- Points paid on a refinance: amortized over the loan term
- If you refinance again: the remaining unamortized points from the prior refinance become fully deductible in the year of the new refinance
- Seller-paid points: deductible by the BUYER (reduces buyer's basis in the property)

### PMI (Private Mortgage Insurance)
- Deductibility has expired and been reinstated repeatedly; check current-year status
- When available: treated as deductible mortgage interest on Schedule A
- Phase-out applies at higher income levels

### Property Taxes
- **Check for multiple property tax bills**: school tax may be billed separately from county/city tax
- Some localities bill quarterly, semi-annually, or annually -- ensure all payments in the tax year are captured
- Supplemental tax bills (CA and other states): issued when property changes hands or is reassessed; these are also deductible
- Subject to the personal SALT cap (combined with state income tax)

### Energy Credits -- Section 25C / Section 25D
- **Section 25C (Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit)**: 30% of cost, up to $1,200/year for most items ($2,000 for heat pumps)
  - Exterior doors ($250/door, $500 max), windows ($600 max), insulation, central A/C, furnaces, water heaters
  - **Heat pumps**: 30%, up to $2,000 (separate from the $1,200 cap)
  - Home energy audit: 30%, up to $150
- **Section 25D (Residential Clean Energy Credit)**: 30% of cost, NO cap
  - Solar panels (electric and water heating), wind turbines, geothermal, battery storage (3 kWh+), fuel cells
  - This credit alone can be worth $5,000-$15,000+ for a solar installation
- Both are non-refundable but excess Section 25D carries forward
- **Commonly missed**: taxpayers install energy-efficient equipment and don't realize credits exist

### Home Office Depreciation (Owned Home)
- If you use the regular home office method and OWN the home: you must depreciate the business-use portion
- Many taxpayers claim home office expenses but skip the depreciation component
- Depreciation on the home office portion reduces basis -- this is recaptured at sale (Section 1250), but the annual tax savings usually far exceed the eventual recapture

## Investors

### Foreign Tax Credit -- Section 901
- International mutual funds and ETFs often pay foreign taxes on dividends
- Reported in **Box 7 of Form 1099-DIV** (foreign tax paid)
- Many taxpayers see the 1099-DIV and only enter Box 1a (dividends) -- they ignore Box 7
- For most taxpayers with less than $300/$600 (single/MFJ) in foreign taxes: can claim directly on Form 1040 without filing Form 1116
- A credit (dollar-for-dollar tax reduction) is almost always better than a deduction
- **This is one of the single most commonly missed items on investment returns**

### Margin Interest (Investment Interest) -- Section 163(d)
- Interest on margin loans used to purchase taxable investments is deductible
- Limited to net investment income (investment income minus investment expenses)
- Excess carries forward to future years
- Report on **Form 4952** and Schedule A (requires itemization)
- Often missed because the interest appears on the brokerage statement, not on a standard tax form

### Capital Loss Carryforward
- Net capital losses are limited to **$3,000/year** ($1,500 MFS) against ordinary income
- Excess carries forward **indefinitely**
- **Commonly missed**: taxpayers who had large losses in a prior year forget to carry forward the remaining loss
- Verify against prior year's **Schedule D, Line 21** (if there is a carryforward amount)
- When switching tax preparers: the new preparer may not have the prior-year carryforward information

### Wash Sale Basis Adjustments
- When a wash sale occurs (repurchase within 30 days): the disallowed loss is added to the basis of the replacement shares
- Brokers report wash sales on 1099-B, but may NOT correctly adjust basis if the wash sale spans multiple accounts
- If you sold at a loss in one brokerage and bought within 30 days in ANOTHER brokerage: the wash sale must be manually tracked
- Failure to adjust basis can result in double-taxation of the loss amount

### Worthless Securities -- Section 165(g)
- Securities that become completely worthless: claim as a capital loss on the LAST DAY of the tax year they became worthless
- Must be completely worthless (not merely declined in value)
- 7-year statute of limitations (vs. normal 3 years) for claiming refund on worthless securities
- Commonly missed because there is no sale -- just a disappearance of value

## Parents and Families

### Child Tax Credit -- Section 24
- **$2,200 per qualifying child** under 17
- Up to $1,700 refundable (ACTC)
- Phase-out: $200K single / $400K MFJ
- Ensure SSN (not ITIN) is on file for each child
- ITIN children: only $500 Other Dependent Credit (non-refundable)

### Dependent Care Credit -- Section 21
- 20-35% of up to $3,000 (1 child) / $6,000 (2+ children)
- Coordinate with DCFSA -- see NEW-CHILD.md for detailed analysis
- Don't forget summer camp (day camp qualifies; overnight camp does NOT)
- Before/after school programs qualify

### American Opportunity Tax Credit -- Section 25A
- **$2,500 per student** (40% refundable)
- First 4 years of college only
- Coordinate with 529 distributions -- see COLLEGE-STUDENT.md
- Even if 529 covers everything: consider paying $4,000 out of pocket for tuition to claim full AOTC

### 529 State Tax Deduction
- **35+ states** offer deductions or credits for 529 contributions
- New York: $5,000/$10,000 (single/MFJ) deduction
- Colorado: full deduction for contributions
- Indiana: 20% credit up to $1,500
- Many taxpayers contribute to 529 plans but fail to claim the state deduction
- Some states require using the in-state plan; others accept any state's plan

### Medical Expenses for Dependents
- If you claim someone as a dependent: their medical expenses count toward your 7.5% AGI threshold
- Includes: braces, therapy, glasses, prescriptions, dental work for children
- Often missed when child's medical expenses alone are small but combined with parents' push over the threshold

### Adoption Credit -- Section 23
- Up to **$17,280** per child (non-refundable, 5-year carryforward)
- Special needs adoption: full credit regardless of expenses
- See NEW-CHILD.md for full details

### Child and Dependent Care Credit for Disabled Dependents
- No age limit for a disabled dependent (does not need to be under 13)
- If caring for a disabled spouse or dependent of any age: qualifies for the credit

## Rental Property Owners

### Depreciation -- THE Most Commonly Missed Rental Deduction

- **Residential rental property**: 27.5-year straight-line depreciation
- **Commercial property**: 39-year straight-line
- **Land is NOT depreciable** -- must allocate purchase price between building and land
- Typical allocation: 75-85% building / 15-25% land (based on property tax assessment ratios or appraisal)
- Even if you "forget" to claim depreciation: the IRS treats it as "allowed or allowable" -- basis is reduced WHETHER OR NOT you actually claimed it
- This means skipping depreciation is pure loss: you get no current deduction, but your basis is reduced anyway

#### Cost Segregation (Catch-Up via Form 3115)
- Cost segregation study reclassifies building components into shorter-lived assets (5, 7, 15 years)
- Can be done retroactively and caught up in a single year via **Form 3115** (Change in Accounting Method)
- No amended returns needed -- the entire catch-up is taken in the year of change
- Typical benefit: $20,000-$100,000+ in accelerated deductions for properties valued above $500,000

#### Partial Asset Disposition -- Reg. 1.168-6(d)
- When you REPLACE a component (roof, HVAC, appliances, flooring): you can "dispose" of the old component
- Deduct the remaining undepreciated basis of the old component as a loss in the year of replacement
- Then capitalize and begin depreciating the new component
- Example: replace a $15,000 roof with 10 years of depreciation remaining; deduct the remaining $5,455 basis as a loss AND capitalize the new $20,000 roof
- File Form 3115 for the partial disposition election
- **Frequently missed**: most landlords just capitalize the new item without disposing of the old one

### Other Commonly Missed Rental Deductions

- **Travel to rental property**: mileage at $0.70/mile (2025) or actual costs
- **Insurance**: hazard, flood, liability, umbrella (rental-allocated portion)
- **HOA fees**: fully deductible as rental expense
- **Property management fees**: typically 8-12% of gross rent
- **Maintenance and repairs**: distinguish from improvements (repairs are immediately deductible)
- **Utilities paid by owner**: water, sewer, garbage, electricity (if owner-paid)
- **Advertising**: listing fees, photography, online listing subscriptions
- **Legal and accounting fees**: related to rental activity
- **Pest control, landscaping, snow removal**
- **Loan origination fees/points on rental property**: amortized over loan term (not immediately deductible like a primary residence)

## Charitable Contributions

### Non-Cash Donations (Clothing, Furniture, Household Items)
- Deductible at **Fair Market Value (FMV)** -- typically the thrift-store/resale value
- Must be in "good or better" condition
- Donations over $250: need written acknowledgment from the charity
- Donations over $500: Form 8283 (Noncash Charitable Contributions)
- Donations over $5,000: generally need a qualified appraisal
- Often missed because taxpayers don't track donation drop-offs or undervalue items

### Charitable Mileage
- **$0.14/mile** for driving related to charitable service (2025)
- Plus tolls and parking
- Example: driving to volunteer at food bank, delivering meals, transporting goods to Goodwill
- Must maintain a mileage log

### Out-of-Pocket Volunteer Expenses
- Supplies purchased for charitable work (ingredients for charity bake sale, materials for habitat build)
- Uniforms worn in charitable service (if not suitable for everyday wear)
- Travel expenses for charitable conventions/meetings
- Phone calls, postage, and other costs incurred while serving

### Stock Donations -- Section 170(e)(1)(A)
- Donate appreciated stock held >1 year directly to charity
- Deduction = FMV of stock on date of donation
- NO capital gains tax on the appreciation (unlike selling and donating cash)
- Example: stock bought for $5,000, now worth $20,000; donate stock and deduct $20,000 while avoiding $15,000 x 23.8% = $3,570 in capital gains tax
- Limited to 30% of AGI (vs. 60% for cash donations)
- Excess carries forward 5 years

### Donor-Advised Fund (DAF) Strategy
- Contribute appreciated stock to DAF: immediate deduction at FMV
- Distribute grants to charities over time
- Ideal for "bunching" charitable deductions: contribute 2-3 years of charitable giving in one year to exceed the standard deduction, then use the standard deduction in the other years
- Often missed as a planning tool

## Medical Expenses (Schedule A, >7.5% AGI)

### HSA Contributions (Above-the-Line)
- Not technically a "medical deduction" but often missed in the same category
- Direct contributions to HSA: deductible on Schedule 1 (above the line)
- Verify that payroll contributions + direct contributions do not exceed the annual limit

### Long-Term Care Insurance Premiums
- Age-based deductible limits (see DISABILITY.md)
- Deductible as medical expenses on Schedule A (subject to 7.5% AGI floor)
- For self-employed: deductible as self-employed health insurance (above-the-line, within the age-based limits)
- Often missed because the premiums are paid separately from health insurance

### Medical Travel
- **$0.22/mile** (2025 medical mileage rate)
- Plus tolls and parking
- Includes: travel to doctors, hospitals, therapy, pharmacy, dental, vision
- Lodging while away from home for medical treatment: up to $50/night per person ($100 total for patient + companion)
- Commonly missed because the amounts per trip seem small -- but they add up over a year

### Medical Equipment
- Hearing aids, CPAP machines, blood glucose monitors, blood pressure monitors
- Prescription eyeglasses and contact lenses
- Dental expenses: cleanings, fillings, crowns, braces, implants
- Therapy: physical, occupational, speech, mental health, substance abuse treatment
- Service animals (purchase, training, care)

## Retirees

### Qualified Charitable Distribution (QCD) -- Section 408(d)(8)
- At age 70.5+: distribute up to **$108,000/year** (2025, indexed) directly from IRA to charity
- Counts toward RMD but does NOT increase AGI
- Reduces: taxable income, Medicare IRMAA exposure, Social Security taxation, net investment income tax
- Far better than taking the RMD as income and then donating cash (which requires itemizing)
- **Commonly missed**: retirees take RMD as income, then donate cash and miss the QCD benefit

### State Pension Exclusions
- Many states exclude some or all pension/retirement income from state tax
- IL: excludes all retirement income; PA: excludes most retirement distributions; MS: excludes all retirement income
- Retirees who move states may not know their new state's exclusion rules
- Some states have income-based phase-outs on the exclusion

### Property Tax Credits/Exemptions for Seniors
- Many states and localities offer property tax reductions for seniors (typically 65+)
- Must apply separately (not automatic on the tax return)
- Can reduce property tax by hundreds to thousands per year
- Often missed because it requires a separate application to the county assessor

## State-Specific Frequently Missed Items

### New York
- 529 plan deduction: up to $5,000/$10,000 (single/MFJ) for contributions to NY 529
- New York City: UBT credit against personal income tax for LLC/S-Corp owners
- STAR credit/exemption for homeowners (property tax relief)

### California
- Renter's credit: $60 (single) / $120 (MFJ) for qualified renters (income limits apply)
- SDI (state disability insurance) deduction on Schedule A as state income tax
- Qualified home mortgage interest credit (for first-time homebuyers via CHFA)

### Various States
- Property tax circuit breakers: many states offer credits/refunds when property taxes exceed a percentage of income (commonly for seniors and low-income)
- State EITC: approximately 30 states offer their own EITC (typically 10-40% of federal EITC)
- State child tax credits: CA, CO, CT, ID, ME, MD, MA, MN, NJ, NM, NY, NC, OK, OR, VT, and others
- State education credits: IN (20% of 529 contributions), MN (K-12 education credit), SC (tuition tax credit)
- State itemized deduction differences: some states allow deductions that are not available federally (unreimbursed employee expenses, tax preparation fees)

## Checklist for Return Review

Use this checklist to verify that no common deductions have been missed:

- [ ] Prior-year capital loss carryforward (check prior Schedule D, Line 21)
- [ ] Foreign tax credit on 1099-DIV (Box 7)
- [ ] Rental property depreciation (27.5-year straight-line)
- [ ] Two 1098s from mortgage servicer change
- [ ] HSA contribution maximized
- [ ] 529 state tax deduction
- [ ] Educator expense deduction ($300)
- [ ] Student loan interest ($2,500)
- [ ] Self-employed health insurance deduction
- [ ] Self-employed retirement contributions (SEP/Solo 401k)
- [ ] Home office depreciation component
- [ ] Energy credits (solar, heat pump, windows)
- [ ] QCD from IRA for retirees 70.5+
- [ ] Non-cash charitable donations documented
- [ ] Medical mileage tracked
- [ ] State-specific credits and deductions
- [ ] Estimated tax payments made (check all four quarters)
- [ ] Prior-year state refund (1099-G) -- only taxable if prior year itemized with SALT benefit
