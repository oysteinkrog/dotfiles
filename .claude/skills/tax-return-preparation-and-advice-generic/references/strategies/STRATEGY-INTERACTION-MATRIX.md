# STRATEGY INTERACTION MATRIX — How Tax Strategies Combine, Conflict, and Chain

## Overview

Tax strategies do not exist in isolation. Every strategy interacts with others — sometimes synergistically (stacking), sometimes destructively (conflicting), and sometimes as prerequisites (chaining). Applying strategies without understanding these interactions is how taxpayers end up overpaying, getting audited, or creating compliance nightmares.

This document maps the critical interactions.

## STRATEGY CHAINS — Must Execute in Order

### Chain 1: S-Corp Optimization Stack

```
1. Form LLC (or use existing sole proprietorship)
2. Elect S-Corp taxation (Form 2553, must be filed by March 15 for calendar year)
3. Set up reasonable compensation payroll (before ANY distributions)
4. Establish Solo 401(k) or SEP-IRA (employee contributions require W-2)
5. Consider PTET election (requires entity-level election, typically before year-end of prior year)
6. Open HSA if on HDHP (payroll deducted reduces SE tax if through S-Corp payroll)
```

**Why order matters**: You cannot make 401(k) employee deferrals without W-2 income. You cannot have W-2 income without payroll. You cannot run payroll without entity formation. PTET requires an entity and a state-level election with specific deadlines. Breaking the chain at any point invalidates downstream strategies.

### Chain 2: Real Estate Professional Loss Unlocking

```
1. One spouse qualifies as Real Estate Professional (750+ hours, >50% of total services)
2. Make grouping election (Reg. ss1.469-9(g)) to treat all RE activities as one
3. Materially participate in the grouped activity (500+ hours combined)
4. Passive losses from all grouped RE become non-passive
5. Claim losses against W-2 and other active income
6. Perform cost segregation studies on each property for maximum depreciation
7. Elect PTET at entity level for additional SALT benefit
```

**Critical dependency**: The grouping election must be made on a TIMELY filed return (including extensions) for the FIRST year it is relevant. Missing this deadline means each property is treated separately — material participation must be met property by property.

### Chain 3: Startup Founder Tax Optimization

```
1. Incorporate as C-Corp (QSBS requires C-Corp)
2. File ss83(b) election within 30 days of stock grant
3. Confirm gross assets < $50M at each funding round
4. Hold stock for 5+ years (QSBS holding period)
5. Before exit: gift QSBS to non-grantor trusts (multiply $10M exclusion)
6. At exit: claim ss1202 exclusion ($10M+ per taxpayer)
7. For gains above exclusion: invest in QOF within 180 days
8. Hold QOF for 10+ years for FMV step-up on QOF appreciation
```

**Failure points**: Missing the 83(b) deadline (irrecoverable). Exceeding $50M gross assets (disqualifies future rounds). Gifting to grantor trusts instead of non-grantor trusts (no exclusion multiplication). Missing the 180-day QOF window (deferral lost).

### Chain 4: Short-Term Rental Tax Elimination

```
1. Purchase property suitable for STR (vacation destination, urban)
2. Ensure average guest stay < 7 days (verify via booking platforms)
3. Personally manage: 100+ hours AND more than any other individual
4. Obtain cost segregation study (before filing the return for the placed-in-service year)
5. Claim 100% bonus depreciation on 5/7/15-year property
6. Report as non-passive activity (STR exception + material participation)
7. On sale: 1031 exchange to defer recapture and gain
8. Repeat on replacement property
```

### Chain 5: Charitable Concentrated Stock Exit

```
1. Transfer appreciated stock to CRT BEFORE sale (avoid assignment of income doctrine)
2. CRT sells stock tax-free (ss664(c))
3. CRT invests proceeds and pays annuity/unitrust amount to donor
4. Capital gains tier distributions from CRT: invest in QOF within 180 days
5. QOF: defer gain to 2026, then hold 10 years for FMV step-up
6. Charitable deduction from CRT: offset other income (up to 30% AGI for appreciated capital gain property)
7. Carry forward unused deduction for 5 years
```

### Chain 6: QSBS + QOZ Overflow

```
1. Founder holds Qualified Small Business Stock (C-Corp, 5+ year hold)
2. At exit: claim §1202 QSBS exclusion on first $15M of gain (post-OBBBA cap)
3. Gain in excess of QSBS exclusion cap is recognized as taxable capital gain
4. Within 180 days of the sale: invest excess gain in a Qualified Opportunity Fund (§1400Z-2)
5. QOZ investment defers tax on the excess gain until 2026 (or earlier disposition)
6. Hold QOF investment for 10+ years: exclude ALL appreciation on the QOF investment from tax
```

**Why this chains two of the most powerful tools**: The QSBS exclusion eliminates tax on the first $15M of gain entirely. The QOZ deferral and eventual exclusion reduces the tax on the remainder. Combined, a founder with $25M in gain could exclude $15M (QSBS), defer and reduce tax on $10M (QOZ), and ultimately pay tax only on the deferred portion at 2026 rates with no tax on QOF appreciation if held 10+ years.

### Chain 7: 1031 Exchange + Cost Segregation

```
1. Sell investment/business real property
2. Complete §1031 like-kind exchange into replacement property (45-day ID, 180-day close)
3. Commission cost segregation study on replacement property
4. Reclassify components (5, 7, 15-year property: appliances, fixtures, paving, etc.)
5. Claim bonus depreciation (100% under OBBBA) on reclassified short-life components
6. Depreciation deductions offset other income (if RE Professional or non-passive activity)
7. On future disposition: repeat 1031 exchange to defer again
```

**Why this is the single most valuable real estate tax combination**: The 1031 exchange defers all gain and depreciation recapture from the relinquished property. The cost segregation study on the replacement property resets bonus depreciation on reclassified components — generating massive Year 1 deductions on property acquired with deferred-gain dollars. Especially powerful with 100% bonus depreciation restored under OBBBA. A $2M replacement property might yield $400K-$600K in Year 1 depreciation from reclassified components alone.

### Chain 8: Defined Benefit Plan + S-Corp Reasonable Salary

```
1. Establish S-Corp taxation for the business
2. Determine reasonable salary (this is the control variable)
3. Establish defined benefit plan through third-party administrator
4. Actuary calculates maximum deductible DB plan contribution based on W-2 compensation
5. Higher reasonable salary → higher DB plan contribution ceiling → larger deduction
6. Optimal salary = point where marginal DB plan tax savings exceed marginal payroll tax cost
7. Fund DB plan before entity tax year-end (or extended due date for deduction)
```

**Why salary determination is critical**: The S-Corp reasonable salary directly controls the DB plan contribution ceiling. A higher salary allows a larger DB plan deduction but increases payroll tax (FICA: 15.3% up to wage base, 2.9% Medicare above). For high-income S-Corp owners age 50+, the DB plan deduction can reach $200K-$300K+, making the incremental payroll tax cost trivial by comparison. Example: increasing salary by $50K costs ~$7,650 in payroll tax but may unlock $100K+ in additional DB plan deductions, saving $37,000+ at the 37% bracket. The DB plan also provides asset protection and forced retirement savings.

## STRATEGY CONFLICTS — Cannot Use Both

### Conflict 1: Simplified Home Office vs Regular Method

| Simplified Method | Regular Method (Form 8829) |
|---|---|
| $5/sq ft, max 300 sq ft = $1,500 max | Actual expenses (mortgage interest, taxes, insurance, utilities, depreciation) |
| No depreciation, no carryover | Depreciation of home office portion (recaptured at sale) |
| No Schedule C impact beyond $1,500 | Can create significant deductions |

**Rule**: Cannot use BOTH in the same year for the same home office. Must choose one method each year (can alternate between years).

**Interaction note**: If using the regular method and later selling the home, the depreciation claimed on the home office portion is NOT eligible for the ss121 exclusion — it is recaptured as unrecaptured ss1250 gain at 25%. The simplified method avoids this recapture.

### Conflict 2: Standard Mileage Rate vs Actual Vehicle Expenses

| Standard Mileage | Actual Expenses |
|---|---|
| $0.70/mile (2025) | Gas, insurance, repairs, depreciation, lease payments |
| Must use from first year of business use (or cannot switch to actual later) | Can switch to standard mileage later (with limits) |
| Cannot use for 5+ vehicles simultaneously | Can use for any fleet size |

**Rule**: If actual expenses are used in the first year, you can switch to standard mileage later. But if you claimed depreciation using MACRS (not straight-line) or took a ss179 deduction on the vehicle, you CANNOT switch to standard mileage for that vehicle — ever.

### Conflict 3: S-Corp Accountable Plan vs Personal Form 8829

If a shareholder-employee of an S-Corp claims home office deductions:
- **S-Corp accountable plan**: S-Corp reimburses the shareholder-employee for home office expenses under an accountable plan (substantiated, business purpose, excess returned). Reimbursement is a deduction to the S-Corp, not income to the employee.
- **Personal Form 8829**: Prior to TCJA, employee business expenses were deductible on Schedule A as miscellaneous itemized deductions. Post-TCJA, this deduction is SUSPENDED through 2025.

**Conflict**: An S-Corp shareholder-employee CANNOT deduct home office expenses on their personal return (TCJA suspended the deduction). The ONLY path is through the S-Corp accountable plan. Using BOTH is not possible and would be duplicative.

### Conflict 4: Standard Deduction vs Itemized Deductions

Cannot use both. Choose the higher. Critical interactions:
- If taking the standard deduction: state income tax, mortgage interest, and charitable contributions provide NO federal benefit
- Charitable bunching strategies exist specifically to overcome this (alternate years of standard deduction and itemizing)
- QCDs (for retirees 70.5+) provide a benefit REGARDLESS of standard vs itemized — they reduce AGI, not just taxable income

### Conflict 5: MACRS Bonus Depreciation vs ADS

- MACRS with bonus depreciation: 100% deduction in Year 1 for eligible property
- ADS (Alternative Depreciation System): Straight-line over longer recovery period, no bonus depreciation
- Required for certain foreign-use property, tax-exempt use property, and listed property used <50% for business
- Electing ADS eliminates the AMT depreciation adjustment — but at the cost of much slower depreciation

## PTET — PREREQUISITE REQUIREMENTS AND INTERACTIONS

### What PTET Requires

Pass-Through Entity Tax (PTET) requires:
1. An entity classified as a partnership or S-Corporation for federal tax purposes
2. The state must have enacted PTET legislation (now 36+ states)
3. An annual election (typically irrevocable, with state-specific deadlines)
4. Estimated tax payments at the entity level

### Entities That DO NOT Qualify

- **Single-member LLC (disregarded entity)**: NOT a partnership or S-Corp — does NOT qualify for PTET. Must add a member or elect S-Corp taxation.
- **Sole proprietorship**: Not an entity — does NOT qualify.
- **C-Corporation**: Pays its own income tax — PTET is irrelevant.

### PTET Interaction with Federal Deduction

- The PTET paid by the entity IS deductible on the entity's federal return (reduces pass-through income to owners)
- Owners receive a state tax credit for the PTET paid (on their personal state return)
- Net effect: the state tax that was limited by the personal SALT cap is now an ABOVE-THE-LINE
  business deduction
- Reduces federal taxable income, SE income (for partners), and AGI

### PTET + S-Corp + Solo 401(k) Triple Stack

One of the most powerful combinations for self-employed taxpayers:

1. **S-Corp election**: Reduce SE tax (only pay FICA on reasonable salary, not distributions)
2. **Solo 401(k)**: $23,500 employee deferral + 25% employer match on W-2 (up to $70,000 total in 2025)
3. **PTET**: State tax paid at entity level, bypassing the personal SALT cap

**Worked Example** (California S-Corp, single owner, $300K net income):
- Reasonable salary: $100,000
- Distribution: $200,000
- Solo 401(k): $23,500 (employee) + $25,000 (employer 25% of W-2) = $48,500
- PTET (CA 9.3%): $300,000 x 9.3% = $27,900 (deducted on S-Corp return)
- SE tax savings (vs sole prop): ~$18,850 (15.3% on $200K distribution avoided, minus additional payroll taxes)
- SALT benefit from PTET: $27,900 x 37% marginal rate = ~$10,323 (federal tax savings from deducting the state tax)
- Retirement savings: $48,500 x 37% = $17,945 (current-year tax savings)
- **Combined annual tax savings: ~$47,000+**

## RETIREMENT PLAN INTERACTIONS

### Contribution Limit Aggregation Rules

**ss415(c) — Annual Addition Limit ($70,000 in 2025)**:
Applies PER EMPLOYER. Employer contributions to ALL defined contribution plans of the SAME employer (401(k), SEP, profit-sharing) cannot exceed $70,000 per participant.

**ss402(g) — Employee Deferral Limit ($23,500 in 2025)**:
Applies ACROSS ALL EMPLOYERS. If you have a W-2 day job with a 401(k) AND a side business with a Solo 401(k), your total employee deferrals across both plans cannot exceed $23,500.

### Entity + Retirement Matrix

| Entity Type | Solo 401(k) | SEP-IRA | SIMPLE IRA | Traditional 401(k) |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| Sole Proprietorship | YES (if no other employees) | YES | YES | YES (expensive) |
| Single-Member LLC | YES (if no other employees) | YES | YES | YES |
| S-Corp (owner-only) | YES | YES | YES | YES |
| S-Corp (with employees) | NO (must include employees) | YES (must include eligible employees) | YES (must include employees) | YES (must include employees) |
| Partnership | Each partner separately | YES (each partner) | YES | YES |
| C-Corp | NO (not self-employed) | YES (employer plan) | YES | YES |

### Common Trap: Multiple Businesses

An individual with a W-2 job at Company A (with 401(k)) AND a side S-Corp:
- Company A 401(k) employee deferral: $23,500 (maxed out)
- Side S-Corp Solo 401(k) employee deferral: $0 (aggregation rule — already at limit)
- Side S-Corp Solo 401(k) EMPLOYER contribution: Up to 25% of W-2 from S-Corp (this is separate — ss415(c) is per employer)
- If S-Corp W-2 is $50,000: employer contribution up to $12,500
- **Total retirement savings: $23,500 + $12,500 = $36,000**

## REAL ESTATE + ENTITY MATRIX

### RE Professional Status Requirements

- 750+ hours in real property trades or businesses in which the taxpayer materially participates
- More than 50% of personal services performed are in real property trades or businesses
- Hours counted: development, construction, acquisition, management, operation, leasing, brokerage
- Each rental property is a SEPARATE activity unless the grouping election is made (Reg. ss1.469-9(g))

### Which Spouse Qualifies

Only ONE spouse needs to qualify as a Real Estate Professional. But:
- The qualifying spouse's hours are INDIVIDUAL — cannot count the non-RE spouse's hours
- For material participation in EACH activity (or the grouped activity): BOTH spouses' hours can be counted together (ss469(h)(5) — married filing jointly)
- Strategy: Non-working spouse manages real estate full-time (750+ hours, >50% of services), both spouses participate in management for material participation

### Entity Selection for Rental Real Estate

| Structure | Pros | Cons |
|---|---|---|
| Personal name / Revocable trust | Simplicity, no entity maintenance, step-up at death | No liability protection |
| Single-member LLC (disregarded) | Liability protection, tax simplicity | Cannot use PTET, no SE tax benefit |
| Multi-member LLC (partnership) | Liability + PTET eligibility + flexibility | Complexity, Form 1065 filing |
| S-Corp holding rental | PTET eligibility | Loss of ss1031 exchange simplicity, potential issues with rental at entity level |
| C-Corp holding rental | NONE for rental RE | Double taxation, no ss1031, no pass-through of losses |

### Grouping Election Implications

The grouping election under Reg. ss1.469-9(g) treats ALL rental real estate as a single activity:
- **Benefit**: Only need to materially participate in the GROUPED activity (500+ hours total across all properties), not each individually
- **Risk**: If you dispose of one property, you cannot treat it as a complete disposition of a separate activity (losses may not be fully released)
- **Irrevocability**: Generally irrevocable once made (can be changed only with material change in facts and circumstances)

## INCOME TIER STRATEGIES — PRIORITIZED BY AGI

### Tier 1: AGI $100,000-$250,000

**Priority order** (highest EV first):
1. **Maximize retirement contributions**: 401(k) to max, HSA to max, IRA if eligible
2. **Tax-loss harvesting**: Offset capital gains, $3,000 against ordinary income
3. **Charitable bunching**: Alternate years of standard deduction and itemizing with large charitable gifts in bunching year
4. **529 contributions**: State tax deduction in many states
5. **Mortgage interest optimization**: Ensure deductible interest exceeds standard deduction (with other itemized deductions)

### Tier 2: AGI $250,000-$500,000

**All of Tier 1, plus**:
1. **S-Corp election** (if self-employed): Save 15.3% on distributions above reasonable salary
2. **PTET election**: Bypass the personal SALT cap
3. **Backdoor Roth IRA**: $7,000/year (no income limit via backdoor)
4. **Mega Backdoor Roth**: After-tax 401(k) contributions converted to Roth (up to $70K total, if plan allows)
5. **Cost segregation** on rental properties: Accelerate depreciation
6. **HSA optimization**: High-deductible plan + max HSA + invest the balance

### Tier 3: AGI $500,000-$1,000,000

**All of Tiers 1-2, plus**:
1. **Short-term rental loophole**: $50K-$150K in non-passive losses against W-2
2. **Deferred compensation** (ss409A): Defer bonus/compensation to lower-income future years
3. **Donor Advised Fund**: Large contribution for multi-year charitable deduction in one year
4. **Oil and gas investments**: Intangible drilling costs deduction (if risk-tolerant)
5. **Installment sale (ss453)**: Spread gain from asset sale over multiple years to manage brackets
6. **IRMAA management**: If approaching Medicare age, manage AGI to stay below IRMAA thresholds

### Tier 4: AGI $1,000,000-$5,000,000

**All of Tiers 1-3, plus**:
1. **GRATs** for appreciated assets: Transfer appreciation tax-free
2. **CRTs** for concentrated stock positions: Tax-free sale, charitable deduction, income stream
3. **QSBS planning**: If applicable, structure for ss1202 exclusion
4. **FLPs/LLCs with valuation discounts**: Transfer wealth at discounted value
5. **SLATs**: Use estate tax exemption before TCJA sunset
6. **Dynasty trust**: Multi-generational wealth transfer with GST exemption

### Tier 5: AGI $5,000,000+

**All of Tiers 1-4, plus**:
1. **Installment sale to IDGT**: Transfer $10M-$50M+ with minimal gift tax
2. **Private foundation**: Ongoing family charitable vehicle with control
3. **Opportunity Zone investments**: Exclude appreciation on large capital gains
4. **Defined benefit plan**: Up to $275,000+ annual deductible contributions
5. **Captive insurance (ss831(b))**: Small insurance company owned by family
6. **International tax planning**: Qualified Intermediary structures, treaty benefits
7. **State residency planning**: Establish domicile in no-income-tax state

### Annual Tax Optimization Sequence (Calendar Year)

| Month | Action |
|---|---|
| **January** | PTET election deadline (some states). Review prior year for 65-day trust election. Begin Roth conversion planning. |
| **February** | 65-day election deadline for trust distributions (March 6). File Form 2553 for S-Corp election retroactive to Jan 1 (if filed by March 15). |
| **March 15** | S-Corp election deadline. K-1 preparation. First estimated tax payments due (calendar-year partnerships). |
| **April 15** | Individual returns due. First estimated payment. IRA/Roth IRA contributions for prior year. HSA contributions for prior year. |
| **June 15** | Second estimated payment. Review mid-year income projection. |
| **September 15** | Third estimated payment. Extended S-Corp/partnership returns due. Last chance for retirement plan establishment for prior year (certain plans). |
| **October** | Run AMT projection. Determine ISO exercise headroom. Evaluate charitable strategies. |
| **November** | Tax-loss harvesting scan. Determine Roth conversion amount. Review IRMAA impact. |
| **December** | Execute tax-loss harvesting (mind wash sale rules through Jan 30). Complete Roth conversions. Make charitable contributions. Make 529 contributions for state deduction. Establish Solo 401(k) for current year (if new). Last PTET estimated payments. |
| **December 31** | Deadline for most current-year actions. QOF investments must be made within 180 days (may fall here). |

## AGGRESSIVE POSITION STACKING AND AUDIT RISK

### DIF Score Considerations

The IRS Discriminant Information Function (DIF) score flags returns that deviate significantly from norms for similar taxpayers. Each aggressive position adds to the return's deviation:

| Position | Audit Risk Level | DIF Impact |
|---|---|---|
| Home office deduction | LOW (common post-COVID) | Minimal |
| Schedule C with high deductions | MODERATE | Moderate |
| Real Estate Professional with losses | HIGH | Significant |
| Short-term rental losses offsetting W-2 | MODERATE-HIGH | Significant |
| Large charitable contributions (>30% AGI) | MODERATE | Moderate |
| Cost segregation (first year) | LOW-MODERATE | Moderate |
| FLP valuation discounts (>35%) | HIGH | Significant |
| Captive insurance (ss831(b)) | VERY HIGH | Very significant |
| Multiple aggressive positions stacked | **COMPOUNDING** | **Multiplicative** |

### Stacking Limits — Practical Guidelines

**Conservative approach** (minimize audit risk while maximizing savings):
- Limit to 2-3 significant aggressive positions per return
- Ensure each position has substantial authority (>40% likelihood of being sustained)
- Obtain professional opinions for positions over $100K in tax impact
- Disclose uncertain positions on Form 8275 or 8275-R

**Aggressive approach** (maximum savings, higher audit tolerance):
- Stack 4-6 positions, each individually defensible
- Maintain contemporaneous documentation for every position
- Engage tax counsel to prepare penalty protection opinions
- Consider audit insurance products

### Penalty Protection Hierarchy

| Penalty | Protection Method |
|---|---|
| Accuracy-related penalty (ss6662, 20%) | Reasonable cause + good faith. Adequate disclosure (Form 8275). Substantial authority. |
| Reportable transaction penalty (ss6707A) | Proper disclosure on Form 8886 |
| Economic substance penalty (ss6662(b)(6), 40%) | Cannot be reduced by reasonable cause — transaction must have economic substance |
| Fraud penalty (ss6663, 75%) | None — requires intentional fraud (extremely rare for legitimate strategies) |

## KEY TAKEAWAYS

1. **Strategy chains must be executed in order** — a broken link invalidates everything downstream
2. **Simplified home office and regular method cannot coexist** in the same year
3. **PTET requires a partnership or S-Corp** — single-member disregarded LLCs do not qualify
4. **Retirement contribution limits aggregate across employers** for employee deferrals, but NOT for employer contributions
5. **RE Professional status is per-spouse** — but material participation hours can be combined (MFJ)
6. **Grouping elections are powerful but generally irrevocable** — make them deliberately
7. **Income tier determines strategy priority** — a $250K earner and a $5M earner have completely different playbooks
8. **State strategy availability varies enormously** — always check state conformity before assuming a federal strategy works at the state level
9. **Stacking aggressive positions compounds audit risk** — prioritize the highest-EV strategies and document thoroughly
10. **Run the numbers annually in October** — most year-end strategies require action by December 31
