# Energy and Green Tax Credits -- Comprehensive Guide

## Overview

The Inflation Reduction Act (IRA) of 2022 created the largest package of energy tax incentives in US history, with credits extended and expanded through 2032 and beyond. Combined with existing credits and the OBBBA modifications, energy tax credits represent some of the most generous dollar-for-dollar tax reductions available to individuals and businesses. Understanding the qualification requirements, income limits, and stacking opportunities is essential for maximizing these benefits.

All figures and rules reflect the 2025 tax year.

---

## Residential Clean Energy Credit (IRC Section 25D)

### Credit Rate

**30% of qualified expenditures** through December 31, 2032, then:
- 2033: 26%
- 2034: 22%
- 2035 and after: 0% (expires)

### No Dollar Cap

Unlike the old residential energy credit, section 25D has **no maximum credit amount**. The credit equals 30% of total qualified costs regardless of how large the installation is.

### Qualified Property

| Property Type | Qualifies? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Solar electric (photovoltaic) | YES | Panels, inverters, mounting, wiring, installation |
| Solar water heating | YES | Must be SRCC-certified, at least half for domestic use |
| Small wind energy | YES | Turbines generating electricity for home use |
| Geothermal heat pump | YES | Must meet Energy Star requirements |
| Battery storage (>=3 kWh) | YES | Standalone eligible since IRA (no solar pairing required) |
| Fuel cells | YES | $500/0.5 kW cap per residence |
| Biomass stoves (>=75% thermal efficiency) | NO (moved to section 25C) | Removed from section 25D |

### Key Rules

- **Primary or secondary residence** -- must be your home (can be a second home, vacation home)
- **NOT for rental property** -- property you rent to others does NOT qualify for section 25D. Instead, energy improvements to rental property are depreciable assets (and may qualify for business energy credits)
- **New construction:** Qualifies if property is installed on a home you own and use as a residence
- **Carryforward:** Unused credit carries forward indefinitely to future tax years
- **Non-refundable:** Cannot generate a refund -- reduces tax liability to $0, remainder carries forward
- **No income limit:** Available at all income levels

### Basis Reduction

The credit does NOT reduce the property's basis for depreciation purposes (because it is a personal residence, not depreciable). However, the credit-eligible amount does reduce the cost basis for purposes of calculating gain on sale of the home (but the section 121 exclusion usually makes this moot).

### Worked Example: Solar + Battery

```
Installation:
  Solar panel system (8 kW):          $22,000
  Battery storage (13.5 kWh Tesla):    $12,000
  Installation labor:                  $ 3,000
  Total qualified cost:                $37,000

Section 25D credit: $37,000 x 30% =   $11,100

Tax liability (before credit):         $18,000
Credit applied:                        $11,100
Remaining tax liability:               $ 6,900

Effective cost of system: $37,000 - $11,100 = $25,900
Payback period (assuming $3,000/year energy savings): ~8.6 years
```

## Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit (IRC Section 25C)

### Credit Rate

**30% of qualified expenditures**, subject to annual limits.

### Annual Limits (Reset Each Year)

| Category | Annual Limit | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| **Overall cap** | $1,200/year total | For non-heat-pump items |
| Windows and skylights | $600/year | Must meet Energy Star Most Efficient criteria |
| Exterior doors | $500/year ($250 per door) | Must meet Energy Star criteria |
| Home energy audit | $150/year | By qualified auditor |
| Electrical panel/wiring upgrade | $600/year | Must be for qualified equipment |
| Insulation and air sealing | $1,200/year (part of overall) | Must meet IECC standards |
| **Heat pumps / heat pump water heaters** | $2,000/year | SEPARATE from the $1,200 cap |
| **Biomass stoves/boilers** | $2,000/year | SEPARATE from the $1,200 cap |

### Maximum Annual Credit

The maximum credit in a single year is **$3,200**: $1,200 from the general pool + $2,000 for heat pump/biomass items.

### Annual Reset

Unlike the old lifetime limit, the section 25C credit resets **every year**. A taxpayer can claim up to $3,200 in 2025, another $3,200 in 2026, etc.

### Qualifying Products

Products must meet specific energy efficiency standards (generally Energy Star or IECC criteria). The manufacturer's certification is required -- look for the "Meets or Exceeds 25C Requirements" designation.

| Product | Efficiency Requirement |
|---|---|
| Central air conditioner | SEER2 >= 16, EER2 >= 12.5 |
| Air-source heat pump | SEER2 >= 16, EER2 >= 12, HSPF2 >= 9 |
| Natural gas furnace | AFUE >= 97% |
| Natural gas water heater | UEF >= 0.81 |
| Heat pump water heater | UEF >= 2.2 |
| Electric panel upgrade | 200+ amps, required for qualified equipment |

### Not Eligible

- New construction (must be existing home improvement)
- Rental property improvements
- Improvements to common areas of condos (individual unit improvements may qualify)

## Electric Vehicle Credits

### New Clean Vehicle Credit (IRC Section 30D)

**Credit amount:** Up to **$7,500** for qualifying new electric vehicles.

The credit has two components ($3,750 each):
1. **Critical minerals requirement:** Specified percentage of battery critical minerals must be extracted or processed in the US or a free trade agreement country
2. **Battery component requirement:** Specified percentage of battery components must be manufactured or assembled in North America

| Year | Critical Minerals % | Battery Components % |
|---|---|---|
| 2025 | 50% | 60% |
| 2026 | 60% | 70% |
| 2027 | 70% | 80% |
| 2028 | 80% | 90% |
| 2029+ | 80% | 100% |

### MSRP Limits

| Vehicle Type | MSRP Cap |
|---|---|
| Sedans, hatchbacks, wagons | $55,000 |
| SUVs, vans, pickup trucks | $80,000 |

Classification follows the EPA's CAFE classification (not marketing category). Some vehicles marketed as "sedans" are classified as SUVs by the EPA (check fueleconomy.gov for the official classification).

### Income Limits

| Filing Status | MAGI Limit |
|---|---|
| Single | $150,000 |
| Head of Household | $225,000 |
| Married Filing Jointly | $300,000 |

MAGI is the lesser of the current year or the prior year (taxpayer-friendly: if income dropped this year, use current year; if income was lower last year, use last year).

### Point-of-Sale Transfer

Since January 1, 2024, the credit can be transferred to the dealer at the point of sale, reducing the purchase price immediately. The dealer files the paperwork.

### Final Assembly Requirement

The vehicle must be **finally assembled in North America** (US, Canada, or Mexico). Check the VIN against the DOE's list at fueleconomy.gov/feg/tax2025.shtml.

### Foreign Entity of Concern (FEOC) Restriction

Starting 2025, vehicles with battery components from a "foreign entity of concern" (primarily China, Russia, North Korea, Iran) are disqualified from the $3,750 battery component portion. Starting 2025, critical minerals from FEOC sources disqualify the $3,750 minerals portion.

### Qualifying Vehicles (2025 Examples)

| Vehicle | Credit Amount | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Tesla Model 3 (Long Range RWD) | $7,500 | Must be assembled in US |
| Tesla Model Y | $7,500 | Austin, TX assembly |
| Chevrolet Equinox EV | $7,500 | |
| Ford Mustang Mach-E | $3,750-$7,500 | Depends on battery sourcing |
| Rivian R1S/R1T | $3,750 | Partial qualification |
| Hyundai IONIQ 5 (US-built) | $7,500 | Georgia assembly plant |

**Check fueleconomy.gov for current qualifying vehicle list -- it changes as manufacturers adjust supply chains.**

### Used Clean Vehicle Credit (IRC Section 25E)

**Credit amount:** Lesser of **$4,000 or 30%** of the purchase price.

### Requirements

| Requirement | Detail |
|---|---|
| Purchase location | Must be from a **licensed dealer** (not private party) |
| Model year | At least **2 years** prior to year of sale |
| Price cap | $25,000 or less |
| First transfer | Must be the first transfer of the used vehicle (no second used-vehicle credits on same car) |
| Battery capacity | Minimum 7 kWh |

### Income Limits (Stricter Than New Vehicle)

| Filing Status | MAGI Limit |
|---|---|
| Single | $75,000 |
| Head of Household | $112,500 |
| Married Filing Jointly | $150,000 |

### Point-of-Sale Transfer

Available since January 1, 2024 -- dealer applies credit to reduce purchase price at time of sale.

### Commercial Clean Vehicle Credit (IRC Section 45W)

For vehicles used in a **trade or business** or leased to individuals.

| Vehicle Type | Credit Rate | Maximum Credit |
|---|---|---|
| EV / plug-in hybrid (no gas engine, or battery >=15 kWh for gas+electric) | 30% of basis | $7,500 (GVWR <14,000 lbs) or $40,000 (GVWR >=14,000 lbs) |
| Non-EV (other qualifying vehicles) | 15% of basis | Same caps |

### The Leasing Loophole

When a consumer **leases** an EV, the leasing company (not the consumer) is the purchaser. The leasing company claims the section 45W commercial vehicle credit. Benefits:

1. **No MSRP cap** -- a $90,000 EV qualifies for the commercial credit even though it exceeds the $55K/$80K section 30D cap
2. **No income limit** -- the consumer's income is irrelevant (the leasing company claims the credit)
3. **No assembly or battery sourcing requirements** -- section 45W has different rules than section 30D
4. **Lower lease payments** -- the dealer/leasing company passes credit savings to the lessee as reduced monthly payments

**Strategy:** If your income exceeds $150K/$300K, or you want a vehicle exceeding the MSRP cap, consider leasing instead of buying to capture the credit indirectly.

## Business Energy Investment Tax Credit (IRC Section 48)

### Overview

The ITC provides a percentage credit for the cost of qualifying energy property placed in service by a business.

### Credit Rates

| Scenario | Credit Rate |
|---|---|
| Base rate (without prevailing wage + apprenticeship) | 6% |
| With prevailing wage + apprenticeship (PW+A) requirements met | **30%** |
| PW+A + domestic content bonus | **40%** |
| PW+A + energy community bonus | **40%** |
| PW+A + low-income community bonus | **40-50%** |
| Maximum combined (PW+A + domestic + energy community) | **50%** |

**Prevailing wage requirements:** All laborers and mechanics must be paid prevailing wages (as determined by the Department of Labor) during construction and for 5 years after placed in service.

**Apprenticeship requirements:** Specified percentage of labor hours must be performed by qualified apprentices (12.5% in 2025, increasing to 15% for projects beginning after 2025).

### Qualifying Property

| Property | ITC Eligible? | Notes |
|---|---|---|
| Solar (PV and thermal) | YES | Most common ITC property |
| Small wind (<100 kW) | YES | |
| Geothermal | YES | |
| Battery storage (>=5 kWh) | YES | Standalone eligible since IRA |
| Microgrid controllers | YES | New category from IRA |
| Fuel cells | YES | |
| Combined heat and power (CHP) | YES | |
| Biogas (renewable natural gas) | YES | |
| Interconnection property | YES | Costs of connecting to the grid |
| Waste energy recovery | YES | |

### Recapture

If the property is disposed of or ceases to qualify during the **5-year recapture period**, a portion of the credit must be recaptured:
- Year 1: 100% recaptured
- Year 2: 80%
- Year 3: 60%
- Year 4: 40%
- Year 5: 20%
- After Year 5: no recapture

### Basis Reduction

The depreciable basis of the property is reduced by **50%** of the ITC claimed.

**Example:** $100,000 solar system, 30% ITC = $30,000 credit. Depreciable basis = $100,000 - ($30,000 x 50%) = $85,000. The $85,000 is then eligible for 5-year MACRS + bonus depreciation.

## Production Tax Credit (IRC Section 45)

### Overview

The PTC provides a per-kilowatt-hour credit for electricity generated from qualifying renewable energy sources over a 10-year period beginning when the facility is placed in service.

### Credit Rates (2025 Estimated)

| Scenario | Rate per kWh |
|---|---|
| Base rate (without PW+A) | ~$0.55/kWh |
| With PW+A requirements met | ~$2.75/kWh |

(Rates are indexed for inflation annually; exact 2025 rate published in IRS guidance.)

### Qualifying Sources

Wind, closed-loop biomass, open-loop biomass, geothermal, solar (can elect PTC instead of ITC), small irrigation power, municipal solid waste, hydropower, marine and hydrokinetic.

### PTC vs. ITC Election

Solar and wind projects can elect either the PTC or the ITC (but not both). Generally:
- **PTC is better** for projects with high capacity factors (wind, solar in sunny locations) -- the cumulative 10-year PTC often exceeds the one-time ITC
- **ITC is better** for projects with high upfront costs relative to production (battery storage, geothermal) or when the investor needs immediate tax reduction

### Technology-Neutral Credits (Section 45Y/48E)

Beginning 2025, new "technology-neutral" clean electricity credits (sections 45Y and 48E) supplement the existing technology-specific credits. These apply to any facility with net-zero greenhouse gas emissions, providing flexibility for emerging technologies.

## Energy Efficient Commercial Building Deduction (IRC Section 179D)

### Overview

Section 179D provides an **above-the-line deduction** (not a credit, but economically similar) for energy-efficient improvements to commercial buildings.

### Deduction Amount

| Scenario | Maximum Deduction |
|---|---|
| Base rate (without PW+A) | $1.07/sq ft |
| With PW+A requirements | Up to $5.36/sq ft |

The deduction is calculated based on the percentage improvement in energy efficiency relative to ASHRAE Reference Standard 90.1:
- Must meet **25% energy savings** threshold to qualify
- Sliding scale from 25% to 50% savings

### Qualifying Systems

- Interior lighting
- HVAC and hot water systems
- Building envelope (insulation, windows, roofing)

### Special Rule: Designers of Government Buildings

Architects, engineers, contractors, and designers who create the specifications for energy-efficient improvements to **government-owned buildings** can claim the section 179D deduction themselves (since the government entity is tax-exempt). This is a powerful benefit for design professionals.

### Retrofits

A new "retrofit" pathway allows deductions for improvements to existing buildings that achieve at least 25% reduction in energy use intensity, with a 5-year measurement period.

## Home Battery Storage

### Standalone Eligibility (Since IRA)

Before the IRA, battery storage had to be paired with solar to qualify for the section 25D credit. The IRA made **standalone battery storage** eligible for the 30% residential credit, provided the battery has a capacity of at least **3 kilowatt-hours**.

### Qualifying Systems

| Product | Capacity | Approximate Cost | 30% Credit |
|---|---|---|---|
| Tesla Powerwall 3 | 13.5 kWh | $12,000-15,000 | $3,600-4,500 |
| Enphase IQ Battery 5P | 5 kWh | $5,000-7,000 | $1,500-2,100 |
| LG RESU Prime | 16 kWh | $10,000-13,000 | $3,000-3,900 |
| Generac PWRcell | 9-18 kWh | $10,000-20,000 | $3,000-6,000 |
| Franklin WH aPower | 13.6 kWh | $12,000-15,000 | $3,600-4,500 |

### Use Cases for Standalone Battery (Without Solar)

1. **Backup power:** Grid outage protection (increasingly valuable with extreme weather)
2. **Time-of-use arbitrage:** Charge from grid during off-peak, discharge during peak pricing
3. **Demand charge reduction:** For businesses with demand charges
4. **Grid services:** Participating in utility demand response programs (income from this IS taxable)

## Rental Property Energy Improvements

### Section 25D Does NOT Apply to Rental Property

The residential clean energy credit (section 25D) requires the property to be **used as a residence by the taxpayer**. A property rented to tenants does not qualify.

### What to Do Instead

For rental properties:
1. **Depreciate the improvement** under normal MACRS rules (solar = 5-year MACRS, building improvements = 15 or 39-year)
2. **Claim bonus depreciation** (100% under OBBBA for post-Jan 19, 2025 acquisitions)
3. **Claim the business ITC (section 48)** for commercial rental properties with qualifying energy installations -- this is often MORE valuable than section 25D because of the depreciation combination

### Worked Example: Solar on Rental Property

```
Solar installation on commercial rental building: $100,000
Section 48 ITC (30% with PW+A): $30,000 credit
Depreciable basis: $100,000 - ($30,000 x 50%) = $85,000
5-year MACRS with 100% bonus depreciation: $85,000 deduction in Year 1

Total Year 1 benefit:
  ITC credit: $30,000
  Depreciation deduction: $85,000 x 37% marginal rate = $31,450
  Total tax benefit: $61,450 on a $100,000 investment (61.45% return)
```

This combined ITC + depreciation approach is one of the most powerful tax strategies available for commercial real estate.


## The EV Leasing Loophole -- Detailed Analysis

### Why Leasing Can Be Better Than Buying

For an EV that does NOT qualify for the section 30D consumer credit (foreign assembly, MSRP over cap, income over limit):

```
Scenario: Consumer wants a $65,000 EV assembled outside North America
  Section 30D credit: $0 (fails assembly requirement)
  
  Lease instead:
    Leasing company claims section 45W (30% x $65,000 = up to $7,500)
    Dealer passes through as lower capitalized cost
    Consumer effectively receives $7,500 benefit as lower payments
    
  36-month lease payment reduction: ~$208/month ($7,500 / 36)
```

### Vehicles That Benefit Most from Leasing

1. Foreign-assembled EVs (BMW iX, Mercedes EQS, Polestar 2)
2. EVs exceeding MSRP caps (Rivian R1S fully loaded, high-trim Tesla Model X)
3. Buyers exceeding income limits ($300K+ MFJ)

## Solar + Battery Comprehensive Strategy

### Optimal Configuration

1. **Size solar to offset 100-120% of annual electricity usage** (slight overproduction accounts for degradation)
2. **Add battery storage for**: backup power, time-of-use optimization, and solar self-consumption
3. **Claim 30% section 25D credit** on entire system (solar + battery + installation)
4. **Sell excess power via net metering** (income IS taxable, but usually modest)
5. **Combine with heat pump** for additional section 25C credit ($2,000/year)

### Net Metering Income

Excess electricity sold back to the utility is generally taxable income:
- If you receive cash payments: reported as income
- If you receive bill credits that exceed your annual usage: the excess is income
- If credits merely offset your own consumption: generally not taxable (reduces your cost, not income)

IRS has not issued comprehensive guidance on net metering taxation. Conservative approach: report any net cash received as miscellaneous income.

### 10-Year Financial Model

```
System: 10 kW solar + 13.5 kWh battery
Total cost: $37,000
Federal credit (30%): $11,100
Net cost: $25,900
Annual energy savings: $3,200/year (rising ~3%/year with utility inflation)
System degradation: ~0.5%/year

Year 1: $3,200 savings + $11,100 credit = $14,300 benefit
Year 2-8: ~$3,200-3,800/year savings
Year 8: Cumulative savings = $25,900 (payback achieved)
Year 9-25: Net profit phase -- $3,800-5,500/year in energy savings
25-year net benefit: ~$60,000-80,000 (depending on utility rate growth)
```

## Interaction with Other Tax Provisions

### AMT

Section 25D and section 25C credits are **allowed against AMT** (section 25(e)(1)(C)). This makes them particularly valuable for taxpayers in AMT territory.

### Passive Activity Rules

The section 48 business ITC is subject to passive activity limitations if the taxpayer is a passive investor. However, real estate professionals (750+ hours, material participation) can claim the credit against active income.

### At-Risk Rules

Section 48 ITC is limited to the taxpayer's amount at-risk in the activity. Non-recourse financing from unrelated parties generally counts as at-risk for real estate.

### Section 179D and Section 199A

The section 179D deduction reduces QBI for purposes of the section 199A deduction. This interaction should be modeled before claiming large 179D deductions.
