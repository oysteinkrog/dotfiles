# California Tax Guide

## Overview
- Income tax: Yes
- Top marginal rate: 13.3% (12.3% + 1% Mental Health Services Tax)
- Filing requirement threshold: Approximately $21,540 (Single) / $43,080 (MFJ) for 2025 (approximately; verify against FTB current-year guidance)
- Fiscal year: Calendar year
- Administering agency: Franchise Tax Board (FTB)

## Standard Deduction / Personal Exemption
- Single: $5,540 standard deduction; $144 personal exemption credit
- MFJ: $11,080 standard deduction; $288 personal exemption credit ($144 x 2)
- Head of Household: $11,080 standard deduction; $144 personal exemption credit
- Dependent exemption credit: $446 per dependent
- Phaseout: Personal exemption credits phase out starting at $220,890 (Single) / $441,782 (MFJ)

## Key State-Specific Deductions and Credits
1. **Renter's credit** — $60 (Single) / $120 (MFJ) nonrefundable credit for qualifying renters (AGI limits apply)
2. **CalEITC (California Earned Income Tax Credit)** — Refundable; up to ~$3,529 with qualifying children; earned income must be under ~$30,950
3. **Young Child Tax Credit** — Up to $1,117 refundable credit for qualifying children under 6 (combined with CalEITC eligibility)
4. **Child and Dependent Care Credit** — Based on federal credit, scaled by income
5. **529 Plan** — California does NOT offer a state tax deduction for 529 contributions
6. **Mortgage interest** — Conforms to federal limits ($750K acquisition debt)
7. **Charitable contributions** — Follows federal rules but California does not allow SALT deduction
8. **NOL limitations** — California frequently suspends NOL deductions and business credit usage for high earners
9. **Itemized deductions** — No deduction for state/local taxes paid (California does not allow SALT deduction on state return)
10. **Capital gains** — Taxed as ordinary income (no preferential rate)

## Filing Requirements
- Forms: Form 540 (resident), Form 540NR (nonresident/part-year), Form 540 2EZ (simplified)
- Due date: April 15
- Extension: Automatic 6-month extension to October 15 (no form needed; just pay estimated tax due)
- E-file options: CalFile (FTB free e-file), FTB-approved commercial software
- Mandatory e-file for preparers with 100+ returns

## Estimated Tax Payments
- Required when: Expected tax liability exceeds $500 ($250 MFS) after withholding/credits
- Due dates: April 15 (30%), June 15 (40%), January 15 (remaining 30%) — NOTE: California uses an unusual 30/40/0/30 schedule, NOT equal quarters
- Safe harbor: 100% of prior year tax or 90% of current year tax; for AGI over $1M in prior year, must pay 90% of current year (no prior-year safe harbor)

## Pass-Through Entity Tax (PTE Elective Tax)
- Available: Yes — California calls it the **PTE Elective Tax** (not PTET)
- Election deadline: Made on an original, timely filed return; first payment due June 15
- Rate: 9.3% on qualified net income of consenting members
- Mechanics: Electing entities pay 9.3% at entity level. Qualified members receive a nonrefundable credit against California personal income tax. Credit can reduce tax to the tentative minimum tax. Excess credit carries forward 5 years.
- **Important**: The 1% Mental Health Services Tax is NOT included in the PTE rate — members earning over $1M still owe the extra 1% personally
- Two required payments: (1) $1,000 or 50% of prior year's PTE tax by June 15; (2) remainder by original return due date

## Local/City Income Taxes
- California does not have local/city income taxes
- San Francisco has a payroll tax and gross receipts tax on businesses, but no individual income tax
- Los Angeles has a business tax but no individual income tax

## Special Features and Gotchas
1. **Highest tax rate in the US** — The 13.3% combined rate (12.3% + 1% Mental Health Services Tax) is the highest state income tax rate
2. **No capital gains preference** — Long-term capital gains are taxed at full ordinary income rates, making California especially expensive for asset sales
3. **Estimated payment schedule is unusual** — 30/40/0/30 (not 25/25/25/25); missing the June 15 payment (40%) is costly
4. **$1M+ filers lose prior-year safe harbor** — Must pay 90% of current year tax; cannot rely on 100% of prior year if prior-year AGI exceeded $1M
5. **Community property state** — Income splitting rules apply; each spouse owns half of community income
6. **IRC conformity gaps** — California often does not conform to recent federal changes; always verify current conformity date
7. **NOL/credit suspensions** — FTB periodically suspends NOLs and certain credits for high-income filers during budget crises

## Retirement Income Treatment
- **Social Security**: Fully exempt from California income tax
- **Pensions (public and private)**: Fully taxable as ordinary income — no exclusion
- **401(k)/IRA distributions**: Fully taxable as ordinary income
- **Military pensions**: Fully taxable (California does not exempt military retirement)
- **Railroad Retirement Tier I**: Exempt (follows federal treatment)
- **Overall**: California is one of the least retirement-friendly states due to high rates and no pension/retirement exclusions
