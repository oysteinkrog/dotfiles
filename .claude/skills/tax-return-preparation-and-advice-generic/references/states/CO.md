# Colorado Tax Guide

## Overview
- Income tax: Yes
- Top marginal rate: 4.4% (flat rate, reduced from 4.55% effective 2024)
- Filing requirement threshold: Must file if required to file federal return and had Colorado-source income
- Fiscal year: Calendar year
- Administering agency: Colorado Department of Revenue

## Income Tax Rates (2025)

### All Filing Statuses
| Taxable Income | Rate |
|---|---|
| All Colorado taxable income | 4.4% |

Colorado uses a flat rate applied to federal taxable income with Colorado modifications. The rate has been reduced through TABOR refund mechanisms: 4.63% (through 2019) -> 4.55% (2020-2023) -> 4.4% (2024+).

## Standard Deduction / Personal Exemption
- Colorado uses **federal taxable income** as its starting point, so the federal standard deduction or itemized deductions are already incorporated
- No separate Colorado standard deduction or personal exemption
- Colorado additions and subtractions modify federal taxable income to arrive at Colorado taxable income

## Key State-Specific Deductions and Credits
1. **TABOR refunds** — Colorado's Taxpayer Bill of Rights (TABOR) requires excess state revenue to be refunded to taxpayers. Mechanism varies by year (sales tax refund, EIC enhancement, or flat amount per filer).
2. **State EITC** — 25% of the federal Earned Income Tax Credit (refundable); among the most generous state EITCs
3. **Child tax credit** — Colorado enacted a state child tax credit; up to $1,200 per child under 6, phasing down for older children and higher incomes
4. **529 Plan deduction** — Full deduction for contributions to CollegeInvest 529 plans (no dollar cap); must be Colorado plan
5. **Charitable contribution deduction** — Taxpayers who claim the federal standard deduction can still deduct 50% of charitable contributions over $500 on their Colorado return
6. **Wildfire mitigation credit** — Up to $2,500 for qualifying wildfire mitigation measures on property
7. **Renewable energy credit** — Various credits for solar, EV, and energy efficiency improvements
8. **Military pension subtraction** — $15,000 for ages 55-64; $20,000 for ages 65+
9. **Social Security subtraction** — Fully deductible for ages 65+; up to $20,000 for under 65 (with income limits)
10. **Property tax/rent/heat credit (PTC)** — Refundable credit for low-income elderly/disabled residents

## Filing Requirements
- Forms: DR 0104 (individual income tax return), DR 0104AD (additions/subtractions)
- Due date: April 15
- Extension: Automatic 6-month extension to October 15 (no form needed; pay any estimated tax due by April 15)
- E-file options: Revenue Online portal, approved commercial software

## Estimated Tax Payments
- Required when: Expected net tax liability exceeds $1,000
- Due dates: April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15
- Safe harbor: Pay 70% of current year tax or 100% of prior year tax
- Penalty rate: Assessed quarterly on underpayments

## Pass-Through Entity Tax (PTET)
- Available: Yes (enacted 2022, SB 22-124)
- Election deadline: Made on a timely filed composite return (due date of entity's return, including extensions)
- Rate: 4.4% (same as individual flat rate)
- Mechanics: Electing PTE pays tax on behalf of members at entity level; members claim a credit on their Colorado return. Available for partnerships, S corporations, and LLCs taxed as partnerships.

## Local/City Income Taxes
- **Denver** — Occupational Privilege Tax (OPT): $5.75/month for employees, $4/month for employers, per employee. This is not an income tax but is commonly encountered.
- **Aurora, Greenwood Village, Sheridan, Glendale** — Also levy OPT at similar low amounts
- No Colorado city imposes a true local income tax on a percentage of income
- Various local sales/use taxes apply

## Special Features and Gotchas
1. **TABOR is unique nationally** — Colorado is the only state with a constitutional requirement to refund excess revenue; the refund mechanism changes annually and can be confusing
2. **Federal taxable income is the starting point** — Changes to federal deductions/credits automatically flow through to Colorado; no separate state standard deduction to claim
3. **Charitable deduction for standard deduction filers** — Unusual provision allowing partial charitable deduction even when taking the federal standard deduction
4. **529 deduction has no cap** — Colorado is one of the few states allowing unlimited 529 deductions (must be Colorado plan)
5. **Flat rate but effectively progressive** — Because Colorado starts from federal taxable income (which includes progressive federal deductions), the system is more progressive than a simple flat rate suggests

## Retirement Income Treatment
- **Social Security**: Fully exempt for ages 65+; up to $20,000 subtraction for under 65 (income-limited)
- **Federal/state/local pensions**: Subtraction of up to $20,000 for ages 55-64; $24,000 for ages 65+
- **Military pensions**: $15,000 subtraction for ages 55-64; $20,000 for ages 65+
- **401(k)/IRA distributions**: Qualify for the pension subtraction amounts above
- **Colorado is moderately retirement-friendly** especially for ages 65+ with the Social Security exemption and pension subtraction
