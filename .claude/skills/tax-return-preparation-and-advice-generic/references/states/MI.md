# Michigan Tax Guide

## Overview
- Income tax: Yes
- Top marginal rate: 4.25% (flat rate)
- Filing requirement threshold: Gross income exceeds the current Michigan filing threshold/personal exemption amount; verify the annually indexed Treasury figure
- Fiscal year: Calendar year
- Administering agency: Michigan Department of Treasury

## Income Tax Rates (2025)

### All Filing Statuses
| Taxable Income | Rate |
|---|---|
| All Michigan taxable income | 4.25% |

Michigan uses a flat rate. The rate was temporarily reduced to 4.05% for 2023 via a TABOR-like revenue trigger, but reverted to 4.25% for 2024+.

## Standard Deduction / Personal Exemption
- Michigan does not use a standard deduction
- **Personal exemption**: Indexed annually; verify the current Treasury amount before finalizing the return
- **Dependent exemption**: Generally matches the current personal exemption amount
- Michigan starts with federal AGI and applies its own exemptions and deductions
- Exemptions phase out for high-income filers

## Key State-Specific Deductions and Credits
1. **Homestead Property Tax Credit** — Refundable credit for property taxes exceeding 3.5% of household income. Max credit $1,700 for most filers; $1,500 for seniors. Income limits: $63,000 for non-seniors; $67,700 for seniors.
2. **Home Heating Credit** — Refundable credit for low-income households to offset heating costs (income-limited)
3. **529 Plan deduction** — Up to $5,000 ($10,000 MFJ) for contributions to Michigan Education Savings Program (MESP)
4. **Child/dependent care credit** — Based on federal credit
5. **Michigan EITC** — 30% of federal EITC (refundable); significantly expanded in 2023
6. **Retirement income subtraction** — Complex; depends on birth year (see Retirement Income section below)
7. **Charitable contributions** — Not separately deductible (Michigan does not allow itemized deductions)
8. **Stillborn child exemption** — A special exemption is available; use the current annually indexed exemption amount

## Filing Requirements
- Forms: MI-1040 (all individual filers), MI-1040CR (homestead property tax credit), MI-1040CR-2 (home heating credit)
- Due date: April 15
- Extension: Automatic 6-month extension to October 15 if federal extension is filed; otherwise file Form 4
- E-file options: Michigan Treasury Online (MTO), approved commercial software

## Estimated Tax Payments
- Required when: Expected tax liability exceeds $500 after withholding and credits
- Due dates: April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15
- Safe harbor: Pay 100% of prior year tax or 90% of current year tax

## Pass-Through Entity Tax (PTET)
- Available: Yes (enacted 2023, HB 4288)
- Election deadline: Made on or before the original due date of the entity's return for the election year
- Rate: 4.25% (same as individual flat rate)
- Mechanics: Electing entities pay 4.25% at entity level. Members receive a refundable credit on MI-1040. Available for S-corps, partnerships, and LLCs taxed as partnerships.

- **24 Michigan cities** levy local income taxes
- Nonresident rate is typically half the resident rate
- City taxes are filed on separate city returns (not on the state MI-1040)
- Employer withholding is required for city taxes

## Special Features and Gotchas
1. **City income taxes are separate filings** — Unlike Maryland's piggyback tax, Michigan city taxes require separate returns filed directly with the city. Detroit's 2.4% rate is significant.
2. **Retirement income rules changed dramatically** — A 2023 law changed retirement income taxation based on birth year (see below). This is complex and frequently misunderstood.
3. **No standard deduction and no itemized deductions** — Michigan only allows its personal exemptions. There is no standard deduction or ability to itemize — a unique feature that increases taxable income relative to other states.
4. **Homestead Property Tax Credit requires separate form** — Must file MI-1040CR even if not otherwise required to file a return; many eligible filers miss this refundable credit.
5. **Flat rate but multiple local taxes** — The 4.25% state rate looks low, but a Detroit resident pays 6.65% combined (4.25% + 2.4%). Plan for city taxes.

## Retirement Income Treatment
Michigan's retirement income treatment depends on birth year (post-2023 reform):

**Born before 1946:**
- Public pensions: Fully exempt
- Private pensions/401(k)/IRA: Exempt up to $61,062 (Single) / $122,123 (MFJ)
- Social Security: Fully exempt

**Born 1946-1952:**
- May choose between: (a) pension/retirement subtraction of $20,000 (Single) / $40,000 (MFJ), OR (b) Senior standard deduction of $20,000/$40,000 against all income
- Social Security: Fully exempt

**Born 1953-1958:**
- Pension/retirement subtraction of $20,000 (Single) / $40,000 (MFJ)
- Social Security: Fully exempt

**Born 1959-1966:**
- Pension/retirement subtraction phasing in at age 62
- Social Security: Fully exempt (phased in per the 2023 law)

**Born after 1966:**
- No pension subtraction until age 67
- Social Security: Fully exempt once fully phased in

**Military pensions**: Fully exempt regardless of birth year
