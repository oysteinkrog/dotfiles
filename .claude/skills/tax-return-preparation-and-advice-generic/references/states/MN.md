# Minnesota Tax Guide

## Overview
- Income tax: Yes
- Top marginal rate: 9.85%
- Filing requirement threshold: Gross income exceeds Minnesota standard deduction + personal exemption for filing status
- Fiscal year: Calendar year
- Administering agency: Minnesota Department of Revenue

## Income Tax Rates (2025)

### Single
| Minnesota Taxable Income | Rate |
|---|---|
| $0 - $31,690 | 5.35% |
| $31,691 - $104,090 | 6.80% |
| $104,091 - $183,340 | 7.85% |
| Over $183,340 | 9.85% |

### Married Filing Jointly
| Minnesota Taxable Income | Rate |
|---|---|
| $0 - $46,330 | 5.35% |
| $46,331 - $184,040 | 6.80% |
| $184,041 - $321,450 | 7.85% |
| Over $321,450 | 9.85% |

Brackets are indexed annually for inflation.

## Standard Deduction / Personal Exemption
Minnesota generally conforms to federal standard deduction amounts:
- Single: $15,750
- MFJ: $31,500
- Head of Household: $23,625
- Minnesota does not allow personal exemptions (conforms to TCJA elimination)
- Dependent exemption: $4,950 per dependent (Minnesota-specific)
- Minnesota allows itemized deductions as an alternative (with some modifications from federal)

## Key State-Specific Deductions and Credits
1. **Minnesota Child Tax Credit** — Up to $1,750 per qualifying child (refundable); income limits apply. One of the most generous state child credits.
2. **Working Family Credit (state EITC)** — Percentage of federal EITC (45% for filers with children; up to 72% for childless filers). Refundable.
3. **K-12 Education Credit** — Up to $1,000 per child for qualifying K-12 expenses (income-limited)
4. **K-12 Education Subtraction** — Up to $1,625 per child in grades K-6; $2,500 per child in grades 7-12 (no income limit)
5. **529 Plan subtraction** — Up to $1,500 ($3,000 MFJ) for contributions to any state's 529 plan; increased for lower-income filers
6. **Property tax refund** — Separate filing on Form M1PR; refund for homeowners whose property tax exceeds percentage of income, and for renters (19% of rent deemed property tax)
7. **Social Security subtraction** — Partial or full subtraction based on income (see Retirement section)
8. **Marriage credit** — Reduces marriage penalty for two-income couples
9. **Credit for non-MN state taxes** — Generous credit for taxes paid to other states

## Filing Requirements
- Forms: M1 (all individual filers), M1PR (property tax refund — separate filing)
- Due date: April 15
- Extension: Automatic 6-month extension to October 15 if federal extension is filed; otherwise file Form M13
- E-file options: Minnesota e-Services portal, approved commercial software

## Estimated Tax Payments
- Required when: Expected tax liability exceeds $500 after withholding and credits
- Due dates: April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15
- Safe harbor: Pay 100% of prior year tax or 90% of current year tax (110% if prior year income exceeds $150,000 / $75,000 MFS)

## Pass-Through Entity Tax (PTET)
- Available: Yes (enacted 2023, HF 1938)
- Election deadline: Made on or before the due date (including extensions) of the entity's return
- Rate: Progressive rates matching individual brackets (5.35% / 6.80% / 7.85% / 9.85%)
- Mechanics: Electing entities pay tax at entity level on qualifying members' share of Minnesota income. Members receive a refundable credit on Form M1. Available for partnerships, S-corps, and LLCs.

## Local/City Income Taxes
- Minnesota does not have local/city income taxes
- Municipalities rely on property taxes (which are relatively high)
- Local sales taxes exist in some cities (up to 2% on top of the 6.875% state rate)
- Special taxing districts may apply in metro areas (e.g., Hennepin County 0.15% sales tax)

## Special Features and Gotchas
1. **Property tax refund is a separate filing** — Form M1PR must be filed separately from Form M1, with a different deadline (August 15). Many taxpayers miss this refundable benefit.
2. **High rates begin at modest incomes** — The 9.85% top rate applies starting at $183,340 (Single), making Minnesota one of the highest-tax states for upper-middle-income earners
3. **Social Security taxation** — Minnesota partially taxes Social Security (one of a handful of states), though it has been steadily expanding the exemption
4. **Child tax credit is very generous** — The $1,750 per child refundable credit is among the most generous in the US
5. **K-12 subtraction AND credit** — Minnesota uniquely offers both a subtraction (no income limit) and a credit (income-limited) for K-12 expenses; optimize between them
6. **AMT** — Minnesota imposes its own Alternative Minimum Tax (6.75% on Minnesota AMTI over $40,000 Single / $60,000 MFJ)

## Retirement Income Treatment
- **Social Security**: Partially exempt — Minnesota provides a subtraction that phases out at higher income levels. For 2025, income below $78,000 (Single) / $100,000 (MFJ) gets full subtraction; phases out above.
- **Public pensions (MSRS, TRA, PERA)**: Taxable as ordinary income (no exclusion)
- **Federal pensions**: Taxable as ordinary income
- **Military pensions**: Fully exempt from Minnesota income tax (enacted 2023)
- **Private pensions/401(k)/IRA**: Taxable as ordinary income
- **Minnesota is NOT retirement-friendly** — High rates, Social Security partially taxed, and no pension exclusion (except military)
