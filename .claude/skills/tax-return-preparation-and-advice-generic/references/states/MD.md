# Maryland Tax Guide

## Overview
- Income tax: Yes
- Top marginal rate: 5.75% state + up to 3.2% county = up to 8.95% combined
- Filing requirement threshold: Gross income exceeds Maryland's minimum filing requirement for the taxpayer's filing status; use the current Comptroller chart rather than prior-year estimates
- Fiscal year: Calendar year
- Administering agency: Comptroller of Maryland

## Standard Deduction / Personal Exemption
- Standard deduction: 15% of Maryland AGI, with minimum $1,800 and maximum $2,550 (Single); minimum $3,600 and maximum $5,150 (MFJ)
- Personal exemption: $3,200 per person
- Dependent exemption: $3,200 per dependent
- Phaseout: Exemptions phase out at higher income levels ($150,000+ Single / $200,000+ MFJ)

## Key State-Specific Deductions and Credits
1. **Earned income credit** — 45% of federal EITC (refundable for filers with qualifying children; 100% of federal EITC for childless filers) — one of the most generous state EITCs
2. **Property tax credit** — Up to $1,750 Homeowners' Tax Credit based on income and property tax
3. **Renters' tax credit** — Up to $1,000 for qualifying renters (income-limited, age 60+ or disabled)
4. **529 Plan deduction** — Up to $2,500 per beneficiary ($5,000 MFJ) for Maryland 529 contributions; unlimited carryforward
5. **Child and dependent care credit** — Percentage of federal credit based on income
6. **Preservation and conservation easement credit** — For qualifying donations of land
7. **Student loan debt relief credit** — Up to $5,000 for qualifying student loan payments (competitive application)
8. **Venison donation credit** — Up to $50 per deer donated to food banks (uniquely Maryland)
9. **Long-term care insurance credit** — For qualifying premiums

## Filing Requirements
- Forms: Form 502 (resident), Form 505 (nonresident), Form 515 (nonresident local tax return)
- Due date: April 15
- Extension: Automatic 6-month extension to October 15 if federal extension is filed; otherwise file Form 502E
- E-file options: Comptroller iFile portal (free), approved commercial software
- Maryland requires electronic filing for most tax preparers

## Estimated Tax Payments
- Required when: Expected state + county tax liability exceeds $500 after withholding and credits
- Due dates: April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15
- Safe harbor: Pay 110% of prior year tax or 90% of current year tax

## Pass-Through Entity Tax (PTET)
- Available: Yes (enacted 2020, one of the earliest states)
- Election deadline: Annual election; made on the entity's return
- Rate: 8% on resident members' share; for nonresident members, combined state + county rate (up to ~8.95%)
- Mechanics: Electing entities (S-corps, partnerships, LLCs) pay tax at entity level. Members receive a credit on Form 502. Maryland's PTET rate is set at 8%, which approximates the combined state + county rate for most members.

## Local/City Income Taxes
- **All 23 counties and Baltimore City** levy a "piggyback" county income tax — see rate table above
- County tax is filed on the same state return (Form 502); no separate county filing
- Rates range from 2.25% (Worcester) to 3.20% (Baltimore City, Baltimore County, Howard, Montgomery, Prince George's)
- County tax is based on county of residence on the last day of the tax year
- The combined state + county rate can reach 8.95% (5.75% + 3.20%)

## Special Features and Gotchas
1. **County piggyback tax is mandatory** — Every Maryland resident pays both state AND county tax. The combined rate of up to 8.95% makes Maryland one of the highest-tax states in practice.
2. **Standard deduction is percentage-based** — Maryland's standard deduction is 15% of AGI with a cap, not a fixed dollar amount. This means it grows with income but is capped at $2,550/$5,150.
3. **PTET at flat 8%** — Maryland's PTET rate is a flat 8%, not the marginal rate. This works well for high earners but may overtax lower-income PTE members.
4. **Nonresident filing complexity** — Maryland requires both a state (Form 505) and local (Form 515) return for nonresidents. The local tax return is easily overlooked.
5. **Two-income married credit** — Maryland offers a credit for two-income MFJ couples to reduce marriage penalty
6. **Baltimore City considerations** — Baltimore City residents face the highest combined rate (8.95%) and must be aware that Baltimore City is independent from Baltimore County

## Retirement Income Treatment
- **Social Security**: Exempt for filers under income thresholds ($75,000 Single / $100,000 MFJ); partially taxable above
- **Pensions**: Up to $39,500 exclusion per person (age 65+; lesser amounts for younger retirees) for qualifying pension income
- **401(k)/IRA distributions**: Qualify for the pension exclusion above
- **Military pensions**: First $12,500 exempt for retirees under 55; full pension exclusion for 55+
- **State/local pensions (Maryland)**: Qualify for pension exclusion
- **Maryland is moderately retirement-friendly** for those qualifying for pension exclusion, but high county taxes reduce the benefit
