# Texas Tax Guide

## Overview
- Income tax: No (constitutionally prohibited)
- Top marginal rate: N/A (no personal income tax)
- Filing requirement threshold: N/A

## Income Tax Rates (2025)
Texas imposes no personal income tax. The Texas Constitution (Article VIII, Section 24-a, adopted 1993) explicitly prohibits a personal income tax unless approved by voters in a statewide referendum.

## Standard Deduction / Personal Exemption
- N/A (no income tax)

## Key State-Specific Deductions and Credits
- N/A for individual income tax purposes
- **Franchise (Margin) Tax**: Applies to businesses (see Business Tax section)
- No estate tax
- No inheritance tax

## Filing Requirements
- No individual income tax return required
- Businesses with total revenue exceeding $2,470,000 must file franchise tax report
- Employers have no state income tax withholding obligation

## Estimated Tax Payments
- N/A for individual income tax
- Franchise tax is paid annually with the report

## Pass-Through Entity Tax (PTET)
- Not applicable (no individual income tax to work around)
- Note: Texas franchise tax already applies at the entity level to pass-throughs

## Local/City Income Taxes
- None. No Texas municipality levies an income tax
- Texas law prohibits local income taxes

## Special Features and Gotchas
1. **High property taxes**: Texas compensates for no income tax with some of the highest property taxes in the nation (effective rates 1.6-2.5%+)
2. **No income tax is constitutional**: Requires voter approval to enact; very unlikely to change
3. **Franchise tax catches pass-throughs**: Unlike most no-income-tax states, Texas has a franchise (margin) tax that applies to LLCs, partnerships, and S corps — not just C corps
4. **Sales tax**: 6.25% state rate; combined with local rates up to 8.25%
5. **No state capital gains tax**: Investment income is untaxed, making TX attractive for investors and retirees

## Retirement Income Treatment
- **Social Security**: Not taxed (no income tax)
- **Pensions/401(k)/IRA**: Not taxed (no income tax)
- **Military retirement**: Not taxed (no income tax)
- Texas is highly favorable for retirees (but high property taxes may offset benefit for homeowners)

## Business Tax Considerations
### Franchise (Margin) Tax
- **Applies to**: Most business entities (corporations, LLCs, partnerships, LPs, LLPs, professional associations) with Texas revenue
- **Exempt**: Sole proprietorships and general partnerships wholly owned by natural persons are exempt
- **No-tax-due threshold**: Entities with total revenue at or below $2,470,000 (2025) owe no tax but may still need to file
- **E-Z computation**: Entities with revenue up to $20M can use simplified computation
- **Rate**: 
  - General: 0.75% of taxable margin
  - Retail/wholesale: 0.375% of taxable margin
  - E-Z computation: 0.331% of total revenue (no deductions)
- **Taxable margin**: Lesser of: (a) 70% of total revenue, or (b) total revenue minus COGS, OR total revenue minus compensation, OR total revenue minus $1M
- **Due date**: May 15 annually (calendar year; 30 days after federal for fiscal year)
- **Extension**: Automatic extension to November 15

### Important Franchise Tax Notes
- The franchise tax is sometimes called the "Texas margin tax"
- S corps and LLCs are NOT exempt (unlike pure no-income-tax states)
- Professional service firms (lawyers, doctors, accountants) cannot use COGS method
- Combined group reporting required for affiliated entities

## Property Tax
- **Among highest in the nation**: Effective rates typically 1.6-2.5%
- **Homestead exemption**: $100,000 for school district taxes (increased from $40,000 in 2023)
- **Over-65 exemption**: Additional $10,000+ for school districts; tax ceiling freeze
- **Disabled veteran exemption**: Up to 100% exemption for qualifying disabled veterans
- **No state property tax**: All property tax is local (school districts, counties, cities, special districts)
- **Protest rights**: Robust appraisal protest process (file by May 15 or 30 days after notice)

## Multi-State Considerations
- Texas residents working remotely for employers in income-tax states may owe tax to the employer's state
- Texas has no reciprocity agreements (none needed since no income tax)
- Popular relocation destination, especially from California and New York
- Day-trading and investment income: fully untaxed at state level
