# Massachusetts Tax Guide

## Overview
- Income tax: Yes
- Top marginal rate: 9% (5% base + 4% millionaire's surtax on income over $1M)
- Filing requirement threshold: Gross income exceeds $8,000 (Single) or filing status thresholds
- Fiscal year: Calendar year
- Administering agency: Massachusetts Department of Revenue (DOR)

## Income Tax Rates (2025)

### All Filing Statuses
| Taxable Income | Rate |
|---|---|
| All income up to $1,000,000 | 5% |
| Income over $1,000,000 | 9% (5% + 4% surtax) |

The 4% surtax on income over $1M was enacted by ballot initiative (Question 1, "Fair Share Amendment") in November 2022, effective for tax year 2023 and beyond. The $1M threshold is indexed for inflation.

### Short-Term Capital Gains
- Short-term capital gains (assets held < 1 year): 12% flat rate
- This is HIGHER than the regular income tax rate — a unique Massachusetts feature

### Long-Term Capital Gains
- Long-term capital gains (assets held 1+ year): 5% (9% above $1M with surtax)

## Standard Deduction / Personal Exemption
Massachusetts does not use a traditional standard deduction. Instead:
- **Personal exemption**: $4,400 (Single), $8,800 (MFJ)
- **Dependent exemption**: $1,000 per dependent
- Massachusetts uses its own income categories and starts from Massachusetts gross income, not federal AGI
- No itemized deductions in the traditional sense; Massachusetts has specific allowed deductions

## Key State-Specific Deductions and Credits
1. **No-tax status** — No tax if Massachusetts AGI is below $8,000 (Single) / $14,400 (MFJ)
2. **Rental deduction** — 50% of rent paid, up to $4,000 (not available to those who own their home)
3. **Commuter deduction** — Reasonable transit commuting costs, up to $750
4. **529 Plan** — Massachusetts does NOT offer a state tax deduction for 529 contributions
5. **Child and dependent care credit** — Based on federal credit; limited
6. **Earned income credit** — 40% of federal EITC (refundable); among the more generous state EITCs
7. **Senior circuit breaker credit** — Refundable credit up to $2,590 for qualifying seniors (65+) whose property tax (or 25% of rent) exceeds 10% of income
8. **Lead paint credit** — Up to $1,500 for qualifying lead paint removal
9. **Septic system repair credit** — Up to $6,000 for failed Title 5 septic system repair (over 4 years)
10. **Charitable deduction** — Allowed as a deduction from Massachusetts gross income (with limits)

## Filing Requirements
- Forms: Form 1 (resident), Form 1-NR/PY (nonresident/part-year)
- Due date: April 15
- Extension: Automatic extension to October 15 if no additional tax is due; file Form M-4868 if payment is needed
- E-file options: MassTaxConnect portal, approved commercial software

## Estimated Tax Payments
- Required when: Expected tax liability exceeds $400 after withholding and credits
- Due dates: April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15
- Safe harbor: Pay 80% of current year tax or 100% of prior year tax (Massachusetts has a lower current-year threshold than many states)

## Pass-Through Entity Tax (PTET)
- Available: Yes (enacted 2021, effective for tax years beginning on or after January 1, 2021)
- Election deadline: Made on entity's return; annual election required
- Rate: 5% on electing members' share of income (does NOT include the 4% surtax)
- Mechanics: Electing entities pay 5% at entity level. Members receive a 90% credit against their Massachusetts personal income tax (not 100% — the 10% haircut is important). Available for S-corps, partnerships, and LLCs.
- **Note**: The 4% millionaire's surtax still applies at the individual level on income over $1M, even for PTET participants

## Local/City Income Taxes
- Massachusetts does not have local/city income taxes
- No municipality in Massachusetts levies an income tax
- Municipalities rely on property taxes (which can be very high in eastern Massachusetts)
- Boston, Cambridge, and other cities have high property tax rates

## Special Features and Gotchas
1. **Millionaire's surtax creates a cliff** — At $1M of income, the marginal rate jumps from 5% to 9%. Income just above $1M is taxed at an effective marginal rate of 9%. This creates strong incentive for income timing/deferral strategies.
2. **Short-term capital gains taxed at 12%** — Massachusetts taxes short-term gains at 12%, HIGHER than the regular 5% rate. This is the highest short-term CG rate of any state and penalizes frequent trading.
3. **PTET credit is only 90%** — Unlike most states that give a 100% credit, Massachusetts only gives 90%. The 10% reduction must be factored into PTET benefit calculations.
4. **No traditional standard deduction or itemized deductions** — Massachusetts uses its own deduction system; you cannot simply transfer federal itemized deductions
5. **Inventory of deductions is limited** — Only specific deductions are allowed (rent, commuter, charitable, medical); there is no general "itemized deductions" category
6. **Two-income couple deduction** — MFJ filers where both spouses have earned income can deduct up to $1,200

## Retirement Income Treatment
- **Social Security**: Fully exempt from Massachusetts income tax
- **Government pensions (federal/state/local)**: Fully exempt if from a qualifying contributory plan
- **Military pensions**: Fully exempt
- **Private pensions**: Taxable as ordinary income (5% / 9% above $1M)
- **401(k)/IRA distributions**: Taxable as ordinary income
- **Massachusetts is moderately retirement-friendly** for government/military retirees but not for private sector retirees
