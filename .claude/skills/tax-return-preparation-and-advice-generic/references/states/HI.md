# Hawaii Tax Guide

## Overview
- Income tax: Yes
- Top marginal rate: 11% (second highest in the US)
- Filing requirement threshold: Gross income exceeds the current federal filing threshold (about $15,750 Single for 2025)
- Fiscal year: Calendar year
- Administering agency: Hawaii Department of Taxation (DOTAX)

## Standard Deduction / Personal Exemption
- Single: $2,200 standard deduction; $1,144 personal exemption
- MFJ: $4,400 standard deduction; $2,288 combined personal exemptions
- Head of Household: $3,212 standard deduction; $1,144 personal exemption
- Dependent exemption: $1,144 per dependent
- Hawaii has its own standard deduction amounts (much lower than federal)

## Key State-Specific Deductions and Credits
1. **General Excise Tax (GET) credit** — Refundable credit of $35-$50 per exemption for low-income filers (AGI under $30,000 Single / $50,000 MFJ)
2. **Refundable food/excise tax credit** — Up to $110 per exemption (income-limited)
3. **Renewable energy credit** — 35% of installed cost for solar energy systems (one of the most generous in the US)
4. **Child and dependent care credit** — Based on federal credit
5. **Low-income household renter's credit** — $50 per qualified exemption (AGI under $30,000)
6. **Exceptional tree credit** — Unique to Hawaii; credit for maintaining exceptional trees on property
7. **529 Plan** — Hawaii does not offer a state deduction for 529 contributions
8. **No SALT deduction** — Hawaii does not allow deduction of state taxes on the state return
9. **Cost of living adjustment** — Hawaii's lower bracket thresholds partially reflect COLA considerations

## Filing Requirements
- Forms: N-11 (resident), N-15 (nonresident/part-year)
- Due date: April 20 (NOTE: Hawaii deadline is April 20, not April 15)
- Extension: Automatic 6-month extension to October 20 (file N-200V with payment)
- E-file options: Hawaii Tax Online portal, approved commercial software

## Estimated Tax Payments
- Required when: Expected tax liability exceeds $500 after withholding and credits
- Due dates: April 20, June 20, September 20, January 20 (all shifted from typical 15th)
- Safe harbor: Pay 100% of prior year tax or 60% of current year tax (Hawaii's safe harbor is lower than most states)

## Pass-Through Entity Tax (PTET)
- Available: No — Hawaii has not enacted a PTET election as of 2025
- Hawaii PTE members cannot use a SALT cap workaround through the state

## Local/City Income Taxes
- Hawaii does not have local/city income taxes
- The state's General Excise Tax (GET) functions similarly to a sales tax but is imposed on businesses
- County surcharge on GET: Honolulu adds 0.5% surcharge (total 4.5% in Honolulu vs 4% in other counties)
- Maui and Hawaii counties may also impose surcharges

## Special Features and Gotchas
1. **April 20 filing deadline** — Hawaii's deadline is April 20, not April 15. This is unique.
2. **General Excise Tax (GET) is NOT a sales tax** — GET is imposed on the business, not the consumer. It applies to virtually all business activity including services, rent, and interest. Businesses often pass it through. Rate is 4% (4.5% in Honolulu).
3. **12 tax brackets** — Hawaii has more income tax brackets than any other state (12), creating a highly progressive structure
4. **Very low standard deduction** — At $2,200 (Single), Hawaii's standard deduction is much lower than federal, increasing taxable income
5. **High cost of living** — Bracket thresholds have not kept pace with Hawaii's extremely high cost of living, pushing middle-income earners into high brackets
6. **No capital gains preference** — Capital gains are taxed as ordinary income at rates up to 11%

## Retirement Income Treatment
- **Social Security**: Fully exempt from Hawaii income tax
- **Public pensions (state/local)**: Distributions from employer contributions are taxable; distributions from employee contributions are tax-free (return of basis)
- **Private pensions**: Taxable as ordinary income
- **401(k)/IRA distributions**: Taxable as ordinary income
- **Military pensions**: Taxable as ordinary income (no special exclusion)
- **Hawaii is NOT retirement-friendly** — High rates, no general pension exclusion, and high cost of living
