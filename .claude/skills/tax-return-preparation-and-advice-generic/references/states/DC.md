# District of Columbia Tax Guide

## Overview
- Income tax: Yes
- Top marginal rate: 10.75%
- Filing requirement threshold: Gross income equals or exceeds the DC filing chart amount: $15,000 (Single/MFS/dependent filer), $22,500 (HOH), or $30,000 (MFJ/QSS)
- Fiscal year: Calendar year
- Administering agency: DC Office of Tax and Revenue (OTR)

## Income Tax Rates (2025)

### All Filing Statuses (Same Brackets)
| DC Taxable Income | Rate |
|---|---|
| $0 - $10,000 | 4% |
| $10,001 - $40,000 | 6% |
| $40,001 - $60,000 | 6.5% |
| $60,001 - $250,000 | 8.5% |
| $250,001 - $500,000 | 9.25% |
| $500,001 - $1,000,000 | 9.75% |
| Over $1,000,000 | 10.75% |

Note: DC uses the same brackets regardless of filing status (Single, MFJ, HoH all use the same table).

## Standard Deduction / Personal Exemption
- DC uses its own 2025 basic standard deduction amounts rather than simply following the newer federal amounts:
  - Single/MFS/dependent filer: $15,000
  - Head of Household: $22,500
  - MFJ / Qualifying surviving spouse: $30,000
- DC explicitly decoupled from the higher post-OBBBA federal basic standard deduction and several new federal above-the-line deductions
- Personal exemption: DC provides a $4,150 personal exemption per person (DC-specific, not tied to federal which eliminated personal exemptions under TCJA)
- Dependent exemption: $4,150 per dependent

## Key State-Specific Deductions and Credits
1. **DC EITC** — 70% of federal EITC for filers without qualifying children; 40% for filers with qualifying children (one of the most generous in the US)
2. **DC Child Tax Credit** — Up to $420 per qualifying child
3. **Property tax credit** — Schedule H credit for homeowners and renters (income-limited; up to $1,425 for 2025)
4. **529 Plan deduction** — Up to $4,000 per beneficiary ($8,000 MFJ) for contributions to DC 529 plan
5. **DC Homeowner/Renter credit** — Additional credits for long-term DC residents
6. **First-time homebuyer credit** — DC offers a credit/deduction for first-time homebuyers
7. **Long-term care insurance credit** — 10% of premiums paid
8. **Federal employee considerations** — DC residents who are federal employees are fully subject to DC income tax on all wages

## Filing Requirements
- Forms: D-40 (resident), D-40B (nonresident requesting refund), D-40EZ (simplified)
- Due date: April 15
- Extension: Automatic 6-month extension to October 15 (file FR-127 to request; pay estimated tax by April 15)
- E-file options: MyTax.DC.gov portal, approved commercial software

## Estimated Tax Payments
- Required when: Expected tax liability exceeds $100 after withholding and credits
- Due dates: April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15
- Safe harbor: Pay 110% of prior year tax or 90% of current year tax

## Pass-Through Entity Tax (PTET)
- Available: No — DC has not enacted a pass-through entity tax election as of 2025
- DC residents who are members of PTEs in other states should check if those states offer PTET elections

## Local/City Income Taxes
- DC functions as both a state and a city, so there is no additional local tax layer
- The DC income tax IS the local tax
- No additional county or municipality taxes
- DC has a 6% sales tax (10% on restaurants/prepared food, 10.25% on hotels)

## Special Features and Gotchas
1. **Federal employees** — A large percentage of DC residents work for the federal government. All federal wages are taxable in DC; there is no reciprocity agreement reducing DC tax for federal workers.
2. **No voting representation** — DC residents pay full federal and DC taxes but have no voting representation in Congress (taxation without representation)
3. **High income tax rates** — The 10.75% top rate makes DC one of the highest-tax jurisdictions, combined with high cost of living
4. **Same brackets for all filing statuses** — MFJ filers get no bracket advantage over Single filers, creating a marriage penalty
5. **Reciprocity agreements** — DC has reciprocity with Maryland and Virginia for commuters, but DC residents working in MD/VA may still owe DC tax on all income
6. **Estate tax** — DC imposes its own estate tax with a $4,528,800 exemption (2025, indexed), lower than the federal exemption

## Retirement Income Treatment
- **Social Security**: Fully exempt from DC income tax
- **Federal pensions (CSRS/FERS)**: Taxable as ordinary income (no special exclusion)
- **Military pensions**: Taxable as ordinary income
- **DC government pensions**: Taxable as ordinary income
- **401(k)/IRA distributions**: Taxable as ordinary income
- **DC is NOT retirement-friendly** — High rates and no pension exclusions make it expensive for retirees
