# Construction Trades — Tax Reference Guide (TY 2025)
## Electricians, Plumbers, Carpenters, HVAC, Ironworkers, Masons, Roofers

## 1. Overview

Construction tradespeople work as W-2 union employees, nonunion employees, or self-employed
contractors. The tax landscape revolves around tool deductions, safety equipment, union
considerations, travel to temporary jobsites, per diem, equipment depreciation for
self-employed contractors, and the critical distinction between temporary and permanent work
locations for mileage deductions. Post-TCJA, W-2 employees lost most unreimbursed expense
deductions federally, making state-level deductions and proper employment classification
more important than ever. Construction trades are NOT specified service trades or businesses
(SSTB), meaning the full 20% QBI deduction is available at all income levels.

## 2. Common Income Types

- **W-2 salary/wages** — Union and nonunion employees. Includes base hourly rate, overtime
  (time-and-a-half, double-time), shift premiums, per diem (if from employer).
- **Union supplemental benefits** — Pension contributions, health insurance, annuity fund
  contributions (employer-paid, not taxable to worker until distributed).
- **1099-NEC** — Self-employed subcontractors. Common for specialty trades and independent
  contractors working for general contractors.
- **Per diem from employer** — Tax-free if under accountable plan and within federal rates.
  Common on out-of-town projects.
- **Prevailing wage jobs** — Davis-Bacon Act (federal) and state prevailing wage laws require
  higher pay on government-funded projects. The prevailing wage includes both the basic
  hourly rate and fringe benefits. Employers may pay fringe benefits as cash (taxable) or
  as benefits (health insurance, pension — generally not taxable until distributed). All
  prevailing wage cash compensation is taxed as ordinary income.
- **Unemployment insurance** — Taxable. Common in seasonal construction markets.
- **Side work ("side jobs")** — Cash payments for residential work. MUST be reported as
  taxable income even without 1099.

## 3. Key Deductions

### W-2 Employees (Limited Post-TCJA)
- **Unreimbursed expenses** — NOT deductible on federal return (2018-2025). This includes
  tools, safety equipment, work boots, and union dues. Major change from pre-TCJA law.
- **Union dues** — NOT deductible federally post-TCJA (2018-2025). This is a significant
  loss for construction union members paying $500-$2,000+/year in dues. However, union
  dues remain deductible in approximately 15 states including CA, NY, NJ, PA, MN, HI,
  AR, and others. File state itemized return to claim.
- **Per diem from employer** — If employer provides per diem under accountable plan, it's
  tax-free income (not on W-2).
- **State-level deductions** — Several states still allow unreimbursed employee expenses
  (see state section). Worth filing state itemized return.

### Self-Employed Subcontractors (Schedule C)

#### Tools and Equipment
- **Hand tools** — Hammers, drills, levels, measuring tools. Deduct in year purchased if
  useful life under one year or cost under de minimis threshold ($2,500 per item under
  Reg. 1.263(a)-1(f)(1)(ii)(D)). No depreciation needed for items under $2,500.
- **Power tools** — Table saws, compressors, nail guns, rotary hammers. Section 179
  ($2,500,000 limit) or depreciate over useful life (5-7 year MACRS).
- **Heavy equipment** — Excavators, skid steers, boom lifts, scaffolding systems. Section 179
  for items placed in service during the year, or MACRS depreciation: 5-year for
  construction machinery, 7-year for other equipment.
- **Equipment rental** — Renting specialized equipment (crane, trencher, concrete pump)
  for specific jobs is fully deductible as a business expense in the year paid.
- **Tool replacement** — Replacement of worn/broken tools deductible immediately.
- **Tool insurance** — Deductible if insuring business tools.

#### Safety Equipment
- **Work boots / safety shoes** — Steel-toe, composite-toe boots required by jobsite.
  Deductible for self-employed. Must be required as a condition of employment AND not
  suitable for everyday wear. Steel-toed boots clearly qualify. Standard work boots
  (without safety features) are more debatable.
- **Hard hats, safety glasses, hearing protection** — Required PPE, fully deductible.
- **Fall protection** — Harnesses, lanyards, retractable lifelines for self-employed.
- **High-visibility clothing** — Required on jobsites, deductible.
- **Specialized clothing** — Welding jackets, FR (fire-resistant) clothing, insulated
  coveralls for cold-weather work.

#### Vehicle and Travel
- **Temporary vs. permanent jobsite** — This is the KEY distinction for construction
  worker mileage deductions (see Section 7 for detailed rules).
- **Standard mileage** — $0.70/mile (2025). Must track with contemporaneous log.
- **Work truck** — If used for hauling tools/materials to jobsites. Section 179 for vehicles
  over 6,000 lbs GVWR (up to $31,300 for SUVs; no limit for vehicles not designed for
  personal use like cargo vans, pickup trucks with full-size bed, and utility trucks).
  A $60,000 work truck (Ford F-250, Ram 2500) over 6,000 lbs GVWR can be fully expensed
  under Section 179.
- **Multi-jobsite travel** — Travel BETWEEN two jobsites in a single day is deductible
  (not commuting). Example: electrician works at site A in the morning, drives to site B
  in the afternoon. The drive from A to B is deductible regardless of whether the sites
  are temporary or permanent.
- **Per diem for out-of-town work** — Away from home overnight, $69/day (CONUS) for meals.
  50% deductible (NOT 80% — the 80% rate is only for DOT hours-of-service workers like
  truck drivers, not construction workers).

#### Business Operations
- **License and certification** — Journeyman/master licenses, OSHA-10/OSHA-30 certifications,
  EPA certifications (HVAC refrigerant handling), backflow certification (plumbers).
  All deductible as business expenses.
- **Continuing education** — Code update courses, license renewal CE, manufacturer training
  (e.g., specific HVAC equipment certification). Deductible.
- **Insurance** — General liability ($500-$3,000+/year), workers' comp (if self-employed with
  employees), builders risk, umbrella/excess liability.
- **Bonding costs** — Surety bond premiums for licensed contractors. Required for many
  government and commercial contracts. Deductible.
- **Subcontractor payments** — Must issue 1099-NEC for $600+ to subs.
- **Materials (if not reimbursed)** — Supplies and materials for jobs.

## 4. Worker Classification — Deep Dive

Worker classification is THE biggest audit and enforcement issue in construction. The IRS,
DOL, and state agencies aggressively target construction misclassification.

### Federal Tests

- **IRS 20-Factor Test (Revenue Ruling 87-41)** — Examines behavioral control (instructions,
  training, set hours), financial control (unreimbursed expenses, investment, profit/loss
  opportunity, market availability), and relationship type (employee benefits, permanency,
  integral to business). No single factor is determinative.
- **Economic Reality Test (DOL/FLSA)** — Focuses on economic dependence: is the worker
  economically dependent on the employer, or in business for themselves?

### State Tests — Often Stricter

- **California AB5 / ABC Test** — Presumes ALL workers are employees unless the hiring
  entity proves all three factors: (A) worker is free from control and direction, (B) work
  is outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business, (C) worker is customarily
  engaged in an independently established trade. Factor B is the killer for construction:
  a framing subcontractor hired by a framing contractor is doing work "within the usual
  course" of the hiring entity's business. AB5 has specific construction exemptions for
  licensed subcontractors, but the exemption conditions are strict.
- **Massachusetts** — Presumes employee status unless 3-part test is met (similar to ABC).
- **New York** — Uses separate tests for different purposes (UI, workers' comp, wage/hour).
  Construction industry has special presumption of employment.
- **New Jersey** — ABC test with strict interpretation. Heavy enforcement.

### Penalties for Misclassification
- **Employer**: Back employment taxes (100% of employee share + employer share), penalties
  (up to 100% of taxes owed), interest, state UI/WC deficiency, potential fraud penalties.
- **Worker**: May owe SE tax on income that should have had FICA. But worker can file
  Form SS-8 for IRS determination and may receive retroactive W-2 treatment.

## 5. Temporary vs. Permanent Jobsite — The Key Rule

*Peurifoy v. Commissioner* (358 U.S. 59, 1958) established the principle that construction
workers at temporary jobsites can deduct transportation costs, while workers at permanent
locations cannot.

### Current Rule (Rev. Rul. 99-7)

- **Temporary jobsite** — Expected to last ONE YEAR OR LESS. Travel from home (or between
  jobsites) is deductible. Per diem is available if away overnight.
- **Permanent jobsite** — Expected to last MORE THAN ONE YEAR. Travel from home is
  commuting — NOT deductible. No per diem.
- **Realistic expectation** — The expectation must be realistic at the time work begins.
  If a 6-month project is extended repeatedly and eventually reaches 14 months, it becomes
  permanent PROSPECTIVELY from the date the expectation changed.
- **Worked example**: Ironworker is assigned to a bridge project expected to take 10 months.
  He travels 45 miles each way from home. Deduction: 90 miles/day x $0.70 x 220 work
  days = $13,860. After 8 months, the project is extended to 18 months total. From the
  date of the extension, the site becomes permanent, and remaining mileage is not deductible.

### Apprenticeship Tax Credit (IRC 45S)

- **Employer credit** — Available to employers who hire registered apprentices. Credit equals
  the lesser of $1,500 per apprentice or 10% of apprentice wages paid during the tax year.
  For apprentices in their first or second year.
- **How it works** — GC or subcontractor employing registered apprentices through a DOL-
  registered apprenticeship program can claim the credit. Applies to both union and non-union
  apprenticeships.

## 6. Recommended Entity Structure

- **Union employee** — W-2. No entity needed. Focus on state-level deductions and retirement.
- **Independent subcontractor** — Sole proprietor initially. LLC for liability protection.
  S-Corp election when net income exceeds $60K-$80K.
- **Licensed general contractor** — LLC or S-Corp. Required by most states for GC license.
  S-Corp saves SE tax on income above reasonable salary.
- **Small construction company** — LLC taxed as S-Corp (1-2 owners) or partnership (3+ owners).
  Must carry workers' comp, general liability. Payroll obligations for employees.
- **Multiple entity strategy** — Operating company (S-Corp) owns the business; separate LLC
  holds equipment and leases to operating company. Protects high-value equipment from
  business liability claims. The equipment LLC receives lease income (not subject to SE
  tax if the member is not materially participating in the operating company through the
  equipment LLC).

## 7. Retirement Plan Strategy

- **Union employees** — Multi-employer pension fund (defined benefit), union annuity fund
  (similar to 401(k)). Benefits negotiated in collective bargaining agreement. Vesting
  varies (typically 5-10 years for pension). Portability: union members who work for
  multiple signatory contractors keep accruing in the same multi-employer plan.
- **Union supplemental contributions** — Some unions allow voluntary additional contributions
  to annuity fund.
- **Non-union employees** — Employer 401(k) if available. Many small construction companies
  do not offer retirement plans.
- **Self-employed** — Solo 401(k) or SEP-IRA. Discretionary contributions handle income
  volatility. Solo 401(k) preferred for maximum flexibility.
- **Defined benefit plan** — For successful contractors age 45+ with consistent high income.
  Shelter $280,000+ annually.
- **IRA** — $7,000/$8,000 (50+). Roth preferred for workers in moderate brackets.
  Saver's Credit available for lower-income workers.

## 8. Common Audit Triggers

- **Employee vs. contractor misclassification** — THE biggest audit issue in construction.
  IRS and state agencies (especially in CA, NY, IL, MA) aggressively audit.
- **Cash income not reported** — Side jobs paid in cash. IRS uses lifestyle analysis and
  bank deposit method to identify unreported income.
- **Large tool deductions** — Self-employed workers claiming $10K-$20K+ in tools per year
  need detailed records (receipts, list of items, business necessity).
- **Mileage claims** — Must distinguish temporary vs. permanent jobsites. A jobsite where
  you work for 14 months is a permanent location — mileage is NOT deductible.
- **Per diem without overnight stay** — Per diem only available when away from home overnight.
  Day-trip per diem claims are invalid.

## 9. Profession-Specific Rules

- **Temporary vs. permanent jobsite (Rev. Rul. 99-7)** — See Section 5 above for detailed rules.
- **Section 199A (QBI)** — Construction trades are NOT SSTBs. Full 20% QBI deduction
  available at all income levels (W-2/UBIA limitations apply above threshold).
  Significant benefit for self-employed tradespeople.
- **Apprenticeship tax credits** — IRC 45S employer credit for registered apprentices. Work
  Opportunity Tax Credit (WOTC) may also apply for certain targeted groups.
- **IRC 460 — Long-term contracts** — Contracts spanning more than one tax year: percentage-
  of-completion method required for AMT and for regular tax if contract exceeds $30M.
  Under $30M: completed contract method allowed (defer income until project complete).
  Small contractors under $30M average annual gross receipts can use the completed
  contract method or exempt-contract percentage-of-completion.
- **Section 179D — Energy-efficient commercial buildings** — Contractors working on energy-
  efficient building improvements may benefit from this deduction (allocated to designer
  for government buildings). The deduction can be up to $5/sq ft for buildings meeting
  certain energy efficiency standards.
- **Prevailing wage and apprenticeship requirements** — For qualifying for full value of
  clean energy tax credits (IRA 2022), construction projects must meet prevailing wage
  and apprenticeship requirements. This affects contractors on solar, wind, EV charging,
  and other clean energy projects.

## 10. State Considerations

- **Worker classification enforcement** — CA (AB5), MA, NJ, NY have extremely aggressive
  enforcement of contractor vs. employee classification. Many states presume construction
  workers are employees unless proven otherwise.
- **State licensing** — Most states require contractor licenses. Fees and CE costs are
  deductible for self-employed.
- **Union dues deductibility** — NOT deductible federally (2018-2025). Deductible in states
  that allow unreimbursed employee expenses: CA, NY, NJ, PA, MN, HI, AR, and others.
- **Workers' compensation** — Required in nearly all states for employees. Self-employed
  may purchase voluntarily. Premiums deductible.
- **State prevailing wage laws** — States like CA, NY, IL, MA have their own prevailing
  wage requirements beyond federal Davis-Bacon. Rates are often higher than federal.
- **Seasonal unemployment** — Construction is seasonal in northern states. UI benefits are
  taxable. Plan estimated payments accordingly.
- **State-level unreimbursed expenses** — Critical for W-2 construction employees. File
  state return claiming tools, boots, safety gear, travel in states that allow.

## 11. De Minimis Safe Harbor and Tool Deductions

Self-employed construction workers should understand the de minimis safe harbor election:

- **$2,500 per item threshold** — Under Reg. 1.263(a)-1(f)(1)(ii)(D), a taxpayer with no
  applicable financial statement can elect to deduct tangible property costing $2,500 or less
  per item or invoice. No depreciation or capitalization required.
- **Application to tools** — A $400 impact driver, a $200 circular saw, or a $1,800 laser
  level can each be expensed immediately under this safe harbor. Each item is evaluated
  independently — a $2,000 tool set purchased as a single unit is one item.
- **Over $2,500** — Items costing more than $2,500 must be capitalized and depreciated (MACRS)
  or expensed under Section 179. A $3,500 table saw would be Section 179 or 5-year MACRS.
- **Annual election** — The de minimis safe harbor election is made annually by attaching a
  statement to the return.

## 12. Common Mistakes

1. **Not deducting mileage to temporary jobsites** — If jobsite is under one year, travel
   from home is deductible. Many workers treat all jobsite travel as commuting.
2. **Missing state-level deductions** — W-2 workers in states allowing unreimbursed expenses
   should claim tools, boots, union dues on state return.
3. **Employee misclassification** — Both sides get hurt: GCs face back taxes and penalties;
   workers miss employer-paid FICA, unemployment, and workers' comp.
4. **Not keeping tool receipts** — Self-employed workers need receipts for all tool purchases.
   Hardware store receipt + credit card statement makes a strong audit record.
5. **Confusing temporary and permanent jobsites** — A large project expected to last 18 months
   is a permanent jobsite from day one. No mileage deduction. Must track expected duration.
6. **Not electing S-Corp** — Self-employed contractors netting $80K+ save thousands in SE
   tax. Must file Form 2553 timely.
7. **Missing estimated tax payments** — Self-employed construction workers with variable
   income often underpay estimates in good quarters. Annualized method (Form 2210 AI) helps.
8. **Not claiming QBI deduction** — Construction qualifies for the 20% 199A deduction.
   Self-employed workers must claim it on their return.
9. **Ignoring retirement savings** — Many tradespeople rely solely on union pension without
   additional savings. Solo 401(k) or IRA can supplement significantly.
10. **Applying 80% meal rate** — The 80% DOT meals rate is only for DOT hours-of-service
    workers (truckers, airline pilots, etc.). Construction workers use the standard 50% rate.
    This is a common error.
