# Healthcare Worker — Tax Reference Guide (TY 2025)
## Nurses (RN/LPN), Physician Assistants, Therapists, Pharmacists

## 1. Overview

Healthcare workers below the physician level face distinct tax challenges: shift differentials,
travel nursing assignments with complex tax-home rules, continuing education requirements,
license/certification costs, and significant student loan debt. Travel nurses in particular
must navigate the IRS's tax-home doctrine carefully — non-taxable per diem and housing stipends
are enormous benefits when properly structured, but can create major tax problems if the
rules are violated. The tax-home doctrine (*Henderson v. Commissioner*) and temporary
assignment rules are the defining tax issues for this profession.

## 2. Common Income Types

- **W-2 salary** — Staff positions at hospitals, clinics, long-term care facilities, pharmacies.
  Includes base pay, shift differentials (evening, night, weekend), overtime, holiday pay.
  Shift differentials and overtime are all ordinary income — there is no special tax rate.
  They are withheld at the same marginal rate (or supplemental 22%/37% rate for bonuses).
- **W-2 with per diem** — Travel nursing agencies pay a base hourly rate (taxable) plus
  non-taxable per diem for housing and meals (IF tax home rules are met).
- **1099-NEC** — Per diem/locum positions, telehealth consulting, independent contractor
  therapy practices, private-duty nursing.
- **PRN/per diem shifts** — Extra shifts at secondary facilities. Usually W-2 from staffing
  agency or facility. PRN workers who control their own schedule, provide their own
  equipment, and work for multiple facilities may be treated as independent contractors
  (1099) in some arrangements. Classification depends on the economic reality test and
  the specific arrangement. Misclassification is a significant risk area.
- **Stipends** — Housing and travel stipends from travel nursing agencies. Tax-free if
  traveler maintains a tax home. TAXABLE if no tax home maintained.
- **Sign-on bonuses** — Taxable as supplemental wages. Often withheld at 22% flat rate
  (or 37% if over $1M). If a repayment clause requires return of bonus upon early
  departure, the repayment is deductible in the year paid (IRC 1341 claim of right).
- **Hazard pay** — Additional compensation for dangerous conditions (infectious disease
  units, disaster response). Fully taxable as ordinary income. No special exclusion.
- **Tuition reimbursement** — Tax-free up to $5,250/year under IRC 127 from employer.
  Amounts above $5,250 are taxable W-2 income.

## 3. Key Deductions

### W-2 Employees (Limited Post-TCJA)
- **Unreimbursed employee expenses** — NOT deductible federally for W-2 employees (2018-2025).
  This includes scrubs, stethoscopes, continuing education, license renewals — none deductible
  on federal return if you're a W-2 employee. Some states still allow (see state section).
- **Student loan interest** — Up to $2,500 above the line. Phase-out: $80K-$95K single /
  $165K-$195K MFJ. Many healthcare workers fall within this range.
- **SALT** — Capped at $10,000.
- **Charitable** — Itemized deduction if exceeding standard deduction.

### Self-Employed / Independent Contractor
- **Scrubs and uniforms** — Deductible if required by employer and not suitable for everyday
  wear. Per *Pevsner v. Commissioner* (628 F.2d 467, 5th Cir. 1980), clothing must be
  (1) required as a condition of employment, AND (2) not suitable for general everyday use.
  Scrubs with facility logos clearly qualify. Plain scrubs are more debatable — but the
  IRS generally accepts scrubs as not suitable for everyday wear. White lab coats,
  surgical gowns, and protective gear clearly qualify.
- **Licensing and certification** — State RN/LPN/PA license renewal, DEA registration (PAs),
  specialty certifications (CCRN, CEN, ACLS, BLS, PALS, NRP). Multi-state licenses:
  individual state license fees are deductible for each state in which the worker practices.
  Nurse Licensure Compact (NLC) multi-state license: single fee covers 40+ states, fully
  deductible.
- **Continuing education (CME/CEU)** — Required for license maintenance. Course registration,
  travel to conferences, materials, online CE subscriptions. The hours required vary by
  state and specialty (typically 20-50 CEUs per renewal cycle).
- **Malpractice insurance** — Individual policies for NPs, PAs, CRNAs. Typically $1,000-
  $5,000/year depending on specialty and state. Fully deductible.
- **Equipment** — Stethoscope, diagnostic tools, medical reference apps/books (UpToDate,
  Epocrates, medical calculators).
- **Home office** — If running independent practice from home for admin/telehealth.
- **Health insurance** — Self-employed health insurance deduction (above the line).
- **Professional memberships** — ANA, AAPA, APTA, APhA, specialty nursing organizations.

## 4. Travel Nursing — Deep Dive

Travel nursing is one of the most tax-advantaged employment arrangements in any profession,
but only if the rules are followed precisely.

### Tax Home Doctrine

Per *Henderson v. Commissioner* (T.C. Memo 1986-559) and Rev. Rul. 99-7, the IRS defines
"tax home" as follows:

- **Primary definition** — Your tax home is your regular place of business (NOT your
  personal residence). If you have a regular place of business, that is your tax home.
- **No regular place of business** — If you have no regular place of business (as is common
  for travel nurses), your tax home is your regular place of ABODE — your personal residence,
  provided it is a "real and substantial" home, not just a mailing address.
- **No tax home at all** — If you have neither a regular place of business NOR a regular
  place of abode, you are an "itinerant" and your tax home is wherever you work. You are
  NEVER "away from home" and CANNOT claim tax-free stipends or travel deductions.

### Maintaining a Tax Home (Critical Requirements)

To claim tax-free housing and meal stipends, travel nurses MUST maintain a tax home:

1. **Permanent residence with real costs** — Must have a home (owned or rented) that incurs
   real, ongoing expenses: rent/mortgage, utilities, insurance. Living with parents rent-free
   or having a family member's address as mailing-only does NOT establish a tax home.
2. **Return periodically** — Must return to the permanent residence between assignments,
   during breaks, and/or for holidays. Periodic returns demonstrate the residence is genuine.
3. **Duplicate expenses** — Must be paying for BOTH the permanent residence and the travel
   housing simultaneously. This duplication is what creates the "away from home" status.

### Temporary vs. Indefinite Assignment Rule

- **Temporary assignment (<1 year)** — If the assignment in a single metropolitan area is
  realistically expected to last less than 1 year AND does actually last less than 1 year,
  the worker is "away from home" and qualifies for tax-free stipends and travel deductions.
- **Indefinite assignment (>1 year)** — If the assignment is expected to last more than
  1 year, or actually does last more than 1 year in a single area, that area becomes the
  tax home. All stipends for that assignment become TAXABLE, potentially retroactively.
- **Extensions** — If a 13-week contract is extended repeatedly in the same metro area,
  the clock keeps running. Once total time in the area exceeds or is expected to exceed
  12 months, tax home shifts to the assignment location.
- **Safe harbor** — Keep assignments under 12 months per metro area. Take breaks between
  assignments in the same area to reset the clock (though the IRS can look through
  artificial breaks).

### Per Diem and Stipend Rules

- **Accountable plan** — Travel nursing agencies typically pay per diem under an accountable
  plan (IRC 62(c), Reg. 1.62-2). Under an accountable plan: (1) expenses must have a
  business connection, (2) must be substantiated within a reasonable time, (3) excess
  must be returned. Amounts within these rules are excluded from W-2 income.
- **GSA rates** — Per diem amounts should not exceed the GSA (General Services Administration)
  rates for the assignment location. Rates vary by city and are updated annually.
  Two components: lodging and M&IE (meals and incidental expenses).
- **M&IE breakdown (2025)** — Standard CONUS rate: $69/day total M&IE. Breakdown: breakfast
  $16, lunch $17, dinner $31, incidentals $5. First and last day of travel: 75% rate.
- **Housing stipend** — Must not exceed actual housing costs or the GSA lodging rate for the
  area. If the stipend exceeds actual costs, the excess is taxable income.
- **What happens without a tax home** — ALL stipends become taxable W-2 income. The agency
  should report them in Box 1. If the agency fails to do so, the nurse is still responsible
  for reporting. IRS audits of travel nurses who lack tax homes result in back taxes on
  all stipends plus penalties and interest. Exposure: $15,000-$30,000+ per year in
  additional taxable income.

## 5. Shift Differentials, Overtime, and Hazard Pay — Tax Treatment

A common misconception among healthcare workers is that overtime or shift differentials are
"taxed differently." They are not:

- **All ordinary income** — Shift differentials (evening $2-$5/hr extra, night $3-$8/hr extra,
  weekend $5-$10/hr extra), overtime (time-and-a-half, double-time), holiday premium pay,
  and hazard pay are all ordinary W-2 income taxed at the worker's marginal rate.
- **Withholding confusion** — Employers may withhold overtime and bonuses at the flat 22%
  supplemental rate (or aggregate method). This can make it APPEAR that overtime is taxed
  at a different rate. The actual tax due is computed on the annual return at marginal rates;
  any overwithholding is refunded.
- **Higher bracket** — A nurse earning $75K base salary who works significant overtime ($25K)
  has $100K total income. The overtime income is taxed at the marginal rate for $75K-$100K,
  which is 22% federal (2025 MFJ). It is not taxed at a higher rate than other income in
  the same bracket.
- **Hazard pay** — No special federal exclusion exists for hazard pay. Some states created
  temporary COVID-era exclusions that have since expired. All hazard pay is taxable.

## 6. Recommended Entity Structure

- **Staff nurse/PA/pharmacist** — W-2 employee. No entity needed.
- **Travel nurse through agency** — W-2 employee of agency. No entity needed.
  Focus on maintaining tax home for stipend exclusion.
- **Independent contractor (1099)** — Sole proprietor initially. S-Corp election when net
  income exceeds ~$60K. Common for CRNAs, NPs with own practice, independent therapists.
- **Group therapy practice** — Multi-member LLC taxed as partnership or S-Corp.
- **Telehealth practice** — LLC with S-Corp election. Multi-state licensing may be required.

## 6. Retirement Plan Strategy

- **Hospital 403(b)** — $23,500 deferral (2025), $31,000 if 50+. Employer match varies.
- **457(b)** — If available at government hospital, contribute to BOTH 403(b) and 457(b)
  for up to $47,000 in deferrals.
- **Hospital pension** — Some health systems offer defined benefit pensions. Understand
  vesting requirements (often 5 years).
- **HSA** — If on HDHP: $4,300 individual / $8,550 family (2025). Triple tax advantage.
  Many healthcare workers with employer insurance qualify.
- **Roth IRA** — Direct contribution if within income limits. Backdoor Roth if above.
- **Solo 401(k) / SEP-IRA** — For self-employed NPs, CRNAs, independent therapists.
- **Travel nurse challenge** — Changing employers frequently means 401(k) contributions may
  be complicated. Ensure rollovers to IRA when leaving assignments. Some agencies offer 401(k).
- **PSLF strategy** — Healthcare workers at non-profit hospitals (most hospitals qualify)
  should pursue PSLF. Income-driven repayment minimizes payments; forgiveness after 120 payments.

## 7. Student Loan Strategies

- **PSLF** — Non-profit hospital employees qualify. Most hospitals are 501(c)(3) or
  government entities. After 120 qualifying payments under income-driven repayment,
  remaining balance forgiven tax-free.
- **NURSE Corps Loan Repayment Program** — Up to 85% of qualifying nursing education loans
  repaid for nurses working at Critical Shortage Facilities. Tax-free. Two-year initial
  commitment with optional third year.
- **NHSC (National Health Service Corps)** — Loan repayment for primary care providers
  (including NPs, PAs, CNMs) serving in Health Professional Shortage Areas (HPSAs).
  Up to $50K for 2-year commitment. Tax-free under IRC 108(f)(4).
- **State-specific programs** — Many states offer loan repayment for healthcare workers
  serving in underserved areas. Tax treatment varies; most are tax-free.

## 8. Common Audit Triggers

- **Travel nurse tax home** — IRS actively audits travel nurses claiming non-taxable stipends
  without maintaining a legitimate tax home. If no tax home, ALL stipends are taxable income.
  Must demonstrate: (1) regular place of abode in a real and substantial sense, (2) incurring
  duplicate living expenses, (3) not abandoning former home area.
- **Duplicate housing deductions** — Claiming both stipend exclusion AND housing deduction
  for the same expense is double-dipping. Choose one or the other.
- **High unreimbursed expenses on state returns** — States allowing these deductions may
  audit large claims.
- **Mileage between facilities** — Must properly separate commuting (not deductible) from
  travel between work locations (deductible).
- **CE expenses disproportionate to income** — Exotic locations for CE courses with minimal
  educational content will be scrutinized.

## 10. Profession-Specific Rules

- **Section 199A (QBI)** — Healthcare services are an SSTB. QBI deduction phases out above
  $197,300/$394,600. Many healthcare workers with self-employment income fall below threshold
  and qualify for the full 20% deduction. CRNAs and NPs in private practice with income
  above $247,300 (single) / $494,600 (MFJ) get NO QBI deduction.
- **Nurse Licensure Compact (NLC)** — Multi-state license allows practice in 40+ member
  states with one license. Compact license fee is deductible for self-employed. States must
  be NLC member states; non-compact states require separate individual licenses.
- **IRC 108(f)** — Student loan forgiveness from qualifying employer (PSLF, NHSC) is
  excludable from gross income. IDR forgiveness tax-free through 2025.
- **Uniform/scrub deductibility test (*Pevsner*)** — Must be (1) required by employer AND
  (2) not suitable for everyday wear. Scrubs with employer logos or that cannot be worn
  outside the facility clearly qualify. Plain scrubs available at retail stores are more
  debatable but generally accepted by IRS as unsuitable for streetwear.

## 11. Multi-State Licensing Costs

Healthcare workers, especially travel nurses and locum tenens providers, often hold licenses
in multiple states. Tax treatment:

- **Self-employed** — All license and certification fees are deductible as business expenses
  on Schedule C. This includes: initial license application, renewal fees, fingerprint/
  background check fees, compact license fees, specialty certification exams and renewals.
- **W-2 employees** — NOT deductible federally post-TCJA. Deductible on state returns in
  states that allow unreimbursed employee expenses.
- **Compact license (NLC)** — A single license fee covering 40+ states is far more
  cost-effective than individual state licenses ($150-$400 per state). For travel nurses
  working in compact states, this is the preferred approach.
- **Reciprocity vs. endorsement** — Some states offer license by endorsement (faster, cheaper)
  for nurses already licensed in another state. Others require full application. All fees
  are deductible for self-employed workers.
- **CME/CEU costs** — Continuing medical education requirements vary by state and specialty.
  Typical costs: $500-$3,000/year for courses, conferences, and online CE. Deductible
  for self-employed; not deductible federally for W-2 employees.

## 12. State Considerations

- **Travel nurse state taxes** — Must file in: (1) tax home state, (2) each state worked in
  (if that state has income tax). Credit in home state for taxes paid to work states.
  Working in 3-5 states per year means 3-5 state returns.
- **States with no income tax** — TX, FL, WA, NV, TN, AK, SD, WY, NH. Popular tax home states
  for travel nurses. Maintaining a tax home in a no-income-tax state while working in taxed
  states provides maximum benefit (tax-free stipends PLUS no home-state tax).
- **Compact license states** — NLC allows practice in 40+ member states with one license.
  Saves multi-state licensing costs.
- **State-level unreimbursed employee expense deductions** — Available in: CA, NY, NJ, PA,
  MN, HI, AR, and others. Allows deduction of scrubs, CE, license fees, union dues on
  state return even though not deductible federally.
- **Union dues** — State-deductible in states that allow. Nursing unions (NNU, SEIU) have
  significant dues ($800-$1,500+/year).
- **California** — Taxes all income earned in CA. Travel nurses working CA assignments owe
  CA tax on that income even if domiciled elsewhere. CA also allows unreimbursed employee
  expenses on the state return.

## 13. Common Mistakes

1. **Travel nurse without tax home** — Working travel assignments without maintaining a
   genuine tax home makes ALL stipends taxable. The IRS back-taxes stipends plus penalties.
   A properly maintained tax home is worth $15,000-$30,000/year in tax savings.
2. **Not filing in all work states** — Travel nurses must file in every state where they
   worked (if that state has income tax). Missing state returns triggers notices.
3. **Treating stipends as always tax-free** — Stipends are only tax-free if maintaining a
   tax home and duplicating expenses. Many travel nurses are incorrectly told by agencies
   that stipends are "automatically" tax-free.
4. **Missing student loan strategies** — Not enrolling in income-driven repayment for PSLF.
   Not applying for NURSE Corps or state loan repayment programs.
5. **Not deducting expenses at state level** — W-2 healthcare workers in states that allow
   unreimbursed employee expenses miss scrubs, CE, license fees, and union dues deductions.
6. **Forgetting to roll over 401(k) from prior employer** — Frequent job changes (especially
   travel nurses) leave small 401(k) balances scattered. Consolidate into IRA.
7. **Not contributing to both 403(b) and 457(b)** — Hospital employees with access to both
   plans should maximize both for up to $47,000 in tax-deferred savings.
8. **Paying SE tax on travel stipends** — If stipends are properly excluded, they are not
   subject to SE tax either. Incorrect reporting can result in paying SE tax on excluded amounts.
9. **Exceeding 12-month rule** — Staying at one assignment location beyond 12 months makes
   that location your tax home, eliminating per diem and housing exclusions retroactively.
10. **PRN worker misclassification** — PRN/per diem workers may be incorrectly classified as
    1099 contractors when they should be W-2 employees. If the facility controls when, where,
    and how you work, you are likely an employee regardless of what the contract says.
    Misclassified workers can file Form SS-8 for IRS determination.
11. **Not claiming state deductions for scrubs and CE** — W-2 healthcare workers in states
    that allow unreimbursed employee expenses (CA, NY, NJ, PA, MN, HI, AR) can deduct
    scrubs ($200-$500/year), CE courses ($500-$3,000/year), license fees ($100-$400/year),
    stethoscopes, and professional memberships. These add up to $1,000-$4,000+ in state
    deductions even though they produce zero federal benefit.
12. **Sign-on bonus repayment not deducted** — If a healthcare worker leaves before the
    commitment period and must repay a sign-on bonus, the repayment is deductible under
    the claim of right doctrine (IRC 1341). Many workers do not realize they can recover
    tax on the original bonus through either a deduction or credit in the repayment year.
