# Freelancer / Consultant — Tax Reference Guide (TY 2025)

## 1. Overview

Freelancers and independent consultants operate as self-employed individuals receiving 1099-NEC
income from multiple clients. This is the most common self-employment tax situation in the US,
spanning fields from marketing and design to management consulting and data analysis. The core
tax challenges are self-employment tax management, quarterly estimated payments, business
deduction optimization, and deciding when to elect S-Corp status.

## 2. Common Income Types

- **1099-NEC** — Primary income from clients paying $600+. Each client issues a separate 1099.
- **1099-K** — Payment processor income (PayPal, Stripe, Venmo for Business). Threshold is
  more than $20,000 and more than 200 transactions for TY 2025 under current IRS guidance.
  May overlap with 1099-NEC; do not double-report.
- **Direct payments under $600** — Still taxable income even if no 1099 issued. Must self-report.
- **Retainer income** — Monthly retainers taxed when received (cash method) or earned (accrual).
- **Project-based income** — Milestone payments, completion bonuses.
- **Royalties / passive income** — Course sales, digital products, licensing fees.
- **Foreign client income** — US citizens/residents taxed on worldwide income. Foreign clients
  may not issue 1099 but income is still reportable.

### Multiple 1099 Client Management
- Reconcile all 1099-NECs and 1099-Ks received against your own books. Total reported to IRS
  must match or exceed the sum of all information returns.
- Income received but NOT reported on a 1099 (foreign clients, payments under $600) must still
  be included on Schedule C. The IRS matches 1099s; unreported 1099 income generates CP2000 notices.
- If a 1099 amount is incorrect: request a corrected 1099 from the issuer. If they refuse,
  report the correct amount and keep documentation supporting the discrepancy.
- Track income by client for internal management, but Schedule C reports a single gross receipts total.

## 3. Key Deductions

### Home Office
- **Simplified method** — $5/sq ft, max 300 sq ft = $1,500 max. Easy but often undervalues.
- **Actual method** — Percentage of rent/mortgage interest, utilities, insurance, repairs,
  depreciation based on square footage ratio. Often $3,000-$8,000+ for dedicated office.
- **Requirement** — Regular and exclusive use as principal place of business.
- **Principal place of business test** (Commissioner v. Soliman, 506 U.S. 168 (1993)):
  for consultants who also work at client sites, the home office qualifies if (1) it is where
  the most important business functions are performed (billing, planning, admin), OR (2) there
  is no other fixed location where substantial business is conducted. Most freelancers meet
  this test because they have no other office.
- **Bonus** — Having a qualified home office makes ALL business travel from home deductible
  (eliminates commuting exclusion problem). Without a home office, travel to your first and
  last client each day is nondeductible commuting.

### Equipment and Technology
- **Computer, monitor, peripherals** — Section 179 or bonus depreciation. Must be >50% business use.
- **Software subscriptions** — Adobe, Figma, Slack, project management tools, accounting software.
- **Internet and phone** — Business-use percentage. Document allocation method.

### Professional Development
- **Courses, certifications, books** — Must maintain or improve skills in current business.
- **Conferences** — Registration, travel, lodging. Must be directly related to business.
- **Professional memberships** — Industry associations, networking groups.

### Business Operations
- **Professional liability insurance (E&O)** — Deductible.
- **Business insurance** — General liability, cyber liability.
- **Accounting and legal fees** — Tax preparation (business portion), legal advice, bookkeeping.
- **Subcontractor payments** — Payments to other freelancers you hire. Must issue 1099-NEC if $600+.
- **Coworking space** — Monthly membership or day passes. Fully deductible if used for business.
- **Business meals** — 50% deductible. Must document business purpose, attendees.
- **Marketing** — Website, portfolio hosting, business cards, advertising.

### Vehicle Deduction for Client Visits
- **Standard mileage rate (2025)**: $0.70/mile for business use.
- **Actual expense method**: gas, insurance, repairs, depreciation, prorated by business-use %.
- **First-year lock-in**: must choose standard mileage in the first year the vehicle is used
  for business. If you start with actual expenses, you can NEVER switch to standard mileage
  for that vehicle. If you start with mileage, you CAN switch to actual later.
- **Home office makes ALL client travel deductible**: without a home office, driving from home
  to first client and from last client to home is nondeductible commuting. With a qualified
  home office, home-to-client is business travel (Rev. Rul. 99-7).
- **Keep contemporaneous mileage log**: date, destination, business purpose, miles driven.
  Apps like MileIQ or Everlance automate this.

### Above-the-Line Deductions
- **Self-employed health insurance (§162(l))** — 100% of premiums for self, spouse, dependents.
  Cannot exceed net SE income. Cannot claim if eligible for employer-sponsored plan (e.g., spouse's).
  **Interaction with marketplace Premium Tax Credit (PTC)**: creates a circular calculation
  because the health insurance deduction reduces AGI, which affects PTC eligibility, which
  affects the net premium, which is the deduction. IRS provides an iterative calculation
  method in Pub 974. Tax software handles this automatically.
- **Deductible portion of SE tax** — 50% of SE tax is above-the-line deduction (automatic).
- **Retirement contributions** — SEP-IRA, Solo 401(k) deductible above the line.

## 4. Recommended Entity Structure

- **Starting out / income under $40K net** — Sole proprietor, report on Schedule C. No entity
  formation needed. Low administrative burden.
- **$40K-$80K net income** — Form single-member LLC for liability protection. Continue filing
  on Schedule C (disregarded entity). Start evaluating S-Corp timing.
- **$80K+ net income** — Elect S-Corp taxation (Form 2553). Pay reasonable W-2 salary,
  take remaining profit as distribution. Typical SE tax savings: $3,000-$15,000+/year.

### S-Corp Election Timing (Form 2553)
- **Standard deadline**: File by March 15 of the year the election is to take effect
  (2 months, 15 days into the tax year for calendar-year entities).
- **Late election relief (Rev. Proc. 2013-30)**: available if filed within 3 years and 75 days
  of the intended effective date, if: (1) the entity intended to be classified as S-Corp,
  (2) reasonable cause for late filing, (3) the entity and shareholders reported income
  consistent with S-Corp status. Write "FILED PURSUANT TO REV. PROC. 2013-30" on Form 2553.
- **New LLC wanting S-Corp for current year**: form the LLC, then file Form 2553 within
  2 months 15 days of formation. If you miss this, use late election relief.
- **Existing Schedule C business**: form an LLC, elect S-Corp status. If done mid-year,
  the S-Corp election is effective from the date specified (can be retroactive to Jan 1
  if filed timely or under late relief).

### S-Corp Break-Even Analysis
- S-Corp adds costs: payroll service ($500-$3,000/year), payroll taxes on salary, additional
  tax return (Form 1120-S, ~$1,000-$2,500 to prepare), state entity taxes (e.g., CA $800).
- Net savings positive when net income exceeds ~$50K-$60K after expenses.
- **SE tax savings formula**: (net income - reasonable salary) x 3.8% (Medicare + additional).

### Reasonable Salary Determination
- Must be "reasonable" for services performed. IRS tests: training/education, experience,
  duties performed, time devoted, comparable compensation in similar businesses.
- **Data sources**: Bureau of Labor Statistics (BLS) Occupational Employment Statistics,
  Salary.com, Glassdoor, Robert Half salary guides, industry-specific surveys.
- **General guideline**: 40-60% of net income for established consultants. Higher percentage
  when income is lower; lower percentage when income is very high.
- **Documentation**: prepare a salary analysis memo citing comparable data. Keep it in your
  tax file. This is your defense if IRS challenges the salary as too low.
- **Example**: marketing consultant with $200K net income. BLS shows median marketing manager
  salary at $140K. Reasonable salary range: $90K-$140K. Set at $100K; take $100K as distribution.
  SE tax savings: $100K x 3.8% = $3,800/year (plus savings on the employer side of FICA).

## 5. Retirement Plan Strategy

### Solo 401(k) vs. SEP-IRA Decision Matrix
| Factor | Solo 401(k) | SEP-IRA |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| Employee deferral | Yes ($23,500 / $31,000 if 50+) | No |
| Employer contribution | 25% of W-2 (S-Corp) or ~20% net SE | 25% of W-2 or ~20% net SE |
| Total max | $70,000 (2025) | $70,000 (2025) |
| Roth option | Yes (employee deferral) | No |
| Loan provision | Yes (up to $50K) | No |
| Setup deadline | Dec 31 of tax year | Filing deadline (incl. extensions) |
| Admin complexity | Moderate (annual 5500-EZ if >$250K) | Very low |
| Employees | Cannot have non-owner employees | Must include all eligible employees |
| Backdoor Roth impact | None (not an IRA) | YES — SEP balance triggers pro-rata |

- **Best for most freelancers**: Solo 401(k) — higher contribution potential at moderate income,
  Roth option, loan access, and does NOT interfere with backdoor Roth IRA.
- **Best for last-minute setup**: SEP-IRA — can establish and fund up to filing deadline.

### Additional Plans
- **SIMPLE IRA** — $16,500 employee deferral (2025). Lower limit than 401(k). Must be
  established by October 1. Less common for solo freelancers.
- **Traditional/Roth IRA** — $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+). Roth income limits: $150,000 single /
  $236,000 MFJ (2025 MAGI). Backdoor Roth available above limits.
- **HSA** — If on HDHP: $4,300 individual / $8,550 family (2025). Triple tax advantage.

## 6. QBI Deduction — SSTB Rules for Consultants

### Consulting IS an SSTB (IRC §199A(d)(2), Reg. §1.199A-5(b)(2)(vii))
- "Consulting" is explicitly listed as a Specified Service Trade or Business.
- **Below threshold** ($197,300 single / $394,600 MFJ): full 20% QBI deduction available.
- **Phase-out range** ($197,300-$247,300 single / $394,600-$494,600 MFJ): partial deduction.
- **Above phase-out**: NO QBI deduction. At all. Zero.
- **Example**: freelance consultant with $200K Schedule C income, single.
  QBI deduction = $200K x 20% = $40,000 deduction. Tax savings at 32% bracket = $12,800.
- **Example**: same consultant with $250K income (above $247,300): QBI deduction = $0.
- **Strategy for high-income consultants**: the QBI deduction disappears above threshold,
  so there is NO way to recover it through planning. Focus on other strategies (retirement,
  charitable, timing income).

### Non-Consulting Freelancers
- If your business is NOT an SSTB (e.g., freelance manufacturing, construction, certain
  creative work), the full 20% QBI deduction is available above the threshold, subject to
  the W-2/UBIA limitation (greater of 50% of W-2 wages or 25% of W-2 wages + 2.5% of UBIA).
- S-Corp owners paying themselves W-2 wages increase their QBI limitation (the W-2 wages
  are used in the limitation formula). Strategy: for non-SSTB businesses above the threshold,
  paying higher W-2 wages can actually INCREASE the net benefit by raising the QBI cap.

## 7. PTET Election for S-Corp Freelancers

- Pass-through entity tax (PTET) elections are available in 30+ states (CA, NY, NJ, CT, etc.).
- The S-Corp pays state income tax at the entity level, and the shareholder gets a credit
  on their individual return, bypassing the personal SALT cap.
- **Requirements vary by state**: election deadlines, irrevocability, minimum tax rates.
- **Example**: CA consultant with $500K S-Corp income and significant property taxes. Without
  PTET, the personal SALT cap can leave a large share of CA tax nondeductible. With PTET:
  the S-Corp pays CA tax, gets the federal deduction at the entity level, and the shareholder
  claims the CA credit. Net savings can still be substantial at higher incomes.

## 8. Common Audit Triggers

- **Schedule C losses for multiple years** — Losses 3+ consecutive years trigger hobby loss
  scrutiny. Must demonstrate profit motive: business plan, effort to make profitable,
  expertise, appreciation of assets, history of income in similar activities.
- **High deduction-to-income ratio** — Deductions exceeding 50%+ of gross income draw attention,
  especially meals, travel, and vehicle.
- **Home office on Schedule C** — Still a flag, though less so than historically. Strong
  documentation (photos of dedicated space, floor plan) helps.
- **Round numbers** — Reporting $5,000 for supplies, $10,000 for travel suggests estimation
  rather than actual tracking. Use precise figures.
- **Cash-intensive businesses** — Low reported income relative to lifestyle. IRS uses bank
  deposit analysis to verify income.
- **Large vehicle deduction** — Must have contemporaneous mileage log. Business use percentage
  must be reasonable for the type of consulting.

## 9. Estimated Payment Safe Harbors — First-Year Pitfalls

### Standard Safe Harbors (IRC §6654)
- **Prior-year safe harbor**: pay 100% of prior year tax liability (110% if AGI > $150,000).
- **Current-year safe harbor**: pay 90% of current year tax.
- Meet EITHER safe harbor to avoid underpayment penalty.

### First-Year Freelancer Problem
- If you had a W-2 job last year with $50K tax liability, the prior-year safe harbor requires
  paying $50K (or $55K if AGI > $150K) in estimated payments — even if your freelance income
  is much lower. This is safe but may overpay.
- If transitioning mid-year: your W-2 withholding counts toward estimated payments. Calculate
  the gap between expected withholding and total safe harbor amount.
- **True first-year with no prior tax**: technically no prior-year liability, so the prior-year
  safe harbor is $0. But you MUST still pay 90% of current-year tax or face penalties.
  Estimate conservatively.

### Annualized Income Installment Method (Form 2210, Schedule AI)
- For freelancers with uneven income (e.g., big project in Q4), the annualized method
  calculates the required payment based on income actually earned each quarter.
- Prevents penalty when income is back-loaded even though full-year estimated payments
  were insufficient.

### Penalty Computation
- Penalty rate: IRS short-term rate + 3% (approximately 8% annualized for 2025).
- Computed on a per-quarter basis. Each quarter's underpayment accrues separately.
- Even small underpayments compound: a $10K Q1 shortfall at 8% = ~$600 penalty for the year.

## 10. State Considerations

- **California** — LLC fee: $800 annual minimum franchise tax + gross receipts fee ($900 at
  $250K-$500K revenue, scaling up). S-Corp: $800 minimum + 1.5% net income tax.
- **New York** — Self-employment income subject to state + city tax. Unincorporated business
  tax (UBT) of 4% in NYC applies to sole proprietors/partnerships.
- **Texas** — No income tax. Franchise tax applies to entities over $2.47M revenue.
- **States with no income tax** — AK, FL, NV, NH (interest/dividends only), SD, TN, TX, WA, WY.
  Freelancers in these states save 5-13% on state taxes compared to high-tax states.
- **PTE elections** — S-Corp freelancers in states with PTE tax elections can deduct state
  taxes beyond the personal SALT cap. Available in 30+ states.
- **Multi-state clients** — Most states tax income where services are performed, not where
  client is located. Remote freelancers generally owe tax only in their state of residence.
  Exceptions exist (e.g., NY convenience rule).

## 11. Worked Example: $200K Consulting Income — Schedule C vs. S-Corp

### Scenario: Marketing consultant, single, no employees, home office, HDHP

**Option A: Schedule C (Sole Proprietor)**
| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Gross 1099-NEC income | $200,000 |
| Business expenses (home office, software, etc.) | ($20,000) |
| Net Schedule C income | $180,000 |
| SE tax (15.3% on 92.35% x $176,100 + 2.9% on remainder) | $24,500 |
| 50% SE tax deduction | ($12,250) |
| Self-employed health insurance | ($12,000) |
| Solo 401(k) (employee $23,500 + employer ~$31,200) | ($54,700) |
| QBI deduction (20% of $180K) | ($36,000) |
| AGI | ~$101,050 |
| Federal income tax (est.) | ~$15,500 |
| **Total federal tax (income + SE)** | **~$40,000** |

**Option B: S-Corp (salary $90K, distribution $90K)**
| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Gross revenue | $200,000 |
| Business expenses | ($20,000) |
| S-Corp net income | $180,000 |
| Officer W-2 salary | $90,000 |
| Employer payroll taxes (7.65% of $90K) | ($6,885) |
| S-Corp distribution | $83,115 |
| Employee payroll taxes (7.65% of $90K) | $6,885 |
| Self-employed health insurance | ($12,000) |
| Solo 401(k) (employee $23,500 + employer 25% of $90K = $22,500) | ($46,000) |
| QBI deduction (20% of $180K, below threshold) | ($36,000) |
| AGI | ~$86,000 |
| Federal income tax (est.) | ~$12,500 |
| Total payroll taxes (employer + employee) | $13,770 |
| **Total federal tax (income + payroll)** | **~$26,270** |

**S-Corp annual savings: ~$13,730**
Minus S-Corp compliance costs (~$3,000 payroll + tax return): **Net savings ~$10,730/year**

## 12. Common Mistakes

1. **Not making quarterly estimated payments** — Results in underpayment penalty (~8% annualized
   rate for 2025). Set aside 25-30% of each payment received.
2. **Missing the S-Corp election window** — Form 2553 due March 15 for current-year effect.
   Late election requires reasonable cause under Rev. Proc. 2013-30. Missing this costs
   thousands in SE tax savings.
3. **Commingling personal and business finances** — Use separate business bank account and
   credit card. Commingling weakens LLC protection and complicates bookkeeping.
4. **Not tracking expenses in real time** — Reconstructing expenses at year-end misses
   deductions and creates audit risk. Use accounting software (QuickBooks, Wave, FreshBooks).
5. **Overlooking self-employed health insurance deduction** — Above-the-line deduction often
   worth $5,000-$15,000+ in premium deductions. Cannot claim if eligible for spouse's employer plan.
6. **Reporting 1099-K and 1099-NEC income twice** — Same income may appear on both forms.
   Reconcile total income to avoid double-reporting.
7. **Not claiming home office** — Fear of audit leads freelancers to skip legitimate deduction.
   If you meet the requirements, claim it.
8. **Forgetting to issue 1099s to subcontractors** — Must issue 1099-NEC to any sub you pay
   $600+ in a year. Penalty for failure: $60-$310 per form depending on lateness.
9. **Inadequate retirement contributions** — Freelancers without employer match must be
   self-directed. Many under-save. Solo 401(k) can shelter $50K+ per year.
10. **Setting S-Corp salary at $0 or unreasonably low** — IRS will reclassify distributions as
    wages and impose back payroll taxes, penalties, and interest. Use BLS/comparable data.
11. **Ignoring QBI SSTB rules** — Consulting is SSTB. Above $394,600 MFJ ($247,300 single),
    the 20% deduction disappears entirely. Do not plan around QBI if above threshold.
12. **Not maximizing health insurance deduction / PTC coordination** — The circular calculation
    between SE health insurance deduction and marketplace PTC requires careful iteration.
    Underclaiming either leaves money on the table.
