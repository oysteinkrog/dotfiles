# Clergy — Tax Reference Guide (TY 2025)
## Ministers, Pastors, Priests, Rabbis, Imams, and Other Ordained/Licensed Religious Leaders

## 1. Overview

Clergy members face one of the most complex tax situations of any profession due to their
unique dual tax status: they are treated as employees for income tax purposes but as
self-employed for Social Security/Medicare tax purposes. The parsonage/housing allowance
(IRC 107) provides a powerful exclusion from income tax, but the same amounts remain subject
to self-employment tax. Additionally, clergy can irrevocably opt out of Social Security/Medicare
through Form 4361. These overlapping rules create confusion even for experienced tax preparers.

## 2. Common Income Types

- **W-2 salary** — Compensation from church/religious organization. Box 1 should EXCLUDE
  the housing allowance (if properly designated). Boxes 3, 4, 5, 6 are typically BLANK
  because clergy are exempt from FICA withholding (they pay SE tax instead).
- **Housing/parsonage allowance** — Designated by employer in advance. Excluded from income
  tax (IRC 107) but subject to SE tax. Should NOT appear in W-2 Box 1 but may be shown
  in Box 14 for informational purposes.
- **Fair rental value of parsonage** — If church provides housing (parsonage), FRV is excluded
  from income tax but subject to SE tax.
- **Love offerings and gifts** — Tax treatment depends on facts: recurring gifts from
  congregation for services rendered are generally taxable income. Per *Goodwin v. United
  States* (67 F.3d 149, 8th Cir. 1995), payments received by a minister from his
  congregation were not excludable gifts under IRC 102 because they were made in return
  for services performed. The court applied the *Duberstein* test: the critical question
  is whether the payment proceeds from a "detached and disinterested generosity" or from
  the constraining force of an obligation. Routine "love offerings" collected from the
  congregation as a whole, especially those tied to special occasions (pastor appreciation
  month, Christmas) and encouraged by church leadership, are generally taxable compensation.
  Truly personal gifts from individual parishioners (wedding gift, baby shower) may
  qualify as tax-free gifts under IRC 102.
- **1099-NEC** — Guest speaking fees (honoraria), wedding/funeral fees not from employing
  church, writing/publishing income, conference speaking.
- **Book royalties** — Taxable income reported on Schedule C or as royalty income.
- **Denominational pension distributions** — 403(b)(9) church retirement plans have special
  housing allowance treatment even in retirement.

## 3. Dual Tax Status — Unique in Tax Law

This is the single most important concept in clergy taxation. No other profession has
this exact structure:

### Income Tax Treatment — Employee
- Clergy performing services in the exercise of their ministry for their employing church
  are treated as EMPLOYEES for income tax purposes (common-law employee standard).
- Church CAN agree to withhold federal income tax from the minister's pay, but is not
  required to. Many clergy prefer to make quarterly estimated tax payments instead.
- Income tax applies to salary, bonuses, and other compensation. Housing allowance is
  EXCLUDED from income tax.

### Self-Employment Tax Treatment — Self-Employed
- Under IRC 1402(a)(8), ALL income from services performed in the exercise of ministry
  is treated as self-employment income for SE tax purposes — even though the minister
  is an employee for income tax.
- Minister pays FULL 15.3% SE tax (12.4% Social Security + 2.9% Medicare) on net earnings
  from ministry, which includes: W-2 salary + housing allowance (or FRV of parsonage).
- NO FICA withholding — W-2 Boxes 3-6 are blank. Minister reports SE tax on Schedule SE.
- The 50% SE tax deduction (the "employer half") is available as an above-the-line deduction.

### Why This Matters
A minister earning $60,000 salary + $24,000 housing allowance:
- **Income tax**: Taxed on $60,000 only (housing allowance excluded).
- **SE tax**: Taxed on $84,000 ($60,000 + $24,000). SE tax = $84,000 x 0.9235 x 15.3%
  = approximately $11,868. The 50% deduction reduces AGI by ~$5,934.
- Many ministers are surprised by the SE tax bill because they expect the housing allowance
  to be completely tax-free. It is free from INCOME tax, not from SE tax.

## 4. IRC 107 — Housing/Parsonage Allowance (The Core Benefit)

The housing allowance is the single most valuable tax benefit available to any profession
in the IRC. It can exclude tens of thousands of dollars from income tax annually.

### Two Forms

1. **Church-provided parsonage** — The fair rental value of the parsonage (including
   furnishings and utilities paid by the church) is excluded from income tax. The minister
   reports nothing for income tax but includes FRV in SE tax calculation.

2. **Cash housing allowance** — For ministers who own or rent their own home. The church
   designates a portion of compensation as "housing allowance." This amount is excluded
   from income tax to the extent it is used for qualifying housing expenses.

### Designation Requirement — MUST Be In Advance

- The church governing body (board, session, vestry, deacons, etc.) must officially
  designate the housing allowance amount BEFORE the period to which it applies.
- Typically done at an annual board meeting in December for the following calendar year.
- The designation should be documented in board minutes with specific dollar amount.
- **Retroactive designation is INVALID** — If the church fails to designate before
  January 1, the minister loses the exclusion for that year. This is the single most
  common and costly clergy tax mistake.
- The designation can be amended mid-year (prospectively only) if circumstances change.

### Housing Allowance Limits — Three-Way Cap

The excludable housing allowance is the LOWEST of:

1. **Amount officially designated by the church** — Cannot exclude more than what was
   designated, even if actual expenses are higher.
2. **Actual housing expenses** — All qualifying costs actually paid during the year.
3. **Fair rental value of the home** — FRV of the home, furnished, plus utilities. This
   is an independent appraisal/estimate, not what the minister actually pays.

**Worked example**: Church designates $36,000. Minister's actual housing expenses are $32,000.
Fair rental value (furnished + utilities) is $30,000. The exclusion is limited to $30,000
(the lowest). The excess $6,000 ($36,000 - $30,000) must be reported as taxable income.

### Qualifying Housing Expenses

Include: Rent or mortgage payments (BOTH principal and interest), property taxes, homeowner's
insurance, utilities (electric, gas, water, sewer, trash, phone, internet), furnishings and
appliances, repairs and maintenance, renovations, landscaping, pest control, HOA dues,
down payment on purchase, real estate commissions on sale (if home was purchased with
housing allowance funds).

### Critical Double-Benefit Prohibition

Mortgage interest and property taxes used for the housing allowance exclusion CANNOT also
be deducted as itemized deductions. If the minister excludes $15,000 in mortgage interest
via housing allowance, that $15,000 cannot appear on Schedule A.

### Housing Allowance Still Subject to SE Tax

Housing allowance is excluded from income tax but INCLUDED in net earnings for
self-employment tax under IRC 1402(a)(8). This catches many ministers and preparers.

### Retired Clergy — Housing Allowance Continues

Distributions from a 403(b)(9) church retirement plan can be designated as housing
allowance in retirement, retaining the income tax exclusion. The retired minister must
still have qualifying housing expenses to exclude. This benefit does NOT apply to
regular 403(b), 401(k), or IRA distributions.

## 5. Form 4361 — SE Tax Opt-Out (IRC 1402(e))

### The Election

Clergy who are conscientiously opposed to receiving public insurance benefits (Social
Security, Medicare) based on religious principles — OR whose ordaining religious organization
is opposed — may irrevocably opt out of the SE tax system.

### Filing Requirements

- Must be filed by the due date of the tax return (including extensions) for the SECOND
  tax year in which the minister has net SE earnings from ministry of $400 or more.
- Example: Minister ordained in 2024, earns $400+ net SE income in 2024 (first year) and
  2025 (second year). Form 4361 must be filed by April 15, 2026 (or October 15, 2026
  with extension) — the due date of the 2025 return.
- If the deadline is missed, the election is PERMANENTLY LOST. Cannot be made later.

### Consequences — IRREVOCABLE

- **No Social Security credits** earned from ministry income.
- **No Social Security retirement benefit** based on ministry earnings.
- **No Medicare Part A** free coverage (must purchase Part A or obtain through spouse/
  other employment).
- **No disability insurance** through Social Security.
- **Must plan privately** for retirement income and health insurance.
- The election is irrevocable — once made, it cannot be undone.

### The Religious Conviction Requirement

- The election is NOT a financial decision. IRS can revoke if it determines the election
  was made for economic rather than religious reasons.
- The minister must certify they are conscientiously opposed to acceptance of any public
  insurance benefits (including SS retirement, disability, Medicare) based on religious
  principles or the principles of their religious organization.
- Simply not wanting to pay 15.3% SE tax is not a valid basis.

## 6. Key Deductions

### Self-Employment Tax Deductions
- **50% of SE tax** — Deductible above the line (same as all self-employed individuals).
  Critical because clergy pay FULL 15.3% SE tax on net earnings from ministry.

### Business Expenses (Schedule C for 1099 income)
- **Unreimbursed ministry expenses** — If church does not have an accountable plan, clergy
  must report expenses on Schedule C (for 1099 income) or cannot deduct federally if
  W-2 only (post-TCJA). Best practice: church should maintain an accountable plan.
- **Books, periodicals, commentaries** — Biblical/theological reference materials.
- **Vestments and clerical clothing** — Deductible if not suitable for everyday wear.
  Roman Catholic vestments, Jewish tallitot, clerical collars clearly qualify. A business
  suit worn for preaching does NOT qualify.
- **Professional dues** — Denominational dues, ministerial association memberships.
- **Continuing education** — Seminary courses, conferences, retreats that maintain/improve
  ministerial skills.
- **Auto/mileage** — Hospital visits, shut-in visits, community outreach, denominational
  meetings (not commuting to primary church). $0.70/mile (2025).
- **Office supplies, computer** — If maintaining home office for ministry work.
- **Travel for ministry** — Mission trips, denominational conferences, speaking engagements.

## 7. 403(b)(9) — Church Retirement Plans

The 403(b)(9) is a retirement plan type available ONLY to churches and qualified
church-controlled organizations. It has unique features:

- **Contribution limits** — Same as regular 403(b): $23,500 deferral (2025), $31,000 if 50+.
  The 15-year catch-up ($3,000/year additional) may apply for long-serving ministers.
- **NOT subject to ERISA** — Church plans are exempt from ERISA, which means no annual
  Form 5500 filing, no PBGC premiums, but also no ERISA protections for participants.
- **Housing allowance in retirement** — THE key benefit. Distributions from a 403(b)(9) plan
  can be designated as housing allowance by the plan administrator. The retired minister
  excludes the designated amount from income tax (to the extent of qualifying housing
  expenses). This benefit is NOT available from regular 403(b), 401(k), or IRA plans.
- **Unique matching provisions** — Church plans may have more flexible matching and
  contribution formulas than commercial plans.
- **Denominational pension boards** — Many denominations (ELCA, UMC/Wespath, PCUSA/BOP,
  SBC/GuideStone, RCA) operate 403(b)(9) plans for their clergy. Ministers should
  prioritize contributions to 403(b)(9) over regular IRA or other retirement vehicles.
- **Planning priority** — Maximize 403(b)(9) contributions throughout career. A minister
  contributing $23,500/year for 30 years at 7% growth accumulates approximately $2.1M.
  In retirement, designating $50,000/year as housing allowance = $50,000 tax-free income
  annually. This is an extraordinary retirement benefit.

## 8. Bivocational Ministers

Ministers who split time between ministry and secular employment face additional complexity:

- **Allocation** — Must allocate income and expenses between ministry and secular employment.
  Only ministry income is subject to the dual-status rules.
- **Secular W-2 income** — Normal FICA withholding applies to the secular job. SS/Medicare
  credits are earned from the secular employment.
- **Ministry income** — Subject to SE tax (unless Form 4361 filed). Housing allowance
  available only for the ministry portion.
- **Combined SE tax** — Total SE tax from ministry income + FICA from secular employment
  cannot exceed the maximum Social Security wage base. If secular wages reach the SS
  wage base ($176,100 in 2025), no additional 12.4% SS portion on ministry income
  (but 2.9% Medicare still applies with no cap).

## 9. Recommended Entity Structure

- **Most clergy** — W-2 employee of church. Church withholds income tax (voluntarily agreed
  to by clergy) or clergy makes estimated payments. NO FICA withholding.
- **Traveling evangelist/speaker** — Schedule C sole proprietor for speaking and ministry
  income not from a single employer.
- **Author/speaker with significant income** — May benefit from LLC/S-Corp for non-ministry
  business income (book sales, speaking fees). S-Corp does NOT help with SE tax on
  ministry income (SE tax is on net earnings from self-employment in the exercise of ministry).
- **Para-church organization founder** — May incorporate as 501(c)(3). Salary is W-2 from
  the organization.

## 10. Common Audit Triggers

- **Excessive housing allowance** — Cannot exceed the LOWEST of: (1) actual housing expenses,
  (2) FMV of home (including furnishings, utilities), (3) amount officially designated by
  church. IRS scrutinizes designations that are clearly above actual expenses.
  **Audit defense**: Maintain documentation of ALL housing expenses (receipts, mortgage
  statements, utility bills, insurance declarations, repair invoices). Keep the church
  designation letter with tax records. Calculate the FRV independently using comparable
  rental listings.
- **Love offerings** — Large amounts from congregation that are not properly characterized.
  Must determine if truly gifts or compensation for services (*Goodwin*).
- **Dual-status errors** — Filing as fully exempt from SE tax without valid Form 4361, or
  vice versa.
- **Home office combined with housing allowance** — Complex interaction. Cannot double-dip:
  expenses used for housing allowance exclusion cannot also be used for home office deduction.
- **Form 4361 without proper grounds** — Must be conscientiously opposed to receiving public
  insurance benefits (SS/Medicare) based on religious principles. Cannot opt out merely
  to save money.

## 11. State Considerations

- **State housing allowance treatment** — Most states conform to the federal housing
  allowance exclusion, but a few do not or have modifications. Verify state conformity.
- **State income tax on housing allowance** — Generally excluded from state income tax
  in conforming states.
- **Clergy in parsonages** — Property tax exemption for church-owned parsonages varies by
  state. Some states fully exempt; others treat as taxable real property.
- **State SE tax equivalent** — No state imposes SE tax, but some states (CA, NJ) have
  state-level disability or family leave taxes that may apply.
- **Multi-state ministry** — Traveling evangelists/speakers working in multiple states may
  owe tax in each state. Track income by state.
- **Sales tax exemption** — Church purchases may be sales-tax exempt. Clergy personal
  purchases are not exempt.

## 12. Common Mistakes

1. **Not designating housing allowance in advance** — Must be officially designated by
   church board BEFORE the period to which it applies. Retroactive designation is invalid.
   Missing this means losing the entire exclusion for the year.
2. **Excluding housing allowance from SE tax** — Housing allowance is excluded from income
   tax but MUST be included in SE tax calculation. Many clergy (and preparers) miss this.
3. **Filing Form 4361 for financial reasons** — Election must be based on religious conviction,
   not tax savings. IRS can revoke. And the election is irrevocable — permanently gives up
   SS/Medicare benefits.
4. **Missing Form 4361 deadline** — Must be filed by due date of return for second year with
   $400+ ministry SE income. If missed, election is permanently lost. Cannot be filed late.
5. **Double-counting housing expenses** — Cannot claim mortgage interest and property taxes
   as both housing allowance exclusion AND itemized deductions. Must reduce itemized
   deductions by amounts excluded under housing allowance.
6. **Exceeding housing allowance limits** — Excluding more than actual expenses or FRV.
   Excess must be reported as taxable income.
7. **Not using 403(b)(9) plan** — The ability to designate retirement distributions as
   housing allowance is extraordinary. Clergy using regular 403(b) or IRA miss this benefit.
8. **Omitting love offerings** — Regular payments from congregation are taxable income,
   not tax-free gifts (*Goodwin*). Must report on Schedule C or include in W-2.
9. **Incorrect W-2 preparation by church** — Small churches often prepare W-2s incorrectly:
   including housing allowance in Box 1, or entering FICA amounts in Boxes 3-6.
   Clergy should verify W-2 accuracy before filing.
10. **Not making estimated tax payments** — With no FICA withholding and often no income
    tax withholding, clergy must make quarterly estimated payments to avoid underpayment penalty.
    Alternative: request the church increase voluntary income tax withholding to cover
    both income tax AND SE tax (by filing Form W-4 with extra withholding).
11. **Bivocational ministers not allocating properly** — Must separate ministry income
    (dual-status rules, housing allowance eligible) from secular income (normal W-2/FICA).
12. **Not maintaining housing expense documentation** — In an audit, the IRS will request
    proof of every dollar excluded. Keep receipts for mortgage, utilities, furnishings,
    repairs, insurance, and all other qualifying housing costs.
