# Teacher / Educator — Tax Reference Guide (TY 2025)

## 1. Overview

Teachers and educators — from K-12 classroom teachers to college professors — face a tax
landscape shaped by moderate salaries, significant unreimbursed classroom expenses, pension
systems, student loan burdens, and summer income opportunities. The $300 above-the-line
educator expense deduction is the most well-known provision, but proper planning around
retirement accounts (especially the 403(b) + 457(b) dual contribution strategy), loan
forgiveness programs, and supplemental income can substantially improve a teacher's
financial position.

## 2. Common Income Types

- **W-2 salary** — Primary income from school district, private school, or university.
  Typically $45,000-$95,000 for K-12 (varies widely by state/district). College professors
  range from $50K (adjunct/community college) to $200K+ (full professor at research university).
- **Summer employment** — Camp counselor, curriculum development, tutoring. May be W-2 or 1099.
  Camp counseling is W-2 income from the camp employer. Summer curriculum writing for the
  district is typically W-2 from the district (supplemental contract).
- **Tutoring / test prep** — Private tutoring on the side. If regular activity, reported on
  Schedule C as self-employment income subject to SE tax. If through company (Kaplan,
  Wyzant), may receive W-2 or 1099 depending on classification.
- **Coaching stipends** — Athletic coaching, club advisor payments. Usually included in W-2
  from school district as supplemental pay. Coaching stipends range from $1,000-$10,000+
  depending on sport and level.
- **Adjunct teaching** — Additional income from community college or online courses. W-2
  from each institution.
- **Curriculum/content creation** — Teachers Pay Teachers (TPT) income, course creation
  (Udemy, Coursera, Outschool). 1099-K or 1099-NEC. This is self-employment income
  reported on Schedule C.
- **Substitute teaching** — W-2 income, sometimes from multiple districts.
- **Grant/fellowship income** — Taxable if used for living expenses. Tax-free if used for
  tuition and required fees at degree-granting institution.

## 3. Key Deductions

### Educator Expense Deduction — IRC 62(a)(2)(D) (Above-the-Line)

This is the signature tax benefit for educators. Key details:

- **$300 per educator** ($600 for married couple both educators) for 2025.
- **Above-the-line** — Claimed on Schedule 1, line 11. Reduces AGI regardless of whether
  itemizing or taking the standard deduction. This is "free money" for every teacher.
- **Eligibility** — K-12 teacher, instructor, counselor, principal, or aide who works at
  least 900 hours during the school year in a school providing elementary or secondary
  education (as determined under state law).
- **Qualifying expenses include**:
  - Books and supplementary materials
  - Supplies (markers, paper, art supplies, lab materials)
  - Computer equipment and software used in the classroom
  - Other equipment (calculators, microscopes, musical instruments for music teachers)
  - Professional development courses related to curriculum being taught
  - COVID-era protective items (PPE, disinfectant, physical barriers, air purifiers)
  - Athletic supplies if used for health/PE courses teaching physical fitness
- **NOT qualifying**:
  - Home schooling supplies
  - Expenses reimbursed by employer or grants
  - Non-athletic supplies for health or physical education courses (e.g., health textbooks
    are qualifying; dodgeballs are qualifying; but first aid kits for non-PE courses are not)
- **Expenses exceeding $300** — The federal deduction is capped at $300. Expenses above
  that amount are NOT deductible federally post-TCJA (the unreimbursed employee expense
  deduction was eliminated). However, some states allow the excess on state returns.

### Self-Employment Deductions (Tutoring/Side Business)
- **Home office** — If tutoring from home regularly and exclusively.
- **Supplies and materials** — Workbooks, educational materials, software.
- **Marketing** — Website, advertising for tutoring services.
- **Mileage** — If traveling to students' homes for tutoring ($0.70/mile).
- **Professional development** — Courses that improve skills in current tutoring business.
- **SE tax deduction** — 50% of self-employment tax is deductible above the line.

### Standard Deductions for All Educators
- **Student loan interest** — Up to $2,500 above the line. MAGI phase-out: $80,000-$95,000
  single / $170,000-$200,000 MFJ (2025).
- **Tuition for advanced degree** — If pursuing master's/PhD to improve skills in current
  position (maintaining or improving existing skills), the cost may be deductible as
  education expense on Schedule C if self-employed, or as part of employer's tuition
  reimbursement (up to $5,250 tax-free under IRC 127).
- **Charitable contributions** — If itemizing. Donations to school fundraisers, PTA/PTO.

## 4. Retirement Plan Strategy — The Teacher's Power Play

### 403(b) Plans
- **Available to** — Public school employees and employees of 501(c)(3) organizations
  (including private schools). Similar to a 401(k) but for tax-exempt employers.
- **2025 contribution limit** — $23,500 deferral ($31,000 if age 50+).
- **15-year catch-up rule** — Unique to 403(b): employees with 15+ years of service at the
  SAME employer may contribute an additional $3,000/year, up to a $15,000 lifetime maximum.
  This is computed BEFORE the age-50 catch-up. Example: a teacher with 20 years in the
  same district, age 52, can contribute $23,500 + $3,000 (15-year catch-up) + $7,500
  (age-50 catch-up) = $34,000 in a single year. This rule is often overlooked.
- **Investment options** — 403(b) plans may offer annuities (from insurance companies) or
  custodial accounts (mutual funds). Many districts have historically offered high-fee
  annuity products; teachers should compare fees carefully.

### 457(b) Plans — The Double-Dip
- **Available to** — Government (public school) employees. NOT available at private schools.
- **Critical advantage** — The 457(b) limit ($23,500) is SEPARATE from the 403(b) limit.
  A public school teacher can contribute to BOTH plans simultaneously.
- **Combined deferral** — 403(b) $23,500 + 457(b) $23,500 = $47,000 in total deferrals.
  With age-50 catch-up in each: $31,000 + $31,000 = $62,000. This is one of the most
  powerful retirement savings strategies available to ANY worker.
- **457(b) special catch-up** — In the 3 years before "normal retirement age" (as defined
  by the plan), employees can contribute up to $47,000/year to the 457(b) alone. This
  does NOT stack with age-50 catch-up for 457(b), but the 403(b) age-50 catch-up still
  applies.
- **No early withdrawal penalty** — 457(b) distributions are not subject to the 10% early
  withdrawal penalty (unlike 403(b) and 401(k)). Taxed as ordinary income but no penalty
  regardless of age. Excellent for early retirees.

### State Pension Systems
- **Defined benefit pension** — Most states have teacher pension systems. Benefits calculated
  as: years of service x final average salary x benefit multiplier (typically 1.5%-2.5%).
- **Non-Social Security states** — Teachers in approximately 15 states do NOT participate
  in Social Security through their teaching position: AK, CA (CalSTRS), CO (PERA),
  CT (some), GA (some), IL (TRS), KY (some), LA (TRSL), MA (MTRS), ME (MainePERS),
  MO (PSRS), NV (PERS), OH (STRS), RI (some), TX (TRS).
- **WEP/GPO status changed materially in 2025** — The Social Security Fairness Act repealed
  both WEP and GPO for benefits payable after December 2023. Older teacher-retirement advice on
  this point is often stale. Current planning should use actual SSA notices and the post-repeal
  rules, not old haircut assumptions.

### Personal Plans
- **Traditional/Roth IRA** — $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+). Roth often optimal for teachers
  in moderate tax brackets.
- **Saver's Credit** — Teachers with lower AGI may qualify. Worth up to $1,000/$2,000.
- **Solo 401(k) or SEP-IRA** — For tutoring side business income. Shelters self-employment
  income from taxes.

## 5. Student Loan Strategies

### Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF)
- **Eligibility** — Full-time employment at a qualifying public service employer (public
  schools qualify; most private schools do NOT unless 501(c)(3)).
- **Requirement** — 120 qualifying monthly payments under a qualifying repayment plan
  (income-driven repayment: IBR, PAYE, REPAYE/SAVE, ICR).
- **Tax treatment** — Forgiven amount is NOT taxable income under IRC 108(f)(1). This is
  permanent (unlike the temporary provision for IDR forgiveness).
- **Strategy** — Enroll in income-driven repayment IMMEDIATELY upon entering teaching.
  Minimize payments during the 10-year qualifying period. Do NOT pay extra toward principal.
  A teacher earning $55K with $80K in loans could pay ~$300/month for 10 years (~$36K total)
  and have $50K+ forgiven tax-free.
- **Certification** — Submit Employment Certification Form annually to track progress.

### Teacher Loan Forgiveness Program (Separate from PSLF)
- **Amount** — Up to $17,500 forgiven for highly qualified math, science, and special
  education teachers. Up to $5,000 for other qualifying teachers.
- **Requirement** — 5 consecutive years teaching at a low-income school or educational
  service agency.
- **Tax treatment** — Forgiven amount is tax-free.
- **Cannot be combined with PSLF for the same period** — The 5 years used for Teacher Loan
  Forgiveness do not count toward the 120 PSLF payments. Strategy: use Teacher Loan
  Forgiveness first (years 1-5), then switch to PSLF (years 6-16) for remaining balance.

### IDR Forgiveness
- **20-25 year forgiveness** — Remaining balance forgiven after 20-25 years of payments under
  income-driven repayment. Forgiven amount is tax-free through 2025 (IRC 108(f)(5)
  temporary provision). After 2025, forgiveness may be taxable — verify at filing time.

## 6. Recommended Entity Structure

- **Primary teaching job** — W-2 employee. No entity needed.
- **Small tutoring side business** — Sole proprietor, Schedule C. Simple and appropriate
  for most teachers earning under $20K from tutoring.
- **Significant tutoring/content business ($30K+ net)** — Single-member LLC for liability
  protection. Consider S-Corp if net exceeds $50K+.
- **Teachers Pay Teachers / course creation** — Schedule C income. If gross revenue is
  substantial, LLC provides protection.

## 7. Common Audit Triggers

- **Large educator expense claims** — Claims significantly above $300 may draw attention
  (though $300 is the legal limit for the above-the-line deduction).
- **Tutoring income not reported** — Cash payments for tutoring must be reported even without
  1099. IRS can reconstruct income from bank deposits.
- **Home office for W-2 teacher** — NOT deductible federally for W-2 employees (even if
  teaching remotely). Only deductible for Schedule C tutoring business.
- **Education expenses mischaracterized** — Expenses for NEW career (e.g., teacher getting
  law degree) are not deductible. Must maintain or improve skills in CURRENT profession.
- **Schedule C losses from tutoring** — Consistent losses suggest hobby, not business.

## 8. Profession-Specific Rules

- **PSLF (Public Service Loan Forgiveness)** — Public school teachers qualify. After 120
  qualifying payments while employed full-time by qualifying employer. Forgiven amount is
  NOT taxable income. Critical strategy for teachers with large loan balances.
- **Teacher Loan Forgiveness Program** — Separate from PSLF. Up to $17,500 forgiven after
  5 consecutive years teaching in low-income school. Forgiveness is tax-free.
- **Lifetime Learning Credit** — 20% of up to $10,000 qualified education expenses ($2,000
  max credit). MAGI phase-out: $80,000-$90,000 single / $160,000-$180,000 MFJ.
  Available for graduate courses. Cannot be claimed in the same year as AOTC for the
  same student.
- **American Opportunity Credit** — Up to $2,500 per student for first 4 years of higher
  education. $1,000 is refundable. For teachers pursuing undergraduate degree.
- **Employer tuition reimbursement** — IRC 127 allows $5,250/year tax-free from employer
  for education expenses. Many districts offer this.
- **IRC 108(f)** — Student loan forgiveness from qualifying employer (including PSLF) is
  excludable from gross income. IDR forgiveness is tax-free through 2025.
- **Union dues** — NOT deductible federally post-TCJA (2018-2025). Union dues for teachers
  range from $500-$1,200/year depending on state/local union. Deductible in approximately
  15 states on the state return (see state considerations).

## 9. State Considerations

- **Educator expense deduction** — Some states offer larger deductions than the $300 federal.
  Minnesota: additional state subtraction for educator expenses. Several states conform to
  the federal deduction and some add their own provisions.
- **Union dues** — NOT deductible federally (TCJA eliminated miscellaneous itemized deductions).
  However, deductible on state returns in: CA, NY, NJ, MN, PA, HI, AR, and others.
  In CA, union dues are deductible on Schedule CA as an unreimbursed employee expense.
- **State pension tax treatment** — Many states exempt teacher pension income from state
  tax: IL (100% exempt), PA (100% exempt), MS (100% exempt), AL (exempt up to limit).
  Others tax fully or partially. This is a major factor in retirement state selection.
- **Non-Social Security states** — Teachers in AK, CA, CO, CT, GA (some), IL, KY (some),
  LA, MA, ME, MO, NV, OH, RI (some), TX do not participate in SS through their teacher
  pension. This still affects retirement planning, but current-law WEP/GPO haircut assumptions
  need to be updated for the 2025 repeal.
- **Teacher shortage incentives** — Some states offer tax credits or deductions for teachers
  in shortage areas (STEM, special education, rural schools). Verify current provisions.
- **Income tax-free states** — Teachers in FL, TX, TN, WA, NV, WY, AK, SD, NH benefit from
  no state income tax on their salary. Combined with generous pension, this makes these
  states financially attractive for teachers despite sometimes lower nominal salaries.
- **State-level unreimbursed employee expenses** — In states that allow (CA, NY, NJ, PA,
  MN, HI, AR), teachers can deduct classroom supplies exceeding the $300 federal limit,
  union dues, and other unreimbursed expenses on the state return.

## 10. Summer Income — Special Considerations

- **Tutoring as self-employment** — Private tutoring during summer (SAT/ACT prep, subject
  tutoring, enrichment programs) is self-employment income reported on Schedule C. Subject
  to SE tax. Deductions available: mileage, supplies, home office (if exclusive use).
- **Camp counselor** — W-2 income from the camp. Ordinary wages. No special deductions.
- **Curriculum writing** — If contracted by school district for summer curriculum development,
  usually W-2 from the district. If contracted independently, may be 1099.
- **Online course creation** — Teachers Pay Teachers, Outschool, Udemy income is self-
  employment income. Deduct platform fees, materials, software, and home office.
- **Spreading W-2 salary** — Some districts pay teachers over 12 months even though the
  school year is 10 months. This is a payroll convenience; it does not change the total
  annual taxable income. Withholding is spread evenly across all paychecks.
- **Estimated payments for side income** — If summer tutoring/content income is significant
  ($5K+), teachers should make estimated payments to avoid underpayment penalty. The W-2
  withholding from teaching salary may not cover the SE tax on side income.

## 11. Pension vs. Social Security Interaction — Post-Repeal Reality

This is still one of the most misunderstood areas for teacher retirement planning, but the law
changed materially in 2025.

### Current Rule
- The Social Security Fairness Act, signed January 5, 2025, repealed both WEP and GPO for
  benefits payable after December 2023.
- Teachers in non-SS states may still receive a teacher pension plus Social Security benefits
  earned from other covered work without the old WEP formula reduction.
- Spousal and survivor benefits should no longer be reduced by GPO under current law.

### What Teachers Should Actually Check Now
- Whether SSA has correctly updated their monthly benefit.
- Whether retroactive catch-up payments were issued correctly.
- Whether old retirement projections, district seminars, or pension calculators still embed
  pre-2025 WEP/GPO assumptions.
- Whether their state pension, 403(b), 457(b), and Social Security claiming strategy now needs
  to be re-modeled under the repeal.

## 12. Common Mistakes

1. **Not claiming the $300 educator expense deduction** — It's above the line, available even
   with standard deduction. Free money for virtually every teacher.
2. **Missing 403(b) + 457(b) dual contribution** — Public school teachers can defer up to
   $47,000/year in these two plans combined ($62,000 with both age-50 catch-ups). Many
   only contribute to one.
3. **Not pursuing PSLF** — Teachers at public schools qualify. With IBR/REPAYE and 10 years
   of service, forgiveness can be $50K-$200K+. Tax-free.
4. **Paying student loans aggressively while qualifying for PSLF** — If pursuing PSLF,
   minimize payments (income-driven repayment). Excess payments are wasted.
5. **Not reporting tutoring income** — Even small amounts ($500-$5,000) must be reported.
   Cash payments don't have 1099 but are still taxable.
6. **Claiming home office for W-2 teaching** — Federal deduction not available for employees.
   Only for Schedule C side business.
7. **Missing the 15-year 403(b) catch-up** — Teachers with 15+ years at the same employer
   can contribute an additional $3,000/year beyond normal limits. Often overlooked by
   payroll departments and plan administrators. Teacher must request it.
8. **Using stale WEP/GPO assumptions** — Teachers in non-SS states often still receive advice
   based on the pre-2025 rules. Re-run Social Security planning using the repeal and actual SSA
   benefit notices.
9. **Ignoring state-level deductions for union dues and unreimbursed expenses** — Several
   states allow deductions that are no longer available federally. Worth filing state
   itemized return even if taking federal standard deduction.
10. **Using Teacher Loan Forgiveness and PSLF simultaneously** — The 5 years for Teacher
    Loan Forgiveness cannot count toward PSLF's 120 payments. Sequence them strategically:
    Teacher Loan Forgiveness first (years 1-5), then begin PSLF count (years 6-16).
11. **Not choosing the right income-driven repayment plan** — SAVE/REPAYE vs. IBR vs. PAYE
    have different payment calculations. The lowest payment plan maximizes PSLF benefit.
12. **Teachers Pay Teachers income not reported** — TPT income is self-employment income
    even if received via 1099-K. Platform fees are deductible, but the gross revenue must
    be reported on Schedule C. Some teachers mistakenly think small amounts are not taxable.
13. **Not contributing to Roth during low-income years** — Teachers in moderate tax brackets
    should strongly consider Roth contributions (Roth 403(b), Roth IRA) rather than
    traditional. The tax-free growth is more valuable when current rates are low.
