# Real Estate Agent — Tax Reference Guide (TY 2025)

## 1. Overview

Real estate agents are classified as statutory nonemployees under IRC 3508 — they receive
1099-NEC income even when affiliated with a brokerage. This creates both opportunity
(extensive business deductions) and obligation (self-employment tax, quarterly estimated
payments). The profession involves high marketing costs, significant vehicle use, variable
income, and unique deduction categories. Most agents' effective tax rate can be dramatically
reduced with proper planning. Critically, real estate brokerage is NOT a specified service
trade or business (SSTB), meaning agents qualify for the full 20% QBI deduction at ALL
income levels — one of the most valuable profession-specific tax advantages available.

## 2. IRC 3508 — Statutory Nonemployee Status

This is the foundational tax provision for real estate agents. IRC 3508 provides that
qualified real estate agents are treated as non-employees (independent contractors) for ALL
federal tax purposes if three conditions are met:

1. **Licensed real estate agent** — The individual holds a valid real estate license.
2. **Substantially all compensation based on sales or output** — Not based on hours worked.
   Commission-based pay satisfies this. If the brokerage pays a salary or hourly rate for
   floor time, training, or administrative tasks, the agent may fail this test.
3. **Written agreement** — A written contract between the agent and broker states that the
   agent will not be treated as an employee for federal tax purposes.

**Effect**: Even if the brokerage provides office space, sets open house schedules, requires
meetings, or provides leads, the agent is still an independent contractor if the three tests
are met. This overrides the normal common-law employee analysis. No W-2 withholding, no
employer FICA — the agent is responsible for all self-employment tax and estimated payments.

## 3. Common Income Types

- **1099-NEC from brokerage** — Commission income reported by the broker. Statutory
  nonemployee status means no withholding for income tax, Social Security, or Medicare.
- **Referral fees** — Fees received for referring clients to other agents. Taxable income.
  Payer should issue 1099-NEC if $600+.
- **Rental income** — Many agents invest in real estate. Reported on Schedule E.
- **Property management fees** — If providing management services, reported on Schedule C.
- **Teaching/coaching income** — CE instruction, coaching newer agents. 1099-NEC or W-2.
- **Team leader income** — Override commissions from team members. Still 1099-NEC.

## 4. Key Deductions

Real estate agents have among the most deduction-rich Schedule C filings.

### Vehicle / Mileage (typically the largest deduction)
- **Standard mileage rate** — $0.70/mile for 2025. Includes gas, insurance, depreciation,
  maintenance. Must track contemporaneously (mileage log apps: MileIQ, Everlance, Hurdlr).
- **Actual expense method** — Gas, insurance, repairs, registration, depreciation, lease
  payments. Multiply total by business-use percentage. Once chosen for a vehicle you OWN
  and claimed depreciation, you cannot switch to standard mileage for that vehicle.
- **Commuting exclusion** — Drive from home to brokerage office is commuting (not deductible).
  But drive from home office to showings IS deductible if home office qualifies as
  principal place of business.
- **Typical agent mileage** — Agents routinely drive 15,000-25,000+ business miles/year.
  At $0.70/mile, that is $10,500-$17,500+ in deductions. High-volume agents in sprawling
  markets (Houston, LA, Atlanta) may exceed 30,000 miles = $21,000+ deduction.
- **Contemporaneous log requirement** — IRS Reg. 1.274-5T requires recording of: date, destination,
  business purpose, and miles driven at or near the time of each trip. Reconstructed logs
  created at year-end are weak and frequently rejected on audit. App-based tracking
  (GPS auto-detection) is the gold standard.
- **Mixed-use vehicle** — Must allocate between business and personal use. A vehicle used
  80% for business means 80% of actual expenses are deductible, or 80% of miles qualify
  for standard mileage rate.

### Marketing and Advertising
- **Online advertising** — Zillow Premier Agent ($300-$1,000+/month), Realtor.com,
  Google Ads, Facebook/Instagram ads, YouTube pre-roll. Fully deductible.
- **Photography and videography** — Listing photos ($150-$500/listing), drone footage,
  virtual tours, Matterport 3D scans. Deductible per listing.
- **Staging costs** — If agent pays for staging (not reimbursed by seller), deductible
  business expense. Full staging packages can run $2,000-$5,000+ per listing.
- **Signage** — Yard signs, open house directional signs, banner stands. Deductible.
- **Vehicle wrap / magnets** — A full vehicle wrap ($2,000-$5,000) is deductible as
  advertising. It does NOT convert personal vehicle miles into business miles.
- **Website** — Domain, hosting, IDX feed, CRM (kvCORE, Follow Up Boss, LionDesk). Monthly
  subscriptions are fully deductible.
- **Direct mail, postcards, flyers** — Farming/geographic marketing. Postage + printing.
- **Open house expenses** — Food, beverages, printed materials for open houses. The food
  is NOT a meal deduction (it is marketing/advertising to attract potential buyers).
- **Print materials** — Business cards, brochures, feature sheets, listing presentations.

### Professional Fees and Dues
- **MLS fees** — Board/association dues, MLS access fees ($500-$2,000+/year).
- **NAR/state/local association dues** — Required for MLS access in most markets.
- **E&O insurance** — Errors and omissions professional liability. Typically $300-$800/year.
- **License renewal and CE** — State-required continuing education courses. Deductible as
  business expense (maintaining existing license, not qualifying for new career).
- **Desk fees / brokerage fees** — Monthly fees paid to brokerage for office space, tech,
  and support ($200-$1,000+/month). Fully deductible.
- **Split commissions** — When the agent pays a portion of their commission to the brokerage,
  the agent reports gross commission as income and deducts the brokerage split as an expense.
  Alternatively, if the brokerage reports only the agent's net share on 1099-NEC, report
  only the net amount as income.
- **Transaction coordinator fees** — Paid per transaction for file management ($300-$500/deal).
- **Lockbox/key fees** — Supra/SentriLock access fees ($300-$500/year).
- **Assistant/team member expenses** — If agent hires unlicensed assistant or licensed team
  members, their compensation is deductible. Must issue W-2 (employee) or 1099-NEC
  (independent contractor) as appropriate. Payroll tax obligations apply for employees.

### Other Business Expenses
- **Home office** — Must be regular and exclusive. Principal place of business qualifies
  even if agent also has desk at brokerage (if home is where substantial administrative
  work is conducted).
- **Cell phone** — Business-use percentage of plan cost and device.
- **Client gifts** — $25 per recipient per year limit on deductible gifts (IRC 274(b)).
  Closing gifts over $25 per client are NOT fully deductible. Exception: items costing
  $4 or less with business name/logo (branded pens, magnets) are excluded from the
  $25 limit as promotional items.
- **Referral fees paid** — Fees paid to other agents for client referrals. Deductible.
  Must issue 1099-NEC if $600+ to a non-corporate recipient.
- **Business meals** — 50% deductible. Client lunches, team meetings with food.
  Must document: who, where, date, business purpose.

## 5. QBI Deduction — Real Estate Agents' Major Advantage

The Section 199A qualified business income deduction is worth special attention:

- **Real estate brokerage is NOT an SSTB** — Unlike attorneys, accountants, consultants,
  and doctors, real estate agents are not zeroed out solely because of their profession.
  This is confirmed in Reg. 1.199A-5(b)(2) — the list of SSTBs does not include real estate
  brokerage. Above the threshold, however, the W-2/UBIA limitation can still reduce the deduction.
- **Worked example** — Agent has $200K net Schedule C income (after all deductions), MFJ.
  QBI deduction = $200K x 20% = $40,000. At a 24% marginal rate, this saves $9,600 in
  federal tax. At 32% marginal rate, saves $12,800.
- **W-2/UBIA limitation** — At taxable income above $394,600 (MFJ) / $197,300 (single),
  the QBI deduction is limited to the greater of: (a) 50% of W-2 wages paid by the
  business, or (b) 25% of W-2 wages + 2.5% of UBIA (unadjusted basis of qualified
  property). Solo agents with no employees and no depreciable property may see QBI
  limited above the threshold. Hiring a W-2 assistant or investing in depreciable
  business property can preserve the deduction.
- **S-Corp consideration** — The salary paid by the S-Corp IS W-2 wages for 199A purposes.
  This can actually help preserve the QBI deduction at high income levels.

## 6. Real Property Professional Status

Real estate agents who also own rental property have a built-in advantage:

- **Qualification** — Must spend 750+ hours in real property trades/businesses AND more than
  50% of personal services in real property trades/businesses. Active real estate agents
  who show and sell full-time easily meet this threshold.
- **Effect** — Rental losses are NOT subject to the $25,000 passive activity loss limitation
  or the general passive activity rules. Rental losses can offset commission income dollar
  for dollar. This is extraordinarily valuable for agents with leveraged rental properties
  generating depreciation losses.
- **Documentation** — Maintain a contemporaneous time log. Track hours spent on: showing
  properties, listing presentations, marketing, CE classes, MLS research, property
  management, and all other real property activities.
- **Each rental must be elected** — Must make an election under Reg. 1.469-9(g) to treat
  each rental as a separate activity (or aggregate them).

## 7. Recommended Entity Structure

- **New agents (income under $40K net)** — Sole proprietor (Schedule C). Low overhead.
- **Established agents ($60K+ net)** — S-Corp election. Pay reasonable salary (35-50% of
  net income for RE agents, benchmarked to local market). Save SE tax on distributions.
- **High-producing agents ($100K+ net)** — S-Corp becomes critical. Example: agent nets
  $150K. Pays $75K salary (reasonable for an experienced agent). Distributions of $75K
  avoid 15.3% SE tax = approximately $11,475 saved. Must file Form 2553 by March 15
  for calendar year (or within 75 days of forming entity).
- **Real estate teams** — Multi-member LLC taxed as partnership or S-Corp. Team leader
  as managing member.
- **Note on state requirements** — Some states require real estate activity be conducted
  through the licensed broker, not a personal LLC. Structure the S-Corp to receive
  commissions paid through the broker to the agent's entity.

## 8. Dealer vs. Investor — Critical Distinction for Agent-Investors

Agents who invest in real estate must carefully separate their roles:

- **Dealer (flipper)** — Properties acquired with intent to sell quickly for profit. ALL gain
  is ordinary income + SE tax. NO Section 1031 exchange available. NO long-term capital gain
  treatment. Reported on Schedule C. Agents who flip houses are dealers for those properties.
- **Investor (buy-and-hold)** — Properties acquired for rental income or long-term appreciation.
  Gain on sale is Section 1231 / capital gain. 1031 exchange eligible. Reported on Schedule E.
  Depreciation is available.
- **Can an agent be both?** — Yes, but must clearly separate the two activities. Use different
  LLCs, separate bank accounts, and document the intent for each property at acquisition.
  A single agent can be a dealer for flips and an investor for rental properties simultaneously.
- **Why it matters** — An agent who flips a property for $80K profit and treats it as investment
  gain pays ~$12,000 in tax (15% LTCG). If properly treated as dealer/ordinary income, tax
  is ~$28,000+ (35% marginal + SE tax). The difference is significant, and the IRS closely
  scrutinizes agent-investors who claim capital gain treatment on properties that were clearly
  inventory.

## 9. Retirement Plan Strategy

- **Solo 401(k)** — Best option for most agents. Employee deferral ($23,500) plus employer
  profit-sharing (25% of W-2 salary if S-Corp). Variable contributions match variable income.
- **SEP-IRA** — Simpler but less flexible. Up to 25% of W-2 (S-Corp) or ~20% net SE income.
- **Roth IRA** — If income permits direct contribution. Otherwise backdoor Roth.
- **Defined benefit plan** — For top producers age 45+ with consistent $200K+ net income.
  Cash balance plans can shelter $280,000+ annually. Combined with Solo 401(k), total
  deferral can exceed $300K/year.
- **Variable income strategy** — Contribute maximum in high-commission years, minimum in
  slow years. Solo 401(k) profit-sharing component is discretionary.

## 9. Common Audit Triggers

- **High mileage deduction without log** — IRS audits mileage aggressively for real estate
  agents because the deduction is so large. Must have contemporaneous log (date, destination,
  business purpose, miles). App-based tracking is ideal.
- **Meal and entertainment deductions** — High percentage relative to income raises flags.
  Must document who, where, business purpose for each meal.
- **Home office combined with brokerage desk** — If agent has assigned desk at brokerage,
  must demonstrate home office is principal place of business (where substantial admin work
  is conducted and meets clients/colleagues by appointment).
- **Mixed personal/business expenses** — Phone, internet, vehicle must be properly allocated.
  100% business use of personal cell phone will be questioned.
- **Net losses for multiple years** — Agent showing losses with low gross income may face
  hobby loss challenge. IRS may question whether RE is a trade or business.

## 10. S-Corp Timing and Salary Benchmarks for Agents

The S-Corp election is critical for high-producing agents. Key details:

- **When to elect** — When consistent net Schedule C income exceeds $60K-$80K. For agents
  whose income fluctuates wildly between $30K and $150K, consider electing at the $80K+
  average level. The S-Corp has administrative costs (payroll, separate return) of
  $2,000-$4,000/year, so the SE tax savings must exceed these costs.
- **Reasonable salary benchmarks** — IRS examines whether the salary is reasonable for the
  services performed. For real estate agents: entry-level agent $40K-$55K; experienced
  agent $55K-$80K; top producer $80K-$120K. The salary should represent fair compensation
  for the agent's services, considering local market rates and production level.
- **Worked example** — Agent nets $180K through S-Corp. Pays $85K salary (reasonable for
  a mid-market experienced agent). Distributions of $95K avoid 2.9% Medicare tax (plus
  0.9% Additional Medicare Tax above threshold) = approximately $3,600-$4,500 in SE tax
  savings. After S-Corp costs ($3,000), net savings: $600-$1,500. At $250K net income,
  savings grow significantly.

## 13. State Considerations

- **Sales tax on services** — Most states do not tax real estate commissions as services,
  but verify for your state (HI taxes services broadly, NM similarly).
- **State estimated tax** — No withholding means agents must make state estimated payments too.
  Penalties vary by state.
- **California** — LLC annual fee ($800 minimum) plus gross receipts fee ($900 at $250K-$500K,
  up to $11,790 at $5M+). High marginal rates (13.3% top) make S-Corp election savings
  more valuable. CA conforms to IRC 3508.
- **Texas** — No income tax, but franchise (margin) tax applies to entities with revenue
  over $2.47M. Most individual agents are under this threshold.
- **Florida** — No income tax. Popular for high-volume agents. No state estimated payments needed.
- **New York** — High rates. NYC unincorporated business tax (4%) applies to sole proprietors
  and partnerships in the five boroughs. S-Corp avoids UBT. PTE election available for
  S-Corp agent entities.
- **Illinois** — Flat 4.95%. PTE election available for S-Corps and partnerships.
- **Multi-state transactions** — Agents working in multiple states (common in border markets:
  DC/MD/VA, NY/NJ/CT, KS/MO) may owe tax in each state where they earn commissions.
  Track commissions by state.

## 11. Estimated Tax Strategy

Real estate agents have no withholding, making estimated tax planning essential:

- **Safe harbor** — Pay 100% of prior year's tax liability (110% if AGI > $150K) in four
  equal quarterly installments to avoid underpayment penalty. This is the safest approach.
- **Annualized method** — If income is uneven (big commission in Q3 but little in Q1/Q2),
  use Form 2210 Schedule AI to compute penalty based on income actually received per period.
  Can significantly reduce or eliminate penalty.
- **Set-aside rule of thumb** — Set aside 25-30% of each gross commission check immediately
  into a separate tax account. This covers federal income tax, SE tax, and state tax.
  Agents who spend commission checks without reserving for taxes face painful Q4 estimated
  payment bills.
- **State estimated payments** — Must be made separately in every state with income tax.
  Penalties vary by state. CA charges 10%+; NY charges ~8%.

## 12. Common Mistakes

1. **Not tracking mileage** — Reconstructed logs are weak in audit. Start using an app on
   day one. The $10,500-$17,500 deduction is the single largest tax saver for most agents.
2. **Missing quarterly estimated payments** — No withholding means agents must pay quarterly.
   Underpayment penalty applies. Use safe harbor: pay 110% of prior year tax (if AGI > $150K).
3. **Deducting personal vehicle use** — Must separate business from personal miles. Keep
   consistent log. Commute to office is personal; showings and client meetings are business.
4. **Overlooking S-Corp election** — Agents netting $80K+ often save $3K-$8K+/year in SE tax
   with S-Corp. Must be timely filed (Form 2553 by March 15 for calendar year).
5. **Failing to claim home office** — Many agents qualify but don't claim it, leaving the
   deduction (and the ability to deduct all mileage from home) on the table.
6. **Not separating flipping from investing** — Agents who flip and hold rentals must
   carefully separate dealer properties (ordinary income, Schedule C) from investment
   properties (Schedule E, capital gain eligible).
7. **Exceeding $25 gift limit** — Closing gifts over $25 per client are not deductible.
   Structure as marketing/advertising if possible (branded items under $4 are excluded).
8. **Missing real property professional election** — Agents who qualify but don't properly
   document hours miss the ability to deduct rental losses against active income.
9. **Not planning for slow years** — Variable income means tax burden is front-loaded in
   good years. Set aside 25-30% of each commission check for taxes.
10. **Ignoring the QBI deduction** — Real estate agents qualify for the full 20% deduction
    at ALL income levels. Worth $4,000-$20,000+ annually for successful agents. Do not
    confuse real estate brokerage with real estate consulting (which could be SSTB).
11. **Desk fees not deducted** — Monthly fees paid to brokerage are deductible business
    expenses. Some agents treat these as part of the commission split and fail to deduct.
12. **Not issuing 1099s for referral fees paid** — If paying $600+ in referral fees to
    another agent (non-corporate), must issue 1099-NEC. Failure to file: $310 penalty per form.
13. **Staging costs not deducted** — When the agent pays for staging out of pocket (not
    reimbursed by the seller), this is a deductible business expense. Full staging packages
    of $2,000-$5,000 per listing add up over the year.
14. **Open house food classified as meals** — Food and beverages at an open house are
    marketing/advertising expenses (100% deductible), not meals (50% deductible). The food
    attracts potential buyers to the property, not a business meal with a client.
