# Financial Professional — Tax Reference Guide (TY 2025)
## Day Traders, Investment Advisors, Insurance Agents, Financial Planners

## 1. Overview

Financial professionals span a wide range: active day traders seeking the IRC 475(f) mark-to-
market election, registered investment advisors (RIAs) managing client assets, insurance agents
earning commissions and trail income, and financial planners charging fees. The tax landscape
involves complex rules around wash sales, carried interest, business vs. investment expenses,
securities licensing, and technology costs. Day traders face particularly arcane rules that can
make or break their tax position.

## 2. Common Income Types

- **W-2 salary** — Employed at brokerage, bank, insurance company, RIA firm. Includes base
  salary, bonuses, commissions, deferred compensation.
- **K-1 partnership income** — Partners in hedge funds, private equity firms, RIA partnerships.
  Includes ordinary income, short/long-term capital gains, carried interest.
- **1099-NEC** — Independent insurance agents, independent financial planners, commission-based
  advisors not classified as employees.
- **Trading gains/losses** — For day traders: reported on Form 8949 / Schedule D (capital
  treatment) unless IRC 475(f) election made (ordinary income/loss on Form 4797).
- **Trail commissions / renewals** — Ongoing commissions from insurance policies, mutual
  funds, annuities. 1099-NEC or 1099-MISC. Taxable when received. Subject to SE tax.
- **Commissions** — Upfront commissions on insurance sales, mutual fund sales, annuity sales.
- **Performance fees** — Hedge fund managers: typically 20% of profits. Taxed based on
  character of underlying gains (pass-through) for partnership structures.
- **Carried interest** — Must meet 3-year holding period for long-term capital gain treatment
  under IRC 1061 (post-TCJA). Short-term gains recharacterized as STCG regardless.
- **Dividend and interest income** — From personal investment accounts. Qualified dividends
  taxed at preferential rates.
- **Management fees** — RIA firms charging AUM-based or flat fees. Ordinary business income.

## 3. Key Deductions

### Employed Financial Professionals
- **Limited post-TCJA** — Unreimbursed employee expenses (Series exam prep, technology,
  professional dues) not deductible federally. Some states still allow.
- **Investment expenses** — NOT deductible post-TCJA (was 2% miscellaneous itemized deduction).
  This includes investment advisory fees, investment publications, safe deposit boxes.
- **SALT** — Capped at $10,000.
- **Margin interest (IRC 163(d))** — Investment interest deduction limited to net investment
  income. Can elect to treat qualified dividends and LTCG as investment income (but lose
  preferential rate treatment on those amounts). Excess margin interest carries forward
  indefinitely. For traders with 475(f) election operating as a business, margin interest
  may be deductible as a business expense (not subject to the investment interest limitation)
  because the trading activity is a trade or business, not an investment activity.

### Self-Employed / Business Owners
- **Technology and data** — Bloomberg Terminal ($24K+/year), FactSet, Morningstar,
  trading platforms (ThinkOrSwim, Interactive Brokers fees), real-time data feeds,
  news services (Reuters, Dow Jones), research subscriptions.
- **Licensing and registration** — Series 7, Series 66, Series 65, CFA program fees,
  CFP certification, state insurance licenses, RIA registration fees, E&O insurance.
- **Office and equipment** — Multiple monitors (dedicated trading room), high-speed internet
  (dedicated trading line), computer hardware, UPS/battery backup. Day traders commonly
  have 4-6 monitor setups costing $5,000-$15,000 — fully deductible if used exclusively
  for trading business.
- **Professional development** — Conferences (FPA, NAIFA, MDRT), continuing education,
  designation courses.
- **Marketing** — Client acquisition costs, website, advertising, seminars, client events.
- **Compliance costs** — Compliance software, audits, regulatory filing fees.
- **Errors and omissions insurance** — Professional liability coverage.
- **Home office** — Common for independent advisors and traders. Must meet regular and
  exclusive use test. For day traders: a dedicated trading room qualifies.
- **Client meals** — 50% deductible. Must document who, where, date, business purpose.
  Entertainment (golf outings, concerts) is NOT deductible post-TCJA.
- **Staff and support** — Administrative assistants, compliance officers, paraplanner salaries.

## 4. IRC 475(f) — Mark-to-Market Election (Day Traders) — Deep Dive

This election fundamentally changes the tax treatment of all trading activity. It is arguably
the most consequential single election available to any individual taxpayer.

### What It Does
- Treats ALL securities held at year-end as if sold on December 31 at FMV (a "deemed sale").
- All gains and losses become ORDINARY (not capital).
- The 60/40 long-term/short-term gain character for Section 1256 contracts is destroyed.
- **Eliminates wash sale rules (IRC 1091)** — Since all positions are marked to market at
  year-end, the wash sale problem disappears. This alone can be worth tens of thousands
  for active traders.
- **Eliminates the $3,000 capital loss limitation** — Ordinary losses are fully deductible
  against any income, with no annual cap. A trader with $200K in losses can deduct the
  full amount against salary, interest, or other income. Without 475(f), only $3,000/year
  of net capital losses can offset other income.

### Who Should Elect
- Active day traders with substantial losses or wanting to avoid wash sale tracking.
- Traders making hundreds or thousands of trades per year who need to eliminate wash sale
  compliance burden.
- NOT appropriate for long-term investors (forces annual recognition of unrealized gains).
- NOT appropriate for traders with large unrealized long-term gains (forces recognition).

### Who Qualifies as a "Trader" (Threshold Question)
The 475(f) election is only available to taxpayers who qualify as "traders" engaged in a
trade or business of trading securities. The IRS and courts have established criteria:

- **Frequent, regular, and continuous trading** — Per *Groetzinger v. Commissioner* (480 U.S.
  23, 1987): the activity must be pursued full-time or substantially full-time, with
  regularity and continuity, and for the primary purpose of making a living.
- ***Chen v. Commissioner* (T.C. Memo 2004-132)** — 323 trades over 12 months with an
  average holding period of several days was INSUFFICIENT to establish trader status.
  The court found the activity more consistent with active investing.
- ***Endicott v. Commissioner* (T.C. Memo 2013-199)** — Over 1,000 round-trip trades per
  year, executed almost daily, with average holding period under one day. Court found
  taxpayer qualified as a trader.
- **Practical threshold** — Courts generally accept trader status with 500+ round-trip
  trades per year, executed nearly daily, with short holding periods (minutes to days).
  Trading must be the taxpayer's primary activity (not a side hobby).
- **Holding long-term investments on the side** — Does NOT disqualify from trader status,
  but long-term holdings MUST be in a separate account, clearly segregated BEFORE the
  475(f) election takes effect.

### How to Elect — The Deadline Is Everything
- **Deadline** — Must be filed by the due date of the tax return for the year BEFORE the
  election is to take effect. To make the election effective for TY 2025: attach an
  election statement to the TY 2024 return, filed by April 15, 2025.
- **For new businesses** — If the taxpayer starts a trading business mid-year, the election
  can be made within 2 months of starting the business. Attach a statement to the return.
- **NO LATE ELECTIONS** — The IRS does not grant extensions or late elections for 475(f).
  Missing the deadline by one day means waiting until the next tax year.
- **The statement** — Must describe the election, identify it as under IRC 475(f)(1) for
  securities and/or 475(f)(2) for commodities, and specify the first tax year for which
  it is effective.
- **Revocation** — The election can be revoked only with IRS consent (private letter ruling
  required). Practically, it is treated as nearly irrevocable.

### Reporting
- Gains and losses reported on Form 4797 (Sales of Business Property), NOT Schedule D.
- Trading gains/losses are ordinary income/loss.
- Subject to SE tax if the trader operates through an entity paying wages (S-Corp).
  If operating as sole proprietor, 475(f) gains/losses are NOT subject to SE tax
  (they are Section 475 gains, not net earnings from self-employment). However,
  they DO qualify as earned income for retirement plan contribution purposes if
  structured properly through an S-Corp paying wages.

### Risk
- Cannot cherry-pick. ALL trading positions (in the elected category) must be marked to
  market, including positions with large unrealized gains.
- Long-term holdings MUST be in a separate account and identified before the election
  takes effect. If long-term holdings are in the same account, they are subject to MTM.

## 5. Wash Sale Rules (IRC 1091) — Without 475(f) Election

- **30-day window** — Cannot deduct loss if substantially identical security purchased
  within 30 days before or after the sale. Applies across ALL accounts (personal, IRA,
  spouse's accounts).
- **Basis adjustment** — Disallowed loss is added to the basis of the replacement security.
  Not permanently lost, but timing is deferred.
- **IRA trap** — A loss sale in a taxable account followed by purchase of the same security
  in an IRA within 30 days creates a PERMANENTLY disallowed loss. The basis adjustment
  does not apply to IRAs. This is one of the most damaging wash sale scenarios.
- **Tax-loss harvesting** — Must wait 31+ days to repurchase or buy a similar (not identical)
  security. ETF vs. mutual fund tracking the same index may be "substantially identical."
- **End-of-year trap** — December loss sales followed by January repurchases trigger wash sales.

## 6. Carried Interest (IRC 1061)

- **3-year holding period** — To qualify for LTCG rates, assets generating carried interest
  must be held for more than 3 years (not the standard 1 year). Applies to "applicable
  partnership interests" — interests received in connection with performance of services
  by the taxpayer in an "applicable trade or business" (raising/returning capital, investing,
  developing, or disposing of securities).
- **Who this affects** — GP/managing members of: hedge funds, private equity funds, venture
  capital funds, and real estate funds where the interest was received for services.
- **Short-term recharacterization** — Gains on assets held 1-3 years are recharacterized as
  STCG (taxed at ordinary rates up to 37%), even though they would normally be LTCG
  under the standard 1-year holding period.
- **Holding period measurement** — Each underlying asset's holding period matters individually.
  A fund disposing of an asset held 2.5 years generates STCG for the carried interest
  holder, even if the fund has been operating for 10 years.
- **Exceptions** — Capital interests (not received for services), S-Corp interests,
  partnership interests held by corporations, and interests in businesses that did not
  involve raising/returning capital.

## 7. Insurance Agent Specific

Insurance agents have distinct tax considerations:

- **Commission income structure** — First-year commissions on life/health/annuity policies
  are large (50-110% of first-year premium). Trail/renewal commissions are smaller
  (2-10% per year) but build into a significant income stream.
- **Charge-backs** — If a policy lapses within a specified period (typically 1-2 years),
  the agent must repay part or all of the commission. Deduction in year of repayment.
  IRC 1341 (claim of right) may allow favorable treatment for large charge-backs:
  compute tax both ways and use the method that produces lower tax.
- **Trail/renewal income** — Ongoing commissions from book of business. Taxable when
  received. Subject to SE tax. This "passive-like" income can fund retirement plan
  contributions even when the agent is semi-retired.
- **E&O insurance** — Errors and omissions liability coverage. Typically $1,000-$3,000/year.
  Fully deductible.
- **Licensing costs** — State insurance license fees, fingerprinting, background checks.
  CE requirements (24-40 hours per renewal cycle in most states). All deductible.
- **State insurance premium taxes** — Paid by the insurance company, not the agent, but
  can affect commission structures.
- **Client entertainment/meals** — Meals: 50% deductible with documentation. Entertainment
  (golf, sporting events) is NOT deductible post-TCJA, even if business is discussed.

## 8. Recommended Entity Structure

- **Employed advisor/agent** — W-2 employee. Consider LLC for outside consulting or speaking.
- **Independent insurance agent** — LLC with S-Corp election once net commissions exceed
  $60K-$80K. Saves SE tax on trail income. Reasonable salary: comparable to employed
  agent salary for similar production level.
- **Independent RIA** — LLC or S-Corp. Multi-advisor firms: partnership or multi-member LLC.
- **Day trader** — Single-member LLC taxed as S-Corp if making 475(f) election and trading
  consistently. Entity provides clean separation of trading from personal. S-Corp pays
  reasonable salary, which creates W-2 income for retirement plan contributions.
  Some traders use C-Corp for trading entity to access benefits (but double taxation risk).
- **Hedge fund** — Typically LP (limited partnership) or LLC taxed as partnership. GP or
  managing member LLC (S-Corp or C-Corp) earns management fees. Carried interest flows
  through LP structure.
- **Financial planning firm** — LLC taxed as S-Corp for sole practitioners. Partnership
  for multi-advisor firms.

## 9. Retirement Plan Strategy

- **Employed at firm** — 401(k) with employer match. Max $23,500 ($31,000 if 50+).
  Mega backdoor Roth if plan permits.
- **Self-employed advisor/agent** — Solo 401(k) for maximum flexibility. Employee deferral +
  employer contribution. Total up to $70,000 (2025).
- **Defined benefit plan** — Insurance agents and RIA owners with consistent high income
  ($300K+) benefit from cash balance plans. Shelter $280,000+ per year.
- **Day traders** — Solo 401(k) if operating as S-Corp business entity paying reasonable
  salary. The W-2 salary supports retirement plan contributions. Trading gains alone
  (without 475(f) election through entity with SE income) may not qualify as earned
  income for retirement plan contributions. Structure matters enormously:
  S-Corp + 475(f) + reasonable salary = Solo 401(k) contributions up to $70,000.
- **SEP-IRA** — Simple alternative for independent agents. Up to 25% of W-2 (S-Corp) or
  ~20% net SE income.
- **Backdoor Roth** — Standard strategy for high-income financial professionals.
- **HSA** — If on HDHP: $4,300/$8,550. Triple tax advantage.

## 10. Common Audit Triggers

- **Day trader status without qualifying** — IRS requires: (1) substantial, frequent,
  regular, and continuous trading activity, (2) intent to profit from short-term swings,
  (3) trading is the primary income activity. Holding long-term investments on the side
  doesn't disqualify, but must be clearly separated.
- **475(f) election errors** — Must be filed by the due date of the return for the year
  BEFORE the election is effective. Cannot be made retroactively.
- **Wash sale violations** — Buying substantially identical securities within 30 days
  before or after selling at a loss. Disallowed loss added to basis of replacement security.
  Applies across all accounts including spouse's.
- **Business vs. investment expenses** — IRS challenges whether trading activity rises to
  the level of a "trade or business" vs. investment activity. Business expenses are
  Schedule C; investment expenses are nondeductible (post-TCJA).
- **High Schedule C deductions** — Large technology, data, and education expenses relative
  to trading income.
- **Carried interest holding period** — IRC 1061 requires 3-year hold for LTCG treatment.
  IRS scrutinizes compliance.

## 11. State Considerations

- **New York** — Financial capital. High state + city tax. UBT in NYC for sole proprietors/
  partnerships (4%). Many hedge funds relocated to FL/CT/TX post-TCJA.
- **Connecticut** — Hedge fund corridor (Greenwich). State income tax up to 6.99%.
  Pass-through entity tax available.
- **Florida / Texas** — No income tax. Popular relocation for financial professionals.
  Must establish genuine domicile (NY and CT audit aggressively). NY applies the
  "convenience of employer" rule — if your employer is in NY, you owe NY tax unless
  you work remotely out of "necessity," not "convenience."
- **California** — 13.3% top rate. Does not recognize 475(f) election differently from
  federal. High cost for trading businesses.
- **State treatment of capital gains** — Most states tax at ordinary rates. States with
  preferential CG rates: AZ (partial exclusion), WI, SC, others.
- **PTE tax elections** — S-Corp and partnership financial firms should utilize PTE tax
  elections in qualifying states to bypass the personal SALT cap.

## 12. Common Mistakes

1. **Missing 475(f) election deadline** — Must be filed by April 15 of the CURRENT year for
   that year's trades (attached to the PRIOR year's return). One day late and you're stuck
   with capital treatment for the year. Most common and costly mistake for day traders.
2. **Not tracking wash sales across accounts** — Wash sale rules apply across ALL accounts
   including spouse's accounts and IRAs. A loss sale in taxable account repurchased in IRA
   creates a permanently disallowed loss.
3. **Claiming trader status without qualifying** — Making 50 trades/year is investing, not
   trading. IRS requires frequent, substantial, continuous activity. 500+ round-trip trades
   per year, executed nearly daily, is the practical threshold (*Endicott*, *Groetzinger*).
4. **Deducting investment expenses** — Post-TCJA, investment advisory fees, investment
   publications, and other investment expenses are NOT deductible. Only business expenses
   (for qualifying traders or advisors) are deductible on Schedule C.
5. **Insurance agent not using S-Corp** — Agents with $80K+ net commission income pay
   unnecessary SE tax without S-Corp election.
6. **Not segregating long-term investments from trading** — If making 475(f) election,
   long-term holdings in the same account are forced to mark-to-market. Separate accounts
   before election deadline.
7. **Carried interest holding period** — Counting from fund formation rather than when
   underlying assets were acquired. Each asset's holding period matters individually
   under IRC 1061.
8. **Ignoring state residency audit risk** — Financial professionals relocating from NY/CT
   to FL/TX face aggressive audit scrutiny. Must demonstrate genuine domicile change
   (sell/rent NY home, update all ties, spend majority of time in new state).
9. **Not funding retirement plans with trading income** — Day traders structured properly
   (S-Corp with 475(f) election, paying reasonable salary) can contribute $70,000+ to
   Solo 401(k). Many miss this because they don't have the right entity structure.
10. **Insurance agents ignoring IRC 1341 for large chargebacks** — When repaying commissions
    on lapsed policies, the claim of right doctrine allows computing tax both ways and
    using the more favorable method. For large chargebacks ($10K+), this can save thousands.
