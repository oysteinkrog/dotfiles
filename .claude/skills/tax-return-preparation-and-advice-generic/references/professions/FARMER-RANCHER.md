# Farmer / Rancher — Tax Reference Guide (TY 2025)

## 1. Overview

Farmers and ranchers benefit from some of the most generous tax provisions in the IRC,
reflecting agriculture's importance and its unique challenges: weather risk, commodity price
volatility, long production cycles, and high capital investment. Farm income averaging
(Schedule J), special estimated tax rules, conservation deductions, livestock tax treatment,
and accelerated depreciation on equipment make agricultural taxation a specialized field.
Most farm income is reported on Schedule F, though some activities use Schedule C or Schedule E.

## 2. Common Income Types

- **Schedule F income** — Crop sales, livestock sales (raised or purchased for resale),
  dairy/egg/poultry production, custom farming services.
- **Commodity credit loans** — Election to report CCC loans as income in year received
  (IRC 77) or year sold. Timing election can significantly affect tax planning.
- **Crop insurance proceeds** — Taxable income. IRC 451(d) allows deferral to following
  year if crop would normally have been sold in that year.
- **Government program payments** — USDA payments (ARC, PLC, EQIP, CRP, ECP). Generally
  taxable. CRP (Conservation Reserve Program) payments are subject to SE tax for
  taxpayers in the business of farming.
- **Patronage dividends** — From agricultural cooperatives. Taxable (Form 1099-PATR).
  Section 199A(g) provides cooperative-specific QBI deduction.
- **Custom hire income** — Providing farming services (planting, harvesting, spraying) to
  others. Schedule F or Schedule C.
- **Rental of farmland** — Cash rent (Schedule E — not SE tax if not material participation).
  Crop share rent (Schedule F — may be subject to SE tax if material participation).
- **Livestock sale proceeds** — Raised livestock: $0 basis, all gain is ordinary income
  (the costs of raising were deducted as incurred — feed, vet, etc.). Purchased livestock
  held for breeding/dairy/draft: Section 1231 gain if held over required period (12 months
  for most livestock, 24 months for cattle and horses).
- **Timber sales** — Capital gain for timber held as investment. Ordinary income if in
  the trade or business of growing timber (with IRC 631 election options).

## 3. Key Deductions

### Operating Expenses
- **Seed, fertilizer, chemicals** — Fully deductible in year paid (cash method) or incurred.
  Prepaid farm supplies rule: cannot deduct more than 50% of other farm expenses if
  deducting prepaid supplies (unless less than $1M gross receipts).
- **Feed** — Livestock feed. Cash method farmers can deduct when purchased. Prepaid feed
  rules may limit deduction if exceeding 50% threshold.
- **Fuel and oil** — For farm machinery. Also eligible for fuel tax credit (Form 4136)
  for off-highway use (no federal excise tax on farm-use fuel).
- **Hired labor** — Employee wages, contract labor. Must issue W-2/1099.
- **Repairs and maintenance** — Equipment, buildings, fences, irrigation systems.
- **Rent** — Cash rent paid for leased farmland or equipment.
- **Insurance** — Crop insurance premiums, liability, property, livestock mortality.
- **Veterinary and medicine** — Livestock veterinary costs, medications, breeding fees.
- **Utilities** — Farm-related electricity, water, propane/natural gas.
- **Property taxes** — On farmland and farm buildings.

### Depreciation and Capitalized Costs
- **Farm equipment** — Section 179 up to $2,500,000 (2025). Bonus depreciation is generally
  100% for qualified property acquired and placed in service after January 19, 2025; property
  acquired before January 20, 2025 is generally limited to 40%. Tractors, combines, planters,
  sprayers, irrigation equipment.
  MACRS 5-year life for farm machinery and equipment (general depreciation system).
  7-year life for farm buildings used for storage, handling equipment.
- **Farm buildings** — 20-year MACRS (single-purpose agricultural structures: grain bins,
  livestock confinement, greenhouses, fruit/nut drying, poultry houses). General farm
  buildings: 20-year MACRS. Non-agricultural buildings on farm: 39-year (commercial).
- **Fences** — 7-year MACRS property. Section 179 eligible.
- **Land improvements** — Drainage tile, terraces, waterways. Depreciate or elect to deduct
  under IRC 175 (soil and water conservation).
- **Livestock** — Purchased breeding stock: depreciate (3-7 year MACRS depending on species).
  Raised breeding stock: $0 basis, expensed as raised (feed, vet costs deducted as incurred).
- **Orchards and vineyards** — Pre-productive period costs must be capitalized under IRC 263A
  unless farm gross receipts under $30M (small business exception).

### Special Agricultural Deductions

#### Soil and Water Conservation (IRC 175)
- **What qualifies** — Expenses for soil or water conservation, erosion prevention, and
  endangered species recovery on farmland. Includes: terracing, contour farming,
  construction of earthen dams, waterways, outlets, ponds, drainage ditches, leveling,
  grading for water management, planting windbreaks.
- **Annual limit** — Deductible amount cannot exceed 25% of gross farming income for the year.
- **Excess carries forward** — Amounts exceeding 25% carry forward to subsequent years
  (subject to the same 25% limitation each year).
- **Worked example** — Farmer with $400K gross farm income spends $150K on drainage tile
  installation. Maximum IRC 175 deduction: $400K x 25% = $100K. Remaining $50K carries
  to next year.
- **Alternative** — Instead of IRC 175, farmer can capitalize and depreciate as land
  improvement (15-year MACRS). Choose the method that provides better timing benefit.

#### Fertilizer and Lime (IRC 180)
- **Election to expense** — Farmers can elect to deduct fertilizer and lime costs in the
  year of application, even though the benefits extend over multiple years. Without the
  election, costs would be capitalized and amortized over the benefit period.
- **Practical effect** — Most farmers simply deduct fertilizer/lime as an operating expense
  in the year paid. IRC 180 provides the statutory authority for this treatment.

## 4. Farm Income Averaging — Schedule J (IRC 1301)

This is one of the most powerful and underused provisions in the entire IRC:

### How It Works
- **Concept** — Elect to allocate current-year "elected farm income" to the 3 prior base
  years. The excess farm income fills up unused lower tax brackets in those prior years.
  The farmer pays the LOWER of: (1) tax computed normally, or (2) tax computed by
  spreading farm income over the base years.
- **Available ONLY to individuals** — Not C-Corps, estates, or trusts. S-Corp/partnership
  farm income that flows to individual partners/shareholders qualifies.

### Base Years
- The 3 tax years immediately preceding the election year. For TY 2025, base years are
  2022, 2023, and 2024.
- Cannot use the same base year twice for multiple election years. Each base year is
  "used up" once farm income is allocated to it.

### What Qualifies as Farm Income
- Schedule F income (net farm profit)
- Gains from sale of farm assets (Section 1231 farm property)
- Farm partnership/S-Corp income
- **NOT qualifying**: W-2 wages from farm employer, rental income without material
  participation, non-farm income

### Worked Example — The Power of Income Averaging
- Farmer's TY 2025 taxable income: $350,000 (huge crop year after drought recovery).
- Base year taxable incomes: 2022: $40,000; 2023: $30,000; 2024: $45,000.
- Without averaging: Tax on $350K (MFJ) = approximately $63,400.
- With averaging: Allocate excess farm income to base years, filling their unused brackets.
  The prior years had significant unused capacity in the 10%, 12%, and 22% brackets.
  Averaged tax: approximately $48,000. **Savings: ~$15,400.**
- In extreme cases (very high current year, very low base years), savings can exceed $50,000.

## 5. Special Estimated Tax Rule (IRC 6654(i))

Farmers and fishermen enjoy the most lenient estimated tax rules in the IRC:

- **Qualification** — Must receive at least 2/3 of gross income from farming in the current
  year OR the prior year.
- **Rule** — Only ONE estimated tax payment required, due January 15 of the following year
  (not four quarterly payments). The payment must equal 66.67% of the current year's tax
  liability OR 100% of the prior year's tax liability.
- **Even better** — If the farmer files the return AND pays all tax due by March 1
  (not March 15, not April 15 — March 1), NO estimated payment is required at all.
  No underpayment penalty applies.
- **Why this matters** — Farmers with unpredictable income do not need to estimate quarterly.
  Wait until the crop is sold and income is known, then file by March 1 and pay in full.
- **Critical deadline** — March 1 is absolute. Filing on March 2 triggers underpayment
  penalty on the full tax liability for the entire year.

## 6. Livestock Rules — Detailed Treatment

### Raised Livestock
- **Basis** — $0. All costs of raising (feed, veterinary, medicine, pasture, labor) were
  deducted as incurred on Schedule F. The animal has zero basis.
- **Sale** — All proceeds are income. If held for sale to customers (feeder cattle, market
  hogs), gain is ordinary income. If held for breeding, dairy, or draft purposes AND held
  over the required period, gain is Section 1231 (potentially long-term capital gain).

### Purchased Breeding Livestock
- **Basis** — Purchase price. Depreciable asset.
- **MACRS depreciation lives**: 3 years (hogs), 5 years (cattle, sheep, goats), 7 years
  (horses other than racehorses), 3 years (racehorses placed in service after 12/31/2008).
- **Section 1231 treatment** — Gain on sale of livestock held for breeding, dairy, or draft
  purposes is Section 1231 gain if held over: 12 months (hogs, sheep, goats) or 24 months
  (cattle, horses). Section 1231 gains are taxed at long-term capital gain rates; Section
  1231 losses are ordinary losses.
- **Depreciation recapture** — Under IRC 1245, gain attributable to depreciation claimed is
  recaptured as ordinary income. Only the gain exceeding total depreciation claimed gets
  Section 1231 treatment.

### Involuntary Conversion (IRC 1033)
- **Weather/disease/contamination** — Livestock sold or exchanged due to drought, flood,
  disease, or other involuntary circumstance qualifies for gain deferral.
- **Standard rule** — Reinvest in similar property within 2 years.
- **Extended rule for weather-related sales** — If livestock sold due to drought, flood, or
  other weather condition in an area designated by the federal government, the replacement
  period is extended to 4 years after the close of the first year in which any gain is
  realized. IRS may further extend in extreme cases.
- **Worked example** — Rancher forced to sell 200 head of breeding cattle due to severe
  drought (county declared disaster area). Sale proceeds: $400,000. Basis: $0 (raised
  livestock). Without IRC 1033, the entire $400K is taxable income in the sale year.
  With IRC 1033, the rancher has 4 years to reinvest in replacement breeding cattle,
  deferring the entire gain.

## 7. CCC Loans (IRC 77)

- **Commodity Credit Corporation loans** — USDA loans using stored crops as collateral.
- **Default treatment** — Loan proceeds are NOT income when received. Income is recognized
  when the crop is sold or the loan is settled.
- **IRC 77 election** — Farmer can elect to treat CCC loan proceeds as income in the year
  received. This increases basis in the crop to the loan amount. When the crop is later
  sold, only the gain above the loan amount is additional income.
- **Strategy** — Use IRC 77 election to shift income to a low-income year. If the farmer
  receives a CCC loan in a low-income year, electing to report as income fills lower
  brackets. When the crop sells in a higher-income year, less income is recognized.
- **Consistency** — Once elected, the IRC 77 election applies to all CCC loans for that
  year. Changing methods in future years requires IRS approval.

## 8. Crop Insurance Proceeds (IRC 451(d))

- **Deferral election** — If a farmer receives crop insurance proceeds in one year but would
  normally have sold the destroyed crop in the following year, the farmer can elect to defer
  the insurance proceeds to the next year.
- **Requirements** — (1) Must use the cash method of accounting, (2) must be able to show
  that the crop would have been sold in the following year under normal practice.
- **Effect** — Avoids bunching income: insurance proceeds (for the destroyed crop) plus
  the next year's normal crop income would otherwise create a double-income year.
- **Election** — Made on the return for the year the insurance proceeds are received.
  Attach a statement identifying the crops, the proceeds, and the expected year of sale.

## 9. Fuel Tax Credit (IRC 34 / Form 4136)

- **What it is** — Refund of federal excise tax on gasoline and diesel used for farming
  purposes (off-highway use). Farm tractors, combines, irrigation pumps, grain dryers,
  and other equipment that operate off-road.
- **Rates (2025)** — $0.183/gallon for gasoline; $0.243/gallon for diesel.
- **How to claim** — File Form 4136 with the annual return. Credit is refundable (reduces
  tax dollar-for-dollar and generates a refund if no tax is owed).
- **Worked example** — Farmer uses 15,000 gallons of diesel for tractors and equipment.
  Credit: 15,000 x $0.243 = $3,645 refund.
- **Recordkeeping** — Track fuel purchases by use (farm vs. highway). Fuel tank records,
  equipment logs, and purchase receipts substantiate the claim.

## 10. Recommended Entity Structure

- **Family farm** — Most remain sole proprietorships (Schedule F) or husband-wife partnerships.
  Simple, effective, well-understood by USDA and lenders.
- **Growing operation** — LLC for liability protection. Taxed as sole prop (single member)
  or partnership (family members).
- **S-Corporation** — Less common in farming. Can save SE tax but complicates USDA program
  eligibility (payment limitations calculated per "person"). Reasonable salary requirement
  applies. Consider if net farm income consistently exceeds $100K.
- **C-Corporation** — Historically used to retain earnings. 21% flat rate can be attractive
  for reinvesting profits. But loses access to farm income averaging and may complicate
  USDA payment limits.
- **Multiple entities** — Operating LLC (farming operations) + land-holding LLC (owns farmland).
  Protects land from operating business liabilities.
- **Family Limited Partnership (FLP)** — Estate planning vehicle for transferring farm
  interests to next generation at discounted values.

## 11. Farm NOL and Carryback (IRC 172(b)(1)(B))

- **Two-year carryback election** — Farm net operating losses can be carried BACK 2 years
  (unlike general NOLs which have no carryback post-TCJA). This allows the farmer to
  amend the return for 2 years prior and receive a refund.
- **Why it matters** — Farming is inherently cyclical. A drought year producing a large
  NOL can be carried back to a profitable year, generating an immediate tax refund when
  cash is most needed.
- **Election to waive** — Farmer can elect to waive the 2-year carryback and carry forward
  only (indefinitely, subject to 80% taxable income limitation). Useful if the prior 2
  years were also low-income.
- **Interaction with income averaging** — Farm income averaging (Schedule J) and farm NOL
  carryback are separate provisions. They can interact in complex ways. Professional
  computation is recommended.

## 12. Estate Planning for Family Farms

### IRC 2032A — Special Use Valuation
- **What it does** — Allows qualifying farm (or closely held business) real property to be
  valued at its current agricultural use value rather than its "highest and best use"
  (fair market value). This can reduce the taxable estate by up to $1,310,000 (2025).
- **Example** — Family farm: 500 acres valued at $15,000/acre FMV ($7.5M) but only
  $5,000/acre in agricultural use ($2.5M). Special use valuation reduces estate value
  by $5M (capped at $1.31M reduction).
- **Requirements** — (1) Decedent was US citizen/resident, (2) real property is used for
  farming, (3) property passes to qualified heir, (4) farm value is 50%+ of adjusted
  gross estate, (5) real property value is 25%+ of adjusted gross estate, (6) material
  participation by decedent or family for 5 of 8 years before death.
- **Recapture** — If the heir disposes of the property or ceases to use it for farming
  within 10 years of death, the estate tax savings are recaptured.

### IRC 6166 — Installment Payment of Estate Tax
- **What it does** — Allows the estate to defer and pay estate tax attributable to the
  farm in installments over up to 14 years (4-year deferral of principal + 10-year
  installment payments). Interest-only payments during the deferral period.
- **Interest rate** — 2% on the first ~$1.8M of deferred tax (2025); market rate on
  the remainder.
- **Requirement** — Farm must be 35%+ of adjusted gross estate.

### Agricultural Program Payments and Estate Impact
- **USDA payment limitations** — Structured by "person." Entities count as separate
  persons if they have separate management and financial risk. Family farm succession
  planning must consider USDA payment limit implications.

## 13. Common Audit Triggers

- **Farm losses** — Schedule F losses claimed year after year. IRS applies hobby loss rules
  (IRC 183). Farming has special presumption: profitable in 3 of 5 years (vs. standard
  3 of 5). IRS can extend review period to 7 years for farming activities.
- **Large Section 179 deductions** — Buying expensive equipment in profitable years.
  Must actually place in service during tax year. Substantiation required.
- **CRP payments and SE tax** — CRP payments are subject to SE tax if recipient is in the
  business of farming. Some landowners incorrectly exclude from SE.
- **Prepaid expenses** — Large prepaid feed or fertilizer deductions. Must meet 50% test
  or small farmer exception.
- **Personal use of farm assets** — Farm truck, equipment, horses used personally. Must
  allocate between farm and personal use.
- **Conservation easement donations** — IRS heavily scrutinizes syndicated conservation
  easements. Legitimate easements on working farms are generally acceptable with qualified
  appraisal. Syndicated partnerships are high-audit-risk.

## 14. Common Mistakes

1. **Not using farm income averaging** — In a high-income year (big crop, land sale),
   Schedule J can save $10,000-$50,000+ in federal tax. Many farmers and preparers overlook it.
2. **Missing the March 1 filing deadline** — Farmers who don't make January 15 estimated
   payment must file and pay by March 1 to avoid underpayment penalty. Missing by even
   one day triggers the penalty.
3. **Not electing IRC 451(d) for crop insurance** — Crop insurance proceeds received in a
   disaster year can be deferred to the following year. Must elect.
4. **Improper treatment of CCC loans** — Must consistently elect whether to treat CCC loans
   as income in year received or year crop is sold. Changing methods requires IRS approval.
5. **Ignoring fuel tax credit** — Off-highway fuel use on the farm qualifies for excise tax
   refund. On 10,000 gallons of diesel, credit exceeds $2,400.
6. **Not maintaining separate farm records** — Commingling farm and personal expenses
   invites audit and weakens deduction claims.
7. **Failing to depreciate breeding livestock** — Purchased breeding stock should be
   depreciated over MACRS life. Raised stock has $0 basis but raising costs are deducted currently.
8. **Overlooking involuntary conversion deferral** — Livestock sold due to drought or disease
   qualifies for gain deferral under IRC 1033. Must reinvest within time limits.
9. **Estate planning neglect** — Farms are asset-heavy, cash-poor. Without proper succession
   planning (2032A, installment payment under 6166, FLP, buy-sell agreements), heirs may
   have to sell the farm to pay estate taxes.
10. **Not using 2-year NOL carryback** — Farm NOLs can be carried back 2 years for an
    immediate refund. Many farmers carry forward only, missing the cash flow benefit.
11. **Conservation expense over-deduction** — IRC 175 limits soil/water conservation
    deductions to 25% of gross farm income. Exceeding this without carrying forward the
    excess is an error.
12. **Ignoring IRC 77 CCC loan election** — Strategic use of the CCC loan election can
    shift income between years for significant tax savings. Many farmers accept default
    treatment without considering the election.
