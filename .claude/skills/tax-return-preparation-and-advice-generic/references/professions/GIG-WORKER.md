# Gig Worker -- Tax Reference Guide (TY 2025)

## 1. Overview

Gig workers drive for Uber/Lyft, deliver for DoorDash/Instacart/Amazon Flex, rent properties on
Airbnb, perform tasks on TaskRabbit, or sell on Etsy/eBay. All are self-employed independent
contractors. The gig economy creates unique tax challenges: workers often don't realize they're
responsible for self-employment tax, quarterly payments, and meticulous expense tracking. Many
gig workers leave thousands in deductions unclaimed. Proper tax planning can reduce effective
tax rates from 30%+ to under 15%.

## 2. Common Income Types

### Platform-Specific 1099 Rules

- **1099-K** -- Payment card/third-party network transactions. For TY 2025, IRS guidance says
  the federal threshold reverted to **more than $20,000 and more than 200 transactions**.
  All major platforms issue 1099-K:
  - **Uber/Lyft**: 1099-K for ride and delivery earnings. Reports GROSS fares (before
    Uber/Lyft commission). You deduct the commission as a business expense.
  - **DoorDash**: 1099-K or 1099-NEC for delivery earnings. Check whether it includes base pay,
    tips, and bonuses or separates them.
  - **Airbnb**: 1099-K for rental income. Reports GROSS booking amount (before Airbnb service
    fee and cleaning fees you charge).
  - **Etsy**: 1099-K for sales. Reports GROSS sales (before Etsy fees, shipping labels, ads).
  - **Fiverr/Upwork**: 1099-K or 1099-NEC. Reports GROSS earnings (before platform fees).

- **1099-NEC** -- Non-employee compensation. Some platforms use this instead of or in
  addition to 1099-K. Also used for referral bonuses, incentive payments.

- **1099-MISC** -- Occasionally used for incentive/bonus payments, promotional income.

- **Cash tips** -- Must be self-reported even without 1099. Common for delivery drivers.
  All tips are taxable regardless of whether reported on a 1099.

- **Incentive/bonus payments** -- Sign-up bonuses, completion bonuses, surge/peak pay.
  All taxable income.

**Critical**: not receiving a 1099 does NOT mean income is tax-free. ALL income is taxable
regardless of whether a 1099 is issued. If you earned $400 on a platform that did not issue
a 1099, you must still report it.

### Multi-Platform Income Aggregation
- Many workers use 2-5+ platforms simultaneously
- Option 1: one Schedule C for all similar gig activity (recommended for drivers doing
  Uber + Lyft + DoorDash)
- Option 2: separate Schedule C per distinct activity (e.g., one for rideshare/delivery,
  one for Etsy shop, one for Airbnb)
- Must reconcile total income across ALL platforms with what is reported on 1099s

## 3. Key Deductions

### Vehicle / Mileage -- The Big Deduction (50-70% of all deductions for drivers)

#### Standard Mileage Rate
- **$0.70/mile (2025)**. This rate includes gas, depreciation, insurance, maintenance, tires,
  oil changes, and registration.
- Cannot use if you previously used actual expense method for the same vehicle, or if the
  vehicle was depreciated using MACRS/Section 179.
- **Choice is locked in the first year per vehicle**: if you use standard mileage in Year 1,
  you can switch to actual later. If you use actual in Year 1, you are locked into actual
  for that vehicle permanently.

#### Actual Expense Method
- Track ALL vehicle costs: gas, insurance, repairs, tires, oil changes, car washes,
  registration, depreciation (or lease payments), loan interest, parking, tolls.
- Multiply total annual cost by business-use percentage.
- **Better for**: expensive vehicles (luxury cars where depreciation is significant), older
  vehicles with high gas costs relative to value, vehicles with high insurance costs.
- **Standard mileage usually better for**: economy cars, newer cars (where standard rate
  captures more than actual depreciation), gig workers who do not want the recordkeeping burden.

#### Mileage Tracking -- IRS Requirements

The IRS requires a **contemporaneous log** of business mileage. This is non-negotiable.
Without it, the entire vehicle deduction can be disallowed in an audit.

**Required elements for each trip**:
1. **Date** of the trip
2. **Destination** (or route)
3. **Business purpose** (e.g., "DoorDash deliveries", "Uber rideshare")
4. **Miles driven** (odometer start/end or GPS-tracked)

**Tracking apps** (strongly recommended -- manual logs are error-prone):
- **Everlance**: auto-tracking, trip classification, expense tracking
- **Stride**: free, designed for gig workers, tracks mileage and expenses
- **MileIQ**: auto-tracking, swipe to classify trips
- **Gridwise**: designed for rideshare/delivery, integrates with platform earnings
- **Platform tracking**: Uber and Lyft provide annual mileage summaries, but these only
  capture miles during active rides -- NOT miles between rides or driving to pickup locations

**IRS rejects** end-of-year estimates, reconstructed logs, and round-number approximations.
Start tracking from day one.

#### What Counts as Business Miles
- Miles from first pickup/delivery to last dropoff: BUSINESS
- Miles between gigs/deliveries (waiting for next ping): BUSINESS
- Miles driving from home to first pickup: COMMUTING (not deductible) UNLESS you have a
  qualifying home office
- Miles driving home after last delivery: COMMUTING (not deductible) UNLESS home office
- **Home office game-changer**: if you have a dedicated space at home used for scheduling,
  tracking, and admin, it may qualify as a home office. Then ALL miles from home to first
  gig and from last gig to home become business miles. This can add 3,000-5,000+ miles/year.

**Typical deduction**: driver doing 20,000 business miles/year = 20,000 x $0.70 = **$14,000 deduction**.

### Phone and Data Plan
- **Cell phone** -- Business-use percentage of monthly bill. Typically 50-75% for active
  gig workers who use the phone extensively for platform apps, GPS navigation, and
  communication. If you have a second phone used exclusively for gig work, 100% deductible.
- **Phone mount, charger, cables** -- 100% deductible if used for gig work. ~$30-$100.
- **Data plan** -- Portion used for gig apps (GPS, platform apps). Same percentage as phone.
- **Dash cam** -- Deductible for rideshare drivers (safety equipment). Typically $50-$200.
- **Reasonable allocation**: if your total phone/data bill is $100/month and you estimate
  60% business use, deduct $720/year ($100 x 12 x 60%).

### Supplies and Equipment
- **Hot bags / insulated bags** -- Required for food delivery. Fully deductible. $20-$50.
- **Bags, boxes, packaging** -- For Etsy/eBay sellers. Part of COGS or supplies.
- **Cleaning supplies** -- For vehicle cleaning (rideshare), property cleaning (Airbnb).
- **PPE** -- Masks, gloves, sanitizer if required for work.
- **Uniforms/branded gear** -- If required by platform and not suitable for everyday wear.
- **Safety equipment** -- Reflective vests, flashlights, first aid kits for delivery drivers.
  All deductible as business expenses.

### Platform Fees -- Critical Reconciliation

**Key question**: does the 1099-K report GROSS income (before fees) or NET income (after fees)?

- **Uber/Lyft**: 1099-K reports GROSS fares. You must deduct Uber/Lyft's service fee/commission
  (typically 25-30%) as a business expense on Schedule C.
  - Example: 1099-K shows $50,000. Uber took $15,000 in commissions. Report $50,000 income,
    deduct $15,000 platform fees. Net income = $35,000.
- **DoorDash**: may report base pay + tips. Commission structure is different (flat fee per delivery).
- **Etsy**: 1099-K typically reports GROSS. Deduct: listing fees ($0.20/item), transaction fees
  (6.5%), payment processing fees (3% + $0.25), Etsy Ads, shipping labels purchased through Etsy.
- **Airbnb**: 1099-K reports GROSS booking amount. Deduct: Airbnb host service fee (3-5%),
  cleaning expenses, supplies, maintenance, insurance, mortgage interest, property taxes,
  utilities, depreciation.

**Always reconcile**: download the annual tax summary from each platform and compare to 1099.
Discrepancies trigger IRS notices.

### Insurance
- **Rideshare insurance gap** -- Personal auto insurance may not cover gig driving. Rideshare
  endorsement or commercial policy: the additional premium over personal-only coverage is
  deductible (allocated by business-use percentage).
- **Health insurance** -- See Section 7 below.

## 4. Recommended Entity Structure

- **Most gig workers** -- Sole proprietor (Schedule C). Simple, low cost. Appropriate for
  the vast majority of gig workers earning under $50K net.
- **High-earning full-time gig workers ($60K+ net)** -- Consider LLC with S-Corp election.
  However, "reasonable salary" for a delivery driver may consume most income, limiting
  SE tax savings. S-Corp less beneficial for gig workers than for consultants because
  reasonable salary for the actual work performed is relatively high as a % of income.
- **Multiple platform types** -- May file one Schedule C for all driving/delivery or separate
  Schedule C for distinct businesses (e.g., rideshare vs. Etsy shop).

## 5. Retirement Plan Strategy

### Options for Gig Workers

- **Traditional or Roth IRA** -- $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+). Roth is often better for gig workers
  with lower/moderate income (lower tax bracket now, potentially higher later).

- **SEP-IRA** -- If net income is higher. Contribute up to ~20% of net SE income. Can be
  established and funded up to tax filing deadline (including extensions). Simpler than
  Solo 401(k). Good choice if gig work is a side job.

- **Solo 401(k)** -- For full-time gig workers with higher income and no employees (other
  than spouse). Employee + employer contributions can exceed SEP-IRA limits at lower income
  levels. Adoption and contribution timing depend on the worker's entity type and whether the
  contribution is an employee elective deferral or employer contribution. Verify the current IRS
  rules before assuming a blanket December 31 deadline.
  - **Best if gig work is your ONLY income source**: allows both employee deferrals ($23,500)
    and employer contributions (~20% of net SE income).
  - Example: $60,000 net SE income. Solo 401(k) = $23,500 (employee) + ~$11,130 (employer
    20% of $55,655) = $34,630 total contribution. SEP-IRA = ~$11,130 only.

- **Saver's Credit (Form 8880)** -- AGI limits: $39,500 single / $79,000 MFJ (2025).
  Credit of 10-50% of retirement contributions up to $2,000 ($4,000 MFJ). Many gig
  workers qualify for this valuable credit.

## 6. Quarterly Estimated Tax Payments

### The Requirement
If you expect to owe $1,000+ in federal tax at filing time (after withholding and credits),
you must make quarterly estimated payments or face an underpayment penalty.

### Due Dates
| Quarter | Income Period | Due Date |
|---------|---------------|----------|
| Q1 | Jan 1 - Mar 31 | April 15 |
| Q2 | Apr 1 - May 31 | June 15 |
| Q3 | Jun 1 - Aug 31 | September 15 |
| Q4 | Sep 1 - Dec 31 | January 15 (following year) |

### Safe Harbors (avoid penalty by meeting either):
1. **Pay 100% of prior year's total tax** (110% if prior year AGI > $150,000)
2. **Pay 90% of current year's tax**

For most gig workers, the prior-year safe harbor is simpler: divide last year's total tax by 4
and pay that amount each quarter.

### Practical Approach
- Set aside **25-30% of every payment received** in a separate savings account
- Make quarterly payments from that account
- Use IRS Direct Pay (irs.gov/directpay) or EFTPS for easy payment
- State estimated payments are also required in most income-tax states

## 7. Health Insurance for Gig Workers

### Self-Employed Health Insurance Deduction (IRC Section 162(l))
- Deduct 100% of health insurance premiums (including dental and vision) as an above-the-line
  deduction if:
  - You are self-employed with net SE income
  - You are NOT eligible for employer-sponsored coverage (through a spouse's employer, etc.)
  - Deduction cannot exceed net SE income from the business

### Marketplace Premium Tax Credit (PTC)
- If you purchase insurance through healthcare.gov, you may qualify for PTC based on
  household income (100-400% of FPL, or no upper limit for 2025 under ARP extension)
- **Cannot take both**: you cannot deduct premiums under Section 162(l) AND claim PTC on
  the same premiums. Choose the more beneficial option.
- For lower-income gig workers, PTC is usually more valuable than the SE health deduction

### Practical Tip
- Estimate annual income carefully for Marketplace application
- If income is volatile, update your estimate mid-year to avoid large PTC repayment at filing

## 8. Common Audit Triggers

- **No mileage log** -- Single biggest audit risk for gig workers. IRS can disallow the
  entire vehicle deduction without contemporaneous documentation.
- **Deductions exceed income** -- Showing net loss while working full-time as a gig worker
  is a red flag. Must demonstrate profit motive.
- **100% business use of vehicle** -- Almost never credible for a personal vehicle. Must
  have a separate personal vehicle or document that another household member's vehicle
  is used for all personal driving.
- **Round expense numbers** -- $5,000 for gas, $2,000 for phone. Use exact figures from records.
- **Not reporting cash tips** -- IRS knows tip income exists. Under-reporting is audit bait.
- **Mismatched 1099-K amounts** -- If reported income does not match 1099-K, automatic
  IRS notice. Reconcile platform statements with tax return.

## 9. Profession-Specific Rules

- **1099-K threshold** -- Even though the federal TY 2025 threshold reverted to more than
  $20,000 and more than 200 transactions, all income is taxable regardless of whether a 1099-K
  is issued. Do not confuse reporting thresholds with taxability.

- **SE tax** -- 15.3% (12.4% SS + 2.9% Medicare) on 92.35% of net SE income. SS wage base
  $176,100 (2025). Gig workers often owe more in SE tax than income tax.
  - Worked example: $40,000 net SE income.
  - SE tax base: $40,000 x 92.35% = $36,940
  - SE tax: $36,940 x 15.3% = $5,652
  - Deductible half of SE tax: $2,826 (reduces AGI)
  - Income tax (22% bracket, after standard deduction): ~$2,640
  - **Total federal tax: ~$8,292 on $40,000** = effective rate of ~20.7%

- **Section 199A (QBI)** -- Gig work is NOT an SSTB (specified service trade or business).
  The 20% QBI deduction is available regardless of income level (above threshold, W-2/property
  limitations apply but most gig workers are below threshold). This can reduce effective rate
  by 3-4%.
  - Worked example: $40,000 net SE income. QBI deduction = 20% x ($40,000 - $2,826 half SE) =
    $7,435 deduction. At 22% bracket: $1,636 in tax savings.

- **Hobby loss (IRC 183)** -- Part-time gig workers with consistent losses face hobby loss
  risk. If gig work is primarily for supplemental income and shows occasional profit, it
  likely qualifies as a business.

- **Worker classification** -- Some states (California AB5) have stricter tests for
  independent contractor status. If reclassified as employee, platform owes employment taxes
  and worker may not need to pay SE tax. Federal classification still uses common-law test.

- **Airbnb / short-term rental** -- If renting property for less than 15 days/year, income
  is completely tax-free (IRC 280A(g)). Over 14 days: report on Schedule C (if substantial
  services provided) or Schedule E.

## 10. State Considerations

- **State estimated tax** -- Gig workers must make state quarterly payments in addition to
  federal. Penalties vary by state.
- **State income tax** -- Gig income taxed in state of residence. Multi-state driving
  (e.g., delivering in a neighboring state) generally taxed where driver resides, but some
  states may assert nexus if you regularly perform services there.
- **Sales tax on services** -- Some states require sales tax collection on delivery fees
  or rideshare fares. Platform typically handles this.
- **City/local taxes** -- Some cities impose additional taxes on gig work. NYC unincorporated
  business tax (4%) applies to sole proprietors with $100K+ gross.
- **California AB5** -- Strict ABC test for contractor classification. Ongoing litigation
  and exemptions for app-based drivers (Prop 22). Complex state-specific rules.
- **State-specific driver requirements** -- Some states require additional licensing or
  permits for rideshare. Costs are deductible.

## 11. Common Mistakes

1. **Not tracking mileage from day one** -- Reconstructed logs at year-end are weak and
   often inaccurate. The IRS has disallowed hundreds of thousands of dollars in deductions
   for lack of contemporaneous records. Start tracking immediately with an app.

2. **Missing quarterly estimated payments** -- Many gig workers discover they owe $3,000-$8,000+
   at filing time, plus underpayment penalty (currently ~8% annualized). Set aside 25-30%
   of net income each week. Use the prior-year safe harbor (100%/110% of last year's tax).

3. **Deducting total vehicle costs instead of business portion** -- Must allocate between
   business and personal use. Only business miles count. A reasonable business-use percentage
   for a full-time driver is 60-80%; claiming 100% invites audit.

4. **Not claiming all legitimate deductions** -- Hot bags ($25), phone mount ($15), car washes
   ($200/year), parking for pickups ($100/year) -- small items add up to $500+ in savings.

5. **Confusing gross and net income on 1099-K** -- If Uber reports $50,000 gross on 1099-K
   but you only received $35,000 after commissions, you must report $50,000 income and
   deduct $15,000 in platform fees. Reporting only $35,000 triggers an IRS underreporter
   notice for the $15,000 discrepancy.

6. **Ignoring self-employment tax** -- Many gig workers only plan for income tax and are
   shocked by the SE tax bill. Total federal burden = income tax + SE tax. Budget for both.

7. **Not claiming home office** -- If you use a dedicated space at home for admin
   (scheduling, tracking, accounting), it may qualify. And it makes first/last miles deductible,
   which can be worth $1,000-$3,000+ in additional mileage deductions.

8. **Filing incorrectly** -- Gig workers are independent contractors. Report on Schedule C.
   Do not report gig income as "other income" on line 8 (loses deductions and still owes SE tax).

9. **Skipping the QBI deduction** -- The 20% deduction under Section 199A applies to most
   gig workers. Failing to claim it means overpaying by 3-4% of net income. Tax software
   usually catches this, but manual filers and some preparers miss it.

10. **Locking in the wrong vehicle method** -- Using actual expenses in Year 1 permanently
    locks you out of standard mileage for that vehicle. For most gig workers, standard
    mileage is better. Make the right choice in Year 1.

11. **Not coordinating with W-2 withholding** -- If you have a day job with W-2 income,
    increase your W-4 withholding to cover gig tax instead of making separate estimated
    payments. This avoids the quarterly payment burden and provides more consistent coverage.

12. **Not claiming the self-employed health insurance deduction** -- If you buy your own
    health insurance and are not eligible for a spouse's employer plan, deduct 100% of
    premiums above the line. This is not a Schedule C deduction -- it goes on Schedule 1.
