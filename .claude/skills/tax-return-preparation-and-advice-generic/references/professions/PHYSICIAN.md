# Physician — Tax Reference Guide (TY 2025)

## 1. Overview

Physicians face a unique tax landscape shaped by high W-2 income (often $250K-$700K+),
significant student loan debt ($200K-$400K median for medical school), delayed earning years
due to residency/fellowship, practice ownership decisions, and complex retirement planning.
Whether employed by a hospital system or running a private practice, physicians need aggressive
tax optimization to overcome the compressed wealth-building timeline caused by late career starts.

## 2. Common Income Types

- **W-2 salary** — Hospital-employed physicians, academic medicine, VA/government positions.
  Often includes productivity bonuses (wRVU-based).
- **K-1 partnership income** — Group practice partners receive distributive share on Schedule K-1.
  Subject to self-employment tax unless limited partner exception applies.
- **1099-NEC** — Locum tenens work, expert witness testimony, medical directorships,
  consulting for pharma/device companies, telemedicine side gigs.
- **1099-MISC** — Royalties from textbook authorship, patent licensing.
- **Disability insurance benefits** — If premiums paid with after-tax dollars, benefits are
  tax-free. Critical planning point for physicians.
- **Loan forgiveness** — PSLF forgiveness is tax-free. IDR forgiveness (after 20-25 years)
  is currently tax-free through 2025; scheduled to become taxable income in 2026.

## 3. Locum Tenens Taxation

### 1099 vs. W-2 Classification
- **W-2 locum**: staffing agency is the employer; withholds payroll taxes; physician is an
  employee of the agency. Limited deductions (TCJA eliminates unreimbursed employee expenses).
- **1099 locum**: physician is an independent contractor; receives 1099-NEC from each agency
  or facility. Reports on Schedule C. Full deduction for business expenses.
- **Critical distinction**: the IRS applies the common-law test (behavioral control, financial
  control, relationship type). Most locum physicians working through agencies with set schedules
  and facility-provided equipment may technically be employees, but the industry norm is 1099.
  Ensure the arrangement genuinely reflects independent contractor status.

### Tax Home Doctrine (Rev. Rul. 73-529, IRC §162(a))
- A physician's **tax home** is the regular place of business, NOT where the family resides.
- **Itinerant locum**: if the physician has no regular place of business and no fixed abode,
  the tax home is wherever they currently work — NO travel deductions are available.
- **Established tax home**: if the physician maintains a primary practice location (even part-time)
  or a regular residence with significant living expenses, they can deduct travel/lodging expenses
  when working at temporary assignments (expected to last < 1 year).
- **Strategy**: maintain a bona fide home base (own or rent a residence, pay utilities, keep
  professional ties) to establish a tax home and preserve travel deductions.

### Per Diem Rules for Traveling Physicians
- Per diem rates (GSA rates by locality) can be used instead of tracking actual meal/lodging costs.
- IRS high-low substantiation method (2025): $319/day high-cost, $225/day low-cost areas
  ($85/$75 meal-only portion respectively).
- Must still substantiate: dates, locations, business purpose. Per diem is a substantiation
  simplification, not an exemption from documentation.
- Per diem CANNOT be used for lodging if the physician has a financial interest in the property.

## 4. Key Deductions

### Employed Physicians
- **SALT deduction** — Capped at $10,000. Significant limitation for high-income physicians.
- **Charitable contributions** — Up to 60% of AGI for cash, 30% for appreciated property.
  Donor-advised funds (DAFs) allow bunching strategy: contribute several years' worth in one
  year, itemize that year, take standard deduction other years.
- **Student loan interest** — Deduction phases out completely at $90,000 MAGI ($185,000 MFJ).
  Most physicians exceed this; deduction is unavailable.

### Self-Employed / Practice Owners (Schedule C or K-1)
- **CME (Continuing Medical Education)** — Registration, travel, lodging for required CE credits.
  For Schedule C filers: fully deductible as business expense. For W-2 physicians: NOT deductible
  under TCJA (suspended unreimbursed employee expenses 2018-2025) unless employer reimburses
  through an accountable plan.
- **Medical malpractice insurance** — Fully deductible as business expense. Tail coverage
  deductible when paid. For employed physicians: if employer pays, not taxable to physician.
  If physician pays own malpractice (common in independent contractor arrangements): Schedule C
  deduction. Malpractice premiums can range $10K-$200K+ depending on specialty and state.
- **Medical equipment** — Section 179 or bonus depreciation for ultrasound machines, EHR systems,
  office equipment. 5-year or 7-year MACRS for most medical equipment.
- **Licensing and board certification** — State medical license fees, DEA registration,
  board examination fees, hospital credentialing costs.
- **Professional memberships** — AMA, specialty society dues (e.g., ACS, AAP, ACOG).
- **Practice overhead** — Office rent, staff wages, medical supplies, EHR subscriptions,
  billing services, practice management software.
- **Health insurance** — Self-employed health insurance deduction (above-the-line). Includes
  premiums for physician, spouse, and dependents.

## 5. Practice Acquisition and Sale

### Acquiring a Practice — Goodwill Amortization (IRC §197)
- When purchasing an existing medical practice, the purchase price is allocated among tangible
  assets (equipment, furniture) and intangible assets (patient records, non-compete covenants,
  assembled workforce, practice goodwill).
- **§197 intangibles**: amortized ratably over 15 years (180 months). Includes goodwill,
  going-concern value, patient lists/records, non-compete agreements, workforce-in-place.
- **Personal goodwill** vs. **practice goodwill**: personal goodwill (physician's reputation,
  patient relationships) may be separable from the practice entity. This matters enormously
  in both acquisition structuring and sale (see below).
- **Equipment**: tangible assets depreciated under MACRS (5-year or 7-year) with Section 179
  and bonus depreciation available. Allocate as much as supportable to shorter-lived assets.

### Selling a Practice — Asset vs. Stock Sale
- **Asset sale** (most common for partnerships/LLCs): buyer gets stepped-up basis in all assets.
  Seller recognizes ordinary income on depreciation recapture (§1245), capital gain on goodwill
  and other §197 intangibles, ordinary income on inventory and accounts receivable.
- **Stock sale** (for S-Corps/C-Corps): buyer purchases ownership interest. No asset step-up
  unless §338(h)(10) election is made. Seller gets capital gain treatment on entire amount.
- **Personal goodwill strategy** (Martin Ice Cream Co. v. Commissioner, T.C. Memo 1998-246):
  physician argues personal goodwill was never owned by the entity and sells it directly
  to the buyer. Results in capital gain to the physician (no self-employment tax) and
  amortizable §197 asset to the buyer. IRS scrutinizes — must have no employment/non-compete
  agreement with entity that would transfer the goodwill to the entity.

## 6. Recommended Entity Structure

- **Hospital-employed** — W-2 employee. Limited tax planning options beyond retirement and
  charitable strategies. Consider forming LLC for moonlighting/consulting income.
- **Solo practice** — S-Corp election is nearly always optimal. Set reasonable salary
  (benchmarked to MGMA data for specialty), take remaining profit as distributions.
  Saves 3.8% Medicare/additional Medicare tax on distributions above salary.
- **Group practice** — Partnership (LLP) or multi-member LLC. S-Corp election possible for
  the entity. Guaranteed payments vs. distributive share allocation matters for SE tax.
- **Locum tenens / consulting** — LLC taxed as S-Corp once net income exceeds ~$60K-$80K.
  Reasonable salary must reflect actual physician services performed.
- **C-Corp** — Rarely optimal for physician practices due to double taxation. Exception:
  practices planning to sell where QSBS exclusion (IRC §1202) might apply.

### Moonlighting Income
- Employed physicians with side income (expert witness, chart review, telemedicine, medical
  directorships) should report on a separate Schedule C.
- Form a single-member LLC for liability protection. Elect S-Corp when moonlighting net
  income consistently exceeds $60K-$80K.
- Keep moonlighting expenses clearly segregated from employer-related activities.
- Common mistake: not making estimated quarterly payments on moonlighting income, resulting
  in underpayment penalties at high marginal rates.

## 7. Retirement Plan Strategy — Advanced Stacking

Physicians are typically the highest earners who most benefit from tax-deferred retirement.

### Employed Physician Stacking
- **401(k) / 403(b)** — $23,500 employee deferral (2025). $31,000 if age 50+.
  Hospital 403(b) plans may have employer match.
- **457(b)** — If available (government/non-profit hospitals), allows additional $23,500.
  Can contribute to BOTH 403(b) and 457(b) simultaneously — up to $47,000 in deferrals.
- **Backdoor Roth IRA** — Contribute $7,000 to traditional IRA, convert to Roth. Must have
  zero traditional IRA balance to avoid pro-rata taxation (IRC §408(d)(2)). Roll existing
  IRAs into 401(k)/403(b) first.
- **Mega backdoor Roth** — If plan allows after-tax contributions + in-plan Roth conversion.
  Can contribute up to the §415(c) limit ($70,000 in 2025) minus employee deferrals and
  employer match, then convert to Roth. Not all plans offer this.
- **HSA** — $4,300 individual / $8,550 family (2025) if on HDHP. Triple tax advantage.

### Practice Owner Stacking (S-Corp)
- **401(k)** — $23,500 employee deferral from S-Corp W-2.
- **Employer match** — Up to 25% of W-2 compensation (combined employee + employer capped at $70,000).
- **Defined benefit / Cash balance plan** — Practice owners can shelter $280,000+ annually
  (2025 §415(b) limit). Cash balance plans are popular for physicians age 40+ wanting to
  accelerate retirement savings. Requires actuarial administration (~$2K-$5K/year) but
  deduction is substantial. Can run in parallel with 401(k) for total deferrals of $300K+.
- **SEP-IRA** — Up to 25% of W-2 wages (S-Corp) or ~20% of net SE income (sole prop).
  Maximum $70,000 (2025). Simple to administer but no employee deferral component.
  Cannot use SEP if also maintaining 401(k) — use one or the other.
- **Solo 401(k)** — Employee deferral + employer contribution. Better than SEP for most
  solo practitioners. Allows Roth employee contributions.
- **Backdoor Roth IRA** — Same mechanics as employed. Zero out traditional IRA balances first.

### Worked Example: Stacking for Age 52 Practice Owner, $400K W-2 from S-Corp
- 401(k) employee deferral: $31,000 (age 50+ catch-up)
- 401(k) employer contribution (25% of $400K, capped at combined $70K): $39,000
  (Total 401(k): $70,000)
- Cash balance DB plan: ~$180,000 (depends on actuarial calculation, age, interest rate)
- Backdoor Roth IRA: $7,000
- HSA: $8,550 (family)
- **Total tax-advantaged savings: ~$265,550/year**

## 8. Disability Insurance Tax Planning

- **Employer-paid premiums (pre-tax)**: benefits received during disability are FULLY TAXABLE
  as ordinary income. At a $300K annual benefit, this means $100K+ in taxes during the most
  financially vulnerable time.
- **Self-paid premiums (after-tax)**: benefits are TAX-FREE. Always recommend this structure.
- **Split arrangement**: some physicians negotiate a lower base salary with the employer paying
  for a base policy (benefits taxable) and then purchase supplemental own-occupation coverage
  with after-tax dollars (benefits tax-free).
- **Own-occupation coverage**: critical for procedural specialists (surgeons, interventional
  cardiologists). "Any occupation" policies pay less and are taxed the same way.
- **Business overhead policy**: separate policy covering practice expenses during disability.
  Premiums deductible as business expense; benefits are taxable (but offset by the
  deductible practice expenses they cover).

## 9. PSLF for Nonprofit Hospital Physicians

- **Eligibility**: full-time employment at 501(c)(3) hospital, VA, government, FQHC, or
  academic medical center. Most large hospital systems qualify.
- **120 qualifying payments**: must be on an IDR plan (IBR, PAYE, REPAYE/SAVE) while employed
  full-time by qualifying employer. Payments during residency/fellowship count.
- **Tax treatment**: PSLF forgiveness is excluded from gross income (IRC §108(f)(1)).
  No tax bomb, unlike IDR forgiveness after 20-25 years.
- **Strategy**: minimize monthly payments during residency (IBR/REPAYE calculates payments on
  residency income of $55K-$75K). Certify employment annually (PSLF Certification Form).
  After 120 payments, remaining balance is forgiven tax-free.
- **Worked example**: $350K in loans, $600/mo payments during 3-year residency + 7 years as
  attending = 120 payments totaling ~$250K paid. Remaining ~$200K+ (with interest) forgiven
  tax-free. Alternative: paying aggressively would cost $350K+ with no forgiveness.
- **Common mistake**: over-paying loans during residency while pursuing PSLF. Every dollar
  paid above the minimum IDR payment is wasted if pursuing forgiveness.

## 10. Common Audit Triggers

- **High Schedule C income with aggressive deductions** — Locum physicians with >$300K 1099
  income and high travel/meal deductions attract scrutiny.
- **Vehicle deduction** — Physicians claiming business use of luxury vehicles. Must maintain
  contemporaneous mileage log. Personal commuting is never deductible.
- **Home office** — IRS skeptical of physician home offices. Must be regular and exclusive
  use for business (e.g., telemedicine practice, consulting business, not W-2 work).
- **Unreasonable S-Corp salary** — Setting salary too low relative to specialty benchmarks.
  IRS compares to MGMA/AMGA salary surveys. A surgeon paying themselves $80K salary on
  $500K practice income will be scrutinized.
- **Charitable contributions** — Large non-cash donations (art, collectibles) over $5,000
  require qualified appraisal. IRS closely examines high-value charitable deductions.
- **Travel deductions for locum work** — Must prove established tax home (see Section 3).
  Locum physicians claiming $50K+ in travel without a clear home base raise flags.

## 11. Academic Physician Research Deductions

Physicians in academic medicine who also perform consulting, speaking, or independent research
may deduct research-related expenses on Schedule C (if self-employed or through a separate
consulting/speaking entity):

- **Grant-funded equipment purchases** — Equipment purchased with personal funds for grant-related
  research (not reimbursed by the institution) is deductible as a business expense. Section 179
  or bonus depreciation available for qualifying equipment.
- **Lab costs** — Unreimbursed laboratory supplies, reagents, and consumables used for research
  conducted outside the employer relationship (e.g., independent consulting research).
- **Journal publication fees** — Open-access publication fees, page charges, and submission fees
  for peer-reviewed journals. Deductible as ordinary and necessary business expenses when
  related to the physician's consulting or speaking practice.
- **Conference presentation travel** — Airfare, lodging, meals (50%), and registration fees
  for conferences where the physician presents research. Must have a bona fide business
  purpose tied to the self-employment activity (not the W-2 employer relationship).
- **Research-related software and subscriptions** — Statistical software (SAS, SPSS, Stata, R
  Studio Pro), reference management tools (EndNote, Mendeley Premium), journal subscriptions,
  and database access fees (PubMed, UpToDate if not employer-provided). Deductible on
  Schedule C when used for the self-employed research/consulting practice.

**Important limitation:** W-2 employed physicians CANNOT deduct these expenses on their personal
return under TCJA (unreimbursed employee expenses are suspended 2018-2025). These deductions
are only available when the expenses relate to a separate self-employment activity reported
on Schedule C. Push the employer to reimburse through an accountable plan for employer-related
research expenses.

## 12. Telemedicine-Specific Deductions

Physicians with telemedicine practices (Schedule C or through an entity) can deduct:

- **HIPAA-compliant video platform subscriptions** — Doxy.me, Zoom for Healthcare, Amwell,
  Teladoc provider platform fees, and similar HIPAA-compliant telehealth platforms. Fully
  deductible as business expense on Schedule C.
- **Secure messaging software** — HIPAA-compliant messaging and patient communication platforms
  (Spruce Health, OhMD, Klara). Monthly/annual subscription fees are deductible.
- **Dedicated internet connection for telemedicine** — If a separate internet line or upgraded
  service is maintained exclusively for the telemedicine practice, the cost is fully deductible.
  If shared with personal use, allocate based on business use percentage (maintain a log).
- **Multi-state medical licensing fees** — Physicians practicing telemedicine across state lines
  must hold licenses in each state where patients are located. License fees ($300-$1,500 per
  state) are deductible as ordinary and necessary business expenses when required for the
  telehealth practice. Interstate Medical Licensure Compact (IMLC) expedition fees are also
  deductible.
- **Telemedicine-specific equipment** — HD webcams, ring lights or professional lighting,
  dedicated computer or monitor for telemedicine visits, microphone/headset, and green screen
  or professional backdrop. Section 179 or bonus depreciation available. Must be used
  exclusively (or primarily) for the telemedicine business — document business use percentage.

**Home office nexus:** Physicians conducting telemedicine from a dedicated home office may also
claim the home office deduction (Section 280A) if the space meets the regular and exclusive
use test. The telemedicine room must be used solely for patient visits, not for personal
activities or W-2 employer work.

## 13. Profession-Specific Rules

- **PSLF (Public Service Loan Forgiveness)** — See Section 9 above for detailed treatment.
- **Physician mortgage loans** — Not a tax provision, but affects interest deduction planning.
  No PMI, low down payment — full interest deductible on primary residence up to $750K limit.
- **Medical practice goodwill** — Personal goodwill of physician (relationship with patients)
  can be sold separately from practice assets, potentially qualifying for capital gain treatment.
  See Section 5 for detailed treatment.
- **Qualified Business Income (QBI)** — Medical practice income generally qualifies for
  20% §199A deduction. However, "health care" is listed as an SSTB (IRC §199A(d)(2)),
  so deduction phases out above $197,300/$394,600 (2025) and is fully eliminated above
  $247,300/$494,600. Most physician incomes exceed this threshold, making QBI unavailable.
  Exception: physicians below the threshold (e.g., part-time, early career) get the full
  20% deduction.
- **Disability insurance planning** — See Section 8 for detailed treatment.

## 14. State Considerations

### High-Earner State Strategies
- **California (13.3% top rate)**: a physician earning $500K pays ~$45K+ more in state tax
  than the same physician in Texas/Florida. Strategies include: (1) maximizing pre-tax
  retirement contributions to reduce CA AGI, (2) charitable bunching with DAF, (3) PTET
  election for S-Corp practice income to bypass the personal SALT cap, (4) evaluating relocation
  for telemedicine-heavy practices.
- **New York (10.9% + NYC 3.876%)**: combined top rate of ~14.8%. Same strategies as CA plus
  careful domicile planning for physicians splitting time between NYC and other locations.
  NY's statutory residency rules (183 days + permanent place of abode) can trap physicians
  who maintain a NY apartment even after "moving."
- **Multi-state locum tenens** — Locum physicians working in multiple states must file in
  each state. Track days and income per state carefully. Home state provides credit for
  taxes paid to other states (usually). Some states (NY, CT) apply convenience of employer
  rules that can create unexpected tax obligations.
- **State licensing costs** — Deductible for self-employed physicians. Multiple state
  licenses for telemedicine or locum work add up ($300-$1,500 per state).
- **Community property states** — CA, TX, WI, etc. Practice income may be community property.
  Matters for divorce and for allocation between spouses on MFS returns.
- **Malpractice environment** — High-premium states (FL, NY, IL for OB/GYN) mean larger
  deductions but also drive entity structure decisions (asset protection).
- **No-income-tax states** — FL, TX, TN, WA, NV, WY, SD, AK, NH: relocation analysis
  critical for physicians earning $400K+ who can practice remotely (telemedicine,
  consulting, medical directing).

## 15. Worked Example: $500K Physician Income Optimization

**Scenario**: Dr. Smith, age 48, orthopedic surgeon, S-Corp practice, MFJ, spouse not working.

### Gross Income
- S-Corp net practice income: $500,000
- Reasonable W-2 salary from S-Corp: $300,000 (benchmarked to MGMA 50th percentile for ortho)
- S-Corp distribution: $200,000

### Deductions and Tax-Advantaged Savings
| Item | Amount | Tax Savings (est.) |
|------|--------|-------------------|
| 401(k) employee deferral | $23,500 | $8,225 (35%) |
| 401(k) employer contribution (25% of salary, cap) | $46,500 | $16,275 |
| Cash balance DB plan | $120,000 | $42,000 |
| Backdoor Roth IRA | $7,000 | $0 (post-tax) |
| HSA (family HDHP) | $8,550 | $2,993 |
| Self-employed health insurance | $24,000 | $8,400 |
| Charitable (DAF bunching, 3 years) | $45,000 | $15,750 |
| **Total deductions/deferrals** | **$274,550** | **$93,643** |

### SE Tax Savings from S-Corp
- Without S-Corp: SE tax on $500K = ~$27,500+ (2.9% Medicare + 0.9% additional on $200K+)
- With S-Corp: payroll tax on $300K salary only = ~$11,700 (employer Medicare + additional)
- Distribution of $200K: no SE tax
- **SE tax savings: ~$7,600/year**

### Net Federal Tax (Approximate)
- Taxable income after deductions: ~$240,000
- Federal income tax: ~$42,000
- FICA on W-2: ~$21,500 (combined employee + employer)
- NIIT: $0 (net investment income minimal)
- **Effective total federal rate: ~12.7% on $500K gross**

## 16. Common Mistakes

1. **Not maximizing all available retirement vehicles** — Many employed physicians only
   contribute to 401(k), missing 457(b) if available, backdoor Roth, and HSA.
2. **Paying student loans instead of investing** — If pursuing PSLF, minimizing payments
   and investing the difference is often optimal. Over-paying defeats PSLF strategy.
3. **S-Corp salary too low** — IRS can reclassify distributions as wages. Must benchmark
   to specialty survey data (MGMA, AMGA, AAMC). Reasonable for most specialties: 50-70% of
   net income depending on practice overhead structure.
4. **Missing estimated tax payments** — Locum tenens and moonlighting income requires
   quarterly estimated payments. Underpayment penalty adds up quickly at physician income levels.
5. **Failing to use donor-advised fund bunching** — High-income physicians should bunch
   charitable contributions to exceed standard deduction in alternating years.
6. **Ignoring tax-loss harvesting** — After years of high income, taxable investment accounts
   accumulate. Systematic tax-loss harvesting can offset thousands annually.
7. **Disability insurance with pre-tax premiums** — If practice deducts premiums, benefits
   are taxable when most needed. Restructure to after-tax payment.
8. **Not electing S-Corp for side consulting** — Locum, expert witness, and consulting
   income subject to full SE tax without S-Corp election.
9. **Overlooking dependent care FSA** — $5,000 pre-tax for childcare. Small but free savings.
10. **Not establishing tax home for locum work** — Without a tax home, all travel/lodging
    deductions for temporary assignments are lost (can be $30K-$80K/year in deductions).
11. **CME deductions claimed by W-2 physicians** — Under TCJA, unreimbursed employee expenses
    are not deductible. Push employer to reimburse through accountable plan.
12. **Ignoring state PTET elections** — S-Corp physicians in high-tax states (CA, NY, NJ) can
    bypass the personal SALT cap using pass-through entity tax elections, saving material
    federal tax where state taxes exceed the individual cap.
