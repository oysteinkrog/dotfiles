# Attorney — Tax Reference Guide (TY 2025)

## 1. Overview

Attorneys operate across a wide range of practice structures — from BigLaw W-2 associates
to solo practitioners, from equity partners receiving K-1 income to contingency-fee plaintiffs'
lawyers with volatile annual income. Tax planning for attorneys must account for partnership
income allocation, trust account rules, professional development costs, high state bar dues,
and the specific timing rules that apply to legal fee income. Law practice is classified as
a "specified service trade or business" (SSTB) under Section 199A, which eliminates the QBI
deduction for most successful attorneys.

## 2. Common Income Types

- **W-2 salary and bonuses** — Associates and counsel at law firms. BigLaw associates earn
  $225K-$420K+. Bonuses often withheld at 37% supplemental rate.
- **K-1 partnership income (Schedule K-1)** — Equity and non-equity partners. Distributive
  share includes ordinary income, guaranteed payments, and potentially capital gains from
  case settlements. Subject to self-employment tax (see detailed K-1 section below).
- **1099-NEC** — Solo practitioners, of-counsel arrangements, mediator/arbitrator fees,
  expert consulting, legal consulting to other firms.
- **Contingency fees** — Typically 33-40% of settlement/judgment. Income recognized when
  received (cash method) or when right to payment established (accrual method). Attorney
  fee advances for case costs create additional complexity.
- **Court-appointed fees** — Criminal defense, guardian ad litem. Paid by government entity.
- **Referral fees** — Fee-sharing arrangements with other attorneys. Must comply with
  ethical rules and are taxable income.

### Partnership K-1 Income — Deep Dive

Partnership income on Schedule K-1 is the primary income vehicle for law firm partners and
requires careful tax treatment:

- **Guaranteed payments (K-1 Box 4)** — Fixed or minimum payments to a partner regardless of
  partnership income. Treated as ordinary income to the receiving partner under IRC 707(c).
  Guaranteed payments are ALWAYS subject to self-employment tax under IRC 1402(a).
  The partnership deducts guaranteed payments in computing partnership ordinary income.

- **Distributive share (K-1 Boxes 1-3, 5-13)** — Each partner's allocated share of
  partnership income, gain, loss, deduction, and credit. Retains its character when it
  passes through to the partner (capital gain remains capital gain, ordinary income
  remains ordinary income). For general partners, the distributive share IS subject to
  SE tax. For limited partners, the distributive share is generally NOT subject to SE tax
  under IRC 1402(a)(13) — but see *Renkemeyer, Campbell & Weaver, LLP v. Commissioner*
  (T.C. Memo 2011-177): the Tax Court held that partners in a law firm LLP who actively
  practiced law were NOT "limited partners" for SE tax purposes even though they held
  "limited liability" under state law. Their distributive share WAS subject to SE tax
  because they were providing legal services, not merely investing capital. This case
  is critical for law firm partners claiming limited partner status.

- **Self-employment tax on K-1 income** — General partners: SE tax on guaranteed payments
  PLUS distributive share of ordinary income. Limited partners (true passive investors):
  SE tax only on guaranteed payments. Law firm partners who actively practice:
  SE tax on everything (*Renkemeyer*).

- **Capital account and basis tracking** — Partners must track their outside basis in the
  partnership (capital contributions + share of income - distributions - share of losses +
  share of liabilities). Losses are deductible only to the extent of basis (IRC 704(d)).
  Excess losses carry forward.

## 3. Key Deductions

### Employed Attorneys (W-2 — limited post-TCJA)
- **SALT** — Capped at $10,000. Significant for attorneys in NY, CA, IL, DC.
- **Charitable** — Pro bono work itself is not deductible (cannot deduct value of services),
  but out-of-pocket expenses incurred in pro bono representation are deductible as charitable
  contributions if done for a 501(c)(3) organization. Must document: mileage to/from pro
  bono appearances ($0.14/mile charitable rate), parking at courthouse, photocopying,
  postage, filing fees paid personally. Keep a log attributing each expense to the
  qualifying organization.
- **Bar dues and CLE** — NOT deductible for W-2 attorneys post-TCJA (2018-2025). These were
  miscellaneous itemized deductions subject to the 2% AGI floor, now suspended entirely.
  Some states still allow (see state section).

### Self-Employed / Partners
- **Bar dues and licensing** — Annual state bar dues, bar association memberships, specialty
  bar sections. Fully deductible as business expense on Schedule C or as partnership expense.
- **CLE (Continuing Legal Education)** — Registration, travel, materials. Required for license
  maintenance in most states (12-15 hours/year typical). Fully deductible.
- **Malpractice insurance (E&O)** — Professional liability premiums. Deductible business expense.
  Tail coverage premiums (for claims-made policies when retiring or changing firms) are also
  deductible when paid.
- **Legal research tools** — Westlaw, LexisNexis, PACER, Bloomberg Law subscriptions.
- **Office expenses** — Rent, support staff, paralegals, court reporters, filing fees,
  process server fees, expert witness costs (if not advanced to client).
- **Case cost advances** — Costs advanced on behalf of contingency-fee clients. Deductible
  when treated as expense (if attorney bears economic risk). If treated as loan to client,
  not deductible until uncollectible.
- **Business development** — Client meals (50% deductible), networking events, CLE conferences.
- **Pro bono out-of-pocket expenses** — For self-employed attorneys, out-of-pocket costs
  for pro bono work through a qualified 501(c)(3) organization are deductible as charitable
  contributions (not as business expenses). No deduction for the value of services rendered.

## 4. Contingency Fee Timing Rules

Contingency fee income timing is one of the most complex areas for attorney taxation:

- **Cash method (most solo/small-firm plaintiffs' attorneys)** — Income recognized when
  payment is actually or constructively received. Under *North American Oil Consolidated
  v. Burnet* (286 U.S. 417, 1932), income is taxable in the year the taxpayer receives it
  under a "claim of right" without restriction. For contingency fees, the fee is income
  when the settlement check is received and deposited, not when the settlement agreement
  is signed.

- **Constructive receipt doctrine** — Under Reg. 1.451-2, income is constructively received
  when it is credited to the taxpayer's account, set apart, or otherwise made available
  without substantial limitations. If a settlement check is available but the attorney
  delays depositing it to shift income to the next year, the IRS may assert constructive
  receipt in the earlier year. However, genuine legal conditions (court approval pending,
  structured settlement negotiations) are NOT constructive receipt.

- **Accrual method** — Income recognized when all events have occurred establishing the right
  to payment and the amount can be determined with reasonable accuracy. For contingency
  fees, this is typically when the settlement is finalized or judgment entered, regardless
  of when payment is received.

- **Structured attorney fees** — An attorney can structure their fee portion of a personal
  injury settlement into periodic payments, similar to a plaintiff's structured settlement.
  Under IRC 130 (applicable to qualified assignments of structured settlement payment rights),
  the attorney defers income recognition until periodic payments are received. This is
  powerful for smoothing a $2M+ fee over 10-20 years. Must be established BEFORE the
  settlement funds are in the attorney's control.

- **Attorney fee advances for case costs** — Under Rev. Rul. 2008-34 analysis, costs advanced
  to clients may be treated as either loans (not deductible until uncollectible) or expenses
  (deductible when paid), with different tax timing. Must pick one method and apply consistently.

## 5. Recommended Entity Structure

- **BigLaw associates** — W-2 employee. Consider LLC for side work (writing, consulting,
  mediating, teaching).
- **Solo practitioners** — S-Corp election almost always optimal once net income exceeds
  ~$60K-$80K. At $200K+ net income, S-Corp saves $10,000+ annually in SE tax. Example:
  solo attorney nets $250K. Pays reasonable salary of $130K (based on comparable attorney
  compensation data from BLS/Robert Half). Remaining $120K taken as distribution avoids
  the 2.9% Medicare tax (plus 0.9% Additional Medicare Tax above threshold) = savings of
  approximately $4,560-$5,640. At higher income levels the savings compound.
- **Small firm** — Multi-member LLC taxed as partnership or S-Corp. Partnership allows
  flexible income allocation (special allocations with economic effect); S-Corp requires
  pro-rata distribution based on ownership percentage.
- **Large firm** — Most are LLPs (limited liability partnerships). Partners receive K-1.
  The firm itself typically does not pay entity-level tax.
- **Plaintiffs' contingency practice** — S-Corp is powerful due to lumpy high income.
  In a big settlement year, distributions (not salary) avoid SE tax.
- **Asset protection** — LLP or PLLC protects partners from other partners' malpractice.
  Does not protect from own malpractice claims.

## 6. Retirement Plan Strategy

- **Partner in large firm** — Firm 401(k) plan with employer contributions. Max deferral
  $23,500 ($31,000 if 50+). Backdoor Roth IRA.
- **Solo practitioner** — Solo 401(k) is optimal. Employee deferral + 25% employer match
  on W-2 salary (S-Corp). Allows Roth contributions within the plan.
- **High-income partner** — Defined benefit (cash balance) plan can shelter $280,000+.
  Especially valuable for attorneys age 45+ wanting to accelerate retirement savings.
- **Solo 401(k) + Defined Benefit combination** — A solo attorney age 50+ with consistent
  $400K+ income can contribute $31,000 (employee 401(k) with catch-up) + $280,000+
  (defined benefit) = $310,000+ in annual tax-deferred retirement savings. This is the
  single most powerful retirement strategy available to any self-employed professional.
  Requires actuarial plan design and annual administration ($1,500-$3,000/year), but the
  tax deferral dwarfs the cost.
- **Contingency-fee attorneys** — Income volatility makes planning harder. Solo 401(k)
  with discretionary profit-sharing allows flexible contributions in big years. Defined
  benefit plans are problematic with volatile income (minimum funding requirements persist
  even in low-income years).
- **Backdoor Roth** — Standard strategy for all high-income attorneys. Roll any existing
  traditional IRA into firm 401(k) to clear pro-rata issue before executing backdoor.

## 7. IOLTA and Trust Accounting

- **IOLTA interest** — Interest on Lawyers Trust Accounts (IOLTA) belongs to the state bar
  foundation or designated charitable entity, NOT to the attorney. The attorney is merely
  a custodian. IOLTA interest should never appear on the attorney's tax return.
- **Client trust funds** — Funds held in trust for clients are not the attorney's income
  until earned (i.e., until the fee is earned and transferred to the attorney's operating
  account). Commingling trust funds with operating funds creates both ethical violations
  and tax problems.
- **Bank reporting** — Banks issue Form 1099-INT for IOLTA interest to the state bar
  foundation (EIN), not to the attorney. If incorrectly issued to the attorney, file a
  nominee return (Form 1099-INT) to redirect, and do not include on personal return.

## 8. Attorney Fee Awards — IRC 62(a)(20)

- **Above-the-line deduction for employment/whistleblower claims** — IRC 62(a)(20) allows
  an above-the-line deduction for attorney fees and court costs paid in connection with:
  (1) claims of unlawful discrimination (Title VII, ADA, ADEA, etc.), (2) certain claims
  against the federal government, and (3) IRS whistleblower awards under IRC 7623.
- **Why this matters** — Without 62(a)(20), a plaintiff receiving a $500K employment
  discrimination award (with $200K going to attorney fees) would report $500K gross income
  and could not deduct the $200K attorney fee (miscellaneous itemized deduction eliminated
  by TCJA). Section 62(a)(20) allows the $200K as an above-the-line deduction, so the
  plaintiff is taxed only on the $300K net recovery. This is the CLIENT's deduction,
  not the attorney's — but attorneys should advise clients of this critical provision.
- **Personal injury settlements** — Under IRC 104(a)(2), damages received on account of
  physical injury or physical sickness are excluded from gross income entirely (both
  compensatory and punitive in some circuits). The attorney fee portion of excluded
  damages is also excluded. Emotional distress damages (without physical injury) are
  TAXABLE, and only the amount exceeding medical expenses paid is included.

## 9. Common Audit Triggers

- **Contingency fee timing** — IRS examines when income was earned vs. reported. Cash-method
  taxpayers must report when payment is received, not when settlement is signed.
- **Large deductions on Schedule C** — Solo attorneys with high revenue and proportionally
  high deductions (meals, travel, office) attract scrutiny.
- **Trust account issues** — IOLTA/trust account interest belongs to clients or charity,
  not the attorney. Commingling creates both ethical and tax problems.
- **Unreasonable S-Corp salary** — Attorney earning $500K through S-Corp paying $60K salary.
  IRS uses comparable attorney salary data to challenge. Courts have looked at BLS data,
  Robert Half surveys, and local market rates for comparable attorneys.
- **Cash-intensive practice** — Criminal defense attorneys who receive cash payments face
  additional reporting requirements (Form 8300 for cash over $10,000).
- **Vehicle deductions** — Attorneys rarely have substantial business driving needs.
  Large mileage deductions invite questions.
- **K-1 SE tax avoidance** — Law firm partners claiming "limited partner" status to avoid
  SE tax on distributive share. Post-*Renkemeyer*, this is extremely risky for active
  practitioners.

## 10. Profession-Specific Rules

- **Section 199A (QBI)** — Law practice is a "specified service trade or business" (SSTB).
  QBI deduction phases out completely above $247,300 single / $494,600 MFJ (2025).
  Most successful attorneys exceed this threshold and get no QBI deduction. Attorneys
  below the threshold should claim the full 20% deduction.
- **IRC 1341 (Claim of Right)** — If attorney must repay previously reported income
  (e.g., reversed judgment, fee disgorgement), IRC 1341 allows credit or deduction
  in the year of repayment. Compute tax both ways (deduction in current year vs.
  credit based on prior year's higher tax) and use the more favorable method.
- **Structured attorney fees** — Attorney can structure their fee portion of a settlement
  into periodic payments (similar to structured settlements). Defers income recognition.
  Must be established before funds reach attorney's control.
- **IRC 130 — Qualified assignments** — Applies to structured settlement obligations.
  The assignee (typically a structured settlement company) excludes the assigned
  obligation from income. The attorney receives periodic payments that are taxable as
  received.
- **Contingency fee income timing** — Under IRC 451 and related regulations, attorneys on
  the cash method report contingency fees when received. Structured settlement fees can
  defer income.

## 11. State Considerations

- **New York** — High state + city tax. "Convenience of employer" rule taxes remote work
  for NY-based firms. Partnership-level audit adjustments flow through to NY partners.
  Bar admission costs: ~$750 (bar exam fee + admission fee). Annual registration: $375.
- **California** — 13.3% top rate. LLC fee (gross receipts based, $800-$11,790) applies
  to California LLCs. S-Corp franchise tax minimum $800. Bar admission: ~$900+.
  Annual bar dues: ~$510 (active). Cal Bar has trust account (IOLTA) compliance audits.
- **Texas** — No income tax. Bar admission: ~$1,000+. Growing number of firms establishing
  presence. Solo attorneys relocating must establish genuine domicile. TX has no state
  bar dues after initial admission. Franchise (margin) tax applies to entities.
- **Illinois** — Flat 4.95% rate. IL has PTE tax election available. Annual ARDC
  registration: ~$391 (active).
- **Florida** — No income tax. Aggressive domicile audits for attorneys moving from
  NY/NJ/CT. Must sever ties with prior state. Bar admission: ~$1,000.
- **Multi-state practice** — Attorneys admitted in multiple states may owe tax where
  services are performed, not just where they live. Track days and billings per state.
  Use apportionment methods where available.
- **PTE (Pass-Through Entity) tax elections** — Many states (CA, NY, NJ, IL, CT, etc.)
  allow partnerships/S-Corps to pay entity-level state tax, generating federal deduction
  that bypasses the personal SALT cap. Critical for attorney partnerships. Every attorney
  partnership in an eligible state should evaluate this election.

## 12. Pro Bono Work Documentation

- **No deduction for services** — An attorney's time has value, but donated legal services
  are NOT deductible as a charitable contribution. IRC 170 specifically excludes the value
  of services from the charitable deduction.
- **Out-of-pocket expenses ARE deductible** — If the attorney incurs unreimbursed expenses
  in connection with pro bono work for a qualified 501(c)(3) organization, those expenses
  are deductible as charitable contributions: mileage ($0.14/mile charitable rate, 2025),
  parking, photocopying, postage, filing fees, travel expenses, court costs.
- **Documentation** — Maintain records showing: the qualifying organization, dates of service,
  nature of expenses, receipts, and the pro bono engagement letter or confirmation.
- **Self-employed vs. W-2** — For self-employed attorneys, pro bono expenses are charitable
  contributions (Schedule A or above-the-line if qualifying). They are NOT Schedule C
  business expenses (because the services are donated, not sold).

## 13. Common Mistakes

1. **Not using PTE tax election** — Leaving significant SALT deduction savings on the table.
   Every attorney partnership in an eligible state should evaluate this election.
2. **Mishandling trust account reporting** — IOLTA interest is not attorney income. Ensure
   bank Form 1099-INT is correctly attributed.
3. **Ignoring structured fee options** — Large contingency fees can be structured to defer
   income over multiple years, smoothing tax brackets.
4. **S-Corp salary set too low** — IRS compares to attorney salary surveys (BLS, Robert Half).
   A litigator netting $400K and paying $80K salary will be challenged.
5. **Missing estimated payments in contingency years** — Big settlement in Q4 without prior
   estimated payments creates underpayment penalty. Use annualized income installment method
   (Form 2210 Schedule AI) to minimize.
6. **Failing to document pro bono expenses** — Out-of-pocket costs (parking, copies, postage)
   for pro bono work through a qualified organization are deductible if documented.
7. **Ignoring retirement plan opportunities** — Many solo attorneys contribute nothing beyond
   basic IRA. Solo 401(k) + cash balance plan can defer $300K+ annually for older attorneys.
8. **Treating case cost advances inconsistently** — Must pick treatment (loan vs. expense)
   and apply consistently. Switching methods without IRS approval creates problems.
9. **Overlooking referral fee reporting** — Must report referral fees received on Schedule C
   or as part of K-1 income. Payer should issue 1099-NEC.
10. **K-1 SE tax misreporting** — Law firm partners who actively practice law owe SE tax on
    their full distributive share plus guaranteed payments (*Renkemeyer*). Claiming limited
    partner exemption is aggressive and likely to fail on audit.
11. **Not advising clients on 62(a)(20)** — Plaintiffs' attorneys should ensure clients in
    employment discrimination and whistleblower cases claim the above-the-line attorney
    fee deduction. Missing this provision can cost clients tens of thousands in unnecessary tax.
12. **W-2 attorneys deducting bar dues federally** — Not allowed post-TCJA. Check if your
    state still permits on the state return.
