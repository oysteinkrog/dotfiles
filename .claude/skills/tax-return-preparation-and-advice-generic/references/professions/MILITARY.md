# Military Service Member -- Tax Reference Guide (TY 2025)

## 1. Overview

Military members benefit from some of the most generous tax provisions in the IRC. Combat zone
exclusions, tax-free allowances, the continued availability of moving expense deductions (which
were eliminated for civilians by TCJA), and state tax exemptions create a uniquely favorable
tax environment. Active duty, reserve, National Guard, and veterans each face distinct planning
opportunities. Proper tax planning can result in significant portions of military compensation
being completely tax-free.

## 2. Common Income Types

- **W-2 base pay** -- Taxable military salary reported on W-2 from DFAS.
- **Special and incentive pays** -- Flight pay, hazardous duty pay, hostile fire/imminent
  danger pay (HFP/IDP), sea pay, diving pay. Taxability depends on combat zone status.
- **BAH (Basic Allowance for Housing)** -- TAX-FREE. Not reported on W-2. Not included in AGI.
  Significant benefit: BAH can be $1,500-$4,000+/month depending on location and rank.
- **BAS (Basic Allowance for Subsistence)** -- TAX-FREE. ~$460/month (enlisted), ~$317/month
  (officer) for 2025.
- **COLA (Cost of Living Allowance)** -- Tax-free for overseas assignments.
- **Combat zone pay** -- See Section 7 for detailed CZTE rules.
- **Reenlistment bonuses** -- Taxable unless received in combat zone.
- **Separation/severance pay** -- Generally taxable. Disability severance may be partially
  or fully excludable.
- **VA disability compensation** -- 100% tax-free. Not reported anywhere on return.
- **Military retirement pay** -- Taxable (except VA disability offset). Reported on 1099-R.

## 3. Key Deductions

### Military-Specific Deductions
- **Moving expenses (IRC Section 217(g))** -- STILL DEDUCTIBLE for active-duty military
  moving under PCS orders. Civilians lost this deduction under TCJA (2018-2025).
  Deduct cost of moving household goods, travel (including lodging), and vehicle mileage
  ($0.22/mile moving rate for 2025). Reported on Form 3903.
  - Includes: packing, shipping, storage (up to 30 days), travel to new duty station,
    lodging en route, transportation of vehicles and pets
  - Does NOT include: meals during travel, house-hunting trips, temporary living expenses,
    cost of breaking a lease
  - Reimbursed moving expenses: if the military reimburses you, only the unreimbursed
    portion is deductible (or if reimbursement is included in W-2 income, the full amount
    may be deducted)

- **Reserve travel** -- National Guard/Reserve members can deduct travel expenses for drills
  more than 100 miles from home (above-the-line deduction on Schedule 1 line 12).
  Includes transportation, lodging, and meals at 50%. This is an above-the-line deduction
  (not an itemized deduction), so it reduces AGI even if you take the standard deduction.

- **Unreimbursed uniform costs** -- NOT deductible for W-2 employees post-TCJA (2018-2025)
  as a federal itemized deduction. However, some states still allow.

### Standard Deductions Available
- **SALT** -- Capped at $10,000. Service members can choose domicile state for tax purposes.
- **Mortgage interest** -- VA loans require no PMI and no down payment. Interest is deductible.
- **Charitable contributions** -- Including Combined Federal Campaign (CFC) donations.
- **Student loan interest** -- Up to $2,500 above-the-line deduction (income phase-outs apply).

### Self-Employment (Side Business)
- Many military members have side businesses (consulting, real estate, online businesses).
  Full Schedule C deductions available. Must not violate DoD regulations on outside employment.

## 4. Recommended Entity Structure

- **Active duty W-2** -- No entity needed for military pay. For side businesses:
- **Small side business** -- Sole proprietor. Report on Schedule C.
- **Growing side business** -- LLC for liability protection. S-Corp election if net income
  exceeds ~$50K-$60K.
- **Real estate investing** -- Very popular among military members (VA loan advantage). Separate
  LLC per property or series LLC. Report on Schedule E.
- **Military spouse business** -- Spouse's business can be structured independently. Military
  Spouse Residency Relief Act (MSRRA) allows spouse to maintain domicile state.

## 5. Retirement Plan Strategy

- **TSP (Thrift Savings Plan)** -- Military 401(k) equivalent. Traditional or Roth contributions.
  $23,500 limit (2025), $31,000 if age 50+. Government match up to 5% under BRS (Blended
  Retirement System).

### Combat Zone Roth TSP -- The Triple Tax Benefit
This is the single best tax deal available to any American taxpayer:
1. **Tax-free going in**: combat zone pay is excluded from income, so contributions are made
   with tax-free dollars
2. **Tax-free growth**: Roth accounts grow tax-free
3. **Tax-free coming out**: qualified Roth distributions are tax-free

Normally, Roth contributions are made with AFTER-TAX dollars (you pay tax on the income, then
contribute). In combat zones, you pay NO tax on the income AND get Roth treatment on
distributions. Contribute the MAXIMUM during every deployment.

**Additional combat zone TSP benefit**: the annual additions limit ($70,000 for 2025, including
employee + employer contributions) applies, but the normal $23,500 employee deferral limit
may be exceeded for combat zone Roth TSP contributions.

- **IRA** -- Additional $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+) in Traditional or Roth IRA.
- **Combat zone Roth IRA strategy**: contribute tax-free combat pay to Roth IRA. The
  contribution is never taxed (combat zone exclusion) and the withdrawal is never taxed
  (Roth qualified distribution). Double tax-free.
- **Backdoor Roth** -- For higher-ranking officers exceeding Roth income limits.
- **Side business retirement** -- Solo 401(k) or SEP-IRA for self-employment income.
  Can contribute to TSP AND solo 401(k) (aggregate employee deferral limit $23,500 across
  all plans).

## 6. Common Audit Triggers

- **Combat zone income exclusion** -- Must have qualifying service in designated combat zone
  or qualified hazardous duty area. IRS verifies with DFAS records.
- **Moving expense deductions** -- Must be for PCS (permanent change of station) orders, not
  TDY. Keep orders as documentation.
- **Claiming deductions for tax-free income** -- Cannot deduct expenses attributable to
  tax-free income (e.g., housing expenses paid with tax-free BAH).
- **Dual-status state returns** -- Claiming wrong domicile to avoid state tax. Must establish
  genuine domicile (voter registration, vehicle registration, driver's license).
- **Rental property losses** -- Military members with multiple rental properties may trigger
  passive activity loss review. Real estate professional exception requires 750+ hours.
- **Side business losses** -- Hobby loss rules apply. Must demonstrate profit motive.

## 7. Combat Zone Tax Exclusion (CZTE) -- IRC Section 112 Deep Dive

### What Qualifies
- **Designated combat zones**: areas designated by Executive Order (currently includes
  Afghanistan, Iraq, Syria, parts of the Arabian Peninsula, and others)
- **Direct support areas**: areas certified by the DoD as directly supporting combat operations
  (e.g., parts of Jordan, Djibouti)
- **Qualified hazardous duty areas**: areas designated by Congress (currently includes
  Kosovo and the Balkans)

### Exclusion Rules by Rank

**Enlisted members (all grades E-1 through E-9)**:
- ALL military compensation is tax-free for any month served in a combat zone
- Includes base pay, special pay, bonuses, reenlistment bonuses
- No dollar limit

**Officers (O-1 through O-10, W-1 through W-5)**:
- Tax-free up to the highest enlisted pay rate + HFP/IDP for the month
- 2025 approximate monthly cap: ~$10,500 (highest enlisted base pay for E-9 with 26+ years
  + $250 HFP/IDP)
- Any officer pay above this cap is taxable

### Full-Month Rule
- If you serve in a combat zone for ANY part of a month, the ENTIRE month's pay qualifies
  for CZTE
- Deploying on January 31 = all of January's pay is excluded
- Returning on March 1 = all of March's pay is excluded
- This makes timing of arrival/departure strategically important

### What CZTE Covers
- Base pay (within limits for officers)
- Hostile fire/imminent danger pay
- Reenlistment bonuses received while in combat zone
- Accrued leave payment (if for leave earned in combat zone)
- Awards and bonuses earned in combat zone

## 8. Automatic Filing Extensions -- Combat Zone

**IRC Section 7508**: members serving in combat zones receive automatic extensions:

**Extension formula**: 180 days after leaving the combat zone PLUS the number of days
remaining on any deadline when the member entered the combat zone.

**Worked example**:
- Tax return normally due April 15, 2025
- Member enters combat zone March 1, 2025 (45 days remaining before April 15)
- Member leaves combat zone September 15, 2025
- New deadline: September 15 + 180 days + 45 days = March 10, 2026

**Extended deadlines apply to**:
- Filing tax returns
- Paying tax
- Filing refund claims
- IRA contributions
- Roth conversion decisions
- Estimated tax payments
- Tax Court petitions
- Claiming credits/deductions
- Rolling over retirement distributions

**No penalties or interest** accrue during the combat zone extension period.

## 9. SCRA (Servicemembers Civil Relief Act) -- Tax Benefits

### State Tax Protection
- Military income is taxed ONLY in the member's state of legal residence (domicile),
  NOT the state where they are stationed
- **Example**: TX-domiciled member stationed in CA pays no state income tax on military pay
  (TX has no income tax; CA cannot tax the member's military income)
- Must maintain domicile intent: voter registration, vehicle registration, driver's license,
  bank accounts, tax returns all in domicile state

### Financial Protections
- **6% interest rate cap**: pre-service debts (mortgages, car loans, credit cards, student
  loans) are capped at 6% interest during active duty. The creditor must reduce the rate
  upon request with proof of military service.
- **Protection from foreclosure**: cannot foreclose on service member's property during
  active duty and for a period after
- **Right to terminate lease**: can terminate residential and auto leases upon receiving
  PCS orders or deployment of 90+ days
- **Stay of proceedings**: can request postponement of civil legal proceedings

### MSRRA (Military Spouses Residency Relief Act)
- **Spouse can use service member's domicile state** for income tax purposes if the spouse
  moved to the duty station state to be with the service member
- **Requirements**: spouse must have the same domicile as the service member; the spouse
  must be in the duty station state solely to be with the service member
- **Effect**: spouse's income (W-2, self-employment) is taxed only by the domicile state,
  not the duty station state
- **Example**: service member domiciled in FL, stationed in NY. Spouse works in NY. Under
  MSRRA, spouse's NY wages are not subject to NY state tax (only FL, which has no income tax).
- **Filing requirement**: employer must be notified to stop withholding for the duty station
  state. May need to file for withholding exemption.

## 10. USERRA (Uniformed Services Employment and Reemployment Rights Act)

- Protects civilian employment and benefits for service members
- **Pension vesting**: time spent on military leave counts toward pension vesting in civilian
  employer's plan
- **401(k) make-up contributions**: upon returning from military service, employee can make
  up missed 401(k) contributions (including employer match) for the period of service
- **Time limit**: make-up period is 3x the length of military service (up to 5 years)
- **Tax impact**: make-up contributions are treated as if made in the year they would have
  been made, but are actually contributed in the catch-up year

## 11. Other Profession-Specific Rules

- **Tax forgiveness for KIA (IRC Section 692)** -- Federal income tax forgiven for year of
  death and any prior year in which service member served in combat zone. Refund of
  previously paid tax. Also applies to astronauts who die in the line of duty.
- **SBP (Survivor Benefit Plan)** -- Premiums are not deductible. Benefits received by
  surviving spouse are taxable income (reported on 1099-R).
- **GI Bill benefits** -- Tax-free under IRC Section 127. Not reported as income. Includes
  tuition, housing stipend, and book stipend.
- **VA disability rating** -- Service-connected disability compensation is 100% tax-free
  (IRC Section 104(a)(4)). Combat-injured veterans may be able to retroactively exclude
  disability severance pay.
- **EITC and combat zone pay**: service members can ELECT to include combat zone pay in
  earned income for EITC calculation if it increases the credit. This is an election --
  not required. Particularly valuable for lower-ranking enlisted with families.

## 12. State Considerations

### States With No Military Income Tax
- **Nine no-income-tax states**: AK, FL, NV, NH, SD, TN, TX, WA, WY. Popular domicile
  choices for military members.
- **States exempting all active-duty military pay**: numerous states exempt some or all
  military pay:
  - Full exemption: CO (all active-duty), CT, HI, IL, IA, and others
  - Partial exemption: AZ (up to $3,500), KS (partial), and others
  - Check specific state rules as they change frequently

### SCRA Domicile Protection
- Member's income taxed only in domicile state, not duty station state
- Must maintain domicile intent: voter registration, driver's license, vehicle registration,
  bank accounts, tax returns filed in domicile state
- Changing any of these to the duty station state can jeopardize the domicile claim

### MSRRA for Spouses
- Spouse can use service member's domicile state for income tax purposes
- Must meet requirements (same domicile, moved to be with service member)
- **Filing requirement**: must file in domicile state. May need to file nonresident return
  in duty station state for non-military income if applicable.

### State Filing Requirements
- File in domicile state (required)
- May need to file in duty station state for non-military income earned there (side business,
  spouse W-2 from local employer not covered by MSRRA)
- Some states require a return even if no tax is due (to claim exemption)

## 13. Common Mistakes

1. **Not maximizing Roth TSP in combat zone** -- Tax-free in AND tax-free out. Failing
   to contribute maximum during deployment wastes the best tax opportunity available.
   The triple tax benefit does not exist anywhere else in the tax code.

2. **Paying state tax when domiciled in no-tax state** -- Under SCRA, TX/FL-domiciled
   members should not have state tax withheld in CA/NY duty stations. File for exemption
   with your employer/DFAS and with the duty station state.

3. **Not claiming moving expense deduction** -- Only military can deduct PCS moves post-TCJA.
   Include transportation, lodging, shipping of household goods. Form 3903.

4. **Missing combat zone filing extensions** -- Deadlines are automatically extended 180 days
   after leaving the combat zone plus remaining time. No need to rush filing while deployed.
   But also do not miss the extended deadlines.

5. **Forgetting to report side income** -- Military pay on W-2 is straightforward, but rental
   income, side business income, and investment income must all be reported.

6. **Not using tax-free BAH for mortgage** -- BAH is not taxable, so mortgage interest paid
   with BAH still generates a deduction. Effectively deducting expenses paid with tax-free
   money -- one of the best tax arbitrage opportunities.

7. **State domicile documentation failure** -- Must actively maintain domicile in no-tax state.
   Registering to vote or getting a license in duty station state can jeopardize domicile claim.

8. **Overlooking EITC** -- Lower-ranking enlisted with families may qualify. Can elect to
   include combat zone pay in earned income for EITC calculation if it increases the credit.

9. **Not filing when all income is tax-free** -- Even with CZTE, filing a return may be needed
   to claim credits (child tax credit, EITC) or refund of withheld taxes. You can only get
   a refund by filing.

10. **Spouse not electing MSRRA** -- If the spouse works in a high-tax duty station state
    but the service member is domiciled in a no-tax state, MSRRA can save thousands in
    state income tax.

11. **Not making 401(k) catch-up contributions after deployment under USERRA** -- Missed
    contributions during military service can be made up upon return to civilian employment.

12. **Rolling combat zone Roth TSP to Traditional IRA** -- Permanently destroys the triple
    tax benefit. Combat zone Roth TSP contributions should ALWAYS stay in Roth accounts.
