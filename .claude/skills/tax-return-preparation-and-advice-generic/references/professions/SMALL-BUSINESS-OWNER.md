# Small Business Owner — Tax Reference Guide (TY 2025)

## 1. Overview

Small business owners encompass a vast range — from retail shops and restaurants to service
companies and e-commerce businesses. The tax landscape requires navigating entity selection,
payroll obligations, employee vs. contractor classification, inventory accounting, equipment
depreciation, and multi-layered federal/state compliance. Proper planning can reduce the
effective tax rate by 10-20 percentage points compared to unplanned filing.

## 2. Common Income Types

- **Business net income** — Reported on Schedule C (sole prop), Form 1120-S (S-Corp),
  Form 1065 (partnership), or Form 1120 (C-Corp). Flows to individual return via K-1
  for pass-through entities.
- **W-2 salary** — Owner's salary from S-Corp or C-Corp.
- **Guaranteed payments** — From partnerships, reported on K-1 line 4. Subject to SE tax.
- **Distributions** — S-Corp distributions (not subject to SE tax if reasonable salary paid).
  Partnership distributions (tax already paid on distributive share).
- **Rental income** — If business owns the building and rents to operating entity.
  Common tax planning strategy: separate real estate holding LLC.
- **Interest income** — From business bank accounts, customer financing.

## 3. Employee vs. Contractor Classification

### IRS Common-Law Test (20-Factor Test, Rev. Rul. 87-41)
Factors grouped into three categories:
1. **Behavioral control**: Does the business control how/when/where work is done?
   - Instructions on when/where/how to work = employee
   - Training provided by the business = employee
   - Worker sets own hours and methods = contractor
2. **Financial control**: Does the worker have unreimbursed expenses, investment in tools,
   opportunity for profit/loss, multiple clients?
   - Fixed salary = employee
   - Project-based payment, own tools, risk of loss = contractor
3. **Relationship type**: Written contracts, employee-type benefits (insurance, pension, PTO),
   permanency of relationship, key services to business?
   - Indefinite relationship with benefits = employee
   - Defined project with contract = contractor

### ABC Test (Used by CA, NJ, MA, and others for state purposes)
Under California AB5 and similar state laws, a worker is an employee UNLESS:
- **(A)** Free from control and direction in performing work
- **(B)** Performs work outside the usual course of the hiring entity's business
- **(C)** Customarily engaged in an independently established trade

This is MUCH harder to satisfy than the IRS common-law test. Many consultants who pass
the federal test fail the ABC test at the state level.

### Consequences of Misclassification
- **Back employment taxes**: 100% of employer share of FICA (7.65%) + income tax withholding
  (1.5% of wages under §3509 if 1099 was filed, higher if not)
- **Penalties**: failure to withhold, failure to file, negligence penalties
- **State penalties**: additional state unemployment tax, workers' comp violations
- **Form SS-8 determination**: IRS uses this to formally classify a worker
- **Section 530 relief**: safe harbor protection if you had a reasonable basis for treating
  a worker as a contractor (prior IRS audit, industry practice, legal/CPA advice)

### Form W-9 and 1099 Obligations
- Collect Form W-9 from every contractor BEFORE first payment
- Issue 1099-NEC by January 31 for any contractor paid $600+ during the year
- 1099-NEC reports total payments in Box 1 (nonemployee compensation)
- Penalties for failure to file: $60 (if filed within 30 days), $130 (31 days to Aug 1),
  $310 (after Aug 1 or intentional disregard = $660)
- E-filing required if 10+ returns (threshold dropped from 250)

## 4. Key Deductions

### Payroll and Compensation
- **Employee wages** — Fully deductible. Includes salary, hourly wages, bonuses.
- **Employer payroll taxes** — FICA match (7.65%), FUTA ($42/employee max), state unemployment.
- **Employee benefits** — Health insurance premiums, retirement plan contributions, group
  life insurance (up to $50K coverage tax-free to employee).
- **Owner's health insurance** — S-Corp: deductible by corporation, reported on owner's W-2
  Box 1 (not Boxes 3/5). Owner takes above-the-line deduction on Form 1040.
- **Work Opportunity Tax Credit (WOTC)** — Tax credit for hiring individuals from targeted
  groups (veterans, long-term unemployed, ex-felons, SNAP recipients, etc.). Credit = 25-40%
  of first-year wages (up to $6,000-$24,000 depending on target group). Must file IRS Form
  8850 within 28 days of hire start date. Pre-screening is required — cannot claim retroactively.

### Cost of Goods Sold (COGS) and Inventory

#### Inventory Methods
- **FIFO (First-In, First-Out)** — Oldest inventory sold first. In rising prices, results in
  lower COGS and higher taxable income. Most common and simplest.
- **LIFO (Last-In, First-Out)** — Newest inventory sold first. In rising prices, results in
  higher COGS and lower taxable income. Requires conformity rule (must also use for financial
  reporting). Not available under IFRS but allowed for US tax.
- **Average cost** — Weighted average of all units available. Middle ground.
- **Specific identification** — Track exact cost of each item sold. Required for unique/high-value
  items (custom furniture, art, vehicles).

#### UNICAP Rules (IRC §263A)
- **Applies to**: manufacturers, producers, and certain resellers with average annual gross
  receipts exceeding $29 million (3-year average).
- **Requires**: capitalizing certain indirect costs into inventory (portion of rent, utilities,
  depreciation, insurance, warehouse costs, purchasing/receiving/storage labor).
- **Small business exception** ($29M average gross receipts or less): exempt from §263A.
  Most small businesses avoid this complexity.
- If UNICAP applies: additional costs capitalized into inventory are only deducted when the
  inventory is sold (through COGS). This delays deductions.

#### Small Business Taxpayer Exception (IRC §471(c))
- Businesses with average gross receipts of $30M or less (3-year average) can use the cash
  method of accounting and treat inventory as non-incidental materials and supplies.
- Effectively allows immediate deduction of inventory costs when purchased (for tax purposes).
- Significant simplification for qualifying businesses.

### Startup and Organizational Costs

#### Startup Costs (IRC §195)
- Costs incurred BEFORE the business begins active trade or business operations.
- Must be costs that WOULD be deductible if the business were already operating.
- Examples: market research, pre-opening advertising, travel to scope locations, consultant fees.
- **Tax treatment**: first $5,000 immediately deductible, reduced dollar-for-dollar by costs
  exceeding $50,000. Remainder amortized over 180 months (15 years).
- **Investigation vs. active trade**: costs of investigating whether to enter a business
  (before committing) are startup costs under §195. Costs incurred after the business starts
  operations are ordinary deductions.

#### Organizational Costs (IRC §248)
- Costs of creating the legal entity: state filing fees, legal fees for articles of
  incorporation/organization, partnership agreement drafting.
- Same treatment as startup costs: first $5,000 deductible + 180-month amortization.
  $50,000 phase-out.
- Only applies to partnerships (§709) and corporations (§248). Sole proprietors have no
  organizational costs.

### Equipment and Property
- **Section 179** — Expense up to $2,500,000 of qualifying property (2025). Phases out when
  total placed-in-service exceeds $4,000,000. Covers equipment, furniture, vehicles, software,
  certain improvements.
- **Bonus depreciation** — Generally 100% for qualified property acquired and placed in
  service after January 19, 2025. Property acquired before January 20, 2025 is generally
  limited to 40% bonus depreciation.
- **Vehicle limits** — First-year passenger-auto cap is $20,200 with bonus depreciation
  ($12,400 without bonus) for 2025. SUVs over 6,000 lbs GVWR: Section 179 up to $31,300.
- **Qualified Improvement Property (QIP)** — Interior improvements to nonresidential buildings.
  15-year MACRS, eligible for bonus depreciation.

### Operating Expenses
- **Rent** — Business premises. Related-party rent must be at FMV.
- **Utilities, insurance, repairs** — Ordinary and necessary business expenses.
- **Professional services** — Accounting, legal, consulting fees.
- **Marketing and advertising** — Fully deductible in year paid/incurred.
- **Bad debts** — Business bad debts deductible as ordinary loss when worthless (accrual method)
  or when previously included in income (cash method).

### Business Interest Limitation (IRC §163(j))
- **Applies to**: businesses with average annual gross receipts exceeding $29 million
  (3-year average).
- **Limitation**: business interest expense deductible only up to 30% of adjusted taxable
  income (ATI). ATI = taxable income + interest expense + depreciation/amortization
  (for tax years before 2022; starting 2022, depreciation/amortization no longer added back).
- **Carryforward**: disallowed interest carries forward indefinitely.
- **Small business exception**: businesses with gross receipts of $29M or less are exempt.
  Most small businesses are NOT affected.
- **Real property trades or businesses**: can elect out of §163(j) but must use ADS
  depreciation (longer recovery periods) for real property.
- **Electing farming/real estate businesses**: irrevocable election to opt out of the limitation.

### Accounting Method Elections (IRC §446, §448)
- **Cash method**: income recognized when received, expenses when paid. Simpler. Available
  for businesses with average gross receipts of $29M or less (§448 C-Corp/partnership
  threshold). Also available for all sole proprietors regardless of revenue.
- **Accrual method**: income recognized when earned/right to payment established, expenses
  when all events establishing liability have occurred. Required for businesses with
  inventory (unless small business exception applies) or gross receipts > $29M.
- **Hybrid methods**: cash for some items, accrual for others. Must be consistently applied.
- **Change of accounting method**: requires Form 3115 and §481(a) adjustment (spread
  cumulative effect of change over 4 years if unfavorable to taxpayer, all in year 1 if
  favorable).

## 5. Recommended Entity Structure

- **Sole proprietorship** — Simplest. No entity formation. All income on Schedule C. Full
  SE tax exposure. Suitable for very small operations under $40K net income.
- **Single-member LLC (disregarded)** — Liability protection without tax complexity. Same
  Schedule C filing. Can elect S-Corp taxation when income warrants.
- **S-Corporation** — Most common for established small businesses. Pass-through taxation.
  Reasonable salary + distributions saves SE tax. $70K-$80K+ net income makes S-Corp worthwhile.
  Limited to 100 shareholders, one class of stock, US residents only.
- **C-Corporation** — 21% flat rate. Beneficial when retaining earnings in the business.
  Double taxation on distributions (dividends). Consider for: businesses planning to reinvest
  heavily, seeking QSBS exclusion (IRC §1202), or venture funding.
- **Partnership / multi-member LLC** — Flexible allocation of income/loss/credits among partners.
  More complex tax return (Form 1065) but powerful for multiple owners.
- **Separate real estate entity** — Own business real estate in separate LLC. Rent to operating
  entity at FMV. Protects real estate from business liabilities. Rental income may qualify
  for §199A deduction separately.

### Reasonable Compensation Documentation (S-Corp)
- IRS factors: training, experience, duties, time devoted, comparable compensation,
  dividend history, use of formula, comparison to non-shareholder employees.
- **Key case**: David E. Watson, P.C. v. United States (668 F.3d 1008, 8th Cir. 2012) —
  CPA paid himself $24,000 salary on $200K+ profits; court found this unreasonable and
  imposed additional employment taxes plus penalties.
- **Documentation**: maintain a compensation study referencing BLS data, industry surveys,
  job postings for comparable positions. Update annually.

## 6. Retirement Plan Strategy

- **Solo 401(k)** — For businesses with no employees (other than owner and spouse). Employee
  deferral + employer contribution. Total up to $70,000 (2025).
- **SEP-IRA** — Simple to establish (up to tax filing deadline). Employer contributions only:
  up to 25% of compensation. Must cover all eligible employees at same percentage.
- **SIMPLE IRA** — For businesses with under 100 employees. Employee deferral up to $16,500.
  Mandatory employer match (up to 3%) or 2% nonelective contribution. Must be established
  by October 1.
- **401(k) plan** — For businesses with employees. More costly to administer but most flexible.
  Safe harbor 401(k) avoids discrimination testing. Employer match or 3% nonelective.
- **Defined benefit plan** — Cash balance plan allows deductions of $280,000+/year for owner.
  Must cover eligible employees. Expensive to administer ($3K-$10K/year). Best for older
  owners with high income and few/no employees.
- **SEP vs. SIMPLE vs. 401(k)** — Key difference: SEP requires same % for all employees;
  SIMPLE has low contribution limits; 401(k) is most flexible but most expensive to maintain.

## 7. Section 199A QBI Deduction

### Non-SSTB Businesses (Retail, Manufacturing, Construction, etc.)
- Eligible for the 20% QBI deduction even at high income, but above the threshold the
  W-2/UBIA limitation can reduce it.
- **Below threshold** ($197,300 single / $394,600 MFJ): 20% of QBI, no further limitation.
- **Above threshold**: limited to the GREATER of:
  - 50% of W-2 wages paid by the business, OR
  - 25% of W-2 wages + 2.5% of UBIA (unadjusted basis immediately after acquisition) of
    qualified property
- **Strategy**: paying W-2 wages (including owner's salary from S-Corp) directly increases
  the QBI limitation. A business with $500K QBI but only $100K in W-2 wages has a QBI
  deduction limited to $50K (50% x $100K), not $100K (20% x $500K).

### SSTB Businesses
- Consulting, law, medicine, accounting, financial services, performing arts, athletics,
  and businesses where principal asset is reputation/skill of employees.
- Phase-out above $197,300/$394,600. Fully eliminated above $247,300/$494,600.

## 8. Common Audit Triggers

- **Cash-intensive businesses** — Restaurants, retail, service businesses. IRS uses bank
  deposit analysis, lifestyle audits. Must document all cash receipts.
- **High Schedule C income** — Net income over $100K on Schedule C increases audit probability.
- **Employee vs. contractor misclassification** — IRS and state agencies actively audit.
  Use 20-factor test (behavioral control, financial control, relationship type). Penalties
  for misclassification: back employment taxes + penalties + interest.
- **Meals and entertainment** — Disproportionate to revenue. Must document: date, place,
  business purpose, attendees, amount. No deduction for entertainment (post-TCJA).
- **Excessive owner compensation (C-Corp)** — IRS challenges unreasonable salary as disguised
  dividend. Opposite problem from S-Corp (where salary is too low).
- **Related-party transactions** — Rent, salary, services between related entities must be
  at fair market value. Arm's-length standard applies.
- **Inventory discrepancies** — Significant year-over-year changes without corresponding
  revenue changes raise questions.

## 9. Profession-Specific Rules

- **Accountable plan** — S-Corp/C-Corp owners can reimburse business expenses through
  accountable plan (documented, business connection, excess returned). Not taxable income.
- **Augusta rule (IRC §280A(g))** — Rent personal home to business for up to 14 days/year.
  Rental income is tax-free to owner. Business gets deduction. Must charge FMV, document
  business purpose, and maintain records. IRS scrutinizes this strategy.
- **Reasonable compensation** — S-Corp owners must pay reasonable salary. See Section 5 above.
- **IRC §195 — Startup costs** — See Section 4 above.

## 10. State Considerations

- **Nexus** — Physical presence or economic activity in a state creates filing obligations.
  Economic nexus thresholds for sales tax (typically $100K sales or 200 transactions
  per South Dakota v. Wayfair, Inc., 585 U.S. ___ (2018)).
- **State entity-level taxes** — CA ($800 minimum), many states impose franchise/privilege tax.
  PTE tax elections in 30+ states bypass the personal SALT cap.
- **Sales tax** — Must collect in states where nexus exists. Different rules for physical
  products vs. SaaS vs. services. Wayfair decision (2018) established economic nexus.
- **Payroll tax** — State unemployment insurance, workers' comp, state disability (CA, NJ, NY,
  HI, RI). Different rates and wage bases per state.
- **Business registration** — Foreign qualification required in states where business operates
  but is not incorporated. Annual reports and fees vary.
- **Income apportionment** — Multi-state businesses apportion income. Most states use
  single sales factor or three-factor formula (sales, payroll, property).

## 11. Worked Example: Retail Business with $500K Revenue

### Scenario: Gift shop, S-Corp, MFJ, 2 part-time employees

| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Gross revenue | $500,000 |
| COGS (inventory, FIFO method) | ($200,000) |
| Gross profit | $300,000 |
| Employee wages (2 PT) | ($50,000) |
| Employer payroll taxes (FICA + FUTA + SUI) | ($5,500) |
| Rent | ($36,000) |
| Utilities, insurance, supplies | ($18,000) |
| Marketing / advertising | ($8,000) |
| Professional services (CPA, attorney) | ($6,000) |
| Equipment depreciation (§179) | ($12,000) |
| Other operating expenses | ($9,500) |
| **Net S-Corp income** | **$155,000** |

### Owner Tax Computation
| Item | Amount |
|------|--------|
| Owner W-2 salary (reasonable compensation) | $70,000 |
| S-Corp distribution | $85,000 |
| Employee FICA on salary (7.65%) | $5,355 |
| Employer FICA on salary (7.65%) | $5,355 |
| Self-employed health insurance deduction | ($15,000) |
| Solo 401(k) employee deferral | ($23,500) |
| Solo 401(k) employer match (25% of $70K) | ($17,500) |
| QBI deduction: 20% x $155,000 = $31,000 | ($31,000) |
| (QBI W-2 limit check: 50% x $120K total W-2 = $60K > $31K: OK) | |
| AGI (approximate) | ~$98,000 |
| Federal income tax (MFJ, est.) | ~$9,800 |
| Total FICA (employer + employee on $70K) | $10,710 |
| **Total federal tax** | **~$20,510** |
| **Effective rate on $155K business income** | **~13.2%** |

### Without S-Corp (Schedule C, sole prop)
- SE tax on $155K: ~$21,900
- No salary/distribution split: full SE tax on all net income
- **S-Corp savings: ~$11,200/year** (offset by ~$3K compliance costs = ~$8,200 net)

## 12. Common Mistakes

1. **Wrong entity choice** — Staying sole prop too long (overpaying SE tax) or choosing
   S-Corp too early (compliance costs exceed tax savings).
2. **Misclassifying employees as contractors** — Penalties include 100% of unpaid employer
   FICA, 1.5% of wages for income tax, plus negligence penalties. Form SS-8 determination.
3. **Missing depreciation deductions** — Not taking Section 179 or bonus depreciation on
   equipment purchases. Once placed in service, must be claimed or lost (for bonus depreciation).
4. **Inadequate recordkeeping** — Must retain receipts, invoices, bank statements for 3-7 years.
   IRS can disallow deductions without substantiation.
5. **Not separating business and personal finances** — Commingling undermines entity protection
   ("piercing the corporate veil") and creates accounting nightmares.
6. **Ignoring estimated tax obligations** — Owner distributions are not subject to withholding.
   Must make quarterly estimated payments or face underpayment penalty.
7. **Overlooking employment tax deposits** — Semi-weekly or monthly deposits required depending
   on liability size. Penalties for late deposits: 2-15% of underpayment.
8. **Not planning for succession** — Buy-sell agreements, key person insurance, entity structure
   all affect tax treatment of business transfer. IRC §754 election for partnerships.
9. **Failing to maximize retirement plan contributions** — Many small business owners
   under-contribute. Solo 401(k) or cash balance plan can shelter $70K-$280K+ annually.
10. **Missing 1099 filing obligations** — Must issue 1099-NEC to all contractors paid $600+.
    Penalties accumulate per form. E-file required for 10+ forms.
11. **Not electing WOTC before hiring** — Form 8850 must be filed within 28 days of hire.
    Cannot claim retroactively for existing employees.
12. **Ignoring UNICAP when required** — Businesses exceeding $29M that fail to capitalize
    indirect costs into inventory face IRS adjustment and back taxes.
