# Truck Driver — Tax Reference Guide (TY 2025)
## Owner-Operators, Company Drivers, Local and OTR

## 1. Overview

Truck drivers split into two main tax categories: company drivers (W-2 employees) and
owner-operators (self-employed, Schedule C). Post-TCJA, company drivers lost most deductions
(unreimbursed employee expenses eliminated federally), while owner-operators have extensive
deduction opportunities. The DOT-regulated 80% meal deduction rate (vs. standard 50%), per
diem rules for away-from-home travel, and equipment depreciation on trucks/trailers are the
defining features of trucker tax planning. Trucking is NOT a specified service trade or
business (SSTB), so the full 20% QBI deduction is available at all income levels.

## 2. Common Income Types

- **W-2 salary** — Company drivers. Includes base pay, mileage pay, safety bonuses, detention
  pay, layover pay. Per diem payments from employer may be shown in Box 12 Code L.
- **1099-NEC** — Owner-operators leased to carriers, independent haulers.
- **1099-K** — From load boards or freight marketplaces (DAT, Uber Freight, etc.).
- **Fuel surcharge income** — Passed through from broker/shipper. Taxable income offset by
  fuel expense deduction.
- **Accessorial charges** — Lumper fees, detention pay, TONU (truck ordered not used).
- **Lease payments** — Lease-operators receive settlements minus deductions (fuel, truck
  lease, insurance). The GROSS settlement is income; deductions are expenses.
- **Per diem from employer** — If employer pays per diem under accountable plan, it's
  tax-free (not on W-2). If non-accountable plan, it appears on W-2 as taxable income.

## 3. Owner-Operator vs. Company Driver — Critical Tax Distinction

### Company Drivers (W-2)
- **Post-TCJA (2018-2025)** — TCJA eliminated unreimbursed employee expenses. Company
  drivers CANNOT deduct meals, uniforms, or equipment on federal return. This was a
  devastating change for company drivers who previously deducted per diem meals.
- **Per diem from employer** — The ONLY way company drivers can benefit from per diem is
  if their employer pays it under an accountable plan. Some carriers offer a choice:
  higher per-mile rate (fully taxable) or lower per-mile rate plus tax-free per diem.
  The per diem option usually results in lower total tax.
- **State deductions** — Some states still allow unreimbursed employee expenses. Company
  drivers in CA, NY, NJ, PA, MN should file state returns claiming meals and uniforms.

### Owner-Operators (Schedule C)
- Full access to all business deductions listed below.
- Subject to self-employment tax on net profit.
- Must make quarterly estimated tax payments.
- Responsible for all business insurance, equipment, and compliance.

## 4. Key Deductions — Owner-Operators

### Per Diem Meals — The 80% Rate (IRC 274(n)(3))

This is the single most valuable profession-specific tax rule for truckers:

- **80% deduction rate** — Transportation workers subject to DOT hours-of-service regulations
  can deduct 80% of meal costs, not the standard 50%. IRC 274(n)(3) provides this enhanced
  rate for individuals during or incident to any period of duty subject to DOT hours of
  service limitations (49 CFR Part 395 for truck drivers).
- **Why 80% instead of 50%** — Congress recognized that DOT-regulated drivers cannot choose
  where or when to eat; they are constrained by hours-of-service rules, mandatory rest
  periods, and route requirements.
- **Federal per diem rate** — $69/day (2025 CONUS standard rate) or actual expenses. Using
  the per diem rate simplifies recordkeeping — no need to keep individual meal receipts.
  Only need to track days away from home.
- **Partial days** — 75% of per diem for travel days (first and last day of trip).
- **When eligible** — Must be away from tax home overnight and sleeping in berth/hotel.
  Local drivers who return home daily do NOT qualify for per diem.
- **Computation methods** — Two approaches:
  1. **Federal per diem rate** — Use the GSA standard rate ($69/day CONUS). No meal receipts
     needed. Just track days away.
  2. **Actual expenses** — Keep every meal receipt. Deduct 80% of actual cost. More
     recordkeeping but potentially higher deduction if eating at expensive locations.
- **Annual impact worked example** — OTR driver away 280 days:
  - Full days: 260 x $69 x 80% = $14,352
  - Partial days (departure/return): 20 x $69 x 75% x 80% = $828
  - Total per diem deduction: $15,180
  This single deduction can reduce federal + SE tax by $4,000-$6,000+.

### Vehicle and Equipment
- **Truck depreciation** — Heavy trucks (over 6,000 lbs GVWR, which all semi-trucks exceed)
  are not subject to luxury auto limits. Full Section 179 ($2,500,000 limit, 2025) or bonus
  depreciation (generally 100% for qualified property acquired and placed in service after
  January 19, 2025; generally 40% for property acquired before January 20, 2025). MACRS 3-year (OTR trucks) or 5-year
  (other trucks) life depending on use.
- **Worked example — truck purchase**: Owner-operator buys a $180,000 used Peterbilt 579.
  Section 179 election: deduct full $180,000 in year one. At 24% marginal rate + 15.3%
  SE rate (combined ~35%), first-year tax savings approximately $63,000. Alternatively,
  use 5-year MACRS to spread deduction across multiple years.
- **Trailer** — Separate depreciable asset. 5-year MACRS life.
- **Lease vs. buy analysis** — Leasing: lease payments are deductible as paid but no
  depreciation or Section 179. Buying: depreciation/179 provides larger upfront deduction
  but requires capital outlay. If expecting high income in purchase year, buying + 179
  is usually superior. If income is uncertain, leasing provides predictable deductions.
- **Truck lease payments** — Deductible as business expense (if leasing from carrier).
- **Fuel** — Largest expense for most owner-operators. Track by receipt or fuel card statements.
- **Maintenance and repairs** — Tires, oil changes, brakes, engine repair. Deductible when paid.
  Tire expenses can be substantial ($300-$600 per tire, 18 tires on a standard rig).
- **Truck wash** — Exterior and interior cleaning. Deductible.
- **Insurance** — Liability, cargo, bobtail, physical damage. Deductible.
- **Truck payments (interest)** — Interest portion of truck loan is deductible.
  Principal is not deductible (recovered through depreciation).

### Technology and Compliance
- **ELD (Electronic Logging Device)** — Required by FMCSA. Purchase or subscription deductible.
- **GPS / navigation** — Dedicated GPS units, subscription services.
- **CB radio** — Deductible business equipment.
- **Dashcam** — Safety equipment, deductible.
- **Communication equipment** — Cell phone (business %), satellite phone for remote areas.
- **Truck scales / weigh station bypass** — PrePass, Drivewyze subscriptions.

### Other Business Expenses
- **CDL costs** — License renewal, endorsements, DOT physical, drug testing. Deductible for
  self-employed. Initial CDL (qualifying for new profession) is not deductible but may
  qualify for Lifetime Learning Credit.
- **Lumper fees** — Fees paid for loading/unloading. Deductible if not reimbursed. Must get
  receipt from lumper service.
- **Tolls and parking** — Business-related tolls and truck parking fees.
- **Sleeper berth expenses** — Bedding, curtains, small appliances for sleeper cab use.
  Deductible as ordinary and necessary business expenses.
- **Chain and safety equipment** — Tire chains, load straps, binders, tarps, safety triangles,
  fire extinguisher. Deductible.
- **Licensing and permits** — IRP (International Registration Plan), IFTA, oversize/overweight
  permits, HUT (Heavy Use Tax, Form 2290).
- **Factoring fees** — If using invoice factoring service. Deductible as business expense.
- **Association dues** — OOIDA (Owner-Operator Independent Drivers Association), TCA.
- **Satellite radio / streaming** — Business-use portion for work-related programming.

### Form 2290 — Heavy Highway Vehicle Use Tax
- **What it is** — Annual federal excise tax on highway motor vehicles with a taxable gross
  weight of 55,000 lbs or more. Tax ranges from $100 to $550 depending on weight.
- **Due date** — August 31 of each year for vehicles used during July.
- **Filing** — IRS Form 2290. Must be filed to obtain stamped Schedule 1, which is required
  for vehicle registration in all states.
- **Deductible** — The tax itself is a deductible business expense on Schedule C.
- **Suspended vehicles** — If a truck is expected to be driven fewer than 5,000 miles
  (7,500 for agricultural vehicles), it can be filed as "suspended" with no tax due.

## 5. IFTA Reporting

- **International Fuel Tax Agreement** — Quarterly fuel tax reporting for interstate carriers.
  IFTA allocates fuel tax among member jurisdictions based on miles driven vs. fuel
  purchased in each state.
- **Filing** — Quarterly returns filed with the base state. Credits for fuel tax paid in
  excess in some states; debits for underpayment in others.
- **Administrative costs** — IFTA license and decal fees, accountant/software costs for
  IFTA filing. All deductible.
- **Recordkeeping** — Must maintain trip records showing miles driven in each state and
  fuel purchase receipts by state. Keep for 4 years.

### Settlement Sheets for Independent Contractors
- **What they are** — Carrier settlement sheets detail gross revenue, deductions for fuel,
  lease payments, insurance, etc., and net pay.
- **Tax treatment** — Report GROSS settlement amount as income. Deduct each line item
  separately. Do NOT just report the net. The gross must match the 1099-NEC from the carrier.

## 6. Recommended Entity Structure

- **Company driver** — W-2 employee. No entity choice.
- **New owner-operator** — Sole proprietor, Schedule C. Low overhead to start.
- **Established owner-operator ($60K+ net)** — LLC with S-Corp election. Pay reasonable salary
  for driving services, take distributions. SE tax savings significant on $80K-$200K+ income.
  Reasonable salary benchmark: comparable to company driver pay ($55K-$85K for OTR).
- **Small fleet (2-5 trucks)** — LLC taxed as S-Corp. Can hire drivers as W-2 employees.
  Payroll obligations begin with first employee.
- **Lease-operator** — Usually sole proprietor or LLC. Entity should receive settlement
  payments and pay all expenses.

## 7. Retirement Plan Strategy

- **Company drivers** — Employer 401(k) if offered. Many carriers offer match. Max $23,500
  ($31,000 if 50+).
- **Owner-operators** — Solo 401(k) is optimal. Employee + employer contributions up to $70,000.
  SEP-IRA alternative is simpler but limits to employer contributions only.
- **Traditional vs. Roth** — Truck drivers typically in moderate tax brackets. Roth
  contributions may be valuable, especially if expecting higher income in retirement
  or if per diem deductions reduce current taxable income significantly.
- **HSA** — If on HDHP: $4,300/$8,550 (2025). Driver health insurance is a significant
  expense; HDHP + HSA can reduce costs.
- **Self-employed health insurance** — Above-the-line deduction for owner-operators.
  Includes driver, spouse, dependents.

## 8. Common Audit Triggers

- **Per diem without away-from-home documentation** — Must document overnight travel.
  IRS audits per diem claims for local drivers who sleep at home.
- **High fuel deductions without records** — Fuel card statements or receipts essential.
  IRS can estimate fuel consumption based on miles driven and compare to claimed deduction.
- **Truck depreciation** — Large first-year deductions on new trucks attract attention.
  Must have placed in service during the tax year claimed.
- **Personal use of truck** — If using truck for any personal driving, must allocate.
  OTR drivers with truck at home between runs should document personal vs. business use.
- **Cash income** — Some owner-operators receive cash payments for loads. Must report all income.
- **Employee misclassification** — Carriers classifying drivers as 1099 when they should be
  W-2. IRS and DOL actively enforce. Key factors: does the carrier control route, schedule,
  and equipment? If yes, the driver is likely an employee.
- **Inconsistent income/expense reporting** — Settlements from carrier should match income
  reported. Carrier reports on 1099; amounts must reconcile.

## 9. Profession-Specific Rules

- **80% meal deduction (IRC 274(n)(3))** — DOT hours-of-service workers can deduct 80%
  (not 50%) of meal costs. Applies to truck drivers, bus drivers, railroad engineers,
  merchant marines, airline pilots. The single most valuable profession-specific tax rule
  for truckers.
- **Form 2290 (Heavy Highway Vehicle Use Tax)** — Annual tax on trucks 55,000+ lbs.
  Rate: $100-$550 depending on weight. Due by August 31. Deductible as business expense.
- **IFTA (International Fuel Tax Agreement)** — Quarterly fuel tax reporting for interstate
  carriers. Credits and debits based on fuel purchased vs. miles driven per state.
- **Section 199A (QBI)** — Trucking is NOT an SSTB. Owner-operators get full 20% QBI
  deduction regardless of income level (W-2/UBIA limits apply above threshold).
- **IRC 179 for heavy vehicles** — No luxury auto limit for trucks over 6,000 lbs GVWR.
  Can expense full purchase price up to $2,500,000 (2025). A $180,000 truck can be
  fully expensed in year one.
- **Away-from-home rules** — Must be away from tax home and need sleep/rest to claim
  per diem. "Tax home" for OTR drivers is typically the area where they pick up/deliver
  most loads or their residential home if they have no regular business location.

## 10. Away-From-Home Rules — Detailed

The per diem deduction hinges entirely on the "away from home" requirement:

- **Tax home definition** — For OTR drivers, the tax home is typically the metropolitan area
  where the driver picks up or delivers most loads, or the driver's residential home if there
  is no regular business location.
- **Sleep or rest rule** — Per diem is only available when the driver is away from their tax
  home long enough to require rest or sleep. A day trip (depart morning, return evening) does
  NOT qualify, even if the driver eats meals on the road.
- **Local drivers** — Drivers who return home daily never qualify for per diem. This includes
  most local delivery, short-haul, and route drivers.
- **Team drivers** — Both team members are "away from home" simultaneously and both can claim
  per diem for the same days, provided both maintain a tax home.
- **Home time** — Days spent at home between runs are NOT per diem days. Only days away from
  home on a run count.
- **Documentation** — Maintain a log showing: date of departure, date of return, destinations,
  and number of full and partial days away. Trip reports, ELD data, and dispatch records
  serve as corroboration.

## 11. QBI Deduction for Owner-Operators

Trucking is NOT a specified service trade or business. This means:

- **No SSTB phaseout** — Unlike doctors, lawyers, and consultants, owner-operators are not
  zeroed out solely because taxable income is high.
- **W-2/UBIA limitation above threshold** — At taxable income above $394,600 (MFJ) / $197,300
  (single), the QBI deduction is limited to the greater of: (a) 50% of W-2 wages paid by
  the business, or (b) 25% of W-2 wages + 2.5% of UBIA of qualified property. Owner-
  operators with expensive depreciable trucks have high UBIA, which preserves the deduction.
- **Worked example** — Owner-operator with $150K net Schedule C income. QBI deduction:
  $150K x 20% = $30,000. At 22% marginal rate, this saves $6,600 in federal tax.
- **S-Corp consideration** — If the owner-operator forms an S-Corp, the salary paid is
  W-2 wages for 199A purposes, which helps satisfy the W-2 wages limitation at higher
  income levels.

## 12. State Considerations

- **IFTA reporting** — Owner-operators must file quarterly IFTA returns reporting miles
  driven and fuel purchased in each state. Fuel tax credits/debits calculated per state.
- **State income tax** — Truck drivers owe tax in state of domicile. Most states do not
  tax OTR drivers on income earned while driving through the state (mobile workforce
  considerations). But some states (NY, CA) may assert nexus.
- **States with no income tax** — TX, FL, WY, NV, TN, SD, AK, WA, NH. Many OTR drivers
  establish domicile in no-tax states.
- **Fuel tax rates** — Vary widely by state. IFTA handles the settlement, but affects
  route planning and overall expense.
- **State-level unreimbursed employee expenses** — Company drivers in states allowing
  these deductions (CA, NY, NJ, PA, MN) should file state returns claiming meals,
  uniforms, and equipment.
- **Oregon weight-mile tax** — Oregon charges per-mile tax instead of fuel tax for heavy
  trucks. Separate from IFTA. Deductible business expense.

## 11. Common Mistakes

1. **Not claiming per diem at 80%** — Many truckers use 50% rate or don't claim per diem
   at all. The 80% rate is specifically for DOT-regulated transportation workers.
2. **Missing per diem for partial days** — Claim 75% rate on departure and return days.
   Many drivers skip these, losing 50+ partial days per year.
3. **Company drivers claiming unreimbursed expenses federally** — Not deductible on federal
   return post-TCJA. However, still deductible in some states.
4. **Not tracking miles and fuel separately** — Must maintain trip logs to substantiate
   fuel expenses and per diem claims.
5. **Ignoring S-Corp election** — Owner-operators netting $80K+ save $5,000-$15,000+ in
   SE tax with S-Corp election. Many continue as sole proprietors unnecessarily.
6. **Lease-operators not reporting gross income** — Must report full settlement amount as
   income, then deduct lease payments, fuel, insurance as expenses. Don't just report net.
7. **Missing Form 2290** — Heavy Highway Vehicle Use Tax is due annually. Failure to file
   means truck registration cannot be renewed.
8. **Not depreciating truck** — New owner-operators sometimes miss Section 179 or MACRS
   depreciation on their truck purchase. This can be the single largest deduction.
9. **Ignoring IFTA credits** — Over-purchasing fuel in high-tax states and under-purchasing
   in low-tax states wastes money. Route planning should consider fuel tax efficiency.
10. **Not separating truck purchase principal from interest** — Only the interest on a truck
    loan is deductible directly. Principal is recovered through depreciation. Some drivers
    deduct the entire loan payment, which is incorrect.
11. **Failing to use annualized method for estimated taxes** — Owner-operators with seasonal
    income patterns (more loads in Q3/Q4 than Q1/Q2) should use the annualized income
    installment method (Form 2210 Schedule AI) to reduce or eliminate underpayment penalties.
12. **Not claiming self-employed health insurance deduction** — Owner-operators who pay for
    their own health insurance (and are not eligible for employer-subsidized coverage through
    a spouse) can deduct 100% of premiums above the line. This includes dental and long-term
    care insurance. Must have net SE income to claim.
13. **Company drivers not checking employer per diem options** — Some carriers offer per diem
    pay that reduces taxable W-2 income. Drivers should ask their employer about per diem
    pay options under an accountable plan.
