# Athlete / Entertainer — Tax Reference Guide (TY 2025)

## 1. Overview

Professional athletes and entertainers face a uniquely challenging tax landscape defined by
multi-state income allocation ("jock tax"), short career windows requiring aggressive savings
strategies, and complex income streams from endorsements, image rights, and licensing. A
professional athlete playing in 15 states may file 15+ state returns in a single year, with
each state claiming a share of income based on "duty days" allocation. Combined with high
marginal rates (37% federal + state), the effective tax rate on a short career's earnings
can exceed 50% without proper planning. This reference covers the critical tax rules for
professional athletes, musicians, actors, and performing artists.

## 2. Multi-State Taxation — The "Jock Tax"

### How It Works
States impose income tax on nonresidents who earn income within their borders. For athletes
and entertainers, income is allocated to each state where games are played or performances
occur using a "duty days" allocation formula.

### Standard Duty Days Formula
```
State-source income = Total compensation × (Duty days in state / Total duty days)
```

### What Counts as a Duty Day
- Game days (home and away)
- Practice days
- Team meetings, film sessions
- Required training camp days
- Preseason and postseason game/practice days
- Travel days (some states include, some exclude)
- **NOT counted**: Off-season voluntary workouts (varies by state), all-star games (some states),
  vacation days, injured reserve days (varies)

### Worked Example: NFL Player
```
Total compensation: $5,000,000
Total duty days: 200 (preseason through regular season + playoffs)

Games in California (2 away games + practices): 12 duty days
CA-source income: $5,000,000 × (12/200) = $300,000
CA tax (13.3% top rate): approximately $39,900

Games in New York (2 away games + practices): 10 duty days
NY-source income: $5,000,000 × (10/200) = $250,000
NY tax (10.9% top rate): approximately $27,250

Home state (Texas — no income tax): remaining duty days
TX tax: $0

Total: Must file in every state where duty days occurred (potentially 10-15 states)
```

### State-Specific Quirks
- **California**: Aggressive enforcement. Includes all mandatory team activities. FTB audits
  athlete returns frequently. *Bindley v. FTB* confirmed broad "duty day" definition.
- **New York**: Counts practice days, travel days arriving in NY. "Convenience of employer"
  rule may apply to some entertainment income.
- **Ohio**: Cleveland imposes a city-level jock tax in addition to state tax.
- **Pennsylvania**: Uses a flat 3.07% rate but has unusual sourcing rules for certain entertainers.
- **Illinois**: Does not impose jock tax on nonresident athletes due to reciprocity considerations
  (but this is not universal — Illinois taxes most nonresident performers).

### Filing Obligations
- Must file a nonresident return in EVERY state where income is earned
- Resident state provides a credit for taxes paid to other states (but credit may not fully
  offset if resident state rate is lower than the nonresident state rate)
- Some states have de minimis thresholds (e.g., 30 days or a minimum income amount before
  filing is required) — but many do NOT for athletes

## 3. Common Income Types

- **Salary and bonuses** — W-2 income from team/studio. Subject to duty-days allocation.
  Signing bonuses: allocation varies by state — some allocate based on contract period,
  others based on duty days in the signing year.
- **Endorsement income** — 1099-NEC from brands. Generally taxable in the state of the
  athlete's domicile unless the endorsement activities (photo shoots, appearances) occur in
  specific states. *If activity occurs in multiple states, allocation required.*
- **Image rights and licensing** — Royalty income (1099-MISC Box 2) vs. service income
  distinction matters:
  - **Royalty**: Taxed where the licensor is domiciled (generally resident state only)
  - **Service income**: Taxed where the services are performed
  - Structure matters: separate image rights entity can shift income characterization.
    *But see* IRS scrutiny of personal service income recharacterized as royalties.
- **Appearance fees** — Ordinary income, taxed in the state where the appearance occurs.
- **Prize money** — For individual sport athletes (tennis, golf, boxing): ordinary income,
  taxed in the state where the event occurs.
- **Deferred compensation** — Subject to §409A. Common in MLB (deferred salary arrangements).
  Bobby Bonilla's famous deferred compensation deal with the Mets is a §409A-compliant example.

## 4. Agent and Manager Commissions

- **Agent commissions** (typically 3-10% for athletes, 10% for actors): Deductible as a
  business expense if the athlete is self-employed (Schedule C) or as a reduction of
  business income. Must issue 1099-NEC to the agent.
- **Manager commissions** (typically 10-20%): Same treatment. Deductible, must issue 1099.
- **Business manager/financial advisor fees**: Deductible if related to business income.
  Investment management fees are NOT deductible post-TCJA (were 2% miscellaneous itemized).
- **Legal fees**: Deductible to the extent they relate to earning income or tax advice.
  Fees for contract negotiation = business expense.

## 5. Key Deductions

### For Self-Employed Athletes/Entertainers (Schedule C)
- **Training and coaching** — Personal trainers, sport-specific coaches, vocal coaches, acting coaches
- **Equipment** — Sport-specific gear, musical instruments, costumes/wardrobe (if exclusively
  for performance — not dual-use everyday clothing). §179 for major equipment purchases.
- **Travel** — Away-from-home expenses: airfare, hotels, meals (50% for meals). Per diem
  rates available for certain entertainment industry employees.
- **Agent/manager commissions** — See above
- **Health and medical** — Training-related medical expenses, sports medicine, physical therapy
  (if business-related). Self-employed health insurance deduction (above the line) if not
  covered by a spouse's employer plan.
- **Union dues** — SAG-AFTRA, AEA, AFM, players' association dues. Deductible on Schedule C
  for self-employed performers; NOT deductible for W-2 employees post-TCJA.
- **Home gym/studio** — If exclusive use for business (difficult to prove for athletes who
  also use it personally)

### Performing Artist Deduction (§62(a)(2)(B)) — Special Rule
A rare above-the-line deduction for W-2 performing artists who meet ALL requirements:
1. Performed services as an employee for at least 2 employers
2. Received at least $200 from each of 2 employers
3. Business expenses exceed 10% of gross income from performing arts
4. AGI does not exceed $16,000 (NOT indexed — this threshold has been unchanged since 1986)
- **Practical impact**: Almost no professional athlete or successful entertainer qualifies due
  to the $16,000 AGI limit. Congress has proposed increases but none enacted.

## 6. Short Career Planning

### Income Bunching Problem
Athletes and entertainers earn the vast majority of career income in a compressed window
(5-15 years for most athletes, variable for entertainers). This pushes income into the highest
brackets every earning year, with little opportunity for income smoothing.

### Strategies
- **Maximize retirement plan contributions**: Solo 401(k) for self-employed athletes/entertainers
  (up to $70,000 for 2025). Team-employed athletes: max 401(k) at $23,500 + employer match.
- **Defined benefit plans**: For self-employed athletes/entertainers with consistent high income,
  a defined benefit plan can shelter $200,000-$300,000+ per year (depending on age and plan design).
  *Particularly valuable for athletes over 30 with short remaining careers.*
- **Deferred compensation**: Some leagues allow salary deferral (must comply with §409A).
  Defer income to post-career, lower-bracket years.
- **Roth conversions post-career**: Convert traditional retirement accounts to Roth in
  low-income years immediately after retirement (before broadcasting/coaching careers begin).
- **Qualified Opportunity Zone funds**: Invest capital gains in QOZ funds for deferral and
  potential partial exclusion.

## 7. Trust and Estate Planning

### Short-Earning-Window Trusts
- **GRAT (Grantor Retained Annuity Trust)**: Transfer appreciating assets (endorsement contracts,
  image rights entity interests) to a GRAT. If assets appreciate faster than the §7520 rate,
  excess passes to beneficiaries gift-tax-free. Short-term GRATs (2-year rolling) are common.
- **SLAT (Spousal Lifetime Access Trust)**: Irrevocable trust for the benefit of the spouse.
  Uses lifetime gift tax exemption ($13.99M for 2025). Assets grow outside the estate. Spouse
  can access funds, providing a financial safety net.
- **ILIT (Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust)**: Owns life insurance on the athlete's life.
  Death benefit passes outside the estate. Useful for athletes in high-injury-risk sports.
- **Dynasty trusts**: In states with no rule against perpetuities (NV, SD, DE, AK),
  create multi-generational wealth transfer.

## 8. Injury and Insurance

### Disability Insurance Proceeds
- **Employer-paid premiums**: Benefits are taxable as ordinary income.
- **Employee/self-paid premiums (after-tax dollars)**: Benefits are tax-free. §104(a)(3).
- **Strategy**: Pay disability insurance premiums with after-tax dollars to ensure tax-free
  benefits. For a $5M/year athlete, the difference between taxable and tax-free disability
  payments is enormous.
- **Lloyd's of London policies**: Common for athletes. Tax treatment follows the premium-payer rule.

### Workers' Compensation
- Tax-free regardless of who paid the premiums. §104(a)(1).

### Injury Settlements
- **Physical injury**: Tax-free under §104(a)(2). Must be "on account of" physical injury or sickness.
- **Lost wages/career earnings**: If part of a physical injury settlement = tax-free.
  If separate from physical injury (e.g., breach of contract, defamation) = taxable.
- **Punitive damages**: Always taxable. §104(a) flush language.

## 9. Team-Provided Benefits

### Potentially Taxable
- **Housing and meal allowances**: Generally included in income unless §119 applies (meals
  on employer premises for employer's convenience, lodging required as condition of employment).
- **Per diem for road trips**: If within IRS substantiation amounts and properly accounted
  for, per diem is tax-free. Amounts exceeding IRS rates are taxable.
- **Personal use of team facilities**: May be a taxable fringe benefit.
- **Moving expenses**: Taxable (no longer excludable post-TCJA except for active military).
  Teams often gross up for tax.

### Generally Non-Taxable
- **Team-provided meals during mandatory team activities**: §119 exclusion may apply if
  on employer premises for employer convenience.
- **Athletic training and medical care**: Excludable as working condition fringe benefit. §132(d).
- **Qualified transportation fringe**: Up to $325/month for parking (2025). §132(f).

## 10. Residency Planning

### No-Income-Tax States
Strategic domicile selection can save millions over a career:
- **Florida**: No state income tax. Popular for athletes (Miami, Tampa, Jacksonville teams).
  Must establish genuine domicile — FL driver's license, voter registration, homestead exemption.
- **Texas**: No state income tax. Dallas, Houston, San Antonio teams.
- **Tennessee**: No state income tax (dividends/interest tax fully repealed 2021). Nashville teams.
- **Nevada**: No state income tax. Las Vegas teams.
- **Washington**: No income tax, but 7% capital gains tax on gains >$270,000. Seattle teams.

### Residency Audit Risk
High-profile athletes and entertainers are prime targets for state residency audits:
- **California**: FTB is notorious for pursuing departing athletes. "Closer connection" test
  examines: where family lives, where driver's license is issued, voter registration, bank
  accounts, club memberships, vehicle registrations, social ties. *Hyatt v. FTB* is a
  cautionary tale about aggressive CA audit practices.
- **New York**: NYSDTF applies a similar multi-factor test. Maintaining a NYC apartment (even
  if rarely used) can trigger resident status.
- **Clean break checklist**: Change license, voter registration, bank accounts; sell or lease
  the prior-state home; move family; establish social ties in new state; keep detailed calendars
  of physical presence.

## 11. International Athletes and Entertainers

### Nonresident Aliens Performing in the US
- Subject to 30% withholding on US-source income (FDAP — Fixed, Determinable, Annual, Periodic).
  Reported on Form 1042-S.
- **Treaty benefits**: Many US tax treaties reduce the withholding rate or provide exemptions
  for athletes/entertainers below certain income thresholds.
  - Example: US-UK treaty Article 17 allows full US taxation of performer income but provides
    credit in the UK.
- Must file Form 1040-NR to report US-source income and claim treaty benefits.
- **Central withholding agreements** (CWAs): Artists and athletes can negotiate reduced
  withholding with the IRS (Form 13930) based on projected deductions and expenses.

### US Athletes Performing Abroad
- Worldwide income is taxable to US citizens/residents.
- **Foreign tax credit** (§901) available for taxes paid to foreign countries. Form 1116.
- **Foreign Earned Income Exclusion** (§911): Generally NOT available for athletes returning
  to the US frequently (fails physical presence or bona fide residence test).
- Track foreign taxes paid in each country and claim appropriate credits.

## 12. NFT and Digital Collectible Income

### Primary Sale of Self-Created NFTs
Athletes and entertainers increasingly receive income from NFT (non-fungible token) sales —
digital art, highlight clips, music tracks, virtual collectibles, and similar digital assets.

- **Primary sale income**: When an athlete or entertainer creates and sells an NFT, the proceeds
  are **ordinary income** (self-created property — see Section 14 below on §1221(3)(A)). Reported
  on Schedule C if the creator is self-employed, or as other income if a one-off transaction.
- **Secondary sale royalties**: Many NFT smart contracts include a royalty provision (typically
  5-10%) paid to the original creator on each secondary market resale. These royalties are also
  **ordinary income** — they are payments for the use of the creator's intellectual property.

### 28% Collectible Capital Gains Rate
- For NFT purchasers (investors who buy and resell NFTs): the IRS has proposed that digital art
  and similar NFTs may be classified as "collectibles" under IRC §408(m). IRS Notice 2023-27
  indicated the IRS intends to treat certain NFTs as collectibles.
- **Collectible capital gains rate**: 28% maximum (vs. 20% for standard long-term capital gains).
  This higher rate applies to investors who hold NFTs for >1 year and sell at a gain.
- **Practical impact for creators**: The collectible rate is largely irrelevant for the creator
  because the gain is ordinary income regardless (self-created property). It matters for
  athletes/entertainers who *purchase* other people's NFTs as investments.

### Platform Reporting
- NFT platforms (OpenSea, Rarible, Foundation, NBA Top Shot) may issue 1099-K or 1099-MISC
  for sales exceeding reporting thresholds. However, reporting is inconsistent — the taxpayer
  is responsible for reporting all income regardless of whether a 1099 is received.
- Track cost basis, gas fees (Ethereum transaction costs), and platform commissions — these
  reduce the taxable gain.

## 13. Music Catalog Sales

### Characterization: Ordinary Income vs. Capital Gain
The sale of music catalogs (publishing rights, master recordings, song catalogs) has become
a significant liquidity event for musicians and their estates. The tax treatment depends
critically on whether the seller *created* the works or *purchased* them:

- **Creator sells catalog**: Under IRC §1221(3)(A), literary, musical, or artistic compositions
  created by the taxpayer's personal efforts are explicitly excluded from the definition of
  "capital asset." Gain on sale is **ordinary income** taxed at up to 37%.
  - **TCJA musical composition election**: The Tax Cuts and Jobs Act of 2017 added
    §1221(b)(3), allowing taxpayers to elect to treat the sale of self-created musical
    compositions (and copyrights in musical works) as the sale of a capital asset. This
    election was made **permanent** and applies per-sale (the taxpayer can elect on a
    composition-by-composition basis). If elected, gain is long-term capital gain (20% maximum
    rate) if held >1 year.
  - **This election is enormously valuable**: A $200M catalog sale at 37% ordinary = $74M tax.
    At 20% LTCG + 3.8% NIIT = $47.6M tax. Savings: $26.4M.
- **Purchaser sells catalog**: If the seller acquired the catalog by purchase (not personal
  creation), the catalog is a capital asset (or §1231 property if used in a trade or business
  for >1 year). Gain is capital gain — either §1231 gain (long-term capital gain treatment
  if held >1 year in a trade or business) or standard LTCG.
- **Estates and heirs**: If the original creator dies and the estate or heirs sell the catalog,
  the stepped-up basis under §1014 applies. The heir's basis is FMV at date of death. Whether
  the gain is ordinary or capital depends on whether the heir is treated as the "creator" —
  generally, heirs are NOT treated as creators, so the sale may qualify for capital gain
  treatment even without the §1221(b)(3) election.

### Section 1231 Treatment
- If the catalog was held for use in a trade or business for more than one year (e.g., a
  publishing company actively licensing the catalog), §1231 applies. Net §1231 gains are
  treated as long-term capital gains; net §1231 losses are treated as ordinary losses.
- This is the best of both worlds: capital gain rates on profits, ordinary loss treatment
  on losses.

### Installment Sale Considerations
- Large catalog sales may benefit from installment sale treatment (IRC §453) to spread
  the income over multiple years, staying in lower brackets or managing NIIT/IRMAA thresholds.
- However, if the sale is for ordinary income property (self-created, no §1221(b)(3) election),
  the installment method is still available but ALL gain is ordinary income regardless of timing.

## 14. Section 1221(3)(A) — Self-Created Works

### The Rule
IRC §1221(a)(3) excludes from the definition of "capital asset" any:
> "copyright, a literary, musical, or artistic composition, a letter or memorandum, or
> similar property, held by a taxpayer whose personal efforts created such property"

This means gain on the sale of self-created works is **ordinary income**, not capital gain.

### Who This Affects
- **Musicians** selling songs, albums, or catalogs they wrote/performed
- **Authors** selling manuscripts, book rights, or screenplay rights they wrote
- **Artists** selling original paintings, sculptures, or digital art they created
- **Athletes** selling self-created content (autobiographies, highlight compilations, personal brand IP)
- **Comedians** selling joke books, special rights, or content they created

### The Musical Composition Election (§1221(b)(3))
- **Made permanent by TCJA**: Taxpayers can elect to treat the sale or exchange of musical
  compositions or copyrights in musical works created by the taxpayer's personal efforts as
  the sale of a capital asset.
- **Election mechanics**: Made on the return for the year of the sale. Irrevocable once made
  for that specific property. Can be made selectively — elect for one song, not another.
- **Limitation**: This election applies ONLY to musical compositions and copyrights in musical
  works. It does NOT apply to literary works, visual art, screenplays, or other self-created
  property. Authors and visual artists cannot use this election.

### Planning Implications
- Musicians should almost always make the §1221(b)(3) election when selling catalogs or
  individual compositions held >1 year — the rate differential (37% vs. 23.8%) is enormous.
- Visual artists and authors have no equivalent election — their self-created works remain
  ordinary income property. Strategies include: selling through an entity (limited effectiveness),
  installment sales to manage brackets, or charitable donation of appreciated self-created
  works (deduction limited to basis under §170(e)(1)(A) for ordinary income property).
- Consider the charitable donation trap: donating self-created art to charity only yields a
  deduction equal to the artist's cost basis (typically very low — cost of canvas and paint),
  NOT the FMV, because it is ordinary income property.

## 15. Retirement Transition Planning

- **Post-career income sources**: Broadcasting, coaching, endorsements, business ventures.
  Plan for the income gap between active career and post-career income.
- **Roth conversion window**: The years between retirement from playing and starting a
  broadcasting/business career may be the lowest-income years of the athlete's life. Convert
  traditional IRA/401(k) to Roth during this window.
- **Social Security**: Athletes employed by teams earn Social Security credits. 40 credits
  (10 years) needed for full eligibility. Short-career athletes may not have enough credits.
  Self-employment income also earns credits.
- **Pension plans**: NFL, MLB, NBA, NHL all have pension plans with varying vesting requirements.
  Benefits are taxable when received. Some plans offer lump-sum distributions (NUA opportunity
  for employer stock, if applicable).

## 16. Common Mistakes

1. **Failing to file in every required state** — Underfiling nonresident returns triggers
   penalty, interest, and potential fraud charges in aggressive states.
2. **Not tracking duty days contemporaneously** — Reconstructing duty days after the fact is
   unreliable and weak in an audit. Use a calendar system from day one.
3. **Mischaracterizing endorsement income** — Calling service income "royalties" to avoid
   multi-state allocation invites IRS and state challenge.
4. **Paying disability premiums with pre-tax dollars** — Makes the benefits fully taxable.
   The premium savings are minimal compared to the tax-free benefit.
5. **Not maximizing retirement savings during high-earning years** — Career earnings of $20M+
   without tax-deferred savings can leave an athlete with an enormous lifetime tax bill.
6. **Ignoring signing bonus allocation** — States allocate signing bonuses differently. Some
   use duty-day allocation over the contract period; others tax it all in the signing year.
   Must analyze each state's rules.
7. **Residency planning without execution** — Claiming FL domicile while keeping a CA home,
   CA driver's license, and CA social ties is an audit guaranteed to lose.
8. **Forgetting to issue 1099s to agents** — IRS matches 1099s. Agent deductions without
   corresponding 1099 issuance create audit risk.
