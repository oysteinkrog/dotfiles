# Home Purchase and Sale Tax Reference (Tax Year 2025)

## Overview

Buying and selling a home involves significant tax considerations, from the mortgage interest deduction and property tax treatment to the capital gains exclusion on sale. Understanding points deductions, closing cost treatment, and basis tracking can save thousands of dollars. The rules differ based on whether the home is a primary residence, second home, or rental/investment property.

## Home Purchase Tax Implications

### Mortgage Interest Deduction (IRC §163(h)(3))
- **Acquisition debt limit**: interest deductible on up to $750,000 of mortgage debt ($375,000 MFS) for loans originated after December 15, 2017
- **Grandfathered debt**: loans originated on or before December 15, 2017 retain the $1,000,000 limit ($500,000 MFS)
- **Refinanced grandfathered debt**: retains grandfathered status up to the refinanced balance (not new cash-out amounts). The cash-out portion above the original loan balance is subject to the $750K limit.
- Applies to primary residence AND one second home (combined limit)
- Report on Schedule A as itemized deduction; mortgage servicer provides Form 1098

### Home Equity Loan Interest (IRC §163(h)(3)(B)(ii))
- **TCJA change**: home equity loan interest is deductible ONLY if proceeds are used to "buy, build, or substantially improve" the home securing the loan
- Previously (pre-2018): deductible for any purpose up to $100K of home equity debt
- **Example**: $50K HELOC used for kitchen renovation = deductible. $50K HELOC used to pay off credit cards or fund a business = NOT deductible.
- The use of proceeds determines deductibility, not the label of the loan. A "home equity loan" used for home improvement is deductible; a "mortgage refinance" with cash-out used for non-home purposes is not deductible on the cash-out portion.

### HELOC Deductibility
- Same rule as home equity loans: deductible only if funds used for buy/build/improve
- **Tracing rule**: if HELOC draws are used for mixed purposes (some home improvement, some personal), you must trace the proceeds to specific expenditures
- Keep records of how HELOC funds were spent — bank statements showing transfers from HELOC to contractor payments, building supply purchases, etc.
- Interest is reported on Form 1098 by the lender regardless of deductibility; taxpayer must determine the deductible portion

### Points (Loan Origination Fees) — Complete Rules

#### Points on Purchase of Primary Residence (All Pub 936 Conditions)
Points are **fully deductible in the year paid** if ALL of the following are met:
1. The loan is secured by the taxpayer's **primary residence**
2. Charging points is an **established business practice** in the geographic area
3. The points paid are **not excessive** for the area (typically 1-3% of loan amount)
4. The taxpayer uses the **cash method** of accounting
5. The points are **not paid for items normally stated separately** on the settlement statement (appraisal fees, inspection fees, property taxes, title fees, attorney fees)
6. The funds provided at closing (down payment, other closing costs) are **at least equal** to the points charged (points cannot be paid from loan proceeds)
7. The points are computed as a **percentage of the mortgage principal**
8. The amount is clearly designated as **points** on the settlement statement

If ANY condition fails: points must be amortized over the life of the loan.

#### Seller-Paid Points
- Buyer can deduct seller-paid points (reduces buyer's basis by the same amount)
- Reported as a deduction to the buyer even though the seller paid them
- Common in buyer's markets where sellers offer concessions

#### Points on Refinance
- Points paid on a refinance must be **amortized over the life of the new loan**
- Example: $3,000 in points on a 30-year refinance = $100/year ($8.33/month)
- **Exception**: if the refinance replaces a prior refinance, any remaining unamortized points from the old loan are deductible in full in the year the old loan is paid off

#### Refinance for Home Improvement
- If a refinance is used partly to improve the home, the portion of points allocable to the improvement may be deductible in the year paid (same rules as purchase points)
- Example: refinance for $300K; $100K is cash-out for a new addition. One-third of points ($1,000 of $3,000) may be deductible currently; remaining $2,000 amortized over 30 years.

### Property Tax Deduction
- Deductible on Schedule A as part of state and local taxes (SALT)
- **SALT cap**: total state/local income tax (or sales tax) + property tax is subject to the
  current personal SALT cap (generally $40,000, or $20,000 if MFS, for TY 2025)
- Deductible in the year paid (or withheld from escrow)
- Only ad valorem (based on assessed value) taxes qualify; special assessments for improvements generally do not
- Transfer taxes at closing: not deductible but may be added to basis

### Property Tax Pro-Rating at Closing
- Property taxes are allocated between buyer and seller based on the **closing date**
- Seller is responsible for taxes from Jan 1 (or assessment date) through the day BEFORE closing
- Buyer is responsible from the closing date through Dec 31
- This allocation appears on the closing disclosure/HUD-1
- **Buyer's deduction**: only the buyer's share is deductible on Schedule A (subject to SALT cap)
- **Seller's deduction**: seller deducts their share on their Schedule A for the year of sale
- If taxes were prepaid by seller: buyer reimburses seller at closing; that reimbursement is added to buyer's basis

### PMI (Private Mortgage Insurance) Deduction (IRC §163(h)(3)(E))
- PMI deduction has historically been extended by Congress but lapses periodically
- For 2025: check current law at filing time; has been an annual extenders item
- If available: deductible on Schedule A
- **Income phase-out**: AGI between $100,000-$109,000 (phases out ratably; zero above $109K)
- Applies to policies on acquisition debt for primary residence
- VA funding fees and FHA mortgage insurance premiums are treated as PMI for deduction purposes

### Closing Costs Treatment

**Deductible in year of purchase:**
- Mortgage interest (from closing date to end of month — the "prepaid interest" line on the closing disclosure)
- Property taxes (prorated from closing date — buyer's share)
- Points (on primary residence purchase, if all conditions met)

**Added to basis (recovered when you sell):**
- Title insurance and title search fees
- Survey costs
- Appraisal fees (if paid by buyer)
- Transfer taxes and recording fees
- Attorney fees related to purchase
- Inspection fees
- Abstract fees

**Not deductible and NOT added to basis:**
- Homeowner's insurance premiums (including escrow prepayment)
- HOA fees (including transfer fees to HOA)
- Prepaid interest reserves held in escrow beyond first month
- Mortgage insurance premiums held in escrow (separate from PMI deduction above)
- Credit report fees
- Loan application fees (distinct from origination points)

## Home Sale Tax Implications

### Section 121 Exclusion — Complete Mechanics (IRC §121)

#### Full Exclusion Requirements
- **Exclude up to $250,000 of gain (single) / $500,000 (MFJ)**
- **Ownership test**: owned the home for at least 2 of the 5 years before the sale
- **Use test**: lived in the home as primary residence for at least 2 of the 5 years before the sale
- **Frequency**: have not used the exclusion in the prior 2 years
- The 2 years do NOT have to be consecutive (24 months total within the 5-year lookback)
- MFJ $500,000 exclusion: both spouses must meet the use test; only one needs to meet the ownership test

### Partial Exclusion (IRC §121(c))
If you do not meet the full 2-year requirements due to:
- **Job change**: employment at a new location at least 50 miles farther from the home than the old employment location
- **Health condition**: physician-recommended move due to illness, injury, or condition of the taxpayer, spouse, or certain family members
- **Unforeseen circumstances**: death, divorce/separation, multiple births from same pregnancy, job loss, inability to pay mortgage, involuntary conversion (disaster, condemnation), and certain other IRS-approved circumstances (Reg. §1.121-3(e))

**Prorated exclusion**: (months of qualified use / 24 months) x $250,000/$500,000

**Example**: lived in home for 14 months, then relocated for work.
Partial exclusion = 14/24 x $250,000 = $145,833 (single)

### Section 121 + Rental History — Nonqualified Use (IRC §121(d)(6))

When a home is converted from rental to primary residence (or vice versa):
- **Nonqualified use**: any period after December 31, 2008, during which the property was NOT used as the taxpayer's principal residence (with exceptions for temporary absence, deployment)
- **Allocation**: the portion of gain attributable to nonqualified use periods CANNOT be excluded
- **Formula**: (nonqualified use periods / total ownership period) x total gain = non-excludable gain
- **Exception**: nonqualified use AFTER the last date the home was used as a primary residence is NOT counted as nonqualified use. Only periods BEFORE the primary-residence use matter.
- **Depreciation recapture is always taxed**: even if the gain is under $500K and would otherwise be fully excluded, any depreciation taken (or allowed) during the rental period is recaptured at 25% and CANNOT be excluded under §121

**Example**: bought home in 2015, rented it 2015-2019 (5 years), lived in it 2020-2025 (6 years), sold in 2025.
- Ownership: 10 years. Nonqualified use before primary residence use: 5 years (2015-2019 rental).
- 5/10 = 50% of gain is non-excludable (but still eligible for LTCG rate)
- The other 50% is excludable under §121
- ALL depreciation taken during 2015-2019 is recaptured at 25% regardless

### Calculating Gain on Sale
```
Sale price
- Selling expenses (agent commissions, closing costs, staging, repairs for sale)
= Amount realized

Amount realized
- Adjusted basis
= Gain (or loss)

Adjusted basis = Original purchase price
+ Closing costs added to basis at purchase
+ Capital improvements during ownership
- Depreciation taken or allowed (if any portion was rented or used for business)
- Casualty loss deductions taken
- Energy credits that reduced basis (§25C, §25D credits with basis reduction requirements)
```

### Improvement vs. Repair for Basis Tracking
- **Improvements** (add to basis): additions (rooms, deck, garage), remodels (kitchen, bathroom), new roof, HVAC replacement, new windows, landscaping, driveway, pool, solar panels, new appliances that are built-in (not freestanding), rewiring, replumbing
- **Repairs** (do not add to basis): patching a roof, fixing a faucet, repainting (unless part of a larger improvement project), replacing a broken window pane
- **Rule of thumb**: if it adds value, prolongs useful life, or adapts to new use = improvement. If it maintains existing condition = repair.
- Track cumulatively over decades of ownership. A $50K kitchen remodel from 15 years ago still reduces your gain on sale.

### Casualty Loss on Residence (Post-TCJA)
- Under TCJA (2018-2025): casualty losses on personal-use property are deductible ONLY if attributable to a **federally declared disaster** (§165(h)(5))
- No deduction for theft, fire (unless in a declared disaster zone), or other non-disaster casualties
- If deductible: reduces basis of the home by the amount of the loss deduction taken
- Insurance reimbursement reduces the deductible loss

### Section 121 Special Situations
- **Converted rental property**: must meet 2-of-5-year use test; see nonqualified use rules above
- **Surviving spouse**: can use $500,000 exclusion in the year of death (if filing MFJ that year); $250,000 exclusion in subsequent years
- **Divorce**: if one spouse retains the home, the time the other spouse owned it counts toward the ownership test (under §121(d)(3)). If a spouse is granted use under a divorce instrument, that spouse's use counts for the use test even without ownership.
- **Military and government service**: can suspend the 5-year lookback period for up to 10 years of qualified official extended duty (effective 15-year lookback)
- **Home office within the home** (not a separate structure): the entire gain may qualify for exclusion, but **depreciation recapture still applies** on the home office portion. The recapture is taxed at 25% even if the overall gain is within the exclusion amount.
- **Home office as separate structure**: gain allocable to the separate structure does NOT qualify for §121 exclusion; taxed as capital gain on the allocated amount.

## Worked Example: Buy $600K, Sell for $900K

### Facts
- Purchased in 2017 for $600,000 (closing costs added to basis: $8,000)
- Used as home office 15% of square footage for 3 years (2019-2021)
- Made $50,000 in capital improvements over 8 years
- Depreciation on home office portion: $30,000 total (15% x building value x 3 years)
- Sold in 2025 for $900,000 (selling expenses: $54,000 commission + $6,000 closing = $60,000)
- MFJ, both lived there 8 years

### Computation
```
Sale price:                                        $900,000
- Selling expenses:                                ($60,000)
= Amount realized:                                 $840,000

Original purchase price:                           $600,000
+ Closing costs at purchase:                        $8,000
+ Capital improvements:                            $50,000
- Depreciation taken (home office):               ($30,000)
= Adjusted basis:                                  $628,000

Total gain: $840,000 - $628,000 =                  $212,000
```

### Exclusion Analysis
- **§121 exclusion available**: $500,000 (MFJ, both meet use test)
- **Gain of $212,000** is UNDER the $500,000 limit
- **BUT**: $30,000 of depreciation recapture CANNOT be excluded under §121
- Depreciation recapture taxed at 25%: $30,000 x 25% = $7,500 tax
- Remaining gain ($212,000 - $30,000 = $182,000): fully excluded under §121

### Result
- **Tax on sale**: $7,500 (depreciation recapture only)
- **Without the home office depreciation**: $0 tax (entire $212K excluded)
- This illustrates why home office depreciation, while beneficial during use, has a recapture cost at sale

## Optimization Strategies

1. **Time the sale to meet the 2-year use test**: even a few extra months of residence can qualify you for the full exclusion
2. **Maximize basis with documented improvements**: every dollar of basis reduces taxable gain. Keep ALL receipts, contracts, permits, and photos.
3. **Coordinate with spouse for $500,000 exclusion**: both must meet the use test; plan accordingly if one spouse moved out
4. **Consider selling before renting**: if considering renting your home first, remember that nonqualified use (post-2008) reduces the exclusion
5. **Deduct points in the purchase year**: do not miss this deduction on a new primary home purchase
6. **Refinance strategy**: when refinancing, amortize new points but deduct remaining old points immediately
7. **Installment sale**: if gain exceeds exclusion amount, consider installment sale (§453) to spread recognition
8. **Harvest the exclusion**: the $250K/$500K exclusion resets every 2 years; some taxpayers serially renovate and sell primary residences tax-free
9. **HELOC for improvements only**: ensure HELOC proceeds are used for buy/build/improve to preserve interest deductibility. Document the use of every draw.
10. **Track PMI eligibility**: even though the deduction lapses periodically, in years it's available the AGI phase-out ($100K-$109K) makes it valuable for moderate-income buyers

## Common Mistakes

1. **Not keeping closing documents** — the closing disclosure/HUD-1 is essential for basis calculation; keep for life of ownership + 3 years after sale
2. **Forgetting to track improvements** — a $50K kitchen remodel 15 years ago can be forgotten but still reduces gain. Maintain a running log.
3. **Assuming all home sale gains are tax-free** — the exclusion has limits ($250K/$500K) and requirements (2-of-5 years). Gains above the exclusion are taxed.
4. **Not deducting points in the purchase year** — often overlooked, especially by first-time buyers. One of the most commonly missed deductions.
5. **Double-counting property taxes** — escrow payments are not when the tax is deductible; the actual payment to the taxing authority is. The closing disclosure shows the allocation.
6. **Ignoring depreciation recapture** — if you had a home office or rented part of the home, depreciation recapture is taxed at 25% and CANNOT be excluded under §121
7. **Not reporting the sale** — even if fully excluded, the sale may appear on Form 1099-S; IRS may send a notice if not reported. Best practice: report on Schedule D with exclusion noted.
8. **Deducting HELOC interest used for non-home purposes** — under TCJA, only interest on proceeds used for buy/build/improve is deductible
9. **Missing the partial exclusion** — if you moved for work, health, or unforeseen circumstances before 2 years, you still get a prorated exclusion. Don't assume you get nothing.
10. **Not allocating basis between land and improvements** — only the building depreciates, and land/building split matters for depreciation (home office or rental) and for certain basis calculations

## Additional Special Situations

### Home Converted from Rental to Primary Residence
- Must live in the home for at least 2 of the 5 years before sale to qualify for §121
- Nonqualified use periods (rental years after 2008) reduce the excludable gain proportionally
- ALL depreciation taken during rental period is recaptured at 25% regardless of exclusion
- Strategy: convert rental to primary residence, live in it for 2+ years, then sell.
  The non-rental portion of gain is excludable.

### Home Converted from Primary Residence to Rental
- Basis for depreciation = LESSER of FMV at conversion or adjusted basis
- If home has declined in value: basis is reduced to FMV (loss on personal use is not deductible)
- The 5-year lookback for §121 starts from the date of sale, not date of conversion
- Strategy: if converting to rental, sell within 3 years of moving out to stay within the
  5-year window (2 years of use + up to 3 years of rental = 5-year lookback)

### 1031 Exchange into Primary Residence
- After a §1031 exchange, the replacement property cannot qualify for §121 exclusion until
  you have OWNED it for at least 5 years (§121(d)(5)(B))
- Even then, gain attributable to the deferred §1031 gain is NOT excludable
- This prevents using §1031 to convert investment property gain into §121 exclusion

### Divorce and Home Sale
- §1041 transfer: transfer of home between spouses (or ex-spouses incident to divorce) is
  tax-free. Transferee takes over transferor's basis.
- If one spouse remains in the home: they can use the other spouse's ownership period toward
  the 2-year ownership test (§121(d)(3))
- If the divorce decree grants exclusive use to one spouse: that spouse's use satisfies the
  use test even without ownership
- Sale after divorce: each qualifying ex-spouse can exclude up to $250,000 of their share of gain

## State Considerations

- **State capital gains on home sale**: most states follow federal §121 exclusion, but some have differences
- **California**: generally conforms to §121; capital gains taxed at regular income rates (up to 13.3%). Prop 13 limits property tax reassessment except upon transfer (Prop 19 allows seniors to transfer base-year value within CA).
- **Transfer taxes**: vary significantly by state/county:
  - NYC: combined transfer tax of up to 2.625% for properties over $500K; mansion tax (1% on $1M+)
  - CA: county transfer tax ~$1.10/$1,000 + some cities add more (SF: $6.80/$1,000 for >$250K)
  - Many states: $1-$5 per $1,000 of sale price
  - Some states/cities impose buyer transfer taxes as well (e.g., NYC mortgage recording tax)
- **Property tax reassessment**: some states reassess upon sale (affects ongoing costs, not income tax). CA Prop 13 caps increases at 2%/year until transfer.
- **Mortgage recording tax**: NY, FL, and other states charge taxes on the mortgage itself at recording (NY: 1.8%-1.925% of mortgage amount for NYC; FL doc stamp tax: $0.35/$100)
- **Homestead exemption**: many states offer property tax reductions for primary residence (FL unlimited, TX $100K, etc.). Not an income tax item, but affects overall housing cost.
- **State mortgage interest deductions**: some states limit or disallow the mortgage interest deduction differently than federal. Most states that have income tax follow the federal rules.
- **State-specific §121 differences**: most states conform, but verify — some states have lower exclusion amounts or additional conditions. MN, for example, conforms to federal §121.
- **Capital gains surtax**: WA imposes 7% on LTCG above $270K (may apply to home sale gains exceeding §121 exclusion); CA taxes all gains at ordinary rates (up to 13.3%)
- **Energy improvement credits**: federal credits for solar panels (§25D, 30% credit), energy-efficient windows, insulation (§25C) may reduce tax but some also reduce the home's basis. Track carefully for both the credit claim and eventual sale basis calculation.
