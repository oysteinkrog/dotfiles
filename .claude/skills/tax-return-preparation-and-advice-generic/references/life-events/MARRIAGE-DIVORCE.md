# Marriage and Divorce Tax Reference (Tax Year 2025)

## Overview

Marriage and divorce are among the most significant tax events in a person's life, affecting filing status, available deductions and credits, income thresholds, and asset ownership. The tax implications depend on the timing of the event (marital status as of December 31 determines filing status for the entire year), the nature of assets involved, and whether alimony or property transfers are part of the arrangement.

## Tax Implications

### Marriage
- **Filing status determined on December 31**: if married on December 31, you are considered married for the entire tax year
- **Options**: Married Filing Jointly (MFJ) or Married Filing Separately (MFS)
- **Marriage bonus vs. penalty**:
  - Bonus: when spouses have significantly different incomes (combined tax lower than two single returns)
  - Penalty: when spouses have similar high incomes (combined tax higher); mitigated somewhat by TCJA bracket widening
- Cannot file as Single if married on December 31 (exception: abandoned spouse rule for HOH)

### MFJ vs. MFS Detailed Comparison

**MFJ advantages**:
- Lower tax rates (wider brackets)
- Full access to credits: EITC, education credits, adoption credit, CTC (higher phase-out)
- Full student loan interest deduction
- Higher income thresholds for most phase-outs
- Same combined standard deduction as two single filers when both spouses are under 65 ($31,500 MFJ vs. $15,750 each)
- IRA contribution deductibility: higher MAGI threshold

**MFS -- what you LOSE (comprehensive list)**:
- **EITC**: completely unavailable (IRC section 32(d))
- **Child and Dependent Care Credit**: NOT available unless legally separated or abandoned spouse (IRC section 21(e)(2))
- **Education credits (AOTC, LLC)**: completely unavailable (IRC section 25A(g)(6))
- **Student loan interest deduction**: completely unavailable (IRC section 221(e)(2))
- **Roth IRA contributions**: phase-out begins at $0 AGI, completely eliminated at $10,000 AGI -- effectively kills Roth contributions for any MFS filer with income
- **Standard deduction forced parity**: if one spouse itemizes, the other MUST itemize (even if standard deduction would be higher)
- **Social Security benefits**: up to 85% taxable starting at $0 provisional income (no $32K/$44K thresholds that MFJ gets)
- **SALT cap**: generally $20,000 for MFS in TY 2025, versus the larger current-law joint cap
  on an MFJ return, subject to any income-based limitation
- **Adoption credit**: NOT available
- **Exclusion of interest on savings bonds for education**: NOT available
- **Premium Tax Credit**: NOT available (unless victim of domestic abuse or spousal abandonment)
- **Net Investment Income Tax**: 3.8% surtax threshold drops to $125,000 (vs $250,000 MFJ)
- **Child Tax Credit**: phase-out begins at $200,000 (vs $400,000 MFJ)
- **Passive activity loss allowance**: $12,500 (vs $25,000 MFJ) for rental real estate

**MFS advantages (limited situations)**:
- Separate liability for taxes owed (not responsible for spouse's tax debt)
- Income-Driven Repayment (IDR) for student loans: MFS may result in lower payments under PAYE/IBR (but SAVE plan uses combined income regardless)
- Medical expense deduction: 7.5% of individual AGI (lower threshold) may be easier to exceed
- State-specific benefits: some community property states produce better MFS results
- Protection when you suspect spouse of fraud or non-compliance

**Worked example -- MFJ vs MFS comparison**:
- Spouse A earns $120,000; Spouse B earns $40,000. Spouse B has $15,000 in medical expenses.
- **MFJ**: Combined AGI $160,000. Medical threshold = 7.5% x $160,000 = $12,000. Deductible medical = $3,000.
- **MFS**: Spouse B AGI $40,000. Medical threshold = 7.5% x $40,000 = $3,000. Deductible medical = $12,000.
- BUT Spouse B loses education credits, EITC, and faces lower thresholds on other items. Run BOTH scenarios to determine which is actually better.

### Name and SSN Changes
- If name changes due to marriage: update Social Security card BEFORE filing
- File with the name matching your Social Security card to avoid processing delays
- Form SS-5 to update name with SSA; typically takes 2-4 weeks
- IRS matches name + SSN; mismatch causes rejection of e-filed return
- Also update: driver's license, employer records (W-4), bank accounts, investment accounts, passport, insurance policies

### Marriage Penalty and Bonus -- Worked Examples

**Marriage bonus scenario**:
- Spouse A: $150,000. Spouse B: $30,000. Combined: $180,000.
- Tax if single: $26,037 (A) + $2,282 (B) = $28,319
- Tax if MFJ: $26,200
- **Marriage bonus**: $2,119

**Marriage penalty scenario**:
- Spouse A: $200,000. Spouse B: $200,000. Combined: $400,000.
- Tax if single: $43,038 (A) + $43,038 (B) = $86,076
- Tax if MFJ: $88,078
- **Marriage penalty**: $2,002 (mitigated by TCJA but not eliminated for high-income equal earners)

## Community Property States -- MFS Deep Dive

### The 9 Community Property States
**AZ, CA, ID, LA, NV, NM, TX, WA, WI**

### How Community Property Affects MFS Returns
When filing MFS in a community property state, ALL community income must be split 50/50 between spouses, regardless of who earned it:
- **Wages and salary**: split 50/50 (each spouse reports half of combined wages)
- **Self-employment income**: split 50/50 (but SE tax is calculated on the spouse who performed the services)
- **Investment income on community property**: split 50/50
- **Rental income on community property**: split 50/50
- **Separate property income**: reported only by the owning spouse (property owned before marriage or received as gift/inheritance during marriage)

### Filing Mechanics
- **Form 8958** (Allocation of Tax Amounts Between Certain Individuals in Community Property States): REQUIRED for MFS filers in community property states
- Each spouse reports their half of community income PLUS their own separate property income
- This can produce unexpected results: a non-working spouse in CA filing MFS must report half of the working spouse's wages

### Worked Example -- Community Property MFS
- Spouse A earns $200,000 in wages in California. Spouse B earns $0.
- **MFS in non-CP state**: Spouse A reports $200,000; Spouse B reports $0
- **MFS in California**: Spouse A reports $100,000; Spouse B reports $100,000
- Both pay tax on $100,000 each -- may result in lower combined tax due to bracket spreading
- BUT community property MFS still loses all the credits listed above

### State Variations
- **California**: community property extends to all income earned during marriage; separate property must be clearly traced with documentation
- **Louisiana**: unique civil law tradition; concept of "fruits" of separate property may be community
- **Wisconsin**: technically "marital property" state under Uniform Marital Property Act, but treated as community property for federal tax purposes
- **Idaho**: all community property rules apply; aggressive enforcement of community income splitting

## Divorce Tax Considerations

### Filing Status in Year of Divorce
- If divorce is finalized by December 31: file as Single or Head of Household (if qualified)
- If divorce is NOT finalized by December 31: still legally married; must file MFJ or MFS
- Legal separation (under decree of separate maintenance): treated as unmarried in some states

### Head of Household After Separation
- Can file HOH if: unmarried (or considered unmarried) on December 31 AND paid more than half the cost of maintaining a home AND qualifying person lived with you for more than half the year
- "Considered unmarried": lived apart from spouse for last 6 months of year + meet other HOH requirements
- HOH rates are significantly better than MFS

### Alimony

**Pre-2019 divorce agreements (executed before January 1, 2019)**:
- Alimony is deductible by the payer (above-the-line deduction)
- Alimony is taxable income to the recipient
- Both parties report on Form 1040; payer must provide recipient's SSN

**Post-2018 divorce agreements (executed on or after January 1, 2019)**:
- Alimony is NOT deductible by the payer
- Alimony is NOT taxable to the recipient
- Effectively treated like property division

**Modifications**: if a pre-2019 agreement is modified after 2018, the new rules apply ONLY if the modification expressly states it adopts post-2018 tax treatment. Otherwise, the original pre-2019 rules continue.

### Alimony Recapture Rules (IRC Section 71(f)) -- Pre-2019 Agreements

For pre-2019 divorce agreements, alimony recapture prevents front-loading property settlements as "alimony" to get the deduction. Recapture is triggered if payments decrease by more than $15,000 between the first three calendar years:

**How it works**:
- **Year 3 test**: If Year 2 alimony exceeds Year 3 alimony by more than $15,000, the excess is recaptured
- **Year 2 test**: If Year 1 alimony exceeds the average of Year 2 (as adjusted) and Year 3 by more than $15,000, the excess is recaptured
- **Recapture effect**: Payer adds recaptured amount back to income in Year 3; recipient deducts it in Year 3

**Worked example**:
- Year 1: $100,000 alimony paid
- Year 2: $50,000 alimony paid
- Year 3: $20,000 alimony paid
- Year 3 recapture of Year 2 excess: $50,000 - $20,000 - $15,000 = $15,000
- Adjusted Year 2: $50,000 - $15,000 = $35,000
- Year 3 recapture of Year 1 excess: $100,000 - [($35,000 + $20,000) / 2 + $15,000] = $100,000 - $42,500 = $57,500
- Total recapture in Year 3: $15,000 + $57,500 = $72,500
- Payer adds $72,500 to Year 3 income; recipient deducts $72,500 from Year 3 income

### Child Support
- Never deductible by the payer
- Never taxable to the recipient
- No change based on divorce date

### Property Division -- Section 1041 in Detail

**IRC Section 1041**: Property transfers between spouses (or incident to divorce) are tax-free -- NO gain or loss recognized by either party.

**Key rules**:
- "Incident to divorce": transfer occurs within 1 year after the date the marriage ceases OR is related to the cessation and occurs within 6 years after the date the marriage ceases (under a divorce or separation instrument)
- **Basis carries over**: receiving spouse takes the transferring spouse's adjusted basis -- NOT the FMV
- This means the receiving spouse inherits built-in gains (or losses)
- Applies to ALL property: stocks, real estate, business interests, retirement accounts (via QDRO), vehicles, collectibles

**Worked example -- basis trap**:
- During marriage, Spouse A bought stock for $50,000. At divorce, stock is worth $500,000.
- Spouse A transfers stock to Spouse B as part of divorce settlement.
- Spouse B's basis = $50,000 (Spouse A's basis), NOT $500,000
- If Spouse B sells for $500,000, Spouse B owes tax on $450,000 of capital gain
- At 20% LTCG rate + 3.8% NIIT = $107,100 in federal tax on "property" that seemed worth $500,000 but was really worth ~$393,000 after-tax

**Planning point**: When dividing assets in divorce, compare AFTER-TAX values, not gross FMV. $500,000 of stock with a $50,000 basis is NOT equivalent to $500,000 of stock with a $450,000 basis.

### QDRO (Qualified Domestic Relations Order) -- Detailed Mechanics

**What it is**: A court order that divides a qualified retirement plan (401(k), pension, 403(b)) between divorcing spouses. Governed by IRC section 414(p).

**How it works**:
1. Court issues QDRO that names the alternate payee (non-employee spouse) and specifies the amount or formula for division
2. Plan administrator reviews and qualifies the order
3. Assets are divided per the QDRO terms

**Tax treatment of QDRO distributions**:
- **Direct transfer to alternate payee's IRA**: tax-free rollover; no withholding; no 10% penalty; this is the preferred method
- **Distribution paid directly to alternate payee from 401(k)/pension**: taxable as ordinary income to the alternate payee, BUT the 10% early withdrawal penalty does NOT apply (QDRO exception under IRC section 72(t)(2)(C)) -- regardless of age
- **Key distinction**: the QDRO exception to the 10% penalty applies ONLY to employer plans (401(k), pension). It does NOT apply to IRAs

**IRA division in divorce**:
- IRAs do NOT require a QDRO -- they use the "transfer incident to divorce" rules under section 1041
- Transfer of IRA from one spouse to the other per a divorce decree or separation agreement: tax-free
- BUT if the receiving spouse takes a distribution before age 59.5 and no other exception applies, the 10% early withdrawal penalty DOES apply (no QDRO exception for IRAs)

**Timing matters**: QDRO should be drafted and submitted to the plan administrator BEFORE the divorce is finalized if possible. Some plans take 3-6 months to process a QDRO. During processing, the plan may freeze the account.

### Dependent Claims After Divorce
- Default: custodial parent claims the child (physical custody for more than half the year)
- Non-custodial parent can claim if custodial parent signs Form 8332 (Release of Claim to Exemption)
- Form 8332 releases CTC and dependency, but does NOT release: HOH status, EITC, Child and Dependent Care Credit, exclusion for dependent care benefits (these always belong to custodial parent)
- Divorce decree alone does not override the custodial parent rule (Form 8332 specifically required)

## Innocent Spouse Relief -- IRC Section 6015

### Three Types of Relief

**1. Innocent Spouse Relief -- Section 6015(b)**:
- Filed MFJ and there was an understatement of tax attributable to erroneous items of the OTHER spouse
- You must show you did not know, and had no reason to know, of the understatement
- It would be inequitable to hold you liable
- **Threshold**: the understatement must be attributable to the other spouse's erroneous items (unreported income, incorrect deductions, etc.)

**2. Separation of Liability -- Section 6015(c)**:
- Allocates the deficiency between the two spouses based on who was responsible for which items
- **Eligibility**: must be divorced, legally separated, or lived apart from the spouse for 12+ months before filing Form 8857
- Only available for deficiencies (not for underpayment of properly reported tax)
- Knowledge of the understatement disqualifies you for the portion attributable to items you knew about

**3. Equitable Relief -- Section 6015(f)**:
- Catch-all when neither (b) nor (c) applies
- Available for both understatements AND underpayments (e.g., tax was correctly computed but spouse failed to pay)
- IRS considers all facts and circumstances: abuse, financial hardship, knowledge, benefit from the understatement, compliance history
- Revenue Procedure 2013-34 lists factors the IRS weighs

### Filing Deadlines
- **Section 6015(b) and (c)**: must request within 2 years of the date the IRS first began collection activity against you
- **Section 6015(f) -- equitable relief**: NO 2-year deadline. Can be requested at any time during the period of limitation on collection (generally 10 years from assessment) or within the period to claim a refund
- File **Form 8857** (Request for Innocent Spouse Relief)
- IRS must notify the non-requesting spouse and consider their response

### Injured Spouse (Not the Same as Innocent Spouse)

**Injured spouse** applies when a JOINT refund is seized to pay the OTHER spouse's past-due obligations (back child support, defaulted student loans, prior-year federal or state tax debt).

- **Form 8379** (Injured Spouse Allocation): filed to recover YOUR share of the joint refund
- Can be filed with the return or after the refund is offset
- Allocates income, credits, and withholding between spouses to determine each spouse's share
- Not related to errors or fraud -- purely about protecting one spouse's refund from the other's debts

## Optimization Opportunities

1. **Run both MFJ and MFS calculations**: especially if one spouse has high medical expenses, student loans on IDR, or liability concerns
2. **Time the divorce strategically**: divorcing in December vs. January changes the entire year's filing status
3. **Negotiate property division with tax basis in mind**: the FMV is not what matters -- the tax basis determines future tax liability. Compare after-tax values of all assets being divided.
4. **Maximize HOH status**: significantly better brackets and standard deduction than Single or MFS
5. **Plan Roth conversions in year of divorce**: income may be lower, creating an opportunity
6. **Update W-4s promptly**: withholding based on old status will result in underpayment or overpayment
7. **QDRO distributions for immediate cash needs**: the QDRO exception avoids the 10% penalty on 401(k) distributions at any age -- useful if a divorcing spouse needs immediate funds
8. **Community property MFS bracket arbitrage**: in CP states, MFS splits income 50/50 which can reduce combined tax when one spouse earns significantly more (but weigh against lost credits)
9. **Pre-divorce Roth conversions**: if one spouse has a large traditional IRA, converting before divorce may be advantageous depending on income levels

## Common Mistakes

1. **Filing as Single when still legally married on December 31** -- divorce must be finalized
2. **Both parents claiming the same child** -- triggers IRS tiebreaker rules and delays refunds
3. **Not obtaining Form 8332 when divorce decree awards exemption to non-custodial parent** -- the decree alone is insufficient
4. **Ignoring built-in gains on property received in divorce** -- accepting $500K of stock with $100K basis is very different from $500K of stock with $400K basis
5. **Not updating beneficiary designations** -- retirement accounts, life insurance, and TOD accounts pass by beneficiary designation, which may still name the ex-spouse
6. **Missing the community property allocation on MFS returns** -- required in community property states; Form 8958 must be attached
7. **Not claiming innocent spouse relief timely** -- the 2-year window for section 6015(b)/(c) can expire quickly (equitable relief has extended time)
8. **Confusing injured spouse with innocent spouse** -- Form 8379 vs Form 8857; completely different situations
9. **Not accounting for alimony recapture** -- front-loaded alimony payments trigger recapture in Year 3 for pre-2019 agreements
10. **Rolling QDRO funds to an IRA then taking a distribution** -- loses the QDRO exception to the 10% penalty; if the alternate payee needs cash, take the distribution directly from the employer plan
11. **MFS filers in community property states not splitting income** -- IRS matches W-2s to SSNs; failing to report 50% of community income triggers notices

## Wedding and Marriage Year Checklist

1. **Update Social Security records** (Form SS-5) BEFORE filing if name changed
2. **Update W-4 at employer** to reflect new filing status and any change in combined income
3. **Compare MFJ vs MFS** -- run both computations. MFJ is usually better but not always.
4. **Update beneficiary designations** on retirement accounts, life insurance, bank accounts
5. **Review health insurance** -- marriage is a qualifying life event for Special Enrollment Period (60 days)
6. **Consider estate planning** -- new wills, powers of attorney, beneficiary updates
7. **Combine or separate finances** -- tax implications either way in community property states
8. **Student loan strategy** -- if either spouse has student loans on IDR, evaluate MFJ vs MFS impact on payments

## Divorce Year Checklist

1. **Determine filing status** -- divorced by Dec 31 = Single or HOH; not yet = MFJ or MFS
2. **Negotiate property division by after-tax value**, not gross FMV
3. **Prepare QDRO before divorce is finalized** -- plan processing can take months
4. **Obtain Form 8332** if non-custodial parent will claim children
5. **Update W-4** to reflect new filing status immediately
6. **Update beneficiary designations** on ALL financial accounts
7. **Cancel old state estimated payments** if applicable; recalculate for new filing status
8. **Consider innocent spouse relief** if you suspect errors on joint returns filed during the marriage
9. **Preserve documentation** -- tax returns, financial statements, settlement documents for at least 7 years

## State-Specific Notes

- **Community property states** (AZ, CA, ID, LA, NV, NM, TX, WA, WI): unique income splitting rules for MFS; Form 8958 required
- **State filing status**: generally follows federal, but some states have unique rules
- **Alimony**: state treatment may differ from federal -- some states still allow deduction regardless of divorce date:
  - **California**: conforms to pre-2019 federal rule (alimony deductible/taxable) for CA state tax regardless of divorce date
  - **New York**: generally follows federal treatment
  - **Hawaii, Arkansas, Alabama**: may have their own alimony tax rules
- **California**: community property extends to all income earned during marriage; separate property must be clearly traced
- **Texas**: community property state with no income tax, but property division still has federal tax consequences
- **New York**: equitable distribution state (not community property) -- different property division framework but section 1041 still applies to transfers
- **State divorce filing requirements**: some states require a year of separation before divorce (e.g., NC, VA) -- affects which year the filing status changes
- **Domestic partnerships and civil unions**: some states treat these differently than marriage for state tax purposes even though they follow federal treatment for federal returns
