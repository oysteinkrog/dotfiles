# Retirement Event Tax Reference (Tax Year 2025)

## Overview

The year of retirement is one of the most impactful tax planning years in a person's life. Income typically drops significantly, creating unique opportunities for Roth conversions, capital gain harvesting, and Medicare planning. Proper handling of the final W-2, pension decisions, Social Security timing, NUA opportunities, estimated payment setup, and health insurance transitions prevents costly mistakes and positions the retiree for decades of tax-efficient retirement income. The decisions made in the 12 months surrounding retirement can affect tax liability for 30+ years.

## Tax Implications

### Final Year W-2 Optimization

#### Maximize Pre-Tax Contributions Before Separation
- **401(k)**: front-load contributions to reach $23,500 limit (or $31,000 if age 50+, $34,750 if ages 60-63) before separation date
- **HSA**: if on HDHP, contribute up to the annual limit ($4,300 self-only / $8,550 family for 2025) before leaving; employer may prorate
- **Commuter benefits**: use remaining pre-tax transit/parking benefits
- Strategy: increase 401(k) deferral percentage in final months to maximize the pre-tax bucket

#### Final W-2 Items to Verify
- Last W-2 reflects wages through separation date
- Check for: final bonus, unused PTO payout, severance (all taxable as ordinary income)
- Deferred compensation distributions: may begin upon separation from service; reported on W-2 or 1099-R depending on plan type
- Verify Box 12 codes: DD (health insurance cost reporting), W (HSA contributions), D/E (401k deferrals through separation date)
- Check that employer stopped 401(k) deferrals after separation; excess contributions must be corrected
- **Severance**: fully taxable, subject to FICA; may push into higher bracket in the retirement year

#### Use Remaining Income as Roth Conversion Fuel
- After separation, remaining income for the year is known -- calculate exactly how much "room" exists in target brackets
- The drop in income creates space to convert traditional IRA/401(k) to Roth at lower rates
- Front-load HSA to preserve the Roth conversion room later in the year

### Pension Options Analysis

#### Lump Sum vs. Annuity Decision
This is often the largest financial decision at retirement. Factors:

| Factor | Lump Sum | Annuity |
|--------|----------|---------|
| Control | Full control of investments | None -- insurance company manages |
| Tax flexibility | Roll to IRA, convert portions to Roth, manage distributions | Fixed monthly payment, fixed tax |
| Longevity risk | Risk of outliving money | Guaranteed lifetime income |
| Survivor benefit | Passes to heirs via IRA | May stop at death or reduce for survivor |
| Inflation | Can invest for growth | Usually fixed (some have COLA) |
| Interest rate sensitivity | Low rates = higher lump sum offer | Low rates = worse annuity conversion |

#### Lump Sum Mechanics
- **Direct rollover to IRA**: tax-free; no withholding; full amount transferred
- **Cash distribution**: 20% mandatory federal withholding; fully taxable as ordinary income; potential 10% early withdrawal penalty if under 59.5
- **Partial rollover**: roll most to IRA, keep some cash for immediate needs (only the cash portion is taxable)
- If taking lump sum as cash: the entire amount is ordinary income in one year -- can push into the 32% or 37% bracket

#### Annuity Taxation
- Report on Form 1099-R
- **Fully employer-funded pension**: 100% of each payment is taxable ordinary income
- **Partially employee-funded** (after-tax contributions): use the Simplified Method to calculate the tax-free return-of-basis portion per month
- Simplified Method: divide total after-tax contributions by expected number of payments (based on age at annuity start from IRS table)

### Net Unrealized Appreciation (NUA) -- Section 402(e)(4)

#### The Opportunity
- If your 401(k) holds employer stock with significant appreciation, NUA treatment can save enormous amounts in taxes
- At retirement: take a **lump-sum distribution** of the ENTIRE plan balance (must be complete distribution in one tax year)
- Transfer employer stock to a taxable brokerage account (in-kind distribution) and roll everything else to an IRA

#### How NUA Works
- **Cost basis of employer stock**: taxed as ordinary income in the year of distribution
- **Appreciation (NUA)**: NOT taxed at distribution; taxed as LONG-TERM capital gains when stock is later sold, regardless of holding period
- Additional appreciation after distribution: short-term or long-term depending on holding period after distribution

#### Example
- 401(k) has $200,000 of employer stock (cost basis $40,000, FMV $200,000) plus $300,000 in mutual funds
- Take employer stock in-kind to brokerage; roll $300,000 of mutual funds to IRA
- Year of distribution: $40,000 ordinary income (cost basis)
- When stock is sold later: $160,000 taxed at LTCG rate (15-20%) instead of ordinary income rate (up to 37%)
- Tax savings: $160,000 x (37% - 15%) = $35,200 in savings

#### Requirements
- Must be a lump-sum distribution (entire plan balance in one tax year)
- Must be triggered by: separation from service, attaining age 59.5, disability, or death
- Cannot cherry-pick -- must distribute the entire account (though non-stock assets can roll to IRA)

### Social Security Timing Decision

#### Key Ages and Benefit Amounts
- **Age 62**: earliest eligibility; permanently reduced to ~70% of FRA benefit
- **Full Retirement Age (FRA)**: 66-67 depending on birth year (67 for those born 1960+)
- **Age 70**: maximum benefit; ~124% of FRA benefit (8% increase per year of delay past FRA)

#### Earnings Test (Before FRA)
- Benefits reduced by $1 for every $2 earned above $23,400 (2025)
- In the year reaching FRA: $1 for every $3 above $62,160
- After FRA: no reduction regardless of earnings
- Withheld benefits are NOT lost -- they increase future monthly payments

#### Spousal Coordination Strategies
- **Both delay to 70**: maximizes combined lifetime benefits if both healthy
- **Lower earner claims early, higher earner delays**: provides income now while maximizing the larger benefit (and the survivor benefit)
- **Spousal benefit**: up to 50% of higher earner's FRA benefit (only available after higher earner files)
- **Survivor benefit**: up to 100% of deceased spouse's benefit (including delayed credits)

#### Decision Framework
- **Delay if**: good health, other income sources, want to maximize survivor benefit, want to minimize taxable income during Roth conversion window
- **Claim early if**: poor health, need income immediately, both spouses have similar benefits (lower survivor benefit advantage), prefer certainty

#### Tax Torpedo
- The range where Social Security becomes taxable creates effective marginal rates far exceeding the stated bracket
- At the 50% inclusion threshold: each additional $1 of income can cause $1.50 of taxable income (the $1 + $0.50 of newly-taxable SS)
- At the 85% inclusion threshold: each additional $1 can cause $1.85 of taxable income
- This creates effective marginal rates of 22.2% to 40.7% in what appears to be the 12% bracket

## Health Insurance Transition

### Pre-Medicare (Before Age 65)

- **COBRA**: continue employer coverage for 18 months; expensive (full premium + 2% admin fee); not pre-tax
- **ACA Marketplace**: may qualify for Premium Tax Credits based on reduced retirement income
  - For 2025 there is no automatic 400% FPL cliff; model MAGI on Form 8962 instead of
    treating 400% FPL as a hard cutoff
  - Roth conversions, capital gains, and retirement distributions all count as MAGI
  - Extra income can still materially reduce subsidies, so large conversions and capital gains
    should be timed deliberately
- **Self-employed health insurance deduction**: if you have ANY self-employment income (consulting, part-time), premiums are deductible above-the-line
- **HSA**: can continue using HSA funds tax-free for medical expenses (including COBRA premiums); cannot contribute new funds unless still on HDHP

### Medicare Enrollment (Age 65)

#### Enrollment Timing
- **Part A (hospital)**: usually premium-free; automatic if receiving Social Security
- **Part B (medical)**: standard premium ~$185/month (2025); 7-month Initial Enrollment Period (IEP) around 65th birthday (3 months before, birth month, 3 months after)
- **Late enrollment penalty**: 10% surcharge on Part B premium for EACH 12-month period you could have had Part B but didn't
  - This penalty is PERMANENT -- lasts for life
  - Exception: if covered by employer group health plan (employer with 20+ employees), no penalty for delayed enrollment
- **Part D (prescription)**: separate premium; 1% penalty per month of delayed enrollment (also permanent)

#### HSA Interaction -- CRITICAL
- Must STOP HSA contributions at least 6 months BEFORE Medicare Part A enrollment
- Medicare Part A can be retroactive up to 6 months
- If you enroll in Medicare at 65 and are receiving Social Security: Part A is automatic and retroactive
- Excess HSA contributions incur 6% penalty per year until corrected
- Can SPEND existing HSA funds at any time -- the restriction is only on NEW contributions

### IRMAA Planning (Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount)

- IRMAA surcharges on Part B and Part D premiums based on MAGI from 2 years prior
- 2025 premiums based on 2023 MAGI
- Large income events in the retirement year (severance, pension lump sum, capital gains) can trigger IRMAA surcharges 2 years later

| MAGI (MFJ) | Part B Monthly Surcharge | Part D Monthly Surcharge |
|-------------|-------------------------|-------------------------|
| <= $206,000 | $0 | $0 |
| $206,001 - $258,000 | +$74.00 | +$13.70 |
| $258,001 - $322,000 | +$185.00 | +$35.30 |
| $322,001 - $386,000 | +$296.00 | +$57.00 |
| $386,001 - $750,000 | +$406.90 | +$78.60 |
| > $750,000 | +$444.60 | +$85.80 |

- **Life-changing event**: can request IRMAA reconsideration (Form SSA-44) if retirement caused the high-income year
- Qualifying events: work stoppage, work reduction, involuntary loss of income-producing property, loss of pension, employer settlement/judgment

## Roth Conversion Window

### Why the Retirement-to-RMD Gap Is Golden

- Income drops significantly at retirement (partial year wages, no more paychecks)
- RMDs don't begin until age 73 (SECURE 2.0; age 75 starting 2033)
- This gap = potentially 5-15 years of lower income = lower tax brackets = ideal for Roth conversions
- Converting now prevents larger forced RMDs later, reduces future taxable income, and avoids future IRMAA surcharges

### Conversion Strategy

1. Calculate remaining income for the year after retirement date
2. Determine how much "room" exists in the 12%, 22%, or 24% brackets
3. Convert that amount from traditional IRA/401(k) to Roth IRA
4. Factor in: ACA premium credits (conversions increase MAGI), IRMAA (2-year lag), NIIT threshold ($200K/$250K)
5. Consider state tax: may be more beneficial if moving to a no-income-tax state FIRST
6. Multi-year plan: convert a portion each year for 5-15 years rather than all at once

### Worked Example

**MFJ, retiring June 30 with $80K of W-2 income, $2M in traditional IRA**

- Standard deduction: $30,000
- Taxable income from wages: $50,000
- 12% bracket ends at $96,950 (MFJ 2025)
- Room in 12% bracket: $46,950
- Room in 22% bracket: $93,400 additional (up to $190,350)
- **Conservative approach**: convert $47K at 12% = $5,634 in federal tax on conversion
- **Moderate approach**: convert $140K filling 22% bracket = $5,634 + $20,548 = $26,182 tax on conversion
- **Aggressive approach**: fill 24% bracket too (but watch IRMAA and ACA impacts)

If this $47K grows at 7% for 20 years in a Roth: worth ~$182K, ALL tax-free. Without conversion: $182K x 37% potential future rate = $67K in taxes avoided.

## Estimated Payment Setup

### Transitioning from Employer Withholding

- Employment withholding stops at retirement -- you are now responsible for paying taxes directly
- **Options**:
  1. Request withholding on pension payments (Form W-4P)
  2. Request withholding on Social Security (Form W-4V -- limited options: 7%, 10%, 12%, or 22%)
  3. Make quarterly estimated payments (Form 1040-ES)
  4. Combination of all three
- Quarterly due dates: April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15
- Safe harbor: 100% of prior year tax (110% if AGI > $150,000) or 90% of current year tax
- Retirement year: prior year tax may be much higher; paying 90% of current year may be cheaper

### Part-Time / Consulting Income in Retirement

- If doing any consulting or part-time work: new Schedule C
- SE tax (15.3% on first $176,100 of net SE income for 2025, 2.9% above)
- Deductible: 50% of SE tax, home office, business expenses
- Health insurance premiums may be deductible as self-employed health insurance (above-the-line)
- Consider a Solo 401(k) or SEP-IRA for additional pre-tax savings from consulting income

## Optimization Strategies

1. **Roth conversion window**: the years between retirement and RMD start are the golden opportunity -- convert to fill low brackets each year
2. **Manage ACA subsidies**: if under 65, keep MAGI low enough for premium tax credits; a few thousand dollars of excess income can cost thousands in lost subsidies
3. **Harvest capital gains at 0%**: if taxable income is low enough, realize LTCG at the 0% rate (up to $96,700 MFJ in 2025)
4. **Delay Social Security**: if other assets available, delaying to 70 maximizes benefit and reduces taxable income during the conversion window
5. **NUA on employer stock**: at separation, evaluate NUA treatment before rolling 401(k) -- once rolled to IRA, the NUA opportunity is LOST forever
6. **Bunch charitable giving via DAF**: concentrate donations in a high-income year (retirement year with severance), distribute over time
7. **QCD starting at 70.5**: direct IRA-to-charity distributions satisfy RMDs without increasing AGI
8. **State tax planning**: relocate to a lower-tax state BEFORE large Roth conversions or pension lump-sum distributions
9. **Front-load HSA**: max HSA contributions before retirement while still on HDHP, then use the HSA tax-free for decades
10. **Consider pension lump sum rollover**: preserve flexibility, Roth conversion options, and beneficiary designations vs. annuity

## Common Mistakes

1. **Not setting up estimated payments** -- first year of retirement often results in underpayment penalty
2. **Claiming Social Security too early** -- the permanently higher benefit from delaying usually wins mathematically
3. **Missing the Roth conversion window** -- once RMDs begin, conversions are less efficient (RMD must be taken first, cannot be converted)
4. **Ignoring IRMAA** -- a large conversion or lump sum can increase Medicare premiums for 2+ years
5. **Not stopping HSA contributions before Medicare** -- must stop 6 months before Part A enrollment
6. **Overlooking ACA MAGI sensitivity** -- extra income can sharply reduce marketplace premium
   tax credits even though 2025 has no automatic 400% FPL cliff
7. **Taking pension lump sum as cash instead of rollover** -- fully taxable as ordinary income + 20% mandatory withholding
8. **Not requesting IRMAA reconsideration** -- file Form SSA-44 if retirement caused the high-income year
9. **Missing NUA opportunity** -- must decide at the time of distribution; cannot retroactively elect NUA
10. **Not considering part-time consulting for additional retirement plan contributions** -- a Solo 401(k) from consulting income adds another layer of tax-deferred savings
11. **Late Medicare Part B enrollment** -- the 10% per year penalty is permanent and compounds

## State Considerations

- **States that do not tax retirement income**: AK, FL, NV, SD, TN, TX, WA, WY (no income tax); NH (interest/dividends only, phasing out)
- **States with full/partial pension exclusion**: IL, MS, PA (most retirement income exempt); NY (first $20,000 government pension); many others with partial exclusions
- **Social Security taxation**: most states exempt SS benefits; 9 states tax some portion (CO, CT, MN, MT, NM, RI, UT, VT, WV -- each with different rules)
- **Timing the move**: if planning to relocate to a no-tax state, do so BEFORE taking pension lump sums, large Roth conversions, or exercising stock options
- **Part-year filing**: retirement year often involves a state move; file part-year returns in both states
- **State estimated payments**: establish in new state; stop payments to former state
