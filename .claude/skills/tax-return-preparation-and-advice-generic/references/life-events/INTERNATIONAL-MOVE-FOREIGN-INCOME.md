# International Move / Foreign Income -- Life Event Guide (TY 2025)

## 1. Why This Event Is More Dangerous Than It Looks

An international move is not a single tax question. It is a stack of overlapping systems:
- federal worldwide-income rules,
- state residency and domicile rules,
- foreign country residency and payroll rules,
- foreign account reporting,
- FEIE vs FTC decisions,
- self-employment and social-insurance coordination,
- treaty and totalization issues,
- immigration-status transitions for inbound or outbound taxpayers.

The biggest mistakes happen when taxpayers treat the move as "just another address change." It is
not. The move date, domicile break, payroll setup, foreign account opening, and document trail all
matter.

---

## 2. Identify the Move Type First

### Outbound U.S. Citizen / Resident
- U.S. citizen or resident alien leaves the United States for work, family, or lifestyle reasons.
- Core issue: still filing in the U.S. on worldwide income, plus foreign reporting.

### Returning U.S. Person
- U.S. person comes back after living abroad.
- Core issue: split-year facts, foreign tax credit carryovers, account reporting, and resident
  state re-entry.

### Inbound to the United States
- Nonresident becomes U.S. resident for tax purposes.
- Core issue: residency start date, dual-status rules, and which foreign assets or entities
  trigger ongoing reporting.

### Cross-Border Worker / Digital Nomad
- No clean permanent move; location changes repeatedly.
- Core issue: tax home, FEIE eligibility, state-domicile drift, and foreign-country payroll risk.

---

## 3. Before the Move: Pre-Departure Checklist

### Residency and Domicile
- Determine which U.S. state currently claims domicile.
- If trying to break residency from a high-tax state, build the fact pattern **before** leaving:
  home, voter registration, driver's license, employment, family location, club memberships,
  mailing address, time spent, and intent all matter.
- Do not assume leaving the country automatically ends California, New York, Virginia, or similar
  state residency issues.

### Payroll and Withholding
- Confirm whether the employer will keep U.S. payroll, local-country payroll, or both.
- Review withholding, totalization, and shadow-payroll exposure.
- If self-employed, determine whether the foreign country will impose registration or VAT/GST-like
  obligations.

### Accounts and Reporting
- Create a list of all foreign financial accounts expected to exist after the move.
- Understand FBAR and Form 8938 thresholds before opening multiple overseas accounts.
- Avoid accidental PFIC exposure through foreign mutual funds or foreign brokerage defaults unless
  you are intentionally managing that complexity.

### Evidence File
- Preserve passport stamps, visas, lease, move invoices, employment letter, foreign tax ID,
  school enrollment, and utility setup.
- These documents are often what later prove physical presence, bona fide residence, and state
  domicile change.

## 4. The Year of the Move

### Federal
- U.S. citizens and resident aliens generally continue filing U.S. returns reporting worldwide
  income.
- FEIE may not fully shelter the move year if the physical-presence or bona-fide-residence tests
  are not yet met for the intended period.
- FTC may be the better first-line relief when foreign tax is high and the move occurs midyear.

### State
- File part-year or nonresident returns as required.
- The move year is often where states fight hardest because the fact pattern is mixed.
- Do not assume "foreign move = no state return." Some states keep taxing until domicile is
  clearly severed.

### Payroll and Benefits
- Review retirement-plan contributions, RSUs, bonuses, deferred compensation, and equity vesting
  that straddle countries.
- Cross-border equity sourcing can be much messier than salary sourcing.

---

## 5. FEIE vs FTC: The Practical Decision Rule

### FEIE Tends To Fit Better When
- foreign earned income is the main issue,
- the foreign tax rate is low,
- the taxpayer wants simplicity,
- the taxpayer clearly satisfies physical-presence or bona-fide-residence tests.

### FTC Tends To Fit Better When
- the taxpayer is in a high-tax country,
- there is meaningful investment income or non-earned income,
- foreign taxes exceed what FEIE alone would solve,
- future flexibility matters.

### Event-Level Rule
- Do not choose FEIE vs FTC by reflex.
- Model both when the move year is partial, the taxpayer is high income, or state issues remain
  live.

## 6. Foreign Accounts, Entities, and Reporting

### Always Screen For
- foreign bank and brokerage accounts,
- foreign pensions,
- foreign insurance wrappers,
- foreign companies or partnerships,
- foreign trusts,
- signature authority over employer accounts,
- foreign mutual funds or ETFs.

### Reporting Regimes That Often Trigger
- FBAR,
- Form 8938,
- Form 5471,
- Form 8621,
- Form 8865,
- Form 3520 / 3520-A,
- Form 8833 for treaty positions.

### Practical Rule
- A cross-border taxpayer with a foreign brokerage or private-company interest is no longer in
  "simple return" territory until proven otherwise.

---

## 7. Self-Employment and Remote Work Abroad

### Independent Contractor Abroad
- U.S. self-employment tax may still apply even if the taxpayer lives overseas.
- FEIE does not automatically solve self-employment tax.
- Totalization agreements can be decisive; determine which country's social-insurance system
  controls.

### Employee Working Remotely From Another Country
- Employer payroll setup may be wrong even when the tax return is later "fixed."
- Watch for permanent-establishment risk for the employer, local withholding requirements, and
  corporate registration consequences.

### Digital Nomad Trap
- Constant travel can undermine tax-home arguments.
- A taxpayer can spend plenty of time abroad and still have a weak FEIE position if they never
  establish a real foreign base.

---

## 8. Housing, Family, and Cash-Movement Issues

### Housing
- Rent, employer-provided housing, and move reimbursements may have different U.S. and foreign tax
  treatment.
- Keep leases, invoices, and utility proof.

### Family
- Spousal employment, school enrollment, and where children live can strongly influence domicile
  and bona fide residence arguments.

### Cash Movement
- Large transfers between U.S. and foreign accounts are not inherently taxable, but they can
  trigger documentation and reporting issues.
- Keep a transfer ledger showing source of funds and destination.

---

## 9. Common Mistakes

1. Assuming a foreign move ends U.S. filing obligations.
2. Ignoring resident-state domicile until after the move.
3. Opening foreign mutual funds or ETFs without understanding PFIC consequences.
4. Failing to preserve travel and residence evidence.
5. Choosing FEIE without checking whether FTC is better.
6. Forgetting FBAR or Form 8938 because "the account didn't earn much."
7. Ignoring payroll and social-insurance issues for self-employment or remote work.
8. Waiting until filing season to reconstruct the move timeline.

---

## 10. Event Workflow

### Immediate Questions
- Exact move date?
- U.S. citizenship, green card, visa, or dual-status facts?
- Which U.S. state is being left or entered?
- Employee vs self-employed?
- Any foreign accounts or entities?
- Any equity compensation or deferred compensation?

### Documents To Gather
- Passports and travel log.
- Lease or purchase documents abroad.
- Employment contract and payroll records.
- Foreign tax assessments or withholding slips.
- Bank/brokerage statements.
- State-residency evidence before and after move.

### Output the Skill Should Produce
- a residency and filing-jurisdiction map,
- an FEIE-vs-FTC memo,
- a foreign-reporting checklist,
- a state-domicile risk memo,
- an estimated-tax and withholding plan.
