# Disability Tax Reference (Tax Year 2025)

## Overview

Taxpayers who become disabled or who have ongoing disabilities face unique tax considerations related to disability income taxation, medical expense deductions, special accounts (ABLE), penalty exceptions for early retirement distributions, and impairment-related work expenses. Understanding the tax treatment of various disability income sources -- which varies dramatically based on how premiums were paid -- is essential. The single most important question in disability taxation: WHO paid the insurance premiums, and with WHAT kind of dollars?

## Tax Implications

### SSDI (Social Security Disability Insurance) Taxation

SSDI is taxed identically to Social Security retirement benefits using the "provisional income" formula.

#### Provisional Income Calculation
```
Provisional Income = AGI (excluding SS)
                   + Tax-exempt interest (muni bonds)
                   + 50% of Social Security/SSDI benefits
```

#### Taxation Thresholds

| Filing Status | Provisional Income | Taxable Portion |
|--------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Single | < $25,000 | 0% |
| Single | $25,000 - $34,000 | Up to 50% |
| Single | > $34,000 | Up to 85% |
| MFJ | < $32,000 | 0% |
| MFJ | $32,000 - $44,000 | Up to 50% |
| MFJ | > $44,000 | Up to 85% |

#### Precise Calculation (Up to 85%)
- **50% tier**: lesser of (a) 50% of benefits, or (b) 50% of (provisional income minus base amount)
- **85% tier**: 85% of benefits, or sum of: 85% of (provisional income minus adjusted base) + lesser of (amount from 50% tier) or $4,500/$6,000
- Most SSDI recipients with any other income source will have 50-85% of SSDI taxable

#### SSI (Supplemental Security Income)
- **NOT taxable** -- it is a means-tested welfare benefit, not an insurance benefit
- Not reported on SSA-1099
- Receipt of SSI does not affect taxation of other income

#### SSDI Back Pay Lump Sum -- Section 86(e)
- SSDI claims often result in a lump-sum payment covering months or years of back benefits
- **Section 86(e) election**: taxpayer can calculate the taxable portion as if the benefits were received in the prior years they were attributed to
- This often results in LESS total tax because provisional income in prior years may have been lower
- IRS provides worksheets; compute tax both ways and use the method that results in less tax
- Report on Form SSA-1099 (total shown in Box 5; prior-year amounts shown in Box 3 description)

### Disability Insurance Proceeds -- THE Critical Distinction

This is the single most important factor in disability income taxation:

| Who Paid Premiums | Tax on Benefits |
|-------------------|-----------------|
| Employer paid (or pre-tax deduction) | **FULLY TAXABLE** as ordinary income |
| Employee paid (after-tax dollars) | **TAX-FREE** -- Section 104(a)(3) |
| Shared (employer + employee) | Taxable portion = employer-paid share |
| Individual policy (you paid premiums) | **TAX-FREE** |

#### Section 104(a)(3) Analysis
- Benefits received under an accident or health plan for personal injuries or sickness are excluded from gross income
- BUT: if employer paid the premiums (or premiums were pre-tax through cafeteria plan), the exclusion does NOT apply
- The exclusion applies ONLY to the extent premiums were paid by the employee with after-tax dollars

#### Practical Impact Example
- Disability benefit: $5,000/month, 22% federal bracket + 6% state
- **Employer-paid**: $5,000 taxable = $3,600/month after tax
- **Self-paid (after-tax)**: $5,000 tax-free = $5,000/month
- Annual difference: $16,800 -- a staggering difference from the same policy

#### Reporting
- Short-term disability through employer: reported on W-2 (Box 1)
- Long-term disability (employer-paid plan): Form 1099-R or Form W-2 depending on structure
- Self-paid individual policy: may receive Form 1099 but report as non-taxable; be prepared to substantiate premium payment source

#### Strategic Planning (Pre-Disability)
- If employer offers choice: opt for after-tax premium payment (slightly higher cost now, enormous tax savings if disabled)
- Or: negotiate higher salary in lieu of employer-paid disability coverage and purchase your own policy
- Cost of after-tax premiums is modest compared to the tax-free benefit if you ever need it

### Workers' Compensation -- Section 104(a)(1)

- **Generally tax-free** regardless of amount
- Includes: wage replacement, medical expense reimbursement, vocational rehabilitation
- If receiving both workers' comp AND SSDI: Social Security offset may apply (total cannot exceed 80% of pre-disability earnings), but workers' comp portion remains tax-free
- Workers' comp settlements: generally tax-free if structured as workers' comp payments; watch for portions allocated to interest or penalties

### VA Disability Benefits

- **Completely tax-free** regardless of amount or rating
- Veterans receiving both VA disability and military retirement pay may have retirement pay reclassified:
  - **CRDP (Concurrent Retirement and Disability Pay)**: allows receipt of both; retirement pay is taxable, VA disability is tax-free
  - **CRSC (Combat-Related Special Compensation)**: tax-free combat-related disability payment
- VA disability payments do NOT count as provisional income for Social Security taxation purposes

### Personal Injury Settlements

- Physical injury/illness damages: generally tax-free (Section 104(a)(2))
- Emotional distress damages (not from physical injury): **taxable**
- Punitive damages: **always taxable** regardless of injury type
- Lost wages in a settlement: generally taxable as ordinary income
- Medical expense reimbursement: tax-free (but cannot also deduct those same medical expenses)
- Structured settlements: periodic payments retain the tax treatment of the underlying claim (physical injury = tax-free even on the earnings/interest component)
- Attorney fees: for physical injury, attorney's share reduces the tax-free amount (no separate deduction needed since amount is already excluded)

## ABLE Accounts -- Section 529A

### Overview
- Tax-advantaged savings accounts for individuals with qualifying disabilities
- **Age of onset**: disability must have begun before age 26 (SECURE 2.0 increased to age 46, effective for tax years beginning after December 31, 2025 -- i.e., 2026 onward; verify current effective date)
- Must be eligible for SSI or SSDI, OR have a disability certification meeting SSA criteria

### Tax Benefits
- Contributions are NOT deductible for federal tax (some states offer deductions)
- Earnings grow **tax-free**
- Distributions are **tax-free** when used for Qualified Disability Expenses (QDEs):
  - Housing (rent, mortgage, utilities, property taxes)
  - Education (tuition, books, supplies)
  - Transportation (vehicle purchase/modification, public transit)
  - Health care (medical, dental, vision, mental health, prescription drugs)
  - Assistive technology (screen readers, wheelchairs, hearing aids)
  - Employment support (job coaching, job training, assistive equipment for work)
  - Financial management and administrative services
  - Legal fees related to disability
  - Funeral and burial expenses
- Non-qualified distributions: earnings portion taxable + 10% penalty

### Contribution Limits
- **Annual limit**: $18,000 (2025 -- matches annual gift tax exclusion amount; note: this may differ from the $19,000 gift tax exclusion if ABLE limits are set differently; verify current year)
- **ABLE to Work provision**: employed ABLE account holders NOT participating in an employer retirement plan can contribute additional earned income up to the lesser of: (a) their gross compensation, or (b) the federal poverty level for a 1-person household (~$15,650)
- **Lifetime/total balance limit**: varies by state, typically $100,000 - $500,000+
- **SSI interaction**: balance above $100,000 causes SSI cash benefits to be SUSPENDED (not terminated) until balance drops below $100,000; Medicaid eligibility is NOT affected regardless of balance

### ABLE vs. Special Needs Trust Comparison

| Feature | ABLE Account | Special Needs Trust (SNT) |
|---------|-------------|--------------------------|
| Setup cost | Minimal (online enrollment) | $2,000-$5,000+ (legal fees) |
| Annual contributions | $18,000 (+ ABLE to Work) | Unlimited |
| Total balance for SSI | $100K triggers suspension | No limit (properly structured) |
| Medicaid impact | None (any balance) | None (properly structured) |
| Who controls | Beneficiary (or authorized rep) | Trustee (not beneficiary) |
| Eligible expenses | QDEs (broad) | Supplemental needs only |
| Tax treatment | Tax-free growth and QDE distributions | Trust taxed at compressed rates |
| Medicaid payback | Yes (at death, state can recover) | First-party: yes; Third-party: no |
| Age of onset requirement | Before 26 (46 starting 2026) | None |

### 529-to-ABLE Rollover
- Can roll over from a 529 education savings plan to an ABLE account for the same beneficiary (or a family member with a disability)
- Subject to the annual ABLE contribution limit
- Tax-free rollover
- Useful when education savings are no longer needed due to disability

## Medical Expense Deduction -- Section 213

### Threshold
- Deductible: medical expenses exceeding **7.5% of AGI** (Schedule A itemization required)
- Disabled taxpayers frequently exceed this threshold due to substantial ongoing expenses

### Qualifying Expenses Particularly Relevant to Disability

#### Home Modifications for Accessibility
- Ramps, widened doorways, bathroom grab bars, roll-in showers, lowered countertops
- Elevators, stairlifts, wheelchair lifts
- **Capital improvement rule**: deductible to the extent the cost EXCEEDS any increase in home value
- Example: $20,000 bathroom modification increases home value by $5,000; deductible amount = $15,000
- Maintenance costs of disability-related improvements are FULLY deductible (no value-increase offset)

#### Equipment and Technology
- Wheelchairs (manual and power), prosthetics, orthotics
- Hearing aids, cochlear implants
- Service animals: purchase, training, food, veterinary care, grooming (must be trained for specific medical condition)
- Communication devices, screen readers, specialized software

#### Therapy and Treatment
- Physical therapy, occupational therapy, speech therapy
- Mental health counseling, psychiatric treatment
- Special education for learning-disabled children (if recommended by physician)
- Applied Behavior Analysis (ABA) for autism

#### Long-Term Care
- Long-term care insurance premiums (age-based deductible limits for 2025):
  - Age 40 or under: $480
  - Age 41-50: $900
  - Age 51-60: $1,800
  - Age 61-70: $4,790
  - Age 71+: $5,990
- Long-term care facility costs (nursing home, assisted living) if individual is chronically ill and care is pursuant to a prescribed plan
- In-home attendant care (if primary purpose is medical care)

#### Medical Transportation
- Standard medical mileage rate: $0.22/mile (2025)
- Or actual expenses (gas, tolls, parking)
- Ambulance fees, medical transport services
- Travel to conferences for chronic conditions (registration fees deductible; travel and lodging subject to limits)

## Impairment-Related Work Expenses -- Section 67(d)

### Unique Above-the-Line Treatment
- Expenses necessary for a disabled person to work that are NOT required for non-disabled employees
- **Key advantage**: this is an above-the-line deduction -- NOT subject to the 2% AGI floor, and NOT suspended under TCJA
- This is one of the few remaining employee business expense deductions available 2018-2025

### Qualifying Expenses
- Attendant care at the workplace (reader, interpreter, assistant)
- Special transportation to/from work (if regular transportation is not possible due to disability)
- Modified equipment or tools needed because of disability
- Reader services for blind employees
- Sign language interpreter for deaf employees
- Special furniture, ergonomic modifications

### Reporting
- Employees: Form 2106 and Schedule 1 (above-the-line deduction)
- Self-employed: Schedule C (regular business expense)
- Must not be reimbursed by employer (if partially reimbursed, deduct only the unreimbursed portion)

## Early Retirement Distribution Penalty Exceptions

### Disability Exception -- Section 72(t)(2)(A)(iii)

- Distributions from IRAs, 401(k)s, and other qualified plans before age 59.5 normally carry a 10% early withdrawal penalty
- **Exception**: no 10% penalty if taxpayer meets the disability definition under Section 72(m)(7)
- **Definition**: unable to engage in ANY substantial gainful activity by reason of a medically determinable physical or mental impairment that is expected to:
  - Result in death, OR
  - Be of long-continued and indefinite duration
- This is a STRICTER standard than Social Security disability -- merely being unable to do your previous job is not sufficient
- Must provide physician's statement documenting the condition
- File **Form 5329** to claim the exception -- it is NOT automatic; without Form 5329, the IRS will assess the penalty
- The distribution is still taxable as ordinary income -- the exception is ONLY from the 10% penalty

### Other Relevant Exceptions
- **Medical expenses exceeding 7.5% of AGI**: penalty-free early distributions up to the deductible medical expense amount (whether or not you actually itemize) -- Section 72(t)(2)(B)
- **SEPP/72(t) substantially equal periodic payments**: penalty-free distributions based on life expectancy; must continue for 5 years or until 59.5 (whichever is later)
- **Federal disaster distributions**: special rules per NATURAL-DISASTER.md

### Roth Conversion Opportunity During Disability
- If disability income is low (or tax-free from self-paid premiums), taxable income may be very low
- This creates an opportunity to convert traditional IRA to Roth IRA at low tax brackets
- Fill the 10% and 12% brackets with conversions each year
- Particularly powerful if receiving tax-free disability benefits: convert $30K-$50K/year while paying little or no tax

## Credit for the Elderly or Disabled -- Schedule R

- Available to: individuals age 65+ OR permanently and totally disabled under 65
- **Disability qualification**: retired due to permanent and total disability AND received taxable disability income during the year
- Very restrictive income limits: AGI below $17,500 (single) / $20,000 (MFJ, one qualifying) / $25,000 (MFJ, both qualifying)
- Maximum credit: $750-$1,125 (single) / $1,125-$1,687.50 (MFJ)
- Non-refundable; reduced by non-taxable Social Security and certain pension income
- Rarely applicable due to low thresholds, but check for low-income disabled taxpayers

## Optimization Strategies

1. **Structure disability insurance premiums as after-tax**: pay with post-tax dollars to receive tax-free benefits; the premium cost difference is trivial compared to the tax savings if disabled
2. **Maximize ABLE account contributions**: tax-free growth and withdrawals for broad QDEs; protects SSI and Medicaid eligibility
3. **Document ALL medical expenses meticulously**: disabled taxpayers often exceed the 7.5% AGI threshold; keep receipts for home modifications, equipment, transportation, therapy
4. **Claim impairment-related work expenses**: one of the few employee business expenses still deductible under TCJA
5. **File Form 5329 for the disability penalty exception**: do not leave this on the table -- the 10% penalty on early distributions can be thousands of dollars
6. **Use Section 86(e) for SSDI back pay**: calculate tax both ways and use the method producing less tax
7. **Roth conversions during low-income disability years**: if disability benefits are tax-free, fill low brackets with conversions
8. **Consider Special Needs Trust for large amounts**: when ABLE account limits are insufficient, an SNT provides unlimited supplemental support without affecting benefits
9. **Coordinate workers' comp and SSDI**: understand the offset rules to maximize total after-tax income
10. **Review VA disability rating periodically**: increased rating = increased tax-free benefits and may unlock additional state/local tax exemptions

## Common Mistakes

1. **Assuming all disability income is tax-free** -- it depends entirely on who paid the premiums and with what type of dollars
2. **Not filing Form 5329** -- the disability exception to the 10% early withdrawal penalty must be claimed; it is not automatic
3. **Overlooking home modification deductions** -- ramps, bathroom modifications, elevators, and accessibility improvements are medical expenses (to the extent they exceed home value increase)
4. **Missing the ABLE account opportunity** -- particularly for younger disabled individuals with decades of tax-free growth
5. **Not applying for the IP PIN** -- disabled individuals may be at higher risk for identity theft; the IP PIN prevents fraudulent filing
6. **Forgetting to report SSDI income** -- even if ultimately not taxable, it must be reported to determine the taxable portion
7. **Ignoring the Section 86(e) lump-sum election** -- SSDI back pay taxed entirely in one year can push into higher brackets unnecessarily
8. **Not claiming impairment-related work expenses** -- many disabled taxpayers do not realize this above-the-line deduction survived TCJA
9. **Conflating SSI and SSDI** -- SSI is never taxable; SSDI is potentially taxable; they have completely different rules

## State Considerations

- **State disability income taxation**: most states follow federal treatment, but some have additional exclusions
- **State ABLE programs**: each state sponsors its own program; some offer state tax deductions for contributions; beneficiaries can use any state's program
- **State disability benefits (SDI)**: CA, HI, NJ, NY, RI, and PR have state disability insurance programs
  - State SDI benefits are generally tax-free for federal purposes
  - State treatment varies (CA SDI is tax-free for CA state tax; NJ TDI may be partially taxable at state level)
- **Property tax exemptions**: many states and localities offer property tax reductions for disabled homeowners (separate from income tax; often requires application and documentation)
- **State credits for disabled taxpayers**: some states offer additional credits or deductions beyond federal
- **Homestead exemptions**: many states provide enhanced homestead exemptions for disabled homeowners
