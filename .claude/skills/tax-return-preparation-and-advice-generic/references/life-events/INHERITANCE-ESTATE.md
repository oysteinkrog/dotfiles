# Inheritance and Estate Tax Reference (Tax Year 2025)

## Overview

Inheriting assets or dealing with estate administration involves stepped-up basis rules, estate tax filing obligations, inherited retirement account distribution requirements, and gift tax considerations. The 2025 estate tax exemption is historically high but faces a scheduled sunset. Understanding these rules prevents overpayment of tax on inherited assets and ensures compliance with complex distribution timelines.

## Tax Implications

### Stepped-Up Basis -- IRC Section 1014

**General rule**: inherited property receives a basis equal to its fair market value (FMV) at the date of the decedent's death. This eliminates all unrealized capital gains that accrued during the decedent's lifetime.

**Mechanics in detail**:
- Basis = FMV at date of death (default)
- **Alternate valuation date (IRC Section 2032)**: estate executor can elect to value assets 6 months after death instead. Only available if (a) the election reduces both the gross estate value AND the estate tax liability, and (b) a Form 706 is required. All assets must use the same date (some exceptions for assets sold/distributed within 6 months -- those use date of sale/distribution).
- **Step-down**: if property has declined in value, basis also adjusts to the lower FMV at death (no artificial loss preservation)

**Worked example**:
- Decedent purchased stock in 1985 for $10,000. FMV at death (March 15, 2025) is $500,000.
- Heir's basis = $500,000. If sold immediately for $500,000, NO capital gain.
- If held and sold 2 years later for $550,000, gain = $50,000 (only post-death appreciation).

**Applies to**: real estate, stocks, bonds, business interests, personal property, collectibles, partnership interests, rental property (depreciation recapture is eliminated by the step-up)

**Does NOT apply to (IRC Section 1014(c))**: Income in Respect of a Decedent (IRD) -- items like IRA/401(k) balances, unpaid salary, accrued interest, installment sale receivables

### Community Property Full Step-Up

**In community property states (AZ, CA, ID, LA, NV, NM, TX, WA, WI), BOTH halves of community property receive a stepped-up basis when one spouse dies** -- not just the decedent's half.

**Worked example**:
- Husband and wife in California jointly own stock purchased for $100,000 (community property). FMV at husband's death = $1,000,000.
- **Common law state**: only husband's half gets step-up. Surviving wife's basis = $50,000 (her half, no step-up) + $500,000 (husband's half, stepped up) = $550,000. If sold for $1,000,000, gain = $450,000.
- **Community property state**: BOTH halves step up. Surviving wife's basis = $500,000 + $500,000 = $1,000,000. If sold for $1,000,000, gain = $0.
- **Tax savings**: at 23.8% (20% LTCG + 3.8% NIIT), the CP step-up saves $107,100 in this example.

This is one of the most valuable tax benefits in the code. For couples in community property states with highly appreciated assets, the full step-up at first death can save hundreds of thousands.

### Income in Respect of a Decedent (IRD) -- IRC Section 691

IRD consists of income that was earned by or owed to the decedent but not yet received or included in income before death. IRD does NOT receive a stepped-up basis.

**Common IRD items**:
- Traditional IRA and 401(k) balances (the entire balance is IRD)
- Accrued salary, wages, commissions owed to the decedent
- Installment sale payments not yet received
- Accrued bond interest
- Unpaid rent
- Deferred compensation

**Tax treatment of IRD**:
- Taxed as ordinary income to the recipient (heir, estate, or trust) when received
- Character of income carries through (ordinary income remains ordinary, etc.)

**IRC Section 691(c) deduction**: If estate tax was paid on the IRD items, the recipient gets an income tax deduction for the estate tax attributable to the IRD. This prevents full double taxation (estate tax + income tax on the same dollars).

**Worked example -- Section 691(c)**:
- Decedent's estate includes a $1,000,000 traditional IRA. Estate pays 40% estate tax on amounts above exemption.
- Assume $400,000 of estate tax is attributable to the IRA.
- When heir takes IRA distributions, heir includes distributions in income BUT also claims a Section 691(c) deduction for the proportionate estate tax paid.
- If heir takes a $100,000 distribution, the 691(c) deduction = $40,000 ($400,000 x $100,000/$1,000,000).
- Net taxable: $60,000 instead of $100,000.

## Estate Tax -- IRC Section 2001 (Form 706)

### 2025 Exemption
- **Basic exclusion amount**: $13,990,000 per person (indexed for inflation)
- **Married couples**: effectively $27,980,000 with portability (DSUE -- Deceased Spousal Unused Exclusion)
- **Tax rate**: 40% on amounts exceeding the exemption
- **Computation**: Gross estate - deductions = taxable estate x 40% - unified credit = estate tax due

### TCJA Sunset
- The doubled exemption is scheduled to revert to approximately $7,000,000 (indexed) per person after December 31, 2025
- Unless Congress acts, estates will face a much lower threshold starting in 2026
- **Planning urgency**: use the high exemption now through lifetime gifts or irrevocable trusts
- **Anti-clawback rule**: Treasury has confirmed that gifts made using the high exemption will NOT be clawed back if the exemption decreases

### Estate Tax Computation -- Detailed

**Step 1: Gross Estate (IRC Section 2031)**
Includes everything the decedent owned or had an interest in:
- Real estate (full FMV, even if mortgaged)
- Stocks, bonds, mutual funds, brokerage accounts
- Bank accounts, CDs, money market
- Life insurance proceeds (if policy owned by decedent or decedent had incidents of ownership -- IRC Section 2042)
- Jointly held property (portion included depends on contribution rules or 50% for spouses)
- Revocable trust assets (entire trust included)
- Retirement accounts (IRA, 401(k), pension)
- Annuities (present value of future payments)
- Certain powers of appointment (general powers -- IRC Section 2041)
- Transfers with retained interests (IRC Section 2036, 2037, 2038)

**Step 2: Deductions**
- Funeral expenses and estate administration costs
- Debts of the decedent (mortgage, credit cards, medical bills)
- Charitable bequests (unlimited deduction)
- **Marital deduction (IRC Section 2056)**: unlimited for transfers to surviving U.S. citizen spouse
- State death taxes (limited deduction or credit depending on year)

**Step 3: Tax Calculation**
- Taxable estate x graduated rates (18%-40%)
- Subtract unified credit ($5,389,800 for 2025, which shelters the first $13,990,000)
- Result = estate tax payable

### Portability -- DSUEA (Deceased Spousal Unused Exclusion Amount)

**What it is**: the surviving spouse can use the deceased spouse's unused estate tax exemption, effectively doubling the available exemption.

**Critical requirement**: portability is NOT automatic. The executor MUST file Form 706 (estate tax return) even if no estate tax is due and the estate is below the filing threshold.

**Mechanics**:
- First spouse dies in 2025 with a $5,000,000 estate. Exemption used: $5,000,000. Unused: $8,990,000.
- If Form 706 is filed electing portability, the surviving spouse's available exemption = $13,990,000 (own) + $8,990,000 (DSUE) = $22,980,000.
- If Form 706 is NOT filed: surviving spouse only has their own $13,990,000.

**Deadline**: Form 706 is due 9 months after death (with 6-month extension via Form 4768). For portability-only filings, there is a simplified late filing procedure (Rev. Proc. 2022-32) available within 5 years of death.

**Limitation**: DSUE does NOT grow with inflation. It is fixed at the amount unused at the first spouse's death. Also, only the LAST deceased spouse's DSUE is available (remarriage complication).

### Filing Requirements
- Form 706 required if gross estate exceeds $13,990,000 (2025)
- Due 9 months after date of death; 6-month extension available (Form 4768)
- Also file for portability even if below threshold
- Estate gets its own EIN (Form SS-4)

## Generation-Skipping Transfer (GST) Tax

- Separate 40% tax on transfers to individuals more than one generation below the transferor (grandchildren, great-grandchildren, unrelated individuals 37.5+ years younger)
- **GST exemption**: $13,990,000 per person (2025, same as estate exemption)
- Applies to: direct gifts to skip persons, transfers in trust for skip persons, distributions from trusts to skip persons (taxable distributions and taxable terminations)
- GST exemption must be allocated on Form 709 or Form 706
- **Critical difference from estate tax**: GST exemption is NOT portable between spouses
- **Planning**: GST exemption should be allocated to trust transfers during lifetime to shelter future appreciation

## Gift Tax -- IRC Section 2501 (Form 709)

### Annual Exclusion
- **$19,000 per recipient for 2025** ($38,000 for married couples gift-splitting)
- Applies per recipient, per year -- can give $19,000 to an unlimited number of people annually
- No filing required for gifts within the annual exclusion (unless gift-splitting with spouse)

### Lifetime Exemption
- **Unified with estate tax exemption**: $13,990,000 total
- Gifts exceeding the annual exclusion reduce the remaining estate tax exemption dollar-for-dollar
- Report on Form 709 (due April 15 of the year following the gift)
- Gifts to spouse: unlimited marital deduction (no gift tax, no Form 709 needed, unless spouse is not a U.S. citizen -- then $185,000 annual limit for 2025)
- Gifts to charity: unlimited (no gift tax)

### Gifts of Appreciated Property
- Recipient takes the donor's basis (carryover basis) -- no step-up
- Better to hold and transfer at death (gets stepped-up basis) than to gift during lifetime
- **Exception**: if asset is expected to appreciate significantly further, gifting removes future appreciation from the estate

## Inherited Retirement Accounts -- Post-SECURE Act

### Eligible Designated Beneficiaries (EDBs) -- Special Rules Apply

1. **Surviving spouse**: most favorable treatment
   - **Option A**: Roll to own IRA -- no RMDs until age 73/75; treated as if it was always the surviving spouse's account
   - **Option B**: Keep as inherited IRA -- take RMDs based on single life expectancy, recalculated annually; useful if under 59.5 (avoids 10% penalty on distributions)
   - **Option C**: Elect 10-year rule
2. **Minor children of the decedent** (not grandchildren, not minor nieces/nephews): stretch RMDs until age 21, then 10-year clock starts
3. **Disabled individuals** (IRC Section 72(m)(7)): life expectancy stretch permitted
4. **Chronically ill individuals**: life expectancy stretch permitted
5. **Individuals not more than 10 years younger than the decedent**: life expectancy stretch permitted

### Designated Beneficiaries (Non-EDB) -- 10-Year Rule

- Must distribute the ENTIRE account by December 31 of the 10th year following the year of death
- **If the decedent died on or after the Required Beginning Date (RBD)**: annual RMDs are required during the 10-year period (IRS final regulations, 2024), PLUS full distribution by end of year 10
- **If the decedent died before the RBD**: no annual RMDs required during the 10 years; just empty the account by year 10
- Distributions are taxable as ordinary income (traditional IRA)
- Strategy: spread distributions evenly over 10 years to avoid bracket spikes; time distributions for lower-income years

**Worked example -- 10-year rule optimization**:
- Heir inherits a $500,000 traditional IRA in 2025. Decedent died before RBD.
- Option A: Take $500,000 in year 10. At 32% bracket: ~$160,000 in tax.
- Option B: Take $50,000/year for 10 years. At 22-24% bracket: ~$110,000-$120,000 in tax.
- Savings from spreading: ~$40,000-$50,000 in federal tax alone.

### Inherited Roth IRA

- **Spouse**: can roll to own Roth IRA (no RMDs ever); or keep as inherited (10-year rule or life expectancy if EDB)
- **Non-spouse EDB**: life expectancy distributions (tax-free)
- **Non-spouse non-EDB**: 10-year rule; distributions are tax-free but the account must be emptied by year 10
- **Strategy**: delay Roth distributions until year 10 to maximize tax-free growth (no penalty for not taking distributions during years 1-9 if decedent died before RBD)
- Even though no tax is owed, the 10-year deadline still applies and the 25% penalty for failure to distribute applies to Roth inherited IRAs

### Trust as IRA Beneficiary

When a trust is named as IRA beneficiary, the rules depend on trust type:

**Conduit (pass-through) trust**:
- Trust is required to pass all IRA distributions through to the individual beneficiary immediately
- IRS "looks through" the trust to the individual beneficiary for purposes of the distribution rules
- If the beneficiary is an EDB, life expectancy stretch is available
- If non-EDB, 10-year rule applies
- Advantage: avoids trust's compressed tax brackets (37% at $15,650)
- Disadvantage: no asset protection; distributions go directly to beneficiary

**Accumulation trust**:
- Trust CAN accumulate IRA distributions (does not have to pass them through)
- IRS looks through to ALL potential beneficiaries (including remainder beneficiaries) to determine the applicable rule
- If ANY potential beneficiary is not an EDB, the 10-year rule applies
- Income retained in trust is taxed at trust rates (37% at just $15,650 in 2025)
- Advantage: asset protection; control over distributions to beneficiary
- Disadvantage: compressed brackets make it very expensive to retain income in the trust

**See-through trust requirements** (for the trust to qualify as a designated beneficiary):
1. Trust must be valid under state law
2. Trust must be irrevocable (or become irrevocable at death)
3. Trust beneficiaries must be identifiable from the trust document
4. Copy of trust must be provided to the IRA custodian by October 31 of the year following death

### Inherited 401(k)/403(b)
- Similar rules to inherited IRA but administered by the plan
- Many plans require lump-sum distribution (no stretch option) -- check plan document
- If plan forces lump sum: can roll to an inherited IRA to preserve 10-year or life expectancy option
- Must be completed within a reasonable time after death

### Non-Designated Beneficiaries (Estate, Charity, Non-Qualifying Trust)
- If decedent died before RBD: 5-year rule (distribute all by end of 5th year following death)
- If decedent died on/after RBD: distribute over remaining life expectancy of decedent (declining balance)

## Inherited Property

### Real Estate
- Stepped-up basis to FMV at date of death
- Get an appraisal as of the date of death to establish basis
- If sold promptly: little or no gain (basis approximately equals sale price)
- If held: future appreciation is taxable gain above the stepped-up basis
- Rental income: report on Schedule E (or Schedule C if providing services); begin depreciation from the stepped-up basis over a new recovery period
- Section 121 exclusion does NOT apply to inherited property unless the heir has lived in it as a primary residence for 2+ of the 5 years before sale

### Stocks and Securities
- Basis steps up to FMV on date of death (or alternate valuation date)
- Obtain historical price data for the date of death from brokerage or financial data services
- Mutual funds: basis is the NAV per share on the date of death times the number of shares
- Brokerage may or may not carry over the stepped-up basis automatically -- verify and correct if needed

## Trust Income (Forms 1041 and K-1)

### Trusts as Entities
- Trust receives income; distributes to beneficiaries according to trust terms
- **Form 1041**: trust income tax return (trust pays tax on retained income)
- **Schedule K-1 (Form 1041)**: passes through income to beneficiaries
- Trust tax rates are highly compressed: 37% bracket starts at just $15,650 (2025) -- strong incentive to distribute income to beneficiaries in lower brackets

### Distribution Deduction
- Trust gets a deduction for income distributed to beneficiaries (income taxed to beneficiaries, not the trust)
- Distributable Net Income (DNI) determines the character and amount of the distribution deduction
- Beneficiary reports their share on their personal return

## Optimization Strategies

1. **Appraise everything at death**: establish the stepped-up basis with documentation; photograph, appraise, and record FMV of all inherited assets
2. **File Form 706 for portability even if no estate tax is due**: preserves the deceased spouse's unused exemption for the surviving spouse. Use the simplified late election if within 5 years.
3. **Time inherited IRA distributions**: spread over 10 years to smooth tax brackets; take more in low-income years, less in high-income years
4. **Use stepped-up basis strategically**: sell inherited assets with large step-up soon after death to lock in the tax-free gain; reinvest in desired portfolio
5. **Inherited Roth IRA**: delay distributions to maximize tax-free growth (if no annual RMD required)
6. **Gifting during lifetime**: use the $19,000 annual exclusion and the high lifetime exemption before potential sunset
7. **Consider disclaiming (IRC Section 2518)**: beneficiary can disclaim (refuse) an inheritance within 9 months of death to redirect assets to the next beneficiary in line (may achieve better tax result or keep assets in a protected trust)
8. **Claim the Section 691(c) deduction**: if estate tax was paid on IRD items, the income tax deduction can be substantial
9. **Community property full step-up**: for couples in CP states, ensure all community property is properly characterized to maximize the full step-up at first death

## Common Mistakes

1. **Not getting a date-of-death appraisal** -- without it, you cannot substantiate the stepped-up basis; especially critical for real estate, closely held businesses, and collectibles
2. **Paying capital gains on inherited assets using the decedent's original basis** -- the step-up eliminates most or all of the gain
3. **Missing the 10-year distribution deadline on inherited IRAs** -- the 25% penalty (reduced from 50% by SECURE 2.0) on the full remaining balance is devastating
4. **Confusing the 10-year rule with the old stretch IRA** -- post-2020 rules are fundamentally different for most non-spouse beneficiaries
5. **Not filing Form 706 for portability** -- surviving spouse permanently loses the deceased spouse's unused exemption
6. **Failing to take required annual distributions during the 10-year period** -- if the decedent died after their RBD, annual RMDs are required even under the 10-year rule
7. **Gifting appreciated assets instead of holding for step-up** -- during lifetime, gifts carry over the donor's basis; at death, the step-up eliminates the gain
8. **Naming the estate as IRA beneficiary** -- triggers 5-year rule instead of 10-year rule; loses stretch for EDBs
9. **Not claiming the Section 691(c) deduction** -- heirs who pay income tax on IRD and whose estate also paid estate tax on those same items miss a significant deduction
10. **Forgetting the GST exemption is not portable** -- unlike estate tax exemption, both spouses must separately allocate GST exemption during lifetime

## State Considerations

- **State estate/inheritance taxes**: many states have much lower exemptions than federal:
  - Estate tax states: CT ($13.99M), DC ($4.71M), HI ($5.49M), IL ($4M), MA ($2M), ME ($6.8M), MD ($5M), MN ($3M), NY ($6.94M cliff), OR ($1M), RI ($1.77M), VT ($5M), WA ($2.19M)
  - Inheritance tax states (tax on the beneficiary, not the estate): IA (phasing out by 2025), KY, MD (both estate AND inheritance), NE, NJ, PA
- **New York estate tax cliff**: if the estate exceeds 105% of the exemption ($6.94M), the ENTIRE estate is taxed (not just the excess) -- extremely punitive. An estate of $7.3M could face ~$500K+ in NY estate tax that would have been $0 at $6.94M.
- **Community property step-up**: in community property states, BOTH halves of community property get a stepped-up basis at first death. This can save hundreds of thousands in capital gains tax.
- **State trust situs**: trust income tax varies by state; some states tax based on trustee location, others on beneficiary residence, others on grantor domicile. Moving a trust to a no-income-tax state can save significant state income tax on undistributed income.
- **Pennsylvania inheritance tax**: flat rates by relationship -- 0% (spouse), 4.5% (direct descendants), 12% (siblings), 15% (others). Applies regardless of estate size.
- **New Jersey inheritance tax**: 0% for Class A beneficiaries (spouse, children, parents), 11-16% for Class C/D. First $25,000 exempt for Class C.
