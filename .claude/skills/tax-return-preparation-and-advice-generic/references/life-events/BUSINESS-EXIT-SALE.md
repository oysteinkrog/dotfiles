# Business Exit / Sale -- Life Event Guide (TY 2025)

## 1. Why This Life Event Is So High Stakes

A business exit is the kind of tax event where a few documents signed in the wrong order can burn
seven figures of value. The tax outcome is not determined just by the final price. It is driven
by:
- asset sale vs stock sale,
- C-Corp vs S-Corp vs partnership treatment,
- purchase-price allocation,
- installment and earnout design,
- goodwill characterization,
- QSBS / Section 1202 eligibility,
- state residency and sourcing,
- whether planning happens **before** the deal becomes binding.

This guide is the event-driven checklist. It is for the period when a founder, owner, partner, or
rep is actively moving toward a sale, redemption, ESOP, management buyout, merger, or wind-down.

---

## 2. The First Question: What Kind of Exit Is Actually Happening?

### True Third-Party Sale
- Sale to strategic buyer, PE sponsor, competitor, roll-up, or independent acquirer.
- Core tax question: stock/equity sale, asset sale, or deemed asset sale election.

### Internal Exit
- Buyout by co-owner, family transition, management buyout, redemption, or installment buyout.
- Core tax question: sale, redemption, compensation, or disguised dividend?

### Shut Down / Liquidation
- Business is not sold as a going concern; assets are sold off and entity winds down.
- Core tax question: ordinary vs capital character, final returns, payroll closeout, and debt or
  cancellation-of-indebtedness consequences.

### Partial Liquidity
- Recap, dividend, secondary sale, rollover equity, or earnout-heavy transaction.
- Core tax question: which piece is taxed now, which later, and which as wages/compensation?

---

## 3. The Non-Negotiable Rule: Planning Must Happen Before the Deal Is Binding

The highest-EV exit moves usually die once there is a binding obligation to sell.

### Actions That Usually Need To Happen Early
- Confirm or preserve QSBS eligibility.
- Evaluate a residency change if state tax arbitrage is realistic and genuinely supportable.
- Set up charitable planning before the sale becomes inevitable.
- Fix cap-table, basis, and stock-issuance records.
- Analyze whether a reorganization, cleanup, or election should happen before LOI.
- Decide whether earnouts, rollover equity, consulting, or noncompete payments should be handled
  separately.

### Red-Flag Mistake
- Owner signs LOI or reaches a practically binding deal, then asks whether they can "just gift the
  shares to a trust/DAF/charity now." Often too late, or much riskier.

---

## 4. Exit Readiness Checklist

Before modeling tax, build a source-of-truth package:

### Ownership and Basis
- Formation docs, operating agreement, bylaws, shareholder/partnership agreements.
- Cap table and all amendments.
- Stock certificates, option exercises, SAFE/convertible-note conversion records.
- Shareholder basis schedules, debt basis, partner capital accounts.
- Section 83(b) elections, if relevant.

### Asset and Liability Support
- Fixed-asset ledger and depreciation schedules.
- Inventory, receivables, deferred revenue, contingent liabilities.
- Debt payoff amounts and lien releases.
- Prior-year returns for NOLs, credit carryforwards, built-in gains, suspended losses, and PTET.

### Deal Documents
- LOI, term sheet, purchase agreement drafts.
- Purchase-price allocation schedule.
- Earnout formula.
- Employment, consulting, noncompete, and rollover-equity agreements.

### State and Residency Evidence
- Domicile and residency history.
- Entity apportionment factors.
- Work-location and office history.

## 5. Deal Structure Matrix

| Structure | Seller Preference | Buyer Preference | Main Seller Risk |
|---|---|---|---|
| Stock / equity sale | Usually best | Usually worst | Buyer discounts price for lack of step-up |
| Asset sale | Usually worse | Usually best | Ordinary income and recapture |
| 338(h)(10) / 336(e)-style compromise | Mixed | Often attractive | Deemed asset-sale economics |
| Installment sale | Useful for deferral | Mixed | Seller credit risk; Section 453A interest |
| Earnout | Can bridge valuation gap | Often attractive | Recharacterization as compensation |
| Redemption / buyout | Situational | N/A | Dividend vs sale treatment issues |

---

## 6. The Highest-EV Tax Questions To Answer

### C-Corp vs S-Corp vs Partnership
- C-Corp sellers must model double-tax risk on asset sales.
- S-Corp sellers must model ordinary-income buckets, built-in gains history, and shareholder
  basis.
- Partnership/LLC sellers must model hot assets, Section 751 ordinary income, and debt allocation.

### Asset Allocation
- Purchase price allocated to inventory, receivables, depreciation-recapturable assets,
  covenant-not-to-compete, customer lists, software/IP, real estate, and goodwill can move tax by
  huge amounts.
- The seller should not treat the allocation exhibit as boilerplate. It is often the tax deal.

### Compensation vs Purchase Price
- Earnouts tied to personal services, stay bonuses, consulting arrangements, and noncompete
  payments can convert capital gain into ordinary income plus payroll tax.
- Separate real purchase-price economics from post-close labor economics.

### State Tax
- A state move made too late or without true fact-pattern support usually fails.
- Multi-state apportionment and state sourcing may matter even when the owner personally moved.

## 7. The Sophisticated Planning Levers

### QSBS / Section 1202
- If the stock qualifies, confirm holding period, original issuance, gross-asset tests, and active
  business requirements before the exit advances.
- If multiple owners or trusts are involved, basis and exclusion stacking may materially change the
  after-tax result.

### Charitable Planning Before Sale
- Donating appreciated equity **before** the sale is binding can eliminate capital gain on the
  donated slice and create a charitable deduction.
- Common tools:
  - direct gift to public charity,
  - donor-advised fund,
  - charitable remainder trust in the right fact pattern.

### Installment Design
- Useful when the seller wants tax deferral and can tolerate buyer credit risk.
- Must model interest, acceleration of recapture, and whether Section 453A interest charge erodes
  the benefit on larger deals.

### Residency and Domicile Planning
- Only attempt if genuinely supportable and started early enough.
- A rushed move with unchanged home, family, business, and social ties is often worse than not
  attempting the strategy.

### Rollover Equity
- Not "tax-free by default."
- Need to understand whether cash-out and rollover pieces are separate, whether any piece is
  compensation, and how basis carries into the new equity.

## 8. Exit Timeline

### 12-24 Months Before Close
- Clean up books, basis records, and cap table.
- Evaluate entity and ownership structure.
- Assess QSBS, trust, and gifting opportunities.
- Review state residency and apportionment.

### 3-9 Months Before Close
- Model stock vs asset outcomes.
- Negotiate allocation principles early.
- Decide whether charitable, estate, or residency moves are still viable.
- Build the tax workstream into the LOI and purchase agreement process.

### LOI to Signing
- Freeze key facts in a tax memo.
- Keep employment/consulting/noncompete economics separate from purchase price.
- Review estimated taxes and liquidity.
- Prepare for attachments, statements, and post-close reporting.

### Closing Through Filing
- Track escrow, holdback, earnout, indemnity, and true-up mechanics.
- Preserve executed agreements and allocation schedules.
- Reconcile the tax return to the legal documents, not just the wire amount.

---

## 9. Common Mistakes

1. Negotiating price while ignoring tax character.
2. Accepting the buyer's allocation with no seller-side analysis.
3. Failing to document basis and ownership history before diligence begins.
4. Treating earnout, consulting, and noncompete payments as interchangeable.
5. Trying to implement QSBS, charitable, or trust planning after the deal is effectively locked.
6. Modeling only federal tax and forgetting state tax.
7. Forgetting installment-sale interest, recapture acceleration, or buyer credit risk.
8. Not reconciling escrow and holdback mechanics to the reporting year.

---

## 10. Deliverables the Skill Should Produce

For a real exit, the skill should generate:
- a structure memo comparing seller after-tax proceeds under each viable structure,
- an issue list for counsel and CPA,
- a purchase-price allocation review,
- a deadline calendar,
- an estimated-tax and cash-reserve plan,
- a filing checklist for the closing year and any earnout years.
