# Moving Between States Tax Reference (Tax Year 2025)

## Overview

Moving from one state to another during the tax year triggers part-year resident filing obligations in both the origin and destination states. Proper income allocation, timely domicile establishment, and estimated payment adjustments are critical. States have different rules for sourcing income, different tax rates, and different levels of aggressiveness in claiming residents. Careful planning around the move date can yield significant tax savings.

## Domicile vs. Statutory Residency

These are two distinct concepts that states use to claim you as a resident. Understanding both is essential.

### Domicile
- Your permanent, legal home -- the place you intend to return to when away
- You can only have ONE domicile at a time
- Changed by **physical presence** in the new location PLUS **intent** to make it your permanent home
- Once established, domicile persists until you affirmatively establish a new one
- Actions that demonstrate intent: voter registration, driver's license, vehicle registration, where you maintain your primary residence, where your family lives, estate planning documents referencing the new state

### Statutory Residency
- A state can claim you as a statutory resident even if you are NOT domiciled there
- Typical test: **183+ days of physical presence** AND maintaining a **permanent place of abode** in the state
- This means you can be a DUAL RESIDENT -- domiciled in State A but a statutory resident of State B
- Both states may claim full-year taxing rights; you rely on credits for taxes paid to the other state to avoid full double taxation

### Day-Counting Rules -- They Vary by State
- **New York**: counts ANY part of a day as a full day. If you arrive in NY at 11:30 PM, that is a full NY day. 183 days + permanent place of abode = statutory resident.
- **California**: does NOT use a fixed day-count test for residency. Uses totality of circumstances and domicile analysis. There is no safe number of days below which you are guaranteed to be a nonresident.
- **Connecticut**: 183 days + maintaining a permanent place of abode = statutory resident
- **New Jersey**: 183 days of domicile in the state (but no statutory residency concept separate from domicile)
- **Illinois**: 183 days of presence = presumed resident
- Some states count partial days, others require overnight presence, others use the "any part of a day" rule. Check specific state rules.

### "Closer Connection" Test
- Concept from Form 8840 (for aliens claiming closer connection to a foreign country) but applies domestically in practice
- If you maintain ties to two states, the state where you have the "closer connection" is your domicile
- Factors: location of permanent home, family, personal belongings, social/political/cultural/religious organizations, business activities, driver's license, voter registration, where you file as a resident

## Tax Implications

### Part-Year Returns
- **File a part-year resident return in both states**: the former state (for the period you lived there) and the new state (for the period after the move)
- Each state taxes income earned during your period of residency PLUS any income sourced to that state regardless of residency
- Wage allocation: typically based on days worked in each state or income earned during each residency period
- Investment income, retirement distributions, and other non-sourced income: usually allocated based on residency period (days or months)
- Standard deduction and personal exemption: usually prorated by the portion of the year as a resident

### Income Allocation Methods

#### Wages and Salary
- **Actual days method**: allocate wages based on workdays in each state divided by total workdays
  - Example: Moved from CA to TX on July 1. Worked 125 days in CA, 127 days in TX. Total wages $200,000. CA-source wages = $200,000 x (125/252) = $99,206.
- **Paycheck-by-paycheck method**: allocate based on which paychecks were earned while resident in each state
  - Some states require this method; others allow either
- **Remote work complication**: if you continue working for the same employer remotely, the convenience-of-employer rule in some states (NY, PA, NJ, CT) may source income to the employer's state regardless of where you work
- **Timing of bonus/commission**: generally sourced to the state of residency when earned (not when paid); large bonuses may need to be allocated based on services rendered during the period in each state

#### Investment Income
- **General rule**: investment income follows domicile date, not the date the income was earned or accrued
- Capital gains on stock sold after the move: taxed by the new state (state of residence at time of sale)
- Dividends and interest received after the move: taxed by the new state
- **Planning opportunity**: time large capital gain realizations for after moving to a low/no-tax state
- **Exception**: some states source capital gains to the state where the underlying business operates (e.g., gains from sale of a business with operations in the former state)

#### Retirement Income
- **Generally follows residency at time of distribution** -- taxed by the state where you live when you receive the distribution
- **Federal pension protection (4 USC Section 114)**: prohibits states from taxing retirement income of nonresidents -- your old state CANNOT tax your pension or 401(k) distributions just because you earned the benefits there
- **Deferred compensation and stock options**: some states may attempt to source based on where services were performed; allocation may be required

#### Real Property Income
- **Always sourced to the state where the property is located**, regardless of where you live
- Rental income from property in your old state: reported on old state nonresident return even after moving
- Capital gain from selling old state property: taxed by old state as source income

#### Business Income for Multi-State Businesses
- **Sole proprietor with clients in multiple states**: may need to apportion income based on where services are performed or where customers are located
- **Pass-through entities (S-Corp, partnership)**: K-1 income may be sourced based on entity's apportionment factors (sales, payroll, property)
- If the business location does not change with the move: business income may remain sourced to the old state regardless of personal move

### Safe Harbor Timelines -- How Long Before You Are "Safe"

**New York 548-day safe harbor**:
- If you leave NY and spend fewer than 90 days in NY during ANY of the next 3 years AND do not maintain a permanent place of abode in NY, you are safe from statutory residency claims after the 548-day period
- This is the gold standard of safe harbors but requires strict compliance

**California -- no formal safe harbor**:
- CA uses totality of circumstances. There is no number of days or time period that guarantees safety
- FTB can audit and challenge residency changes for years after the move
- Strongest factor: selling the CA home. Maintaining a CA home is the #1 factor in residency audits.

**General guidance**: most practitioners consider 18-24 months of clean separation (no home, limited visits, updated documentation) as the practical threshold for most states

## State Audit Triggers for High-Income Movers

States most aggressive in pursuing departing high-income residents:

### New York
- Tracks departing residents earning $1M+
- Residency audits are common and thorough (inspect cell phone records, credit card statements, EZ-Pass records, social media)
- NY auditors use a "primary factor" and "other factor" analysis across 5 categories
- **NY audit defense**: keep a contemporaneous calendar/diary of daily location. Cell phone GPS data can prove or disprove your location.

### California (Franchise Tax Board)
- No safe harbor rule; totality of circumstances test
- FTB actively audits departing high-income residents
- Particularly aggressive with tech executives, entertainers, and business owners
- **CA audit defense**: document every tie severed and every new tie established in the destination state

### New Jersey
- Requires an exit-year return
- Can challenge domicile change with audit
- Less aggressive than NY/CA but still pursues high-income departures

### Connecticut, Massachusetts, Minnesota
- Active residency audit programs
- Lower thresholds for triggering audits

### What Auditors Look For
States use a comprehensive multi-factor analysis. Be prepared to document:
- **Primary residence**: where did you sleep most nights? Where is your permanent home?
- **Active business involvement**: where do you work, meet clients, conduct business?
- **Time spent**: day counts, travel records, EZ-Pass, airline tickets, credit card receipts
- **Items near and dear**: where are your family, pets, safe deposit box, art, valuables?
- **Family connections**: where does your spouse live/work? Where do your children go to school?
- **Financial ties**: where are your bank accounts, financial advisors, accountants, attorneys?
- **Social/religious**: where do you attend church, belong to clubs, socialize?
- **Documentation to maintain**: contemporaneous calendar or diary of daily location; keep travel receipts, boarding passes, hotel receipts. Cell phone location data is increasingly used by auditors.

### Audit Risk by Income Level
- Under $200K: low risk of residency audit (cost of audit exceeds potential tax recovery)
- $200K-$500K: moderate risk in aggressive states
- $500K-$1M: elevated risk in NY, CA, NJ
- Over $1M: high risk. NY and CA routinely audit departing seven-figure earners.
- Capital gains events (business sale, stock sale) that coincide with a move: extremely high risk regardless of income level

## Telecommuting and Remote Work -- Special State Issues

### Source Rules for Remote Workers
- **General rule**: wages are sourced to where the work is physically performed
- **Convenience of employer rule (NY, CT, NJ, PA)**: if you work remotely for an employer in these states for your own convenience (not the employer's necessity), the income is sourced to the employer's state
- **Post-COVID**: many states issued temporary guidance during COVID; most have reverted to pre-COVID rules. Check current state guidance.

### Multi-State Remote Work Scenarios
- **Scenario 1**: You live in NJ, employer is in NY, you work from home. Under NY's convenience rule, your wages are NY-source. NJ taxes you as a resident. NJ gives credit for NY taxes paid. You pay the higher of the two rates.
- **Scenario 2**: You live in FL (no tax), employer is in NY, you work from home. NY claims your wages under the convenience rule. You owe NY tax with no credit anywhere.
- **Scenario 3**: You live in TX, employer is in TX, you occasionally travel to CA for meetings. CA may tax the wages earned on days you worked in CA (even a few days can trigger a filing requirement for high earners).

## Property Tax Considerations

### Pro-Rating
- Property tax in the old state: deductible for the period you owned/occupied the property
- Property tax in the new state: deductible for the period you own/occupy
- Closing statement shows pro-rated property tax allocation between buyer and seller
- Both amounts contribute to the personal SALT cap on Schedule A

### Homestead Exemptions
- Cancel homestead exemption in old state (or it may auto-cancel upon sale)
- Apply for homestead exemption in new state (deadlines vary; some require application by January 1 or March 1)
- Missing the deadline can cost hundreds or thousands in property tax for the first year

### Capital Gains on Home Sale
- Section 121 exclusion: exclude up to $250,000 (single) / $500,000 (MFJ) of gain on sale of primary residence
- Must have owned AND used the home as primary residence for 2 of the last 5 years
- If you sell the old state home as part of the move: the gain (above exclusion) is sourced to the state where the property is located
- If you sell at a loss: personal residence losses are NOT deductible
- **Timing consideration**: if you convert the old home to a rental before selling, different rules apply (partial exclusion, depreciation recapture)

### State-Specific Property Tax Rates
States vary enormously in property tax rates. Moving can significantly affect your property tax burden:
- High property tax states: NJ (~2.2%), IL (~2.1%), CT (~2.0%), NH (~1.9%), TX (~1.7%)
- Low property tax states: HI (~0.3%), AL (~0.4%), CO (~0.5%), SC (~0.6%)
- Some states cap property tax increases (CA Prop 13, FL Save Our Homes) -- moving resets your assessed value

## Estimated Payment Adjustments

### Transition Steps
1. **Old state**: stop or reduce estimated payments for the remaining quarters after the move (if no longer earning income sourced to that state)
2. **New state**: begin estimated payments in the quarter of (or following) the move
3. **Recalculate**: total expected tax liability in each state for the partial year
4. **Safe harbor**: most states follow federal rules (100% of prior year tax or 90% of current year); but if you had no liability in the new state in the prior year, some states have different requirements for new filers

### Mid-Year Adjustment Strategy
- If you move in Q2, you may have already made Q1 and Q2 estimated payments to the old state
- Reduce or eliminate Q3 and Q4 payments to the old state
- Start Q3 and Q4 payments to the new state based on projected income for the partial year
- Some states allow you to annualize income to reduce penalty exposure on the part-year return

### Common Pitfall
- If you move to a no-income-tax state (FL, TX, NV, etc.): ensure you stop estimated payments to the old state only after actually establishing domicile
- If you move from a no-income-tax state to an income-tax state: begin estimated payments immediately; the old state had no payments to stop

## Health Insurance Marketplace Changes

- Moving to a new state is a **Qualifying Life Event** that triggers a Special Enrollment Period (60 days)
- If you had a Marketplace plan: you must update your application to reflect the new state's marketplace
- Premium Tax Credit (PTC) is recalculated based on the new coverage area's benchmark plan costs
- If your new state uses the federal marketplace (healthcare.gov), apply there; if it has its own exchange (CA, NY, CO, etc.), apply through the state exchange
- COBRA from the old employer: premiums are NOT deductible for W-2 employees (unless self-employed)

- Credit for taxes paid to other states: typically claimed on the new state's resident return for any income that was also taxed by the old state

## Optimization Strategies

1. **Time the move to minimize total state tax**: if moving from a high-tax to low-tax state, move earlier in the year; if moving from low-tax to high-tax, move later
2. **Accelerate or defer income around the move date**: bonuses, stock option exercises, large capital gains -- time them for the period of residence in the lower-tax state
3. **Complete domicile change thoroughly**: half-measures invite the old state to claim continuing residence
4. **Sell the old home**: maintaining a home in the old state is the single biggest factor in a state claiming you are still a resident (or statutory resident)
5. **Track days carefully**: some states use day-count tests (183 days); document your physical location daily
6. **Negotiate signing bonus or relocation timing**: if a new job triggers the move, the timing of signing bonuses and relocation payments matters
7. **Exercise stock options after establishing new domicile**: can save significant state tax if moving to a lower-tax state
8. **Realize capital gains after the move**: investment income follows residency; sell appreciated assets after establishing domicile in the lower-tax state
9. **Charitable contributions timing**: if moving to a state with no income tax, charitable deductions have less state-level value; consider bunching donations before the move

## Common Mistakes

1. **Not filing part-year returns in both states** -- triggers notices and potential penalties from the non-filing state
2. **Incomplete domicile change** -- old state continues to claim you as resident; NY and CA are particularly aggressive
3. **Not updating withholding** -- employer continues withholding for old state; you may not get a refund for months
4. **Forgetting to cancel/start estimated payments** -- over-paying old state while under-paying new state
5. **Double taxation without credit** -- failing to claim credit for taxes paid to the other state
6. **Maintaining home in old state** -- statutory residence trap: keeping an apartment and spending significant time can make you a resident of BOTH states
7. **Moving on paper only** -- states look at substance, not just mailing addresses; if your life (family, work, social ties) remains in the old state, the domicile has not changed
8. **Ignoring local taxes** -- cities like NYC, Philadelphia, and many Ohio cities have their own income taxes with separate part-year rules
9. **Not documenting daily location** -- in an audit, contemporaneous records (calendar, EZ-Pass, cell data) are far stronger than reconstructed estimates
10. **Failing to allocate investment income by residency period** -- dividends received before the move are sourced to the old state; after the move, to the new state
11. **Ignoring state nexus for remote workers** -- working remotely for an employer in the old state may create ongoing filing obligations in that state

## State-Specific Moving Notes

### Aggressive States (Hard to Leave)
- **California**: no fixed day-count test; totality of circumstances; keeps claiming residents who leave without severing ALL ties; presumes continuing residency if you have significant CA connections. FTB audit can go back 4+ years.
- **New York**: 548-day safe harbor if you leave and maintain no NY abode; statutory residence trap (183 days + permanent place of abode = resident even if domiciled elsewhere). NYC tax adds 3.078-3.876% on top of state tax.
- **New Jersey**: exit year return required; specific guidance on establishing non-residency. Part-year return must clearly identify the date of departure.
- **Connecticut**: statutory residency (183 days + permanent place of abode). Has imposed "exit tax" provisions in the past.

### Easy States (Clear Rules)
- **Florida**: no income tax; no return to file; establishing FL domicile is straightforward (Declaration of Domicile filed with county clerk within 30 days of establishing residency)
- **Texas**: no income tax; no return
- **Nevada**: no income tax; no return
- **Wyoming, South Dakota, Alaska**: no income tax; popular domicile choices

### Reciprocity States
- If moving between states with a reciprocity agreement (e.g., PA and NJ, VA and DC/MD/WV, IL and IA/KY/MI/WI): simplified; income generally taxed only by the state of residence
- Still need to file part-year returns for the year of the move
- Reciprocity applies to future years when you are a full-year resident of the new state
- Reciprocity typically covers wages/salary only -- not investment income or business income

## Worked Examples -- State Move Tax Impact

### Example 1: High-income move from CA to TX (no income tax)
- Salary: $400,000. Move date: April 1.
- CA income (Jan-Mar): $100,000. CA tax: ~$9,300 (at marginal rate ~9.3%).
- TX income (Apr-Dec): $300,000. TX tax: $0.
- **Savings vs. staying in CA all year**: ~$27,900 in CA state tax on the $300,000 earned after the move.
- If the move had been January 1 instead of April 1: additional $9,300 saved.

### Example 2: Move from FL (no tax) to NY
- Salary: $200,000. Move date: July 1.
- FL income (Jan-Jun): $100,000. FL tax: $0.
- NY income (Jul-Dec): $100,000. NY tax: ~$5,900 (at marginal rate ~5.9%).
- NY City tax (if NYC): additional ~$3,200.
- **Total new tax burden**: $5,900-$9,100 on the post-move income.
- Planning opportunity: defer bonuses and stock option exercises to the FL residency period.

### Example 3: Statutory residence trap
- Domiciled in CT. Maintain apartment in NY. Work in NY 190 days.
- NY claims statutory residency: 183+ days + permanent place of abode = full-year NY resident.
- CT also claims full-year residency (domicile).
- Result: dual residency. CT gives credit for taxes paid to NY on NY-sourced income, but the interaction can result in paying the higher of the two rates on most income.

## Convenience of Employer Rule -- Moving Implications

- If moving FROM a convenience-rule state (NY, PA, NJ, CT) while keeping the same employer in that state: you may still owe tax to the old state on wages
- If moving TO a convenience-rule state: wages may be sourced there even before you physically work there
- **New York**: if your employer is in NY and you work remotely from another state, NY taxes those wages unless you work remotely out of "necessity" (not convenience). The employer must have a bona fide office in your state that you use regularly.
- **Impact on movers**: simply moving to NJ or CT while keeping a NY employer does NOT eliminate NY tax on those wages. You may be taxed by BOTH states (with credit in the residence state).
- Critical to understand before accepting a remote work arrangement with an employer in a convenience-rule state

## Multi-State Tax Credit Mechanics

- Most states provide a credit on the resident return for taxes paid to other states on the same income
- The credit is limited to the LESSER of: taxes paid to the other state OR the tax your home state would have charged on that income
- Result: you effectively pay the HIGHER rate of the two states on dual-taxed income
- Example: NY taxes income at 8%; NJ taxes at 6%. NJ gives credit for the 6% (its own rate) against NY tax. You effectively pay 8% (NY rate).
- Some states only grant credits for certain types of income (earned income but not investment income)
