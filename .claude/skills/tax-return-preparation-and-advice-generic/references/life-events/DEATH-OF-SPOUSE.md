# Death of Spouse Tax Reference (Tax Year 2025)

## Overview

The death of a spouse triggers immediate and multi-year tax consequences. Filing status changes, stepped-up basis on inherited assets, survivor Social Security benefits, inherited retirement account decisions, estate tax portability elections, and life insurance taxation all demand attention during an already difficult time. The decisions made in the year of death and the two years following have lasting financial implications. Missing the Form 706 portability election or rolling an inherited IRA prematurely can cost hundreds of thousands of dollars over a lifetime.

## Tax Implications

### Filing Status Timeline

| Tax Year | Available Status | Standard Deduction (2025) | Top of 12% Bracket |
|----------|-----------------|--------------------------|---------------------|
| Year of death | MFJ | $31,500 | $96,950 |
| Year 1 after death | QSS (if eligible) | $31,500 | $96,950 |
| Year 2 after death | QSS (if eligible) | $31,500 | $96,950 |
| Year 3+ after death | Single or HOH | $15,750 / $23,625 | $48,475 / $64,850 |

The drop from MFJ/QSS to Single is dramatic -- the "widow(er)'s penalty." Plan for this transition.

### Year of Death -- Filing as MFJ

#### Full MFJ Benefits
- Can file as Married Filing Jointly for the ENTIRE year, even if death occurred on January 1
- Report ALL income for both spouses during the year:
  - Surviving spouse: full year income
  - Deceased spouse: income from January 1 through date of death
- Income earned by the estate AFTER date of death: reported on estate return (Form 1041) or may be reportable on final personal return if received by the surviving spouse as income in respect of a decedent (IRD)
- Full MFJ standard deduction ($31,500 for 2025) and MFJ brackets apply
- All credits and deductions available as MFJ (EITC, education credits, CTC, etc.)

#### Signing the Return
- Surviving spouse signs their own name and writes "Filing as surviving spouse" next to the deceased spouse's name
- Enter the date of death
- If an executor/personal representative has been appointed: the executor may also need to sign (or the surviving spouse signs on behalf)
- If a refund is due and no executor: surviving spouse files Form 1310 to claim it

#### Income Splitting in Year of Death
- Wages: income through date of death on deceased spouse's W-2; any final paycheck received after death may be IRD
- Investment income: dividends declared before death = decedent's income; dividends declared after = estate income or surviving spouse's depending on ownership
- Retirement distributions: distributions before death = decedent's income; distributions after death = inherited account treatment

### Qualifying Surviving Spouse (QSS) -- Years 1 and 2 After Death

#### Requirements (ALL must be met)
1. Did not remarry before the end of the tax year
2. Had a **qualifying child** who lived with you for more than half the year AND whom you can claim as a dependent
3. Paid more than half the cost of maintaining the home for the year
4. Could have filed MFJ in the year of death (even if actually filed MFS)

#### Benefits
- Uses MFJ tax rates and standard deduction ($31,500)
- Dramatically better than Single ($15,750 standard deduction, compressed brackets)
- Available for 2 tax years after the year of death
- Example: spouse dies in 2025; QSS available for 2026 and 2027; must file as Single/HOH starting 2028

#### If Not Eligible for QSS
- No dependent child = cannot use QSS
- Must file as **Single** (no dependents) or **Head of Household** (if other qualifying person such as a dependent parent)
- The jump to Single rates happens immediately in Year 1 after death -- plan accordingly

### The Widow(er)'s Penalty -- Planning for the Transition
- Moving from MFJ to Single: standard deduction drops by $15,750; tax brackets compress by ~50%
- Example: $120,000 of taxable income
  - MFJ: ~$16,190 federal tax
  - Single: ~$21,680 federal tax
  - **Penalty**: ~$5,490/year MORE in tax
- **Mitigation strategies**:
  - Accelerate Roth conversions during QSS years (use MFJ brackets while available)
  - Harvest capital gains at 0% rate during QSS years
  - Bunch itemized deductions in alternating years
  - Establish charitable giving via QCD at 70.5+

## Stepped-Up Basis -- Section 1014

### The Rule
- ALL inherited assets receive a **stepped-up (or stepped-down) basis** to Fair Market Value (FMV) at date of death
- This eliminates ALL unrealized capital gains accumulated during the decedent's lifetime
- The step-up applies regardless of how long the decedent held the asset

### Alternate Valuation Date -- Section 2032
- The executor can elect to value estate assets 6 months after the date of death (or the date of sale if sold within 6 months)
- Only available if: (a) Form 706 is filed, AND (b) it would decrease both the gross estate value AND the estate tax liability
- If elected: the alternate date becomes the basis for stepped-up assets
- Can be strategic if assets declined in value between death and 6 months later

### Community Property States -- THE MAJOR ADVANTAGE

**States**: AZ, CA, ID, LA, NV, NM, TX, WA, WI (and optionally AK if community property trust is created)

- In community property states: **BOTH halves** of community property receive a full step-up in basis
- This is a MASSIVE advantage over common law states

#### Worked Example: Community Property Step-Up
```
Couple in California bought stock in 2005 for $100,000
FMV at spouse's death in 2025: $1,000,000

Common Law State:
  Decedent's half: stepped up to $500,000
  Surviving spouse's half: retains original basis of $50,000
  Total basis: $550,000
  If sold immediately: $450,000 taxable gain

Community Property State:
  Decedent's half: stepped up to $500,000
  Surviving spouse's half: ALSO stepped up to $500,000
  Total basis: $1,000,000
  If sold immediately: $0 taxable gain

  Tax savings at 23.8% (20% LTCG + 3.8% NIIT): $107,100
```

This single provision can save $100,000+ in capital gains taxes. Document community property characterization meticulously.

### What Gets Stepped Up
- Real estate, stocks, bonds, mutual funds, ETFs
- Business interests (LLC, partnership, S-corp)
- Personal property, collectibles, art
- Property held in revocable living trust (included in gross estate = step-up)
- Life insurance owned by the decedent (no step-up needed -- proceeds are income tax-free)

### What Does NOT Get Stepped Up
- **Income in Respect of a Decedent (IRD)**: traditional IRA, 401(k), deferred compensation, unpaid salary, accrued interest, installment sale receivables
  - These are taxed to the recipient when distributed/received (just as they would have been taxed to the decedent)
  - But: if estate tax was paid on these items, the recipient gets a Section 691(c) deduction for the estate tax attributable to the IRD
- Property received by the decedent as a gift within 1 year of death if it passes back to the original donor (anti-abuse rule)
- Property in an irrevocable trust that is NOT included in the gross estate

## Estate Tax Portability -- DSUEA

### The Rule
- The **Deceased Spousal Unused Exclusion Amount (DSUEA)** allows the surviving spouse to "inherit" the unused portion of the deceased spouse's estate tax exemption
- 2025 federal estate tax exemption: $13,990,000 per person
- If the deceased used $2,000,000 of their exemption: the surviving spouse can claim the remaining $11,990,000
- Combined with the surviving spouse's own $13,990,000: total exemption = $25,980,000

### CRITICAL: Must File Form 706

- **Portability is NOT automatic** -- it requires filing Form 706 (Estate Tax Return) even if NO estate tax is owed
- **Deadline**: 9 months after date of death + 6-month extension (Form 4768)
- **Failure to file = exemption LOST FOREVER** -- there is no way to recover it after the deadline
- Late portability election: simplified method available under Rev. Proc. 2022-32 if filed within 5 years of the decedent's death (for estates not otherwise required to file Form 706)
- The cost of filing Form 706 ($5,000-$15,000 in professional fees) is trivial compared to the potential $13.99 million exemption preserved

### Planning Considerations
- Even for "modest" estates: file Form 706. If the surviving spouse later inherits money, receives life insurance, or assets appreciate: the portable exemption could save millions in estate tax
- The exemption amount may decrease in the future (scheduled sunset to ~$7M in 2026 under current law, though this may change)
- Portability does NOT apply to the GST (generation-skipping transfer) exemption -- only the estate/gift tax exemption

## Life Insurance Proceeds -- Section 101

### Generally Tax-Free
- Life insurance death benefits paid to a named beneficiary are **excluded from gross income** (Section 101(a)(1))
- No income tax regardless of the amount
- No limit on the exclusion

### Exceptions and Nuances

#### Transfer for Value Rule -- Section 101(a)(2)
- If the policy was transferred for valuable consideration: proceeds above the buyer's investment are **taxable**
- Exceptions (transfer for value rule does NOT apply if transferred to):
  - The insured
  - A partner of the insured
  - A partnership in which the insured is a partner
  - A corporation in which the insured is a shareholder or officer
  - A transferee whose basis is determined by reference to the transferor's basis

#### Employer-Owned Life Insurance -- Section 101(j)
- Employer-owned policies (EOLI): proceeds may be partially taxable unless specific notice and consent requirements are met
- Employer must: (a) notify the employee in writing that the employer intends to insure them, (b) obtain written consent, and (c) inform the employee of the maximum face amount
- If requirements are met: full exclusion applies
- If not met: only the premiums paid are excluded; the excess is taxable

#### Interest on Proceeds
- If the insurance company holds the proceeds and pays interest: the INTEREST is taxable income
- The death benefit itself remains tax-free
- Settlement options that include an interest component: only the interest is taxable

#### Estate Tax Implications
- If the deceased OWNED the policy (had incidents of ownership): proceeds are included in the gross estate for estate tax
- Strategy: Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust (ILIT) removes the policy from the estate (must be transferred >3 years before death)
- For policies owned by the surviving spouse on the deceased's life: NOT included in the deceased's gross estate

## Inherited Retirement Accounts

### Surviving Spouse -- Unique Options

The surviving spouse has the most flexibility of ANY beneficiary:

#### Option 1: Roll Into Own IRA
- Treat as your own IRA
- No RMDs until YOUR own RMD age (73/75)
- Can name your own beneficiaries
- Future distributions taxed at your rate
- **Downside**: if under 59.5, distributions subject to 10% early withdrawal penalty (unlike inherited IRA)

#### Option 2: Remain as Beneficiary (Inherited IRA)
- Take RMDs based on your single life expectancy (recalculated annually)
- Can access funds before 59.5 WITHOUT the 10% early withdrawal penalty
- Must begin RMDs by the later of: (a) December 31 of the year following death, or (b) December 31 of the year the decedent would have reached 73
- **Best for**: surviving spouses under 59.5 who need access to funds

#### Option 3: Elect 10-Year Rule
- Empty the account by end of the 10th year following death
- No annual RMD requirement (but must fully distribute by year 10)
- Rarely optimal for a spouse (the other options are better)

#### Strategy by Age
| Surviving Spouse Age | Recommended Approach |
|---------------------|---------------------|
| Under 59.5, needs money | Keep as inherited IRA (no 10% penalty); roll to own IRA at 59.5 |
| Under 59.5, does not need money | Roll to own IRA now (defer RMDs until 73/75) |
| 59.5-72 | Roll to own IRA (defer RMDs, name own beneficiaries) |
| 73+ | Roll to own IRA (take RMDs on your own schedule) |

#### Inherited Roth IRA (Spouse)
- Roll to own Roth IRA: NO RMDs ever; tax-free growth and withdrawals forever
- Keep as inherited: subject to RMDs (but distributions are tax-free)
- **Always roll to own Roth IRA** unless you need penalty-free access before 59.5

### Non-Spouse Beneficiaries -- 10-Year Rule
- Most non-spouse beneficiaries must empty the inherited IRA within 10 years of death (SECURE Act)
- Eligible Designated Beneficiaries (EDBs) can use life expectancy: minor children (until majority), disabled/chronically ill, those not more than 10 years younger than the decedent
- Annual RMDs may be required within the 10-year window if the decedent was already taking RMDs (proposed regulations)

## Social Security Survivor Benefits

### Eligibility and Amounts
- **Surviving spouse age 60+**: reduced survivor benefits (~71.5% of deceased's benefit at age 60; increases to 100% at FRA)
- **Surviving spouse age 50-59 (disabled)**: reduced survivor benefits
- **Surviving spouse any age caring for child under 16**: survivor benefits regardless of age
- **Children under 18 (or 19 if in school)**: 75% of deceased's benefit per child (family maximum applies)
- **One-time lump-sum death benefit**: $255 (must be claimed within 2 years)

### Benefit Amount
- At FRA: **100% of the deceased spouse's benefit** (including any delayed retirement credits the deceased earned)
- This means if the deceased delayed to age 70: the survivor gets the enhanced benefit
- Before FRA: benefit is reduced (approximately 71.5% at age 60, increasing linearly to 100% at FRA)

### Switching Strategy
- If surviving spouse has their own Social Security record:
  - **Option A**: take survivor benefit now (starting at 60), switch to own benefit at 70 (if own benefit at 70 > survivor benefit)
  - **Option B**: take own benefit now (starting at 62), switch to survivor benefit at FRA (if survivor benefit > own reduced benefit)
- Run both scenarios -- the optimal choice depends on relative benefit amounts and life expectancy
- This is one of the few remaining "claim one benefit, switch to another" strategies after the Bipartisan Budget Act of 2015

### Key Rules
- Remarriage before age 60: generally disqualifies survivor benefits (but remarriage after 60 does NOT)
- Survivor benefits are taxable under the same provisional income rules as regular Social Security
- Earnings test applies if claiming before FRA

## Final Return Considerations

### Income Through Date of Death
- All income earned by the decedent from January 1 through the date of death is reported on the final return
- W-2 income: employer issues W-2 for wages through date of death
- Final paycheck received AFTER death: may be IRD (reported on estate return or beneficiary's return)
- Investment income: dividends, interest, capital gains realized before death

### Medical Expenses -- Special Rule
- Medical expenses of the decedent paid within **1 year of death** (by the estate or surviving spouse) can be deducted on:
  - The decedent's **final return** (Schedule A), OR
  - The **estate tax return** (Form 706)
- Cannot claim on both -- must elect one
- If expenses are large and the estate is taxable: Form 706 deduction may be more valuable (40% estate tax rate vs. income tax bracket)
- If estate is not taxable: claim on final Form 1040

### Deductions and Credits
- Full standard deduction if filing MFJ ($30,000)
- All itemized deductions for the full year (combine both spouses')
- Full-year credits available (CTC for children living with surviving spouse, education credits, etc.)
- Medical expenses for the full year: both spouses combined if MFJ

## Optimization Strategies

1. **File Form 706 for portability**: even if no estate tax is due -- preserves the $13.99M DSUE exemption; failure to file = exemption lost forever
2. **Use MFJ in the year of death**: always more favorable than MFS; plan income and deductions for the full year
3. **Roth conversion during QSS years**: while MFJ brackets are still available, convert traditional IRA to Roth; this mitigates the future widow(er)'s penalty
4. **Sell appreciated assets immediately after death**: realize gains at the new stepped-up basis (little or no gain); reinvest to diversify or generate income
5. **Document community property**: in community property states, ensure all community property is properly characterized to claim the full double step-up
6. **Inherited IRA timing**: if surviving spouse is under 59.5, keep as inherited IRA until 59.5 for penalty-free access; roll to own IRA later
7. **QCD at 70.5+**: use Qualified Charitable Distributions from IRA to satisfy RMDs without increasing AGI
8. **Social Security switching strategy**: evaluate whether to take survivor benefit first (and delay own) or vice versa
9. **Plan for the widow(er)'s penalty**: the transition from MFJ to Single brackets causes significant tax increases; Roth conversions during QSS years can ease this
10. **Harvest capital gains at 0% during QSS**: take advantage of the MFJ-equivalent brackets while available
11. **Medical expense timing**: expenses paid within 1 year of death can be claimed on the final return; coordinate timing of payments

## Common Mistakes

1. **Not filing Form 706 for portability** -- even if no estate tax is due; this is potentially a multi-million dollar error
2. **Filing as Single in the year of death** -- MFJ is available and almost always better
3. **Overlooking QSS status** -- survivors with dependent children often miss this favorable filing status for 2 years
4. **Not claiming the full step-up in community property states** -- both halves step up; preparers in common law states may not apply this correctly
5. **Rolling inherited IRA to own IRA before 59.5** -- loses penalty-free access; keep as inherited until 59.5
6. **Not reporting estate income on Form 1041** -- income earned after death belongs to the estate, not the final personal return
7. **Ignoring the Social Security switching strategy** -- survivors with their own work record should model both scenarios
8. **Missing the Form 706 deadline** -- 9 months + 6-month extension; mark the calendar; late portability election has a 5-year window but requires professional filing
9. **Not planning for the widow(er)'s penalty** -- the move from MFJ to Single brackets is painful; proactive Roth conversions help
10. **Selling appreciated assets without checking basis** -- the stepped-up basis means there may be little or no gain; conversely, don't hold assets waiting for more appreciation when you could sell tax-free now
11. **Not coordinating medical expense deduction** -- expenses paid within 1 year of death can be claimed on final return or Form 706; choose the one with greater tax benefit

## State Considerations

- **State estate taxes**: many states have LOWER exemptions than federal:
  - MA and OR: $1,000,000
  - NY: $6,940,000 (with a cliff -- if estate exceeds exemption by more than 5%, the ENTIRE estate is taxable, not just the excess)
  - WA: $2,193,000; CT: matches federal; IL: $4,000,000; MN: $3,000,000
  - States with estate tax: CT, DC, HI, IL, MA, MD, ME, MN, NY, OR, RI, VT, WA
- **State inheritance taxes** (tax on the BENEFICIARY, not the estate): IA (phasing out 2025), KY, MD, NE, NJ, PA
  - Rates vary by relationship to deceased; spouses are typically exempt
  - MD is the only state with BOTH estate and inheritance tax
- **Community property states**: double step-up is a FEDERAL rule -- applies regardless of state income tax
- **State survivor benefits**: some states have their own survivor benefit programs or property tax exemptions for surviving spouses
- **State filing status**: generally follows federal; QSS recognized by most states
- **State portability**: some states with estate taxes do NOT recognize portability (e.g., MA, OR) -- file state estate tax return separately
