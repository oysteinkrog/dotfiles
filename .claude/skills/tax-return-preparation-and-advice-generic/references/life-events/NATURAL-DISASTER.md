# Natural Disaster Tax Reference (Tax Year 2025)

## Overview

Taxpayers affected by federally declared disasters have access to special tax provisions including casualty loss deductions, extended filing deadlines, penalty-free retirement withdrawals, and involuntary conversion deferrals. The tax code provides significant relief, but the rules are specific and require careful documentation. Since TCJA, personal casualty losses are ONLY deductible for federally declared disasters. Business casualty losses remain deductible regardless of federal declaration.

## Tax Implications

### Casualty Loss Deduction -- Section 165(c)(3)

#### Eligibility (Post-TCJA)
- **Personal casualty losses are deductible ONLY for federally declared disasters** (TCJA 2018-2025)
- Disaster must be declared by the President under the Robert T. Stafford Disaster Relief Act
- Check FEMA.gov for declared disaster areas and applicable dates
- Business casualty losses remain deductible regardless of federal declaration (Section 165(c)(1))
- **Exception**: personal casualty GAINS can offset personal casualty losses from non-disaster events (Section 165(h)(5) -- see below)

#### Calculation (Personal Casualty Loss) -- Step by Step

```
Step 1: Determine loss amount
  Loss = Lesser of:
    (a) Decline in FMV (FMV before - FMV after), OR
    (b) Adjusted basis of the property

Step 2: Subtract insurance/reimbursement
  Net loss = Loss - Insurance proceeds (received or reasonably expected)

Step 3: Apply $100 per-event floor
  Reduced loss = Net loss - $100

Step 4: Apply 10% AGI threshold
  Deductible loss = Reduced loss - (10% x AGI)
```

#### Determining Fair Market Value Decline

**Safe Harbor Methods** (IRS Rev. Proc. 2018-08 and related guidance):
1. **Appraisal method**: qualified appraisal before and after the casualty (most reliable, most expensive)
2. **Cost of repairs method**: if repairs restore property to pre-casualty condition, the repair cost can serve as evidence of FMV decline (but cannot exceed actual FMV decline)
3. **Insurance company estimates**: adjusters' estimates of damage can support the loss amount
4. **Contractor estimates**: written estimates from licensed contractors for repair/replacement
5. **Real estate comparable sales**: for total destruction, use comparable sales data

#### Adjusted Basis Considerations
- Original purchase price + improvements - depreciation (if applicable)
- For inherited property: stepped-up basis at date of death
- For gifted property: donor's basis (generally)
- Personal property (furniture, clothing, appliances): original cost minus reasonable depreciation for age/condition
- Vehicles: use established valuation guides (KBB, NADA) for before-disaster value

#### Key Rules and Traps
- Must reduce loss by ALL insurance proceeds (received or reasonably expected)
- If you file a claim but have not yet received reimbursement: ESTIMATE and reduce the loss
- If you later receive MORE insurance than expected: include excess as income in the year received (to the extent it provided a tax benefit in the prior year)
- If you do NOT file a claim when insurance is available: deduction may be denied to the extent insurance would have covered it (IRS can argue failure to mitigate)
- Deduct on Schedule A via **Form 4684** (Casualties and Thefts)

### Section 165(h)(5) -- Personal Casualty Gain/Loss Netting

- If you have both personal casualty GAINS and personal casualty LOSSES in the same year:
  - First, net gains against losses
  - If net gain: treat as capital gain (short-term or long-term depending on holding period)
  - If net loss: deductible as itemized deduction (subject to $100 and 10% AGI floors)
- **Key application**: personal casualty gains from insurance reimbursement exceeding basis on destroyed property can OFFSET personal casualty losses from non-federally-declared events
- This creates a narrow exception to the TCJA rule that personal casualty losses require a federal disaster declaration

### Timing Election -- Section 165(i)

#### Deducting the Loss on the PRIOR Year's Return
- You can elect to deduct the casualty loss on the return for the year IMMEDIATELY PRECEDING the disaster year
- This generates a quick refund from the prior year
- Must make the election by the extended due date for the disaster-year return
- Election is made by filing an amended return (Form 1040-X) for the prior year OR by claiming the loss on an original return that has not yet been filed

#### Which Year Is Better? Analyze Both Ways
- **Higher AGI in prior year**: the 10% AGI floor is HIGHER, which means LESS deduction (potentially worse)
- **Higher bracket in prior year**: the deduction is worth MORE per dollar (potentially better)
- **Lower AGI in prior year**: the 10% floor is lower, meaning MORE deduction (potentially better)
- Run the calculation both ways -- the optimal choice depends on the interplay of AGI, bracket, and deduction amount

#### Amended Return for Disasters Declared in Q1
- If a disaster is declared in the first quarter of the current year but the damage occurred late in the prior year: you can claim the loss on the prior year's return being filed right now
- If the disaster declaration covers a prior-year event: can amend the prior year's return

## FEMA Grants and Government Assistance

### Generally Non-Taxable -- Section 139

- **FEMA Individual Assistance grants**: NOT taxable (qualified disaster relief under Section 139)
- **SBA disaster loans**: NOT income (loans that must be repaid)
- **State and local government disaster grants**: generally NOT taxable under Section 139
- **Disaster unemployment assistance**: TAXABLE (treated like regular unemployment compensation)
- **Hazard mitigation grants**: generally NOT taxable

### Section 139 -- Qualified Disaster Relief Payments

- Broadly excludes from income: payments to reimburse reasonable and necessary personal, family, living, or funeral expenses caused by a qualified disaster
- Includes: temporary housing, home repairs, replacement of personal items, medical expenses, childcare, transportation
- Does NOT include: payments for lost income or business revenue (those are taxable)
- No dollar limit on the exclusion
- Cannot also deduct expenses that were reimbursed tax-free (no double benefit)
- Employer disaster relief payments to employees: also excludable under Section 139 if they meet the requirements

## Insurance Reimbursement Interaction

### Timing Issues
- **Same year as loss**: reduce the loss by the reimbursement; only deduct the net difference
- **Reimbursement received in a LATER year**:
  - If reimbursement exceeds the loss claimed: include excess as income (to the extent the prior deduction provided a tax benefit -- the "tax benefit rule")
  - If reimbursement is less than expected: deduct the additional loss in the year you determine no further reimbursement will be received
- **Reasonable prospect of recovery**: must reduce the loss by insurance claims that have a reasonable prospect of recovery, even if not yet paid
- Self-insured or uninsured: no reduction necessary, but document why insurance was not available

### Gain on Insurance Proceeds
- If insurance proceeds EXCEED the adjusted basis of destroyed property: you have a gain
- Example: home with $200,000 basis destroyed; insurance pays $350,000; gain = $150,000
- This gain can be deferred under Section 1033 (involuntary conversion)

## Extended Filing Deadlines

### IRS Automatic Relief
- IRS grants automatic extensions for taxpayers in federally declared disaster areas
- Typical relief: 60-120 days from the end of the disaster period (varies; check IRS disaster relief page for each specific disaster)
- Applies to:
  - Filing individual, business, estate/trust, tax-exempt, and employment tax returns
  - Making tax payments (income, estimated, employment, excise)
  - Contributing to IRAs and other retirement plans
  - 1031 exchange identification and replacement periods
  - Section 1033 replacement periods
  - Rollover periods for retirement plan distributions
  - Filing claims for refund

### How to Identify and Claim
- IRS issues a News Release for each declared disaster with affected areas and extended deadline
- **Automatic**: if your address of record is in the affected area, no action needed
- If records are in the disaster area but you live elsewhere: call IRS at 866-562-5227
- Write the FEMA disaster declaration number on the top of your return (e.g., "FEMA-DR-XXXX")
- No penalty or interest accrues during the extended period

## Penalty-Free Retirement Withdrawals

### Qualified Disaster Distributions -- SECURE 2.0 Section 331

SECURE 2.0 created a **permanent framework** for qualified disaster distributions (for federally declared disasters after January 26, 2021):

- **Maximum**: $22,000 per disaster from IRAs, 401(k)s, 403(b)s, and similar plans
- **No 10% early withdrawal penalty** (regardless of age)
- **3-year income spread**: include 1/3 of the distribution in gross income each year for 3 years (default)
- **Elect to include all in Year 1**: can opt out of the spread if preferred (useful if Year 1 is a low-income year)
- **3-year repayment window**: can repay all or part of the distribution within 3 years; treated as a tax-free rollover (recoup the tax already paid via amended return)
- Report on **Form 8915-F**

#### Multiple Disasters
- If affected by multiple federally declared disasters in the same year: $22,000 limit applies per disaster
- Example: hurricane in June + wildfire in October = up to $44,000 in penalty-free distributions

#### Plan Participation Requirements
- Plan must allow for hardship or disaster distributions (most do under SECURE 2.0)
- Self-certification: taxpayer certifies they lived in or were economically affected by the disaster area
- Plan administrator may rely on self-certification

### Qualified Disaster Recovery Distributions (Additional Provision)
- Some disaster-specific legislation provides additional provisions beyond the standard SECURE 2.0 framework
- Check for disaster-specific enacted legislation for major events

## Section 1033 Involuntary Conversion

### Overview
- When property is destroyed (or condemned) and insurance/condemnation proceeds exceed adjusted basis: the gain can be deferred
- **Requirement**: reinvest proceeds in "similar or related in service or use" replacement property within the replacement period

### Replacement Period
| Type of Property | Replacement Period |
|-----------------|-------------------|
| Personal property (general) | 2 years after end of tax year of gain |
| Real property (federally declared disaster) | 4 years after end of tax year of gain |
| Condemned real property (business/investment) | 3 years after end of tax year of gain |

- IRS can grant extensions for reasonable cause (file request before period expires)

### How Deferral Works
- Reinvest at least as much as the insurance proceeds: **NO gain recognized**
- Reinvest less than proceeds (keep some cash): gain recognized = cash NOT reinvested (but only to the extent of the realized gain)
- Basis in replacement property: FMV minus deferred gain (reduces future basis)

### What Qualifies as Replacement Property
- **Personal residence**: any property used as a personal residence (does not have to be identical)
- **Business property**: property similar or related in service or use (stricter -- same type of use)
- **Disaster-destroyed personal residence**: can also defer gain by purchasing ANY property in the disaster area related to the disaster
- Example: home destroyed, buy a different home in the same area = qualifies

### Election and Reporting
- Make the election on your return for the year of the involuntary conversion
- If replacement has not yet occurred: include a statement with return indicating intent to replace and estimated timeline
- Can revoke the election if replacement does not occur within the period (file amended return)
- If period expires without replacement: amend the year of conversion to report the gain

## Optimization Strategies

1. **Elect prior-year deduction when beneficial**: run calculations for both years; consider AGI, bracket, and total itemized deductions in each year
2. **Document everything immediately**: photograph/video damage BEFORE cleanup; keep receipts for temporary housing and repairs; obtain written estimates; get police/fire reports
3. **File insurance claims promptly**: failure to file when coverage exists can reduce or eliminate the tax deduction
4. **Use Section 1033 to defer gain**: if insurance proceeds exceed basis, reinvest in replacement property to defer recognition
5. **Take advantage of qualified disaster distributions**: the penalty-free, 3-year spread, 3-year repayment framework is extremely generous
6. **Repay disaster distributions if possible**: repaying within 3 years converts the distribution to a tax-free rollover and you get the taxes back via amended returns
7. **Check for state-specific relief**: many states offer additional deductions, credits, or extended deadlines
8. **Coordinate with FEMA grants**: ensure reimbursed expenses are not also claimed as casualty losses (no double benefit)
9. **Use the $100 floor strategically**: each separate casualty event has its own $100 floor; a single disaster is one event regardless of the number of items damaged
10. **Consider the 10% AGI floor impact**: if income is high, the 10% floor can eliminate much of the deduction; coordinate with income timing strategies

## Common Mistakes

1. **Claiming casualty losses for non-federally-declared events** -- under TCJA (through 2025), only federally declared disasters qualify for personal casualty loss deduction
2. **Not reducing loss by expected insurance** -- must reduce even if insurance has not yet paid; adjust later if actual differs
3. **Missing the prior-year election deadline** -- the Section 165(i) election must be made by the extended due date for the disaster-year return
4. **Not documenting pre-disaster FMV** -- appraisals, photos, home improvement records, and purchase records are essential; without them, substantiation is difficult
5. **Double-deducting reimbursed expenses** -- FEMA or insurance reimbursement = cannot also claim casualty loss for the same expense
6. **Forgetting to apply the $100 and 10% AGI floors** -- net deductible loss is significantly less than raw loss
7. **Not spreading disaster retirement distributions over 3 years** -- the default is 3-year spread; electing all in Year 1 may push into higher brackets unnecessarily
8. **Missing the Section 1033 replacement deadline** -- mark the calendar; failure to reinvest in time = gain recognition
9. **Not filing Form 8915-F** -- required to report qualified disaster distributions and claim the penalty exemption
10. **Ignoring the Section 165(h)(5) netting rule** -- casualty gains from one event can unlock deductions for casualty losses from non-disaster events

## State Considerations

- **State disaster declarations**: some states offer relief based on state-level declarations independent of federal
- **State casualty loss deductions**: some states decouple from TCJA and allow casualty losses for non-federally-declared disasters
  - **California**: allows casualty losses for state-declared disasters and wildfires; special extended deadlines for wildfire victims
  - **New York**: generally conforms to federal TCJA treatment
- **Texas, Florida, Wyoming**: no income tax, so state casualty deduction is not applicable; but property tax relief may be available
- **State filing extensions**: states typically follow IRS in extending deadlines for declared disasters; always verify for your specific state
- **State retirement distribution treatment**: most states follow federal treatment of qualified disaster distributions
- **Property tax relief**: many localities offer temporary property tax reductions for damaged/destroyed properties; apply to county assessor
- **State disaster assistance programs**: state-funded grants and loans may have different tax treatment than federal; check state law
