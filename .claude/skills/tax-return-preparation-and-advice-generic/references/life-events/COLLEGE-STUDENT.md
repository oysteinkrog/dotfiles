# College Student Tax Reference (Tax Year 2025)

## Overview

College students and their families face a complex intersection of education credits, scholarship taxation rules, student loan deductions, and 529 plan distribution rules. Understanding who claims the credits (student vs. parent), how to coordinate 529 distributions with education credits, and when scholarships become taxable can save thousands of dollars. The key principle: the same expenses cannot be used for both a tax credit and a tax-free 529 distribution. Optimal coordination between credits, 529 plans, and scholarships is the difference between leaving $2,500 on the table and claiming every dollar.

## Tax Implications

### American Opportunity Tax Credit (AOTC) -- Section 25A(b)

- **Maximum credit**: $2,500 per eligible student per year
- **Calculation**: 100% of first $2,000 of qualified expenses + 25% of next $2,000
- **Refundable portion**: 40% of the credit (up to $1,000) is refundable -- payable even with zero tax liability
- **Eligibility**:
  - First 4 years of post-secondary education ONLY (based on academic standing, not calendar years)
  - Student must be enrolled at least half-time for at least one academic period beginning in the tax year
  - Student must be pursuing a degree or recognized credential
  - Student must not have completed 4 years of post-secondary education before the beginning of the tax year
  - No felony drug conviction at end of tax year
- **Qualified expenses**: tuition, required enrollment fees, required course materials (books, supplies, equipment needed for courses)
- **NOT qualified**: room and board, transportation, insurance, medical, personal expenses, optional fees, student activity fees (unless required)
- **Income phase-out (2025)**: $80,000-$90,000 (single) / $160,000-$180,000 (MFJ)
- **Cannot claim if**: filing MFS; claimed as a dependent on another's return (parent claims instead)
- **Form 8863**; requires Form 1098-T from institution

### Lifetime Learning Credit (LLC) -- Section 25A(c)

- **Maximum credit**: $2,000 per return (NOT per student)
- **Calculation**: 20% of up to $10,000 of qualified expenses
- **Non-refundable** (can only offset tax liability)
- **No year limit**: available for any year of post-secondary education, graduate school, professional development, or even a single course
- Does NOT require half-time enrollment or degree program
- **Income phase-out (2025)**: $80,000-$90,000 (single) / $160,000-$180,000 (MFJ)
- Cannot claim both AOTC and LLC for the SAME student in the SAME year (but can claim AOTC for one student and LLC for another on the same return)
- **Form 8863**

### AOTC vs. LLC Comparison Table

| Factor | AOTC | LLC |
|--------|------|-----|
| Maximum credit | $2,500/student | $2,000/return |
| Refundable | Yes (40%, up to $1,000) | No |
| Per student or per return | Per student | Per return |
| Year limit | First 4 years only | Unlimited |
| Enrollment requirement | At least half-time | Any amount (even 1 course) |
| Degree required | Yes | No |
| Graduate school eligible | No | Yes |
| Professional development | No | Yes |
| Drug conviction disqualifier | Yes | No |
| Qualified expenses include books | Yes (if required) | Only if paid to institution |
| Phase-out (MFJ) | $160K-$180K | $160K-$180K |
| Tax benefit per $1K of expenses | Up to $1,000 (first $2K) or $250 (next $2K) | $200 |

**Decision rule**: claim AOTC for every eligible year of undergrad (first 4 years, at least half-time). Use LLC only when AOTC is unavailable: 5th+ year, graduate school, professional development, less than half-time, degree not being pursued.

### Who Claims the Credit?

- **Student is a dependent**: PARENT claims the credit on their return
- **Student is NOT a dependent**: student claims on their own return
- A student who CAN be claimed as a dependent (even if parent chooses not to) cannot claim education credits on their own return
- **Strategic analysis**: in rare cases, it may be beneficial for the parent to NOT claim the student as a dependent so the student can claim the refundable AOTC ($1,000)
  - This only works if: the parent's tax situation doesn't benefit from the dependency exemption/CTC, and the student has at least $4,000 of qualified expenses
  - Run both scenarios to determine optimal approach

## 529 Plan Distributions

### Tax-Free Qualified Distributions
- Tuition, required fees, books, supplies, equipment for enrollment
- Room and board: up to the school's cost-of-attendance (COA) allowance, or actual cost if living in school-owned housing
- Computer, peripheral equipment, software, and internet access
- K-12 tuition: up to $10,000/year per beneficiary
- Student loan repayment: up to $10,000 lifetime per beneficiary

### Coordination with Education Credits -- THE Critical Strategy

**The Rule**: the same expenses CANNOT be used for BOTH a 529 tax-free distribution AND an education credit. Dollar-for-dollar reduction.

**Optimal Strategy**:
1. Pay $4,000 of TUITION out of pocket (or with loans) to claim the full $2,500 AOTC
2. Use 529 funds for ROOM AND BOARD (which don't qualify for credits anyway)
3. Use 529 for remaining tuition/fees beyond the $4,000 reserved for AOTC

#### Worked Example
```
Total cost of attendance: $30,000
  Tuition and fees: $18,000
  Room and board: $12,000

Optimal allocation:
  Out-of-pocket for tuition: $4,000 --> claim $2,500 AOTC
  529 distribution for room/board: $12,000 --> tax-free (qualified expense)
  529 distribution for remaining tuition: $14,000 --> tax-free
  Total 529 distribution: $26,000
  Total tax benefit: $2,500 AOTC + $0 tax on $26,000 of 529 growth

Wrong approach (all from 529):
  529 pays all $30,000 --> cannot claim AOTC (expenses already covered)
  Lost: $2,500 AOTC credit
```

### Non-Qualified 529 Distributions
- **Earnings portion** is taxable as ordinary income + 10% penalty
- **Basis (contributions)** returned tax-free in all cases
- **Exceptions to penalty** (earnings still taxable):
  - Beneficiary receives a scholarship (withdraw up to scholarship amount penalty-free)
  - Beneficiary attends a U.S. military academy
  - Beneficiary dies or becomes disabled
- Report on Form 1099-Q (issued to account owner or beneficiary depending on who receives)

### SECURE 2.0: 529-to-Roth IRA Rollover

- Starting 2024: unused 529 funds can be rolled to a Roth IRA for the beneficiary
- **Requirements**:
  - 529 account must have been open for **15+ years**
  - Amount subject to **annual Roth IRA contribution limit** ($7,000 for 2025)
  - **$35,000 lifetime cap** per beneficiary
  - Contributions (and earnings on those contributions) made within the **last 5 years** cannot be rolled over
  - Beneficiary must have **earned income** at least equal to the rollover amount
- **Strategy**: if 529 has excess funds after education is complete, roll $7,000/year to the beneficiary's Roth IRA for up to 5 years ($35,000 total)
- This provision effectively eliminates the "what if they don't use it all?" concern about 529 over-funding

## Scholarship Taxation -- Section 117

### General Rules
- Scholarships used for **qualified tuition and related expenses** (tuition, fees, required books/supplies): **TAX-FREE**
- Scholarships used for **room, board, travel, or other non-qualified expenses**: **TAXABLE as ordinary income**
- Scholarships conditioned on **services** (teaching assistantship, research assistantship where services are required): **TAXABLE** regardless of how used (this is compensation, not a scholarship)
  - Exception: if the research is required for a degree and not primarily for the benefit of the grantor, it may still be treated as a tax-free scholarship
- Athletic scholarships: generally tax-free to the extent covering tuition/fees/books (the athletic services are considered part of the educational experience)

### Reporting Taxable Scholarships
- Taxable portion reported on Form 1040 Line 1a (as wages if from services) or Schedule 1 Line 8r (other income)
- **No Form W-2 or 1099 is typically issued** for scholarship overage -- student must self-report
- Form 1098-T Box 1 shows amounts billed; Box 5 shows scholarships/grants; student must determine taxable portion based on actual use

### Strategic Scholarship Allocation
- Sometimes it is BENEFICIAL to let a portion of scholarship be taxable to claim the AOTC on tuition paid from other sources

#### Worked Example: Scholarship + AOTC Optimization
```
Full-ride scholarship: $25,000
  Tuition/fees: $20,000
  Room/board: $5,000

Option A -- No planning:
  $25,000 scholarship covers all expenses
  $20,000 covers tuition (tax-free)
  $5,000 covers room/board (taxable)
  Taxable scholarship income: $5,000
  AOTC: $0 (all tuition covered by scholarship)
  Tax on $5,000 at 10%: $500
  Net cost: $500

Option B -- Strategic allocation:
  Allocate $16,000 of scholarship to tuition (tax-free)
  Allocate $9,000 of scholarship to room/board (taxable)
  Pay $4,000 of tuition from savings/loans
  Taxable scholarship income: $9,000
  AOTC: $2,500 (on the $4,000 out-of-pocket tuition)
  Tax on $9,000 at 10%: $900
  Net benefit: $2,500 - $900 = $1,600 AHEAD

  Result: Option B saves $1,600 by strategically allocating scholarship
```

## Student Employment

### FICA Exemption -- Section 3121(b)(10)
- Students employed by the **school where they are enrolled** and regularly attending classes are exempt from Social Security and Medicare taxes (FICA)
- Applies to: on-campus jobs at the student's own university
- Does NOT apply to: off-campus jobs, jobs at other universities, co-op/intern programs at private employers
- The exemption saves 7.65% for both the student and the university

### Work-Study
- Federal Work-Study compensation is **taxable income** (reported on W-2)
- Subject to federal income tax withholding
- **EXEMPT from FICA** if student is enrolled at least half-time and works for the school
- **Key financial aid benefit**: work-study earnings are excluded from the FAFSA income calculation under the Federal Methodology
- Counts as earned income for: EITC eligibility, Roth IRA contribution eligibility, standard deduction calculation

### Graduate Assistantships
- Teaching/research assistantship stipends: generally **taxable**
- Tuition waivers for graduate assistants: generally **tax-free** under Section 117(d) for education below the graduate level; for graduate students, the tuition waiver may be tax-free if it is a qualified tuition reduction under Section 117(d)(5) for teaching or research assistants
- Health insurance provided as part of assistantship: may be tax-free as employer-provided health coverage

## Filing Requirements for Dependents with Income

### When Must a Dependent File?

For 2025 (verify current thresholds):

| Situation | Must File If |
|-----------|-------------|
| Earned income only | Earned income > $15,750 (2025 standard deduction) |
| Unearned income only | Unearned income > $1,350 |
| Both earned and unearned | Gross income > larger of $1,350 or (earned income + $450, up to $15,750) |
| Any situation | Federal tax was withheld (file to get refund) |
| Any situation | Eligible for refundable credits (EITC, refundable AOTC) |

### Standard Deduction for Dependents
- Greater of: $1,350 OR (earned income + $450)
- But cannot exceed $15,750 (the full standard deduction for a single filer)
- Example: student earns $8,000 from part-time job; standard deduction = $8,000 + $450 = $8,450
- Example: student earns $600 from odd jobs; standard deduction = $1,350 (the minimum)

### Kiddie Tax -- Section 1(g) / Form 8615

- Applies to children under 19 (or under 24 if full-time student) who are claimed as dependents
- Unearned income (investment income, taxable scholarships treated as unearned, trust distributions) above $2,700 (2025) is taxed at the PARENT'S marginal rate

| Unearned Income Range | Tax Treatment |
|----------------------|---------------|
| $0 - $1,350 | Tax-free |
| $1,351 - $2,700 | Child's rate (10%) |
| Above $2,700 | Parent's marginal rate |

- **Impact on students**: if parents are in the 32-37% bracket, the student's investment income above $2,700 is taxed at that rate
- **Taxable scholarship income**: the IRS position on whether taxable scholarship income (room/board portion) is "earned" or "unearned" for kiddie tax purposes is not entirely clear; most practitioners treat it as earned income, which would NOT be subject to kiddie tax
- UGMA/UTMA accounts: particularly affected by kiddie tax

## Student Loan Interest Deduction -- Section 221

- **Maximum**: $2,500 per year
- **Above-the-line deduction** (no need to itemize)
- Applies to interest on qualified education loans (federal or private)
- Loan must be for taxpayer, spouse, or dependent's education expenses
- **Phase-out (2025)**: $85,000-$100,000 (single) / $170,000-$200,000 (MFJ)
- Cannot claim if: filing MFS or claimed as a dependent
- Only the person legally obligated on the loan can claim (student or parent borrower)
- Lender provides Form 1098-E

## Coverdell Education Savings Account (ESA)

- Maximum contribution: $2,000/year per beneficiary
- Tax-free distributions for qualified education expenses (K-12 and higher ed)
- Broader qualified expenses than 529: uniforms, tutoring, supplementary materials, computer equipment
- Income phase-out on contributions: $95,000-$110,000 (single) / $190,000-$220,000 (MFJ)
- Must be used by age 30 (exception: special needs beneficiary)
- Largely superseded by 529 plans for most families (higher limits, no income phase-out, 529-to-Roth rollover)

## Tuition and Fees Deduction

- **Expired** after 2020 (Consolidated Appropriations Act 2021 repealed it and expanded the LLC income limits instead)
- If a prior-year return is being amended for 2020 or earlier: up to $4,000 above-the-line deduction was available
- For 2021+: use AOTC or LLC instead

## Optimization Strategies

1. **Maximize AOTC for all 4 eligible years**: $10,000 total benefit per student; coordinate with 529 to preserve credit eligibility
2. **Use 529 for room and board, out-of-pocket for tuition**: reserve $4,000 of tuition for AOTC, use 529 for expenses that don't qualify for credits
3. **Strategically allocate scholarships**: let some scholarship cover room/board (taxable) to free up tuition for AOTC claim -- net benefit often $1,000-$2,000
4. **Grandparent-owned 529**: starting with 2024-25 FAFSA cycle, grandparent 529 distributions no longer count as student income on FAFSA
5. **529-to-Roth rollover**: if funds remain after education, roll up to $35,000 to beneficiary's Roth IRA (must have been open 15+ years)
6. **File the student's return even if not required**: to claim refundable AOTC, recover withheld tax, or establish filing history
7. **Parent vs. student claiming analysis**: run both scenarios to determine whether the family saves more with parent claiming dependent + credit vs. student claiming independently
8. **Use work-study over regular employment**: same income, but excluded from FAFSA need analysis and often FICA-exempt

## Common Mistakes

1. **Double-dipping 529 and education credits**: using the same $4,000 of tuition for both a tax-free 529 distribution AND the AOTC
2. **Student claiming credit when parent gets more benefit**: if parent is in a higher bracket, the non-refundable portion is worth more to them
3. **Not reporting taxable scholarship income**: the room/board portion is taxable even though no W-2 or 1099 is issued
4. **Missing the student loan interest deduction**: must be claimed by the person legally obligated on the loan
5. **Claiming AOTC in 5th year**: limited to first 4 years of post-secondary education
6. **Ignoring Form 1098-T discrepancies**: amounts billed (Box 1) may not match amounts paid; use actual amounts for credit calculation
7. **Not considering scholarship reallocation strategy**: automatically applying scholarship to tuition first often wastes the AOTC opportunity
8. **Filing MFS and losing education benefits**: both AOTC and LLC are unavailable when filing MFS; student loan interest deduction is also lost

## State Considerations

- **529 state tax deductions**: 35+ states offer deductions or credits; some require the in-state plan; some allow any state's plan
- **State education credits**: IN (20% credit for 529 contributions), MN (K-12 education credit/deduction), SC (tuition tax credit), and others
- **State scholarship taxation**: generally follows federal treatment
- **State student loan interest deductions**: some states offer additional deductions beyond federal
- **State filing requirements for students**: students working in a state different from home state may need to file nonresident returns in the work state and claim credits in the home state
- **State 529 recapture**: some states recapture the state tax deduction if 529 funds are used for out-of-state schools or rolled to another state's plan (varies by state)
