# Military Deployment Tax Reference (Tax Year 2025)

## Overview

Active-duty military personnel, especially those deployed to combat zones, benefit from some of the most generous tax provisions in the Internal Revenue Code. Combat zone tax exclusions, automatic filing extensions, continued deductibility of moving expenses, and special state tax protections under SCRA/MSRRA can dramatically reduce tax liability. The TSP Roth strategy during combat zone deployment is one of the most powerful wealth-building tools available to any American taxpayer. Understanding these provisions ensures service members and their families receive every benefit they are entitled to.

## Tax Implications

### Combat Zone Tax Exclusion -- Section 112

#### Enlisted Members
- **ALL pay** received for any month of service in a combat zone is excluded from gross income
- Includes: base pay, bonuses, reenlistment bonuses, special pay, incentive pay, and awards
- **Full-month rule**: a single day of service in the combat zone during a calendar month qualifies the ENTIRE month's pay for exclusion
- Example: enter combat zone on January 31 = all of January's pay is excluded; leave combat zone on March 1 = all of March's pay is excluded
- Hostile fire/imminent danger pay ($275/month): always excluded from gross income even outside designated combat zones

#### Officers
- Exclusion is CAPPED at the **highest enlisted pay rate** plus imminent danger/hostile fire pay
- For 2025: approximately $10,858.50/month (E-9 with 26+ years of service) + $275/month imminent danger pay = ~$11,133.50/month excluded
- Officer pay ABOVE this amount IS taxable
- The full-month rule still applies to the excludable portion
- **Strategy for officers**: maximize TSP contributions (traditional) from taxable portion to reduce the remaining tax liability; simultaneously maximize Roth TSP from excluded portion for triple-tax-free treatment

#### Designated Combat Zones (Current as of 2025)

| Zone | Includes | Authority |
|------|----------|-----------|
| Arabian Peninsula | Iraq, Kuwait, Saudi Arabia, Bahrain, Qatar, UAE, Oman, Persian Gulf, Gulf of Oman, Arabian Sea, Red Sea, Gulf of Aden | Executive Order 12744 |
| Afghanistan | Afghanistan and surrounding airspace | Executive Order 13239 |
| Kosovo | Kosovo, Yugoslavia (Serbia/Montenegro) area | Executive Order 13119 |
| Sinai Peninsula | Sinai Peninsula (certain multinational force operations) | Designated by DOD |

**Direct Support Areas**: personnel serving in direct support of combat zone operations in adjacent areas may also qualify. The Department of Defense certifies which support areas qualify. This includes personnel serving aboard naval vessels in qualifying waters and aircrews flying missions over combat zones.

#### Qualified Hazardous Duty Areas (QHDA)
- Similar tax exclusion applies (treated identically to combat zones for tax purposes)
- Designated by Congress, not by Executive Order
- Check current DoD and IRS announcements for active QHDAs

### What Is and Is NOT Excluded

#### Excluded from Income
- Wages and salary (subject to officer cap)
- Reenlistment bonuses (if attributable to service in the combat zone)
- Accrued leave payments (if leave was earned while in the combat zone)
- Student loan repayment benefits
- Imminent danger/hostile fire pay ($275/month)
- Awards and bonuses for service in the combat zone
- Combat-Related Special Compensation (CRSC): tax-free always

#### NOT Excluded
- Investment income (dividends, interest, capital gains) -- combat zone status does not affect
- Rental income from properties
- Spouse's income (spouse does not benefit from the exclusion)
- Retirement pay (unless disability-related and combat-connected)
- Income earned before or after combat zone service (outside the full-month period)

## Automatic Filing Extensions -- Section 7508

### Combat Zone Extension Mechanics

The extension is calculated as follows:

```
Extension = 180 days
          + Number of days remaining in the filing period
            when the taxpayer entered the combat zone

Starting from: The later of:
  (a) Last day of service in the combat zone, OR
  (b) Last day of continuous hospitalization from
      combat zone service
```

#### Example Calculation
- Service member deploys to combat zone on March 1, 2025
- Normal 2024 filing deadline: April 15, 2025
- Days remaining when deployed: 45 days (March 1 to April 15)
- Returns from combat zone: August 15, 2025
- Extension: August 15 + 180 days + 45 days = February 9, 2026 (approx.)
- This covers filing, payment, IRA contributions, and all other time-sensitive actions

#### What the Extension Covers
- Filing returns (income, employment, estate, gift, excise)
- Paying tax
- Filing claims for refund
- IRS assessments and collection actions
- Filing claims for credit or refund
- Making IRA/Roth IRA contributions
- Making 401(k)/TSP contributions
- 1031 exchange identification and replacement periods
- Rollover deadlines
- Any other "time-sensitive act" under the IRC

#### Qualifying Hospitalization
- If hospitalized as a result of combat zone service: extension continues until 180 days after release from continuous hospitalization
- Maximum hospitalization period: up to 5 years
- Does not need to be continuous hospitalization in a military facility -- can include VA or civilian hospitals if the hospitalization is a result of combat zone service

#### How to Claim
- Write "COMBAT ZONE" and deployment dates on the top of the return
- No form or application needed -- the extension is automatic
- Spouse filing jointly ALSO receives the extension (even if the spouse was not in the combat zone)
- If filing separately: only the deployed spouse gets the extension

## TSP (Thrift Savings Plan) -- Combat Zone Strategy

### Standard Contribution Limits (2025)
- **Elective deferrals**: $23,500 (traditional or Roth TSP)
- **Catch-up (age 50+)**: additional $7,500
- **Super catch-up (ages 60-63)**: additional $11,250 (SECURE 2.0)
- **Total annual addition limit** (Section 415(c)): $70,000 (includes employee + government contributions)

### The Triple-Tax-Free Roth TSP Strategy

This is one of the most powerful wealth-building provisions in the entire tax code:

1. **Income earned in combat zone is tax-free** (Section 112 exclusion)
2. **Roth TSP contributions grow tax-free**
3. **Qualified Roth TSP withdrawals are tax-free** (Section 402A)

**Result**: money that was NEVER taxed when earned, grows tax-free, and is withdrawn tax-free. The income is NEVER taxed at any point in its lifecycle.

#### How to Execute
- While deployed in a combat zone, maximize Roth TSP contributions
- Use combat zone excluded pay for the contributions
- The income used for contributions was already excluded from tax
- Growth is tax-free (Roth treatment)
- Withdrawals in retirement are tax-free (Roth treatment)
- This is functionally identical to earning money that is never subject to federal income tax -- ever

### Combat Zone Contribution Limits -- Section 415(c)

- Normal elective deferral limit ($23,500) applies to the COMBINED traditional + Roth TSP employee contributions
- BUT: the **annual addition limit ($70,000)** is the ceiling for total contributions including government match
- **In combat zones**: service members can contribute above the normal $23,500 elective deferral limit up to the $70,000 annual addition ceiling through "tax-exempt contributions"
- These excess contributions (above $23,500 but within $70,000) go to a special **tax-exempt balance** in the TSP
- Tax-exempt contributions to traditional TSP: contributions are tax-free (already excluded), but EARNINGS are taxed on withdrawal
- Tax-exempt contributions to Roth TSP: contributions AND earnings are tax-free on withdrawal (the best outcome)

#### Example: Maximizing During 12-Month Deployment
- Monthly base pay (E-6): ~$4,200
- Full-month combat zone exclusion: $4,200/month = $50,400/year excluded
- Government match (5%): ~$2,520/year
- Maximum personal contribution: $70,000 - $2,520 = $67,480
- Allocate as much as possible to Roth TSP for triple-tax-free treatment
- Even $3,000-$5,000/month to Roth TSP during deployment creates an enormous tax-free retirement foundation

## State Tax Protections

### SCRA (Servicemembers Civil Relief Act) -- Deep Dive

#### State Income Tax Protection (50 USC Section 4001 et seq.)
- Military pay is taxed ONLY by the service member's **state of legal residence (domicile)** -- NOT the state where stationed
- Service member maintains domicile in home state regardless of PCS moves
- Can change legal residence at any time (but must demonstrate intent: voter registration, vehicle registration, driver's license, bank accounts)
- **Strategy**: establish legal residence in a no-income-tax state (TX, FL, NV, WA, WY, SD, AK, TN, NH) early in career and maintain it throughout

#### 6% Interest Rate Cap (50 USC Section 3937)
- Pre-service debts: interest rate capped at 6% during military service (and applicable afterward for some categories)
- Applies to: mortgages, credit cards, auto loans, student loans, personal loans
- Must request the reduction from the creditor in writing and provide a copy of military orders
- Creditor must forgive (not defer) the excess interest above 6%
- This is not a tax provision but significantly reduces financial burden

#### Foreclosure and Eviction Protection
- No foreclosure on pre-service mortgages during service + 1 year after service without court order
- No eviction from rented premises if monthly rent is below a threshold (~$4,414.40/month for 2025) without court order
- Lease termination rights: can terminate residential leases upon receiving PCS orders or deployment orders for 90+ days

#### Tax Sale Protection
- Property cannot be sold for unpaid taxes during service + 180 days without court approval

### MSRRA (Military Spouses Residency Relief Act)

- **Spouse can elect to use the service member's state of legal residence** for income tax purposes
- Even if the spouse has NEVER lived in that state
- Applies to the spouse's earned income from services performed in the state where the couple resides due to military orders
- Does NOT apply to: income from property in other states, self-employment income from a business physically located elsewhere
- **Combined strategy**: service member claims TX as legal residence + spouse elects TX under MSRRA = ZERO state income tax for the entire family on wages/salary
- Spouse must file a declaration of domicile / election with their return

#### Qualifying Conditions for MSRRA
- The service member must be present in the state due to military orders
- The spouse must be in the same state to be with the service member
- The service member must have a different state of legal residence than the state where stationed

## Moving Expenses -- Active Duty Exception

### Still Deductible for Military (Post-TCJA)
- Under TCJA: moving expenses are NOT deductible for most taxpayers (2018-2025)
- **Exception**: active-duty military members moving under military orders for a Permanent Change of Station (PCS)
- This is an **above-the-line deduction** (no need to itemize)

### Deductible Expenses
- Transportation and storage of household goods and personal effects
- Travel (including lodging) from old home to new home for the service member and family
- Mileage for driving personal vehicle: standard mileage rate ($0.22/mile for moving in 2025 -- verify; this is the moving-specific rate)

### NOT Deductible
- Meals during the move
- Temporary housing at the new location
- House-hunting trips
- Real estate costs (selling/buying)

### Military Moving Allowances (Tax-Free)
- Dislocation Allowance (DLA): excluded from income
- Temporary Lodging Expense (TLE) / Temporary Lodging Allowance (TLA): excluded
- Monetary Allowance in Lieu of Transportation (MALT): excluded
- These do NOT appear on W-2 as taxable income

## Joint Return Signing During Deployment

### Power of Attorney (POA)
- Deployed service member should sign a POA (Form 2848 or general POA) before deployment authorizing the spouse to sign the joint return
- Must specifically authorize tax return signing
- Attach a copy of the POA to paper-filed return
- For e-filing: enter spouse information as authorized signer; keep POA on file

### Without POA
- Non-deployed spouse can file a joint return by attaching a statement explaining that the deployed spouse is in a combat zone and unable to sign
- Alternatively: file MFS initially, then amend to MFJ when the deployed spouse returns
- Filing MFJ is almost always more beneficial -- make POA a pre-deployment checklist item

## Survivor Benefit Plan (SBP) Taxation

- **SBP premiums**: deducted from retired pay before taxes (pre-tax, reducing taxable retired pay)
- **SBP benefits received by survivor**: fully taxable as ordinary income (since premiums were pre-tax)
- Reported on Form 1099-R to the survivor
- SBP benefits are subject to the Social Security earnings test if the survivor is under FRA and working
- **Dependency and Indemnity Compensation (DIC)**: VA benefit that is TAX-FREE; offsets SBP (SBP reduced by DIC amount under current law, though legislation has been proposed to eliminate the offset)

## Combat-Related Special Compensation (CRSC)

- Tax-free monthly payment for retirees with combat-related disabilities
- Separate from VA disability compensation
- Must apply through the service branch (not VA)
- Not subject to income tax or reporting requirements
- Can receive CRSC concurrently with military retired pay and VA disability

## Earned Income and Credits

### EITC Combat Pay Election
- Combat zone excluded pay can be ELECTED to be treated as earned income for EITC purposes
- This election is optional -- run the calculation both ways
- Including combat pay may INCREASE EITC (if it pushes earned income into a higher credit range)
- Including combat pay may DECREASE EITC (if it pushes income past the phase-out threshold)
- The election is all-or-nothing: include all excluded combat pay or none of it

### Roth IRA Contributions
- Combat zone excluded pay counts as earned income for IRA contribution eligibility
- Service members earning only excluded combat pay CAN still contribute to a Roth IRA
- Maximum: $7,000 (2025) or taxable compensation, whichever is less
- Since the income is excluded: the Roth contribution is made with money that was never taxed, and the growth/withdrawal is tax-free (another form of triple-tax-free)

### Saver's Credit (Form 8880)
- Combat pay counts as compensation for the Saver's Credit
- Low-income service members may qualify for up to $1,000 credit for retirement contributions

## Optimization Strategies

1. **Maximize Roth TSP during deployment**: the triple-tax-free benefit is unmatched -- contribute as much as possible while in a combat zone
2. **Establish domicile in a no-tax state**: FL, TX, NV, WA, WY, SD, AK, TN -- maintains zero state income tax for entire career; change requires updating DD Form 2058
3. **Spouse elects service member's state under MSRRA**: eliminates state tax on spouse's wages too
4. **EITC combat pay election**: run both calculations; for E-1 through E-5 with children, this can mean $1,000-$7,000 in refundable credits
5. **Roth IRA contributions using combat pay**: another layer of never-taxed money growing tax-free
6. **Maximize contributions up to Section 415(c) limit**: in a combat zone, you can put up to $70,000/year into TSP (minus government match)
7. **Time large financial events to deployment year**: capital gains, Roth conversions may be more favorable when combat zone exclusion reduces AGI
8. **Use the filing extension for planning**: the 180-day+ extension provides time for optimal IRA contributions and tax planning
9. **Pre-deployment POA**: ensure spouse can sign returns, manage finances, claim refunds
10. **Request SCRA interest rate reduction**: 6% cap on pre-service debt saves meaningful interest costs

## Common Mistakes

1. **Not verifying W-2 combat pay coding** -- Box 12 Code Q should reflect excluded pay; Box 1 should NOT include combat-excluded pay; errors happen
2. **Missing the Roth TSP opportunity** -- the triple-tax-free benefit during deployment is irreplaceable
3. **Not establishing domicile in a no-tax state** -- continuing to claim domicile in CA, VA, or other high-tax states costs thousands per year
4. **Spouse not electing MSRRA** -- failing to use the service member's state of residence for the spouse's income
5. **Forgetting to file a return** -- even with extensions, refundable credits (EITC, CTC) go unclaimed; must file to receive them
6. **Not using POA for joint return** -- complicated filing when deployed spouse cannot sign
7. **Overlooking moving expense deduction** -- military PCS moves remain deductible; often forgotten in the TCJA era
8. **Not running the EITC combat pay election both ways** -- the optimal choice is not always obvious
9. **Officers not understanding the cap** -- officers cannot exclude all pay; plan TSP contributions to offset the taxable excess
10. **Ignoring SCRA protections** -- the 6% interest rate cap, foreclosure protection, and lease termination rights are significant financial benefits that require the service member to actively request them

## State Considerations

- **Legal residence state**: determined by DD Form 2058; only this state can tax military income
- **State filing requirements**: only file in legal residence state (for military income); file in other states only for non-military income sourced there (rental property, spouse's civilian employment in a non-MSRRA-elected state)
- **California**: does NOT exempt military pay; if CA is your legal residence, all military pay is subject to CA tax; strong incentive to change domicile
- **Virginia**: partial exemption only ($15,000 subtraction with income limits); consider domicile change
- **Texas, Florida, Nevada, Wyoming, South Dakota, Alaska, Washington, Tennessee**: no income tax; ideal legal residence states
- **Property tax**: military homeowners may qualify for special exemptions in many states (especially disabled veterans -- exemptions can reach 100% for highly-rated disabilities)
- **State combat zone provisions**: some states provide additional benefits (state tax credits, bonus payments, property tax freezes) for deployed service members
