## 2. STANDARD DEDUCTION

| Filing Status | Standard Deduction |
|---|---|
| Single | $15,750 |
| Married Filing Jointly (MFJ) | $31,500 |
| Married Filing Separately (MFS) | $15,750 |
| Head of Household (HOH) | $23,625 |
| Qualifying Surviving Spouse (QSS) | $31,500 |

**2025 rule:** Use the current Form 1040 instructions for the target filing status. Do not
infer Single, HOH, or QSS amounts from prior-year figures or rough TCJA/OBBBA summaries.

### Additional Standard Deduction (Age 65+ or Blind)

| Filing Status | Additional Amount (per qualifying condition) |
|---|---|
| Single / HOH | $2,000 |
| MFJ / MFS / QSS | $1,600 |

A taxpayer who is both 65+ and blind gets two additional amounts. A married couple
where both spouses are 65+ gets two additional amounts ($3,200 total for MFJ).

### OBBBA Senior Bonus Deduction (Age 65+)

- **Amount:** $4,000 additional above-the-line deduction for taxpayers age 65 or older
- **Type:** Above-the-line (reduces AGI), separate from the additional standard deduction amounts above
- **MAGI phase-out:** Begins at $75,000 (Single) / $150,000 (MFJ) — fully phased out at $100,000 (Single) / $200,000 (MFJ)
- **Stacks with additional standard deduction:** A 65+ Single filer can claim the $2,000 additional standard deduction AND this $4,000 above-the-line deduction (if under the phase-out)
- **OBBBA provision:** New for 2025, not available in prior years
- **Reported on:** Schedule 1, Part II (Adjustments to Income)

### When to Itemize vs. Standard Deduction

Itemize (Schedule A) only when total itemized deductions exceed the standard deduction.
Primary itemized deductions: mortgage interest, SALT (capped), charitable contributions,
medical expenses (above 7.5% AGI floor), casualty losses (federally declared disasters).

## 3. ADDITIONAL TAXES

### 3A. Self-Employment Tax (SE Tax)

- **Rate:** 15.3% (12.4% Social Security + 2.9% Medicare)
- **Social Security wage base (2025):** $176,100 — only the 12.4% portion is capped
- **Medicare:** 2.9% applies to all net self-employment earnings (no cap)
- **Calculation:** Net SE income x 92.35% = SE tax base; tax = base x 15.3% (up to wage base for SS portion)
- **Deduction:** 50% of SE tax is deductible on Line 15 of Schedule 1 (above-the-line)
- **Coordination with W-2:** If you also have W-2 wages, Social Security tax applies only to combined wages + SE income up to $176,100. W-2 wages count first.
- **Form:** Schedule SE (Form 1040)

**Key strategy:** S-Corp election on an LLC allows the owner to take a "reasonable salary"
(subject to FICA) with remaining profits distributed as non-SE income. At $200K+ SE income,
this can save $10,000-$20,000+ per year. But the salary must be "reasonable" — the IRS
challenges unreasonably low salaries.

### 3B. Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT)

- **Rate:** 3.8%
- **Applies to:** The lesser of (a) net investment income or (b) MAGI exceeding threshold
- **MAGI Thresholds:**

| Filing Status | MAGI Threshold |
|---|---|
| MFJ / QSS | $250,000 |
| Single / HOH | $200,000 |
| MFS | $125,000 |

- **Net investment income includes:** Interest, dividends, capital gains, rental income (for non-RE professionals), royalties, passive business income, annuities
- **Does NOT include:** Wages, SE income, Social Security, distributions from IRAs/qualified plans, active trade or business income, gain on sale of primary residence (up to exclusion)
- **Form:** Form 8960
- **Not indexed for inflation** — these thresholds have not changed since 2013

### 3C. Additional Medicare Tax

- **Rate:** 0.9%
- **Applies to:** Wages, compensation, and SE income exceeding threshold
- **Thresholds:**

| Filing Status | Threshold |
|---|---|
| MFJ | $250,000 |
| Single / HOH | $200,000 |
| MFS | $125,000 |

- **Combined with employer share:** Total Medicare on high earnings = 2.9% (regular) + 0.9% (additional) = 3.8% on wages above threshold
- **For SE:** 2.9% (SE Medicare) + 0.9% (additional) = 3.8% effective Medicare rate
- **Form:** Form 8959
- **Withholding:** Employers must withhold the 0.9% on wages exceeding $200,000 per employer (regardless of filing status). Over/under-withholding is reconciled on the return.

### 3D. Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT)

#### AMT Exemption Amounts (2025)

| Filing Status | Exemption | Phase-out Begins | Exemption Fully Eliminated |
|---|---|---|---|
| Single / HOH | $88,100 | $626,350 | $978,750 |
| MFJ / QSS | $137,000 | $1,252,700 | $1,800,700 |
| MFS | $68,500 | $626,350 | $900,350 |

#### AMT Rates

| AMTI (after exemption) | Rate |
|---|---|
| First $239,100 ($119,550 MFS) | 26% |
| Above $239,100 ($119,550 MFS) | 28% |

#### Key AMT Adjustments and Preferences

- **SALT deduction:** Fully disallowed for AMT (but SALT cap already limits this)
- **State/local tax refunds:** Not included in AMT income if SALT was not deducted
- **Private activity bond interest:** Included in AMT income
- **ISO exercise spread:** The spread on incentive stock option exercises (FMV minus strike price) is an AMT preference item
- **Depreciation:** Pre-1999 property may require AMT depreciation adjustment (less common now)
- **Standard deduction:** Cannot be claimed for AMT (but most AMT taxpayers itemize)
- **Medical expenses:** 7.5% AGI floor applies for both regular tax and AMT (harmonized)

**OBBBA impact:** AMT exemptions are inflation-indexed. The high exemption levels mean
AMT affects far fewer taxpayers than pre-TCJA. Primary AMT triggers now: large ISO
exercises, significant private activity bond income, very high state taxes in combination
with other preferences.

## 4. KEY DEDUCTIONS

### 4A. State and Local Tax (SALT) Deduction — IRC Section 164

- **2025 maximum deduction:** Generally $40,000 ($20,000 if MFS), subject to limitation for
  higher-MAGI taxpayers under the current Schedule A / Form 1040 instructions.
- **Do not assume separate Single or HOH caps** from old drafts or secondary summaries. Verify
  the current-year worksheet / instructions when MAGI is high or filing status is unusual.
- **What counts:** State and local income taxes (or sales tax, if elected), real property taxes, personal property taxes
- **Does NOT include:** Foreign real property taxes (post-TCJA), transfer taxes
- **PTET workaround:** Pass-through entity tax elections in 30+ states allow the entity to deduct state tax at the entity level (bypassing the individual SALT cap). The individual gets a state credit for the PTET paid.

### 4B. Mortgage Interest Deduction — IRC Section 163(h)

- **Acquisition debt limit:** $750,000 ($375,000 MFS) for mortgages originated after Dec 15, 2017
- **Grandfathered debt:** Mortgages originated on or before Dec 15, 2017 retain the $1,000,000 limit
- **Home equity interest:** Deductible ONLY if used to buy, build, or substantially improve the home securing the loan (regardless of when originated)
- **Qualified residence:** Primary home + one second home
- **Points:** Deductible in year paid if for purchase of primary residence; amortized if for refinance
- **Refinance rule:** When refinancing, the new loan inherits the character of the old loan. Unamortized points from the old loan are fully deductible in the year of refinance.
- **Form:** Schedule A, plus Form 1098 from lender

### 4C. Qualified Business Income (QBI) Deduction — IRC Section 199A

- **Deduction:** 20% of qualified business income from pass-through entities (sole proprietorships, partnerships, S-Corps, trusts)
- **Taxable income limits (2025):**
  - Full deduction available below: $197,300 (Single) / $394,600 (MFJ)
  - Phase-out range: $197,300-$247,300 (Single) / $394,600-$494,600 (MFJ)
  - Above phase-out: W-2 wage / UBIA limitations apply; SSTB income is fully excluded
- **Specified Service Trades or Businesses (SSTB):** Health, law, accounting, actuarial science, performing arts, consulting, athletics, financial services, brokerage, investment management, trading. Engineering and architecture are NOT SSTBs.
- **W-2 wage/UBIA limitation (above phase-out):** Lesser of 50% of W-2 wages, or 25% of W-2 wages + 2.5% of UBIA (unadjusted basis immediately after acquisition of qualified property)
- **Aggregation:** Taxpayers may aggregate multiple businesses to meet W-2/UBIA limits (election on Form 8995-A)
- **REIT/PTP income:** REIT dividends and publicly traded partnership income qualify for the 20% deduction without W-2 wage limitations
- **OBBBA:** Section 199A was made permanent (no sunset)
- **Form:** Form 8995 (simplified) or Form 8995-A (with limitations)

### 4D. Business Expense Deduction — IRC Section 162

- **Standard:** Expenses must be "ordinary and necessary" in the conduct of a trade or business
- **Common deductions:** Office supplies, software, professional services, advertising, travel, meals (50% for business meals), insurance, license fees, continuing education
- **Not deductible:** Commuting costs (home to regular workplace), clothing (unless not suitable for everyday wear), political contributions, fines/penalties, most entertainment (post-TCJA)
- **Substantiation (Section 274):** Travel, meals, and listed property require contemporaneous records: amount, time, place, business purpose, and business relationship
- **Form:** Schedule C (sole proprietorship), Form 1065 (partnership), Form 1120-S (S-Corp)

### 4E. Home Office Deduction — IRC Section 280A

- **Requirements:** Regular and exclusive use of a dedicated space as principal place of business, OR a place where the taxpayer meets clients/customers in the normal course of business
- **Simplified method:** $5/sq ft, max 300 sq ft = $1,500 maximum deduction
- **Regular method:** Actual expenses (mortgage interest/rent, utilities, insurance, repairs, depreciation) prorated by business-use percentage (sq ft of office / total sq ft)
- **W-2 employees:** Generally cannot claim a federal home office deduction under current law
- **Direct vs. indirect expenses:** Expenses solely for the office (e.g., painting the office) are 100% deductible. Expenses for the whole house (e.g., utilities) are prorated.
- **Depreciation component:** Under regular method, the business portion of the home is depreciated over 39 years (nonresidential real property). This reduces basis and may trigger depreciation recapture on sale.
- **Form:** Form 8829 (regular method) or deducted directly on Schedule C (simplified)

### 4F. Student Loan Interest Deduction

- **Maximum:** $2,500 above-the-line deduction
- **MAGI phase-out (2025):** $85,000-$100,000 (Single), $170,000-$200,000 (MFJ)
- **Not available to MFS filers**

### 4G. Health Savings Account (HSA) Deduction

- **2025 contribution limits:** $4,300 (self-only), $8,550 (family)
- **Catch-up (age 55+):** Additional $1,000
- **Requires:** Enrollment in a High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP)
- **Above-the-line deduction** — reduces AGI
- **Triple tax benefit:** Deductible going in, tax-free growth, tax-free withdrawals for qualified medical expenses

### 4H. Educator Expense Deduction

- **Maximum:** $300 per educator ($600 for two married educators)
- **Above-the-line deduction** for K-12 educators with 900+ hours

### 4I. OBBBA Deduction for Tips (Tipped Workers)

- **Type:** Above-the-line deduction (reduces AGI)
- **Effective:** Tax years beginning after December 31, 2024 (first applies to 2025)
- **Eligibility:** Taxpayers who receive tips as part of their compensation in a tipped occupation (food service, hospitality, etc.)
- **Amount:** Cash tips received and reported as income are deductible, subject to applicable caps and limitations under the statute
- **Interaction:** Tips remain subject to FICA/Medicare taxes; this deduction applies only for income tax purposes
- **Reported on:** Schedule 1, Part II (Adjustments to Income)
- **OBBBA provision:** New for 2025

### 4J. OBBBA Deduction for Overtime Pay

- **Type:** Above-the-line deduction (reduces AGI)
- **Effective:** Tax years beginning after December 31, 2024 (first applies to 2025)
- **Eligibility:** Taxpayers who receive overtime pay as defined under the Fair Labor Standards Act (FLSA) — generally hours worked beyond 40 in a workweek at 1.5x rate
- **Amount:** Overtime premium pay (the additional amount above straight-time) is deductible for income tax purposes, subject to applicable caps and limitations
- **Interaction:** Overtime pay remains subject to FICA/Medicare taxes; this deduction applies only for income tax purposes
- **Salaried/exempt employees:** Generally not eligible unless the overtime pay qualifies under FLSA definitions
- **Reported on:** Schedule 1, Part II (Adjustments to Income)
- **OBBBA provision:** New for 2025

### 4K. OBBBA Deduction for Auto Loan Interest (Domestically Assembled Vehicles)

- **Type:** Above-the-line deduction (reduces AGI)
- **Effective:** Tax years beginning after December 31, 2024 (first applies to 2025)
- **Eligibility:** Interest paid on auto loans for vehicles that are assembled in the United States
- **Amount:** Interest on qualifying auto loans is deductible, subject to a cap (verify current IRS guidance for the applicable dollar limit)
- **Vehicle requirement:** The vehicle must be assembled domestically; verify the VIN-based final assembly determination similar to the clean vehicle credit rules
- **Not limited to business use:** This deduction applies to personal-use vehicles (unlike standard auto interest, which is generally nondeductible personal interest)
- **Reported on:** Schedule 1, Part II (Adjustments to Income)
- **OBBBA provision:** New for 2025

### 4L. Charitable Contributions — IRC Section 170

- **Cash to public charities:** Up to 60% of AGI
- **Appreciated capital gain property:** Up to 30% of AGI (no capital gains tax on the appreciation)
- **Private foundations:** 30% of AGI (cash), 20% of AGI (appreciated property)
- **Donor-Advised Funds (DAFs):** Treated as public charity contributions (60%/30% limits)
- **Qualified Charitable Distributions (QCDs):** Age 70.5+, up to $108,000 directly from IRA to charity, counts toward RMD but excluded from income
- **Carryforward:** Excess contributions carry forward 5 years
- **Substantiation:** Cash >$250 requires written acknowledgment; non-cash >$500 requires Form 8283; non-cash >$5,000 requires qualified appraisal (with exceptions for publicly traded securities)

### 4M. Medical and Dental Expenses — IRC Section 213

- **Floor:** 7.5% of AGI — only expenses exceeding this floor are deductible
- **What qualifies:** Insurance premiums (not pre-tax), doctors, dentists, hospital, prescriptions, medical equipment, long-term care premiums (age-based limits), transportation for medical care
- **What does not qualify:** Cosmetic surgery (unless for deformity from disease/accident), gym memberships, general health items, over-the-counter medications (unless prescribed)

### 4N. Net Operating Loss (NOL) — IRC Section 172

- **Carryforward:** NOLs may be carried forward indefinitely (no carryback for most taxpayers; limited 2-year carryback exceptions for farming losses)
- **Limitation:** NOL deduction is limited to 80% of taxable income (computed without the NOL deduction) for NOLs arising in tax years beginning after December 31, 2017
- **OBBBA:** The 80% limitation is made permanent (no sunset)
- **Ordering:** NOLs from earlier years are applied before later years
- **Form:** Used on Form 1040 via Schedule 1; detailed computation on Form 172 or attached statement
- **Individual context:** Most common for self-employed taxpayers, rental real estate professionals, and business owners with large losses exceeding other income

### 4O. Excess Business Loss Limitation — IRC Section 461(l)

- **Threshold (2025):** Business losses exceeding $305,000 (Single/HOH) or $610,000 (MFJ) are disallowed in the current year
- **Excess becomes NOL:** Disallowed excess business losses are treated as an NOL carryforward to the following tax year
- **What counts:** Aggregate net losses from all trades or businesses (Schedule C, K-1 business income/loss, rental if materially participating as REP, farm income/loss)
- **What does NOT count:** Capital losses, investment losses, wages (these are not "business" losses for this purpose)
- **OBBBA:** Made permanent (no sunset); originally enacted by TCJA with a 2026 expiration
- **Indexed for inflation** annually
- **Applied after passive activity rules:** First apply Section 469 passive activity limitations, then apply this excess business loss limitation
- **Form:** Form 461

## 5. KEY CREDITS

### 5A. Child Tax Credit (CTC) — IRC Section 24

- **Credit amount (2025 OBBBA):** $2,200 per qualifying child under age 17
- **Refundable portion:** Up to $1,700 (Additional Child Tax Credit via Schedule 8812)
- **Phase-out thresholds:**
  - MFJ: $400,000
  - All other statuses: $200,000
  - Phase-out rate: $50 per $1,000 of MAGI above threshold
- **Qualifying child requirements:** Under 17, US citizen/resident, SSN, lived with taxpayer >6 months, claimed as dependent, not self-supporting
- **OBBBA change:** Increased from $2,000 to $2,200 per child

### 5B. Child and Dependent Care Credit — IRC Section 21

- **Maximum eligible expenses:** $3,000 (one qualifying individual), $6,000 (two or more)
- **Credit rate:** 20%-35% of eligible expenses, based on AGI (35% for AGI up to $15,000, declining to 20% for AGI over $43,000)
- **Qualifying individual:** Child under 13, or disabled spouse/dependent
- **Requirements:** Expenses must be for care to enable taxpayer (and spouse, if married) to work or look for work
- **Cannot use:** Payments to a person the taxpayer can claim as a dependent, or to taxpayer's child under age 19
- **Form:** Form 2441

### 5C. American Opportunity Tax Credit (AOTC) — IRC Section 25A(b)

- **Maximum:** $2,500 per eligible student (100% of first $2,000 + 25% of next $2,000)
- **Refundable:** 40% (up to $1,000 refundable)
- **MAGI phase-out:** $80,000-$90,000 (Single), $160,000-$180,000 (MFJ)
- **Eligibility:** First 4 years of post-secondary education, at least half-time, pursuing degree, no felony drug conviction
- **Expenses:** Tuition, required fees, course materials (not room/board)
- **Form:** Form 8863

### 5D. Lifetime Learning Credit (LLC) — IRC Section 25A(c)

- **Maximum:** $2,000 per return (20% of first $10,000 in expenses)
- **Non-refundable**
- **MAGI phase-out:** $80,000-$90,000 (Single), $160,000-$180,000 (MFJ)
- **Eligibility:** Any post-secondary education or courses to improve job skills, no limit on years
- **Cannot claim both AOTC and LLC for the same student in the same year**

### 5E. Energy Efficient Home Improvement Credit — IRC Section 25C

- **Annual limit:** $3,200 (combined)
  - $1,200 cap for most property (windows, doors, insulation, energy audits)
  - $2,000 cap for heat pumps, heat pump water heaters, biomass stoves
  - $150 for home energy audits
- **Credit rate:** 30% of eligible costs
- **Annual credit** — resets each year (not lifetime)
- **No income phase-out**
- **Must be for taxpayer's principal residence**

### 5F. Residential Clean Energy Credit — IRC Section 25D

- **Credit rate:** 30% through 2032, then phases down (26% in 2033, 22% in 2034)
- **Eligible property:** Solar panels (electric and water heating), wind turbines, geothermal heat pumps, battery storage (3 kWh+), fuel cells
- **No annual dollar cap** (except fuel cells: $500/0.5 kW)
- **No income phase-out**
- **Primary or secondary residence** (fuel cells: primary only)
- **Excess credit carries forward**
- **Form:** Form 5695

### 5G. Clean Vehicle Credit — IRC Section 30D

- **Maximum:** $7,500 for new qualifying EVs/PHEVs
- **Components:** $3,750 for critical minerals requirement + $3,750 for battery components requirement
- **MAGI limits:** $150,000 (Single), $300,000 (MFJ), $225,000 (HOH)
- **MSRP limits:** $80,000 (SUVs/trucks/vans), $55,000 (all other vehicles)
- **Transfer to dealer:** Credit can be transferred to dealer at point of sale as a price reduction
- **Used clean vehicle credit (Section 25E):** $4,000 or 30% of sale price (whichever is less), MAGI limits $75,000 (Single), $150,000 (MFJ)
- **Form:** Form 8936

### 5H. Foreign Tax Credit — IRC Section 901

- **Purpose:** Prevents double taxation of foreign-source income
- **Limitation:** Credit cannot exceed US tax attributable to foreign-source income (Form 1116 calculation)
- **Election:** Can elect to deduct foreign taxes instead of claiming credit (rarely beneficial)
- **Carryforward:** Unused credits carry forward 10 years (back 1 year)
- **De minimis exception:** If total creditable foreign taxes are $300 or less ($600 MFJ), all from qualified passive category income, and reported on Form 1099, can claim credit directly on Form 1040 without Form 1116
- **Form:** Form 1116 (or direct on 1040 if de minimis)

### 5I. Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC) — IRC Section 32

| Qualifying Children | Maximum Credit (2025) | Earned Income Phase-out Begins (Single) | Phase-out Begins (MFJ) |
|---|---|---|---|
| 3+ | ~$7,830 | ~$21,300 | ~$27,400 |
| 2 | ~$6,960 | ~$20,600 | ~$26,700 |
| 1 | ~$4,213 | ~$20,200 | ~$26,300 |
| 0 | ~$632 | ~$10,400 | ~$16,500 |

- **Investment income limit:** $11,600 (2025)
- **Must have earned income, valid SSN, US residency >6 months**
- **Filing status cannot be MFS**
- **Form:** Schedule EIC (for those with qualifying children)

### 5J. Saver's Credit (Retirement Savings Contributions Credit) — IRC Section 25B

- **Credit rate:** 10%, 20%, or 50% of retirement contributions up to $2,000 ($4,000 MFJ)
- **AGI limits (2025):**
  - 50% rate: AGI up to ~$23,750 (Single), ~$47,500 (MFJ)
  - 20% rate: AGI up to ~$25,800 (Single), ~$51,600 (MFJ)
  - 10% rate: AGI up to ~$39,750 (Single), ~$79,500 (MFJ)
- **Non-refundable**
- **Form:** Form 8880

### 5K. Adoption Credit — IRC Section 23

- **Maximum credit (2025):** $17,280 per eligible child
- **Applies to:** Qualified adoption expenses (adoption fees, court costs, attorney fees, travel, other directly related expenses)
- **Special needs adoption:** The full credit amount is available regardless of actual expenses incurred
- **MAGI phase-out (2025):** Begins at ~$252,150, fully phased out at ~$292,150
- **Non-refundable** — but unused credit carries forward up to 5 years
- **Timing:** For domestic adoptions, expenses are claimed the year after they are paid (unless finalized in the same year). For foreign adoptions, expenses are claimed the year the adoption becomes final.
- **Employer exclusion:** Up to $17,280 of employer-provided adoption assistance is excludable from income (same phase-out range)
- **Cannot claim for stepchild adoption**
- **Form:** Form 8839

## 6. CAPITAL GAINS AND LOSSES

### 6A. Long-Term Capital Gains Rates (Assets Held > 1 Year)

**0% Rate:**

| Filing Status | Taxable Income Up To |
|---|---|
| Single | $48,350 |
| MFJ / QSS | $96,700 |
| MFS | $48,350 |
| HOH | $64,750 |

**15% Rate:**

| Filing Status | Taxable Income Up To |
|---|---|
| Single | $533,400 |
| MFJ / QSS | $600,050 |
| MFS | $300,025 |
| HOH | $566,700 |

**20% Rate:** Taxable income above the 15% thresholds.

**Note:** These rates apply to the taxable income amount that falls within each bracket
after accounting for all other income. NIIT (3.8%) may apply on top for high earners.

### 6B. Special Capital Gains Rates

- **Unrecaptured Section 1250 gain (depreciation on real property):** Maximum 25% rate
- **Collectibles (art, antiques, coins, gems, stamps, etc.):** Maximum 28% rate
- **Qualified small business stock (Section 1202):** Up to 100% exclusion for stock acquired after Sept 27, 2010 and held 5+ years. Pre-OBBBA stock: $10M or 10x basis cap. Post-OBBBA stock (issued after July 4, 2025): $15M or 10x basis cap, inflation-indexed starting 2027. See [QSBS-SECTION-1202.md](../strategies/QSBS-SECTION-1202.md).
- **Section 1244 stock:** Ordinary loss treatment up to $50,000 ($100,000 MFJ) on qualifying small business stock

### 6C. Short-Term Capital Gains

- Held 1 year or less — taxed as ordinary income at the taxpayer's marginal rate
- Reported on Form 8949 and Schedule D

### 6D. Capital Loss Rules

- **Net capital losses:** Deductible against ordinary income up to $3,000/year ($1,500 MFS)
- **Excess losses:** Carry forward indefinitely (no carryback for individuals)
- **Ordering rules:** Short-term losses first offset short-term gains; long-term losses first offset long-term gains; then net short and long offset each other
- **Wash sale rule (Section 1091):** Cannot deduct loss on sale of security if "substantially identical" security purchased within 30 days before or after the sale. Disallowed loss is added to basis of replacement security.

### 6E. Form 8949 Categories

| Box | Description | Reporting Basis |
|---|---|---|
| A | Short-term, basis reported to IRS (Form 1099-B box checked) | 1099-B |
| B | Short-term, basis NOT reported to IRS | Taxpayer records |
| C | Short-term, no Form 1099-B received | Taxpayer records |
| D | Long-term, basis reported to IRS | 1099-B |
| E | Long-term, basis NOT reported to IRS | Taxpayer records |
| F | Long-term, no Form 1099-B received | Taxpayer records |

**Adjustment codes:** W (wash sale), B (incorrect basis on 1099-B), O (other), T (short-term per
1099-B but long-term per holding period), and others per Form 8949 instructions.

### 6F. Cryptocurrency

- Treated as property (IRS Notice 2014-21, Rev. Rul. 2019-24)
- Every disposition (sale, exchange, payment for goods/services, swap between cryptocurrencies) is a taxable event
- **Cost basis methods:** Specific identification (if lots can be identified), FIFO (default), HIFO, LIFO (if specifically identified)
- **Zero-basis risk:** If basis records are lost, the IRS may treat the basis as $0 — full proceeds become gain
- **Mining/staking income:** Ordinary income at FMV when received, then capital gain/loss on subsequent disposition
- **Airdrop income:** Ordinary income at FMV when received and taxpayer has dominion/control
- **Form:** Form 8949, Schedule D; income from mining/staking on Schedule 1 or Schedule C

## 7. RETIREMENT PLANS

### 7A. Solo 401(k) — IRC Section 401(k)

- **Employee contribution (2025):** $23,500 (under 50), $31,000 (age 50-59 or 64+), $34,750 (age 60-63 super catch-up)
- **Employer contribution:** Up to 25% of W-2 compensation (S-Corp/C-Corp) or ~20% of net SE income after SE tax deduction (sole proprietorship/partnership)
- **Total combined limit:** $70,000 (under 50), $77,500 (age 50-59 or 64+), $81,250 (age 60-63)
- **Roth option available** — employee contributions can be designated Roth (no current deduction, tax-free in retirement)
- **Adoption / establishment timing:** Verify this from current IRS guidance based on entity type
  and contribution type. For example, a sole proprietor with no employees can adopt a new owner-only
  401(k) by the tax filing deadline without extensions for employer contributions, while employee
  elective-deferral timing is stricter and tied to a timely election.
- **Contribution deadline:** Tax filing deadline including extensions (employer contributions)
- **Loan provision available** (up to $50,000 or 50% of vested balance)
- **Must cover all eligible employees if you have any** — truly "solo" means no common-law employees (spouse can participate)

### 7B. SEP-IRA — IRC Section 408(k)

- **Contribution limit:** 25% of W-2 compensation or 20% of net SE income (after SE tax deduction) — up to $70,000 (2025)
- **Why 20% for SE:** The employer deduction is calculated on compensation net of the SEP contribution itself, creating a circular calculation. The effective rate on net SE income (after 50% SE tax deduction) is 20%.
- **Establishment and contribution deadline:** Tax filing deadline including extensions
- **All eligible employees must be covered** at the same percentage — this makes SEP-IRA less attractive for business owners with employees
- **No employee contributions, no Roth option, no loans**
- **Simplicity advantage:** Easiest to establish and administer, no annual Form 5500 filing

### 7C. Traditional IRA — IRC Section 219

- **Contribution limit (2025):** $7,000 (under 50), $8,000 (age 50+)
- **Deductibility phase-out (if covered by employer plan):**
  - Single/HOH: MAGI $79,000-$89,000
  - MFJ (contributor covered): MAGI $126,000-$146,000
  - MFJ (contributor NOT covered, spouse IS covered): MAGI $236,000-$246,000
- **Not covered by employer plan:** Fully deductible regardless of income
- **Contribution deadline:** April 15 of the following year (no extension)

### 7D. Roth IRA — IRC Section 408A

- **Contribution limit (2025):** $7,000 (under 50), $8,000 (age 50+)
- **MAGI phase-out:**
  - Single/HOH: $150,000-$165,000
  - MFJ: $236,000-$246,000
  - MFS: $0-$10,000
- **Backdoor Roth:** Contribute to non-deductible Traditional IRA then convert. No income limit on conversions. Pro-rata rule applies if you have pre-tax IRA balances (including SEP and SIMPLE IRAs).
- **5-year rules:** (1) Earnings are tax-free only after 5-year holding period AND age 59.5; (2) Each conversion has its own 5-year clock for penalty-free withdrawal of the converted amount
- **No RMDs during owner's lifetime** (beneficiaries have RMDs under SECURE 2.0)

### 7E. HSA (Health Savings Account) — IRC Section 223

- **Contribution limit (2025):** $4,300 (self-only), $8,550 (family)
- **Catch-up (age 55+):** Additional $1,000
- **Requires:** High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP) — 2025 minimum deductible: $1,650 (self), $3,300 (family); max out-of-pocket: $8,300 (self), $16,600 (family)
- **Triple tax advantage:** Deductible contribution, tax-free growth, tax-free withdrawals for qualified medical expenses
- **No deadline pressure:** Receipts can be saved and reimbursed years later; the HSA can function as a stealth retirement account
- **After age 65:** Withdrawals for any purpose taxed as ordinary income (no penalty); medical withdrawals remain tax-free

### 7F. Defined Benefit Plan

- **Maximum annual benefit (2025):** $280,000 (at retirement age 62-65)
- **Contribution:** Actuarially determined — often allows very large deductible contributions ($100K-$300K+/year) for older high-income business owners
- **Best for:** Taxpayers age 50+ with high, consistent income and desire to shelter large amounts
- **Complexity:** Requires annual actuarial valuation, Form 5500 filing, and ongoing administration
- **Can combine with 401(k):** A defined benefit plan + Solo 401(k) combination can shelter $300K+ annually

### 7G. SIMPLE IRA — IRC Section 408(p)

- **Employee contribution (2025):** $16,500 (under 50), $17,500 (age 50-59 or 64+), $21,750 (age 60-63)
- **Employer match:** Dollar-for-dollar up to 3% of compensation (or 2% nonelective for all eligible employees)
- **For businesses with 100 or fewer employees**
- **Cannot maintain alongside another qualified plan for the same year**
- **25% early withdrawal penalty** if within first 2 years of participation (instead of normal 10%)

### 7H. Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs)

- **Beginning age:** 73 (born 1951-1959), 75 (born 1960+)
- **Applies to:** Traditional IRA, SEP-IRA, SIMPLE IRA, 401(k), 403(b), 457(b) (not Roth IRA during owner's lifetime)
- **First year:** Can delay to April 1 of the year after turning RMD age (but must take two distributions that year)
- **Penalty for missed RMD:** 25% of the shortfall (reduced to 10% if corrected within 2 years under SECURE 2.0)
- **Inherited IRAs:** Most non-spouse beneficiaries must empty the account within 10 years (SECURE Act), with annual RMDs required during the 10-year period if the decedent had already begun RMDs

## 8. DEPRECIATION AND EXPENSING

### 8A. MACRS Recovery Periods (GDS — General Depreciation System)

| Property Class | Examples | Convention |
|---|---|---|
| 3-year | Tractor units, racehorses (2+ yrs), qualified rent-to-own property | Half-year/mid-quarter |
| 5-year | Automobiles, computers, office equipment, appliances, carpeting, certain manufacturing equipment | Half-year/mid-quarter |
| 7-year | Office furniture, fixtures, agricultural machinery, railroad track, motorsports facilities | Half-year/mid-quarter |
| 10-year | Vessels, barges, land improvements (certain), fruit-bearing trees | Half-year/mid-quarter |
| 15-year | Qualified leasehold improvements (pre-2018), land improvements (fencing, roads, sidewalks, landscaping, parking lots), qualified restaurant property, retail improvement property | Half-year/mid-quarter |
| 20-year | Farm buildings, municipal sewers | Half-year/mid-quarter |
| 25-year | Water utility property, municipal sewage treatment | Half-year/mid-quarter |
| 27.5-year | Residential rental property | Mid-month |
| 39-year | Nonresidential real property | Mid-month |

### 8B. Section 179 Expensing — IRC Section 179

- **Maximum deduction (2025):** $2,500,000
- **Phase-out threshold:** Begins when property placed in service exceeds $4,000,000; dollar-for-dollar phase-out
- **Qualifying property:** Tangible personal property, off-the-shelf computer software, qualified improvement property, certain nonresidential real property improvements (HVAC, roofing, fire protection, alarm/security)
- **Cannot create or increase a net loss** — limited to taxable income from active trades or businesses
- **Excess carries forward** to future years
- **SUV limitation:** $31,300 for vehicles over 6,000 lbs GVWR (2025)

### 8C. Bonus Depreciation — IRC Section 168(k)

- **OBBBA restoration:** 100% first-year bonus depreciation for qualified property acquired and placed in service after January 19, 2025
- **Prior phase-down (TCJA):** 80% (2023), 60% (2024), 40% (2025 for pre-Jan 20, 2025 acquisitions), 20% (2026) — but OBBBA effectively restores 100% for new acquisitions
- **What qualifies:** New or used property with MACRS recovery period of 20 years or less, certain computer software, qualified film/TV/theater production, qualified improvement property
- **What does NOT qualify:** Property used predominantly outside the US, tax-exempt use property, certain regulated utility property
- **Election out:** Can elect out of bonus depreciation on a class-by-class basis
- **Listed property:** Must meet >50% business use test; if business use drops below 50% in later year, recapture applies

### 8D. Luxury Auto Depreciation Limits (2025) — Passenger Vehicles ≤ 6,000 lbs

| Year | With Bonus Depreciation | Without Bonus Depreciation |
|---|---|---|
| Year 1 | $20,200 | $12,400 |
| Year 2 | $19,800 | $19,800 |
| Year 3 | $11,900 | $11,900 |
| Year 4+ | $7,160 | $7,160 |

**Heavy vehicles (>6,000 lbs GVWR):** Not subject to luxury limits. Can claim full Section 179 (up to $31,300 for SUVs) plus bonus depreciation on the remainder. Popular for business owners purchasing qualifying trucks/SUVs.

### 8E. Cost Segregation (for Real Property)

- **Purpose:** Reclassifies components of a building from 27.5/39-year property to 5, 7, or 15-year property
- **Common reclassifications:** Carpeting (5-yr), decorative lighting (5-yr), certain plumbing (15-yr), parking lots (15-yr), landscaping (15-yr), electrical for specific equipment (7-yr)
- **Typically reclassifies 20-40% of building cost** to shorter-lived assets
- **Requires a cost segregation study** (often $5,000-$15,000 for a study, but can generate $50,000-$200,000+ in first-year deductions on a $500K+ property)
- **Look-back:** Can be done on properties already in service via Form 3115 (change in accounting method) — no amended returns required
- **Pairs with bonus depreciation:** 5, 7, and 15-year property eligible for 100% bonus depreciation (OBBBA)

## 9. PASSIVE ACTIVITY RULES

### 9A. General Rule — IRC Section 469

- **Passive losses can only offset passive income** — cannot offset active (W-2, business where you materially participate) or portfolio (interest, dividends, capital gains) income
- **Suspended losses carry forward** and release when the passive activity is disposed of in a fully taxable transaction
- **Passive activity:** Trade or business in which the taxpayer does NOT materially participate, or any rental activity (with exceptions)

### 9B. Material Participation Tests (Must Meet at Least One)

1. Participated more than **500 hours** during the tax year
2. Participation constituted **substantially all** of the participation in the activity
3. Participated more than **100 hours** and no other individual participated more
4. Significant participation activities: Participated 100+ hours each in multiple activities that together exceed **500 hours**
5. Materially participated in any **5 of the prior 10 tax years**
6. Personal service activity in which you materially participated in any **3 prior tax years**
7. Based on all facts and circumstances, participated on a **regular, continuous, and substantial** basis

### 9C. Rental Activity Special Rules

**Default:** All rental activities are passive regardless of material participation (Section 469(c)(2))

**Exception 1 — Active Participation Allowance:**
- Up to **$25,000** of rental losses can offset non-passive income
- Requires **active participation** (lower standard than material participation — must make management decisions: approve tenants, set rental terms, approve repairs)
- **AGI phase-out:** $100,000-$150,000 (reduced $1 for every $2 of AGI above $100,000)
- **Not available to MFS** filers living together
- **Not available for limited partners**

**Exception 2 — Real Estate Professional (REP) Status:**
- Must meet BOTH:
  1. More than **750 hours** in real property trades or businesses in which taxpayer materially participates
  2. More than **50%** of personal services performed during the year are in real property trades or businesses
- Real property trades or businesses include: construction, development, acquisition, conversion, rental, management, leasing, brokerage
- Each rental property is a separate activity unless the taxpayer elects to **aggregate** all rental interests (election on original timely filed return, irrevocable)
- **With REP status:** Rental activities in which the taxpayer materially participates are treated as non-passive — losses can offset W-2, SE, and other active income without limitation
- **With REP + cost segregation + bonus depreciation:** Can generate massive paper losses that offset high W-2/SE income
- **One spouse can qualify** — REP status is determined per-individual, not per-couple
- **Documentation is critical:** Maintain contemporaneous hour logs showing activities, dates, and hours. IRS frequently challenges REP status.

### 9D. Grouping Election — Reg. Section 1.469-4

- Taxpayers can group activities for material participation testing
- An appropriate economic unit analysis considering: similarities, common control, geographic location, business interdependencies
- Once made, grouping is generally consistent year-to-year
- Rental and non-rental activities generally cannot be grouped (exception: if the rental is insubstantial relative to the non-rental activity, or vice versa)

### 9E. At-Risk Rules — IRC Section 465

- **Separate from passive activity rules** — loss deduction is limited to the amount the taxpayer has "at risk"
- **At-risk amount includes:** Cash invested, adjusted basis of property contributed, amounts borrowed for which taxpayer is personally liable, amounts borrowed secured by property other than property used in the activity (qualified nonrecourse financing for real estate)
- **Qualified nonrecourse financing exception:** Real estate activities can include certain nonrecourse debt from qualified lenders in the at-risk amount
- **Form 6198** required when losses exceed the at-risk amount

### 10F. FBAR (FinCEN Form 114) — Foreign Bank Account Report

- **Filing requirement:** Must file if the aggregate value of all foreign financial accounts exceeds **$10,000** at any point during the calendar year
- **What counts:** Bank accounts, brokerage accounts, mutual funds, and other financial accounts held at foreign financial institutions where the US person has a financial interest or signature authority
- **Due date:** April 15 with an automatic extension to **October 15** (no form required to request extension)
- **Filed with:** FinCEN (not the IRS) — must be filed electronically through the BSA E-Filing System
- **Penalties:** Willful violations up to $100,000 or 50% of account balance per violation; non-willful violations up to $10,000 per account per year
- **Separate from Form 8938:** FBAR is a FinCEN filing; Form 8938 (FATCA) is an IRS filing with higher thresholds. Taxpayers may need to file both.
- **Who must file:** US persons (citizens, residents, entities) with foreign accounts. Applies even if the account produces no income.
