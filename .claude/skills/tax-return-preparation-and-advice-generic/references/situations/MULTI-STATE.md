# Multi-State Taxpayer Reference (Tax Year 2025)

## Overview

Taxpayers who live, work, or earn income in multiple states face complex filing obligations. This includes employees who commute across state lines, remote workers, individuals who move during the year, and those with income-producing assets in multiple states. Each state has its own rules for sourcing income, establishing residency, and providing credits for taxes paid to other states. Filing errors are common and can result in double taxation or missed refunds.

## Key Tax Considerations

### Filing Status Types
- **Resident return**: filed in your state of domicile; reports worldwide income
- **Nonresident return**: filed in states where you earn income but are not a resident;
  reports only income sourced to that state
- **Part-year resident return**: filed when you move into or out of a state during the tax year;
  reports income earned during the period of residency plus any state-sourced income from
  the non-resident period

## Domicile vs. Statutory Residence

### Domicile — Detailed Establishment Checklist
**Domicile** is your permanent home — where you intend to return. Changed by objective acts
demonstrating intent. States examine the totality of circumstances. Key factors:

1. **Driver's license**: must be issued by the new state. Surrendering the old state license
   is critical evidence. Most aggressive states (NY, CA) specifically look for this.
2. **Voter registration**: register in the new state; cancel registration in the old state.
3. **Vehicle registration**: register vehicles in the new state.
4. **Will and estate documents**: update your will to state "I am domiciled in [new state]."
   Name executors, trustees, and attorneys in the new state.
5. **Bank accounts**: open primary accounts in the new state. Close or minimize old-state accounts.
6. **Professional licenses**: if applicable, obtain new-state license; consider surrendering old.
7. **Primary residence**: own or rent in the new state. This is where you sleep most nights.
   If you keep a home in the old state, it must clearly be secondary (reduced furnishings,
   less time spent there).
8. **Family location**: where your spouse/dependents live matters enormously. NY in particular
   scrutinizes whether the family remained in NY while the taxpayer claims to have moved.
9. **Social and religious affiliations**: join clubs, churches, organizations in the new state.
10. **Medical and professional providers**: use doctors, dentists, attorneys, CPAs in the new state.
11. **Mailing address**: update all correspondence (IRS, financial institutions, subscriptions).
12. **Intent documentation**: contemporaneous statements of intent (diary entries, letters to
    family/friends, social media posts about your new home) can be powerful evidence.

### Statutory Residence
Some states tax you as a **resident** even if not domiciled there, if you meet certain tests:

- **New York**: domiciliary OR (maintains permanent place of abode + present 183 days) = resident.
  "Permanent place of abode" is broadly defined — can include an apartment maintained by an
  employer, a home you own, or even a dwelling maintained for your use by a relative.
  Even a pied-a-terre counts if it has sleeping/cooking facilities.
- **California**: presence for purposes other than "temporary or transitory" = resident. No
  specific day-count test (totality of circumstances). "Safe harbor" of 45 days or less for
  nonresidents. CA looks at the "closest connections" test — where are your strongest ties?
- **Connecticut**: 183 days + permanent place of abode = resident
- **New Jersey**: 183 days + maintained "permanent home" in NJ = resident. NJ defines
  "permanent home" more narrowly than NY defines "permanent place of abode."
- **Illinois**: 183 days + place of abode = resident (but IL uses a flat rate, so the
  impact is less variable)

**Dual residency trap**: a taxpayer can be a statutory resident of one state while domiciled
in another — potentially filing resident returns in BOTH states (with credits to attempt to
avoid full double taxation). Example: domiciled in CT, maintain NY apartment, spend 190 days
in NY = statutory resident of both NY and CT.

## Income Allocation Methods

### Wage/Salary Income
- Generally sourced to the state where work is physically performed
- Allocation methods:
  - **Days worked in each state**: most common. (Days worked in state / total workdays) x wages
  - **Convenience of employer rule**: some states source all income to the employer's state
    (see detailed section below)
- Keep detailed records of days worked in each location (calendars, travel records, badge swipes,
  VPN logs, building access records)

### Business Income (Schedule C, K-1)
- Sourced based on state apportionment formulas (varies by state):
  - Single-factor (sales only): majority of states
  - Three-factor (property, payroll, sales): some states, often with double-weighted sales
  - Market-based sourcing vs. cost-of-performance for services
- Each state has its own apportionment rules; research required for each filing state

### Investment Income
- Generally sourced to state of residence (not taxed by nonresident state)
- Exceptions: income from S-Corps, partnerships, and LLCs sourced based on entity's operations
- Real estate income: sourced to the state where the property is located
- Gains from sale of business interests: may be sourced to entity's operating state
  (varies significantly by state — some treat as investment income sourced to residence,
  others "look through" to the entity's state)

## Convenience of Employer Rule — Detailed

### States That Apply This Rule (Each with Different Specifics)

**New York** — most aggressive application:
- Wages are NY-sourced if the employer is in NY unless the employee works from a home office
  for the employer's "necessity" (not merely the employee's convenience)
- Very narrow exception: the employer must not provide a NY office for that employee's use,
  AND the remote work must be a condition of employment (not just allowed or preferred)
- NY Form IT-203 requires allocation of days
- Even a single day of work in NY may require filing a NY nonresident return

**Pennsylvania** — applies a similar convenience rule:
- PA sources wages to PA if the employer is in PA and the employee works remotely for
  their own convenience

**New Jersey** — adopted convenience rule effective 2023:
- NJ now sources wages to NJ if the employer is in NJ and the employee works remotely elsewhere
  for their own convenience (not employer necessity)

**Connecticut** — applies to nonresidents working for CT employers:
- Income is CT-sourced unless the employee is working from a state with which CT has a
  reciprocity agreement (CT does not have many)

**Delaware** — has a convenience rule but less aggressively enforced

**Nebraska** — applies convenience-of-employer sourcing for employees of NE employers

**Massachusetts** — applied an emergency pandemic rule taxing NH residents working remotely
for MA employers. New Hampshire challenged this at the Supreme Court (NH v. MA, 2021);
the Court declined to hear the case. MA's current rule requires monitoring — it was
officially a temporary COVID measure but echoes in ongoing enforcement.

**Arkansas** — has a modified convenience rule

### Credit Implications
- If your home state does not have a convenience rule: you generally get credit on your home
  state return for taxes paid to the convenience-rule state
- If your home state has no income tax (FL, TX, etc.): you may be stuck paying the
  convenience-rule state's tax with no offsetting credit. Example: FL resident working for
  NY employer pays NY tax on all wages with no FL credit (FL has no income tax to credit against).
- **Credit limitation**: the credit is typically the LESSER of the tax paid to the other state
  or the tax your home state would have imposed on that income. If the other state's rate is
  higher, the excess is simply lost.

### How Reciprocity Works in Practice
- Employee must file an exemption certificate with employer (e.g., MI uses MI-W4 to claim
  exemption from MI withholding for OH residents working in MI)
- If employer withholds incorrectly: file nonresident return to get refund; report income
  on home state return
- Reciprocity ONLY applies to wages/salaries — does NOT apply to business income, rental income,
  or other non-wage income
- Part-year residents may not qualify for reciprocity for the period before/after the move

## Credit for Taxes Paid to Other States

### Mechanics (IRC §1 Equivalent at State Level)
- Most states allow residents a credit on their home state return for income taxes paid to
  other states on income sourced to those other states
- Credit generally limited to the lesser of:
  - Tax actually paid to the other state on the income
  - Tax your home state would impose on that same income
- Prevents full double taxation but may not eliminate it entirely (if the other state's rate
  exceeds your home state's rate, the excess is unrecoverable)

### Credit Computation Methods
- **Method 1**: (income taxed by other state / total income) x home state tax
- **Method 2**: compute home state tax on the multi-state income separately, take credit for
  actual tax paid to other state up to that amount
- Each state specifies its own computation method (check specific state instructions)

### Ordering and Limitations
- If income is taxed by multiple states: compute credit for each state separately
- Some states require you to claim credit only against income also taxed by the other state
  (cannot offset unrelated income)
- Capital gains: generally sourced to residence state; if another state also taxes
  (e.g., on real property sale), claim credit in home state

### Addback Requirements
- Some states require you to "add back" state income taxes deducted on the federal return
  before computing state tax (to avoid circular benefit of deducting state tax paid to
  another state on your own state return)

## Telecommuter Taxation

### Post-COVID Landscape
- Many states issued temporary nexus relief during the pandemic; most have expired
- Permanent rules now govern remote work:
  - **Physical presence states**: tax only if employee physically works in the state
  - **Convenience rule states**: tax based on employer location regardless of where work is performed
  - **Employer nexus**: remote employees can create nexus for the employer in the employee's
    state (triggering payroll tax, income tax withholding, and business registration requirements)

### Mobile Employees
- Must track days in each state; even a single day of work may trigger filing obligations
  in some states
- **De minimis exemptions**: some states provide safe harbors:
  - **Threshold states**: OR (>300 working hours or <$300 income), CT (14 days), etc.
  - **Reciprocal**: states with reciprocity agreements (see above)
  - Many states have NO de minimis threshold — one day triggers filing obligation
- Travel for conferences, client meetings, training sessions — all count as days worked
  in that state for allocation purposes

### Digital Nomad / Remote Worker Planning
- If fully remote: generally only owe tax to state of residence (domicile/statutory residence)
- Exception: convenience-rule states (see above)
- Exception: states where you travel to work in-person even occasionally
- Employer must comply with withholding obligations in EACH state where the employee works

## Part-Year Move: Closing Out the Old State

### Filing Requirements
- File **part-year returns in BOTH** the old and new state
- Income earned while resident of old state: reported to old state
- Income earned after move: reported to new state
- Investment income, retirement distributions, non-sourced income: typically allocated based on
  residency period (days or months as resident / 365)
- Deductions and exemptions: usually prorated based on portion of year as resident

### Practical Steps
1. Document the exact date of the move (lease/closing date, moving company receipt)
2. Update employer W-4 and state withholding forms immediately
3. Adjust estimated tax payments between states (may need to start payments in new state,
   stop in old state mid-year)
4. Transfer professional licenses, driver's license, voter registration, vehicle registration
5. Update mailing address with IRS (Form 8822) and all financial institutions
6. Moving expenses: generally NOT deductible under TCJA (exception: active-duty military)

### Income Timing Strategies
- If moving from high-tax to low-tax state:
  - **Defer** bonuses, stock option exercises, large K-1 distributions to post-move
  - **Accelerate** deductions into the high-tax period
- If moving from low-tax to high-tax state:
  - **Accelerate** income into the low-tax period
  - **Defer** deductions to the high-tax period
- Caution: states scrutinize aggressive timing around moves. The income must genuinely be
  earned/recognized in the period claimed.

## Double Taxation Scenarios and Remedies

### Common Double-Tax Situations
1. **Convenience rule + home state**: NY taxes all wages; home state (CT) also taxes as resident.
   CT gives credit for NY tax, but if NY rate > CT rate, the excess is double-taxed.
2. **Statutory resident of two states**: maintain homes in both NY and CT, spend 190 days in each.
   Both treat you as resident. Each gives partial credit for the other, but total tax exceeds
   what either state alone would impose.
3. **Business income sourced to multiple states**: apportionment formulas across states may
   total more than 100% (not mathematically constrained). No federal remedy.
4. **Same income, different character**: one state treats a gain as sourced to their state
   (business situs), while the residence state also claims it. Credit mechanisms are imperfect.

### Remedies
- **Credits**: primary remedy. File for credit in home state for taxes paid to other states.
- **Petition for re-determination**: challenge the sourcing state's classification if incorrect.
- **Supreme Court jurisdiction**: under the Constitution (Art. III, §2), the Supreme Court has
  original jurisdiction over disputes between states. Rarely invoked but available.
- **Competent authority provisions**: some state tax treaties/agreements provide dispute resolution.
- **Planning**: proper domicile establishment, entity structuring, and income timing can
  minimize (but rarely eliminate) multi-state friction.

## Estimated Payment Obligations in Multiple States

- Each state where you earn income may require quarterly estimated payments
- Due dates vary: some states follow federal dates, others differ (e.g., some states have
  different Q4 dates)
- Safe harbor rules differ by state — the 100%/110% prior-year rule is federal; some states
  have 90% current-year only, or different thresholds
- Underpayment penalties accrue per-state, independently
- If you move mid-year: may need to start estimated payments in the new state while potentially
  getting credit for overpayment in the old state

## Optimization Strategies

1. **Establish domicile properly**: if moving to a no-tax state, complete ALL objective indicia
   of domicile change (see checklist above). Incomplete changes are the #1 audit trigger.
2. **Track work days meticulously**: maintain a log of which state you work from each day;
   calendars, VPN logs, badge records, travel receipts. This is your primary defense.
3. **Understand reciprocity**: if a reciprocity agreement exists, ensure employer applies correct
   withholding to avoid filing unnecessary nonresident returns.
4. **Maximize credits**: always claim credit for taxes paid to other states on your resident return.
5. **Consider PTET elections**: pass-through entity tax may help in multi-state situations by
   shifting SALT deduction to entity level, bypassing $10K cap.
6. **Time income recognition**: if moving from high-tax to low-tax state, defer bonuses, stock
   option exercises, or business income to post-move period when possible.
7. **Review employer nexus implications**: remote workers should understand if their presence
   creates tax obligations for their employer in their state.
8. **Consider entity structure**: holding investments in an entity domiciled in a favorable
   state may affect sourcing of certain income types.

## Common Mistakes

1. **Failing to file nonresident returns** — earning income in a state creates a filing
   obligation even if you are not a resident. Many states have aggressive matching programs.
2. **Not claiming credit for taxes paid to other states** — results in double taxation.
3. **Ignoring the convenience of employer rule** — remote workers for NY employers are often
   surprised by NY tax bills totaling tens of thousands of dollars.
4. **Incomplete domicile change** — keeping old state driver's license, voter registration,
   or primary residence undermines the new domicile claim.
5. **Misallocating investment income** — generally sourced to residence, not to the state where
   the brokerage is located or where the issuer is headquartered.
6. **Not adjusting withholding after a move** — W-4 and state withholding forms must be updated
   with employer promptly.
7. **Overlooking local income taxes** — cities like NYC ($3.876%), Philadelphia (3.75%),
   Detroit (2.4%), and many OH cities impose their own income taxes. These are separate from
   state taxes and require separate filings.
8. **Assuming reciprocity covers all income** — reciprocity applies to wages only, not business
   or investment income.
9. **Failing to make estimated payments in each state** — each state has independent estimated
   payment requirements. Missing them triggers state-by-state penalties.
10. **Using the wrong credit computation method** — each state has specific instructions for
    computing the credit for taxes paid to other states. Using the wrong formula over- or
    under-claims the credit.

## State-Specific Notes

- **California**: very aggressive on former residents; maintains residency claim if taxpayer
  has insufficient evidence of domicile change; no specific day-count test (totality of
  circumstances). FTB auditors examine cell phone records, credit card transactions, social
  media check-ins. Safe harbor: 45 days or fewer for nonresidents.
- **New York**: 548-day safe harbor for absence from NY (for domiciliaries who leave —
  must be out of NY for 548+ consecutive days with no more than 90 days in NY during
  that period). Statutory residence trap for those maintaining a NY apartment even if
  domiciled elsewhere. NY is the most litigated state for residency disputes.
- **New Jersey**: exit year return required; may attempt to tax full-year income if move
  is late in year. NJ convenience rule added in 2023 creates new exposure for remote workers.
- **Texas, Florida, Nevada, Wyoming**: no income tax — popular targets for domicile migration.
  TX and FL have no state return to file (simplifies matters). But must fully establish
  domicile — NY/CA will pursue former residents who claim to move but maintain ties.
- **Illinois**: flat 4.95% rate; reciprocity with 4 neighboring states. Relatively
  straightforward multi-state treatment.
- **Massachusetts**: pandemic-era rules taxing NH residents working remotely for MA employers
  were challenged in court; current enforcement posture requires monitoring.
- **Oregon**: no sales tax but high income tax (up to 9.9%); relevant for cross-border
  workers from WA.
- **Colorado**: local income taxes in Denver, Aurora, etc. add complexity for multi-state filers.
