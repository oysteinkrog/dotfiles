# First-Time Filer Reference (Tax Year 2025)

## Overview

This guide is for individuals filing a U.S. federal income tax return for the first time. Whether you are a teenager with a first job, a recent graduate, or an immigrant filing your first U.S. return, this covers the foundational knowledge needed to file correctly: choosing a filing status, understanding withholding, reading tax documents, and claiming credits you are entitled to.

## Filing Requirements for 2025

### Who Must File
You must file a federal return if your gross income exceeds these thresholds:

| Filing Status | Age | Income Threshold |
|--------------|-----|-----------------|
| Single | Under 65 | $15,750 |
| Single | 65 or older | $17,750 |
| MFJ | Both under 65 | $31,500 |
| Head of Household | Under 65 | $23,625 |

### File Even if Below Threshold When:
- Federal income tax was withheld from your pay (file to get refund)
- You qualify for refundable credits (EITC, AOTC refundable portion, Additional CTC)
- You received advance premium tax credits for health insurance (must reconcile on Form 8962)
- You have self-employment income of $400 or more (must pay SE tax regardless of income level)

### Dependents with Income
- If you can be claimed as a dependent by someone else, you must file if:
  - Unearned income > $1,350 (2025)
  - Earned income > $15,750 (2025)
  - Gross income exceeds the larger of $1,350 or earned income + $450 (up to $15,750)
- "Kiddie tax" (Form 8615): unearned income above $2,700 for children under 19 (or under 24 if full-time student) is taxed at parent's rate

## Understanding Marginal vs. Effective Tax Rates

This is the single most common misconception for new filers.

**The myth**: "If I earn $1 more and move into the 22% bracket, ALL my income gets taxed at 22%."
**The reality**: Only the income WITHIN each bracket is taxed at that bracket's rate.

**2025 tax brackets (Single)**:
| Bracket | Taxable Income Range | Tax |
|---------|---------------------|-----|
| 10% | $0 - $11,925 | 10% of amount |
| 12% | $11,926 - $48,475 | $1,192.50 + 12% of excess over $11,925 |
| 22% | $48,476 - $103,350 | $5,578.50 + 22% of excess over $48,475 |
| 24% | $103,351 - $197,300 | $17,651.00 + 24% of excess |
| 32% | $197,301 - $250,525 | $40,199.00 + 32% of excess |
| 35% | $250,526 - $626,350 | $57,231.00 + 35% of excess |
| 37% | $626,351+ | $188,769.75 + 37% of excess |

**Worked example**:
- Gross income: $55,000. Standard deduction: $15,750. Taxable income: $39,250.
- Tax: ($11,925 x 10%) + ($27,325 x 12%) = $1,192.50 + $3,279.00 = **$4,471.50**
- **Marginal rate**: 12% (the rate on the next dollar earned)
- **Effective rate**: $4,471.50 / $55,000 = **8.1%** (actual percentage of total income paid in tax)

Earning more money ALWAYS results in more take-home pay. You will never lose money by moving into a higher bracket.

## Choosing Filing Status

### Single
- Unmarried, divorced, or legally separated on December 31 of the tax year
- Default status for most first-time filers

### Married Filing Jointly (MFJ)
- Married on December 31; combines both spouses' income and deductions
- Usually the most beneficial status for married couples (lower rates, higher thresholds)

### Married Filing Separately (MFS)
- May be beneficial in specific situations (income-driven student loan repayment, one spouse with high medical expenses, liability protection)
- Loses many credits and deductions (EITC, education credits limited, student loan interest deduction, etc.)

### Head of Household (HOH)
- Unmarried (or considered unmarried) + paid more than half the cost of keeping up a home for a qualifying person (child, parent, or other dependent)
- Better rates and higher standard deduction than Single ($23,625 vs. $15,750)
- Common test: you must have lived with the qualifying person for more than half the year (exception: dependent parent does not need to live with you)

### Qualifying Surviving Spouse (QSS)
- Available for 2 years after spouse's death if you have a dependent child
- Uses MFJ rates and standard deduction

## Standard Deduction vs. Itemizing

### Standard Deduction (2025)
| Status | Amount |
|--------|--------|
| Single | $15,750 |
| MFJ | $31,500 |
| HOH | $23,625 |
| Additional (blind or 65+) | $2,000 (single/HOH) / $1,600 (married/QSS/MFS) |

### When to Itemize (Schedule A)
- Total itemized deductions exceed standard deduction
- Common itemized deductions: mortgage interest, property taxes, state/local income taxes (SALT, subject to the current-law personal cap), charitable contributions, medical expenses above 7.5% of AGI
- Most first-time filers take the standard deduction (it is almost always higher for renters without major deductible expenses)

### Special Rule for Dependents
- If you can be claimed as a dependent: your standard deduction is the GREATER of $1,350 or your earned income + $450 (up to $15,750)

## Understanding Withholding and W-4

### How Withholding Works
- Employer withholds estimated federal income tax from each paycheck based on your W-4 elections
- Also withholds: Social Security tax (6.2% on first $176,100), Medicare tax (1.45% on all wages), state income tax (if applicable)
- Withholding is an ESTIMATE -- you reconcile when filing your return (refund if over-withheld, balance due if under-withheld)

### W-4 Optimization Strategy
- **Step 1**: Filing status -- select correctly (Single for most first-time filers)
- **Step 2**: Multiple jobs or spouse works -- use IRS Tax Withholding Estimator (irs.gov/W4App) if applicable
- **Step 3**: Claim dependents (reduces withholding) -- only if YOU are claiming dependents, not if someone else claims you
- **Step 4**: Other adjustments -- additional income not from jobs, deductions beyond standard, extra withholding per period

**Goal**: withholding should approximately equal your actual tax liability. A large refund means you gave the government an interest-free loan. A large balance due means you may owe an underpayment penalty.

**Common mistake**: new filers who start work mid-year often get a large refund because withholding was calculated as if they would earn a full year's salary at that rate, when they actually only worked part of the year.

## How to Read a W-2

| Box | Description |
|-----|-------------|
| Box 1 | Wages, tips, other compensation (federal taxable income) |
| Box 2 | Federal income tax withheld |
| Box 3 | Social Security wages |
| Box 4 | Social Security tax withheld |
| Box 5 | Medicare wages and tips |
| Box 6 | Medicare tax withheld |
| Box 12 | Coded items (DD = employer health insurance cost; W = HSA contributions; D/E = 401k/403b deferrals) |
| Box 14 | Other information (state disability insurance, union dues, etc.) |
| Box 16 | State wages |
| Box 17 | State income tax withheld |

- Box 1 may differ from your gross pay because pre-tax deductions (401k, health insurance, HSA) reduce Box 1
- You should receive your W-2 by January 31 of the following year
- If you don't receive it: contact employer, then call IRS (800-829-1040) after February 15

## Gig Economy Basics for New Graduates

Many first-time filers do gig work (Uber, DoorDash, freelancing, Etsy) without realizing the tax implications.

### Key differences from W-2 employment:
- **No withholding**: platforms do not withhold federal or state tax. YOU are responsible for paying.
- **Self-employment tax**: 15.3% on net self-employment income (12.4% Social Security + 2.9% Medicare) IN ADDITION to income tax. This replaces the employer's share of FICA.
- **Schedule C required**: report income and deductible business expenses
- **Estimated quarterly payments**: if you expect to owe $1,000+ at filing time, make quarterly estimated payments (Form 1040-ES) by April 15, June 15, September 15, January 15.

**Worked example -- gig income surprise**:
- You earned $15,000 from DoorDash. No tax was withheld.
- Income tax (after standard deduction): ~$0 (if your only income, the $15,750 standard deduction covers it)
- SE tax: $15,000 x 92.35% x 15.3% = $2,120 (SE tax applies BEFORE the standard deduction)
- You owe $2,120 even though your income tax is $0. Many first-time gig workers are shocked by this.

## Student Loan Repayment Interaction

### Income-Driven Repayment (IDR) and AGI
- IDR plans (PAYE, IBR, ICR, SAVE) base monthly payments on your AGI
- Lower AGI = lower monthly payments
- Strategies to reduce AGI: Traditional IRA contributions, HSA contributions, pre-tax 401(k) contributions
- **MFS vs MFJ consideration**: some IDR plans use only the borrower's income if MFS. This can reduce payments but costs credits and deductions.

### Public Service Loan Forgiveness (PSLF)
- After 120 qualifying payments while working for government or nonprofit, remaining balance forgiven tax-free
- Track qualifying payments via PSLF Help Tool
- Forgiveness is NOT taxable income (unlike other loan forgiveness programs, which may be taxable after 2025)

## First Job Benefits Enrollment

### 401(k) Match -- Free Money
- If your employer offers a 401(k) match (e.g., "100% match on first 3% of salary"), contribute at least enough to get the FULL match
- A 100% match is a 100% return on your money -- there is no investment that reliably beats this
- **Example**: $50,000 salary, employer matches 100% of first 3%. Contribute $1,500 (3%). Employer adds $1,500. You have $3,000 for the cost of $1,500. Plus your contribution is pre-tax, so it only "costs" you ~$1,200 in take-home pay.

### HSA (Health Savings Account) -- If Available
- Only available with a High Deductible Health Plan (HDHP)
- Triple tax benefit: contributions are tax-deductible, growth is tax-free, withdrawals for medical expenses are tax-free
- 2025 limits: $4,300 (self-only), $8,550 (family)
- Unlike FSA, unused funds roll over indefinitely

### FSA (Flexible Spending Account) -- Use-It-or-Lose-It
- Pre-tax dollars for medical or dependent care expenses
- **Must use by plan deadline** (often March 15 of the following year) or forfeit unused funds
- Only contribute what you are confident you will spend

## Common Credits for First-Time Filers

### Earned Income Tax Credit (EITC)
- Refundable credit for low-to-moderate income workers
- 2025 amounts (approximate): up to $632 (no children), $4,213 (1 child), $6,960 (2 children), $7,830 (3+ children)
- Income limits vary by filing status and number of children (e.g., ~$18,591 single no children; ~$59,899 MFJ 3+ children)
- Must have earned income; investment income must be $11,600 or less
- Age requirement (no children): must be at least 25 and under 65
- Cannot file MFS

### Education Credits
- **American Opportunity Tax Credit (AOTC)**: up to $2,500 per student for first 4 years of college; 40% refundable ($1,000 max refund); need Form 1098-T
- **Lifetime Learning Credit**: up to $2,000 per return; not limited to first 4 years; non-refundable

### Saver's Credit (Retirement Savings Contributions Credit)
- Credit for contributions to retirement accounts (IRA, 401k, 403b)
- 10%-50% of contributions up to $2,000 ($4,000 MFJ)
- AGI limits: $39,500 (single) / $79,000 (MFJ) for any credit; highest rate at lowest income
- Especially valuable for first-time filers with modest income who contribute to a 401(k) or IRA

### Premium Tax Credit
- For health insurance purchased through the Marketplace (healthcare.gov)
- Based on household income relative to Federal Poverty Level
- Reconcile on Form 8962 if you received advance payments

## Free Filing Options

- **IRS Free File**: free federal filing for AGI $84,000 or less (through IRS.gov partner software)
- **IRS Direct File**: free IRS-built filing tool for simple returns (available in expanding number of states)
- **FreeTaxUSA**: $0 for federal filing regardless of income (state filing has a fee)
- **VITA (Volunteer Income Tax Assistance)**: free in-person help for income under $67,000, persons with disabilities, limited English speakers, elderly
- **TCE (Tax Counseling for the Elderly)**: free help for age 60+
- **Free state filing**: varies by state; many offer free filing through state tax agency websites
- **Cash App Taxes**: free federal and state filing (limited complexity)

**Avoid paying for simple returns**: first-time filers with only W-2 income and the standard deduction have the simplest returns. Do not pay $200+ for tax software or preparation when free options exist.

## Identity Protection

### IP PIN (Identity Protection Personal Identification Number)
- 6-digit number that prevents someone else from filing a tax return using your SSN
- Available to all taxpayers (opt-in at IRS.gov/IPPIN)
- New PIN issued annually; must be included on tax return
- Highly recommended if your SSN may have been compromised in a data breach
- Once enrolled, you MUST include the IP PIN on every future return or it will be rejected

### Signs of Tax Identity Theft
- IRS rejects your return because one was already filed with your SSN
- IRS notice about income you didn't earn
- Unexpected tax transcript or IRS letter
- **What to do**: file Form 14039 (Identity Theft Affidavit) and consider requesting an IP PIN

### Protect Your SSN
- Never share your SSN unnecessarily (landlords, employers, and financial institutions have legitimate needs; random online forms do not)
- Monitor your IRS account at IRS.gov (create account to view tax records and detect unauthorized filings)

## Filing Process

### What You Need
1. Social Security Number (SSN) or Individual Taxpayer Identification Number (ITIN)
2. Income documents: W-2, 1099-NEC, 1099-INT, 1099-DIV, 1099-G, 1098-T
3. Prior year AGI (if e-filing; first-time filers enter $0 or "Did Not File")
4. Bank account routing and account number (for direct deposit refund)
5. Health insurance information (Form 1095-A if Marketplace plan)

### Key Deadlines
| Deadline | Event |
|----------|-------|
| January 31 | Employers must provide W-2s; payers must provide 1099s |
| April 15 | Tax return due (or extension request due) |
| October 15 | Extended return due |

### Direct Deposit
- Fastest way to receive refund (typically 21 days or less for e-filed returns)
- Can split refund into up to 3 accounts (Form 8888)
- Can direct refund to savings bond purchase

## Common Mistakes for First-Time Filers

1. **Not filing at all when required** -- even small amounts of income may require a return, especially for dependents with unearned income or self-employment income over $400
2. **Not filing when beneficial** -- leaving refundable credits (EITC, AOTC) unclaimed. Many eligible filers leave hundreds or thousands on the table.
3. **Wrong filing status** -- single parents often qualify for Head of Household (much better rates and higher standard deduction)
4. **Not reporting all income** -- IRS receives copies of all 1099s and W-2s; unreported income triggers automated notices. This includes small amounts from bank interest, gig work, and side jobs.
5. **Forgetting state return** -- most states with income tax require a separate state return
6. **Missing the deadline** -- file on time or request extension (extension extends filing deadline, NOT payment deadline)
7. **Not keeping records** -- save a copy of your return and all supporting documents for at least 3 years (6 years if you underreported income by >25%)
8. **Falling for scams** -- IRS will NEVER call demanding immediate payment, threaten arrest, or ask for gift card payments. Primary contact method is U.S. mail.
9. **Incorrect bank account number for direct deposit** -- double-check; a wrong number can delay or misdirect your refund
10. **Not claiming education credits** -- if you or a parent paid tuition, the AOTC is worth up to $2,500 and $1,000 of it is refundable
11. **Ignoring self-employment tax on gig income** -- SE tax applies even when income tax is zero. Budget 25-30% of gig net income for taxes.
12. **Not contributing to a 401(k) at least up to the employer match** -- this is free money being left on the table

## State-Specific Notes

- **Nine states have no income tax**: AK, FL, NV, NH (interest/dividends only, phasing out), SD, TN (phased out), TX, WA, WY
- **States with flat rates**: CO (4.4%), IL (4.95%), IN (3.05%), KY (4.0%), MA (5.0%), MI (4.25%), NC (4.5%), PA (3.07%), UT (4.65%)
- **Local income taxes**: some cities/counties have their own income tax (NYC, Philadelphia, many OH cities, Detroit, etc.)
- **State returns filed separately** from federal; deadlines usually match federal but some differ (e.g., VA is May 1)
- Many states offer their own free filing tools and low-income credits
- Some states have their own EITC (e.g., CA CalEITC, NY state EITC) that provide additional refundable credits
