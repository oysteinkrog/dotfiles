# Divorce Tax Planning — Comprehensive Reference (TY 2025)

## Overview

Divorce creates a cascade of tax consequences that, if not properly managed, can cost both
parties tens or hundreds of thousands of dollars. The intersection of filing status rules,
§1041 transfers, retirement account division, home sale exclusion, and the pre-2019 vs.
post-2019 alimony divide creates a planning landscape where a single poor decision — like
transferring a low-basis asset without understanding the carried-over basis — can produce a
devastating tax surprise years later. This reference covers the critical tax rules for
taxpayers navigating separation and divorce.

## Filing Status in the Year of Divorce

### The Last-Day-of-Year Rule
Filing status is determined by marital status on **December 31** of the tax year. §7703(a)(1).

| Status on Dec 31 | Available Filing Statuses |
|------------------|--------------------------|
| Legally married (no final divorce decree) | MFJ or MFS |
| Divorced or legally separated under a decree of separate maintenance | Single or HoH (if qualified) |
| Separated but no final decree | MFJ or MFS |

### MFJ in the Year of Separation
If the divorce is not final by December 31, the couple CAN still file MFJ for that year.
This may be advantageous:
- **Lower combined tax** (marriage bonus for unequal incomes)
- Higher standard deduction ($31,800 MFJ vs. $15,900 single for 2025)
- Higher income thresholds for credits and phase-outs

**Risks of MFJ while separating**:
- Joint and several liability — each spouse is liable for the ENTIRE tax on the joint return,
  including the other spouse's income. §6013(d)(3).
- If there is any concern about the other spouse's honesty or hidden income, file MFS.
- Once MFJ is filed, cannot change to MFS after the filing deadline (but MFS can be amended
  to MFJ within 3 years). §6013(b).

### Head of Household — "Considered Unmarried" Rule
A married taxpayer who is still legally married on Dec 31 may file as HoH (not MFS) if ALL:
1. Lived apart from spouse for the last 6 months of the year
2. Maintained a home that was the principal residence of a qualifying child for more than
   half the year
3. Paid more than half the cost of maintaining that home
4. Would be entitled to file MFJ (US citizen/resident)
§7703(b). This is a significant benefit — HoH has a larger standard deduction ($23,850 for 2025)
and wider tax brackets than MFS.

## Section 1041 — Tax-Free Transfers in Divorce

### The Rule
No gain or loss is recognized on transfers of property between spouses, or between former
spouses if the transfer is "incident to the divorce." §1041(a).

### "Incident to Divorce" Means
- Transfer occurs within 1 year after the date the marriage ceases, OR
- Transfer is "related to the cessation of the marriage" — generally, made under a divorce
  or separation instrument and occurring within 6 years after the date the marriage ceases.
  Reg. §1.1041-1T(b), Q&A-7.

### Carryover Basis (The Hidden Tax Bomb)
The receiving spouse takes the **transferor's adjusted basis** in the property. §1041(b)(2).
This is the most critical and most commonly misunderstood rule in divorce taxation.

### Worked Example: The Basis Trap
```
Husband owns stock portfolio:
  Stock A: FMV $200,000, basis $180,000 (little built-in gain)
  Stock B: FMV $200,000, basis $20,000 (massive built-in gain)

Divorce settlement: Wife gets Stock B ($200,000 FMV).
Wife's basis in Stock B: $20,000 (husband's basis carries over)

If Wife sells Stock B immediately:
  Gain: $200,000 - $20,000 = $180,000
  Federal tax (23.8% LTCG + NIIT): $42,840

The $200,000 "equal" division is actually worth only $157,160 after tax to Wife.
Meanwhile, Husband's $200,000 stock has only $20,000 of built-in gain (tax of $4,760).

TRUE AFTER-TAX VALUES:
  Husband: $200,000 - $4,760 = $195,240
  Wife: $200,000 - $42,840 = $157,160
  "Equal" division is actually $38,080 unequal.
```

### Tax-Aware Property Division
- **Always calculate after-tax values** when dividing property.
- Assign high-basis assets to the spouse receiving fewer dollars (or adjust the total split
  to compensate for embedded gains).
- Consider having both spouses receive proportional shares of both high-basis and low-basis
  assets.
- **Cash is always worth face value** — no embedded tax. Offsetting stock with cash avoids
  basis complications.

## Alimony

### Post-2019 Agreements (TCJA Rules)
For divorce or separation agreements executed after December 31, 2018:
- **Payer**: NO deduction for alimony paid.
- **Payee**: Alimony is NOT income.
- Tax cost shifted entirely to the payer.

### Pre-2019 Agreements (Prior Law)
For agreements executed on or before December 31, 2018 (and not modified after that date to
adopt TCJA rules):
- **Payer**: Above-the-line deduction for alimony paid. §215 (repealed for post-2018, but
  preserved for pre-2019 agreements).
- **Payee**: Alimony is included in gross income. §71 (same preservation).

### Requirements for Pre-2019 Alimony Treatment
1. Payment must be in cash (or cash equivalent — check, money order)
2. Payment is received under a divorce or separation instrument
3. Instrument does not designate the payment as "not alimony" (or "not includable/deductible")
4. Spouses are not in the same household at time of payment (if legally separated)
5. No liability to make payments after the payee's death
6. Payment is not treated as child support

### Alimony Recapture — Section 71(f) (Pre-2019 Only)
Prevents front-loading of alimony (disguising property settlements as alimony):
- If alimony payments decrease by more than $15,000 from year 1 to year 2, or year 2 to year 3,
  the excess is "recaptured" — payer must include the excess in income in year 3, and payee
  can deduct it.
- **Year 3 recapture** = (Year 1 alimony - Year 3 alimony - $15,000) + (Year 2 alimony -
  Year 3 alimony - $15,000). Complex formula — use IRS worksheets.
- Exceptions: payments ending due to death or remarriage; fluctuating payments tied to a
  percentage of income from a business, property, or employment.

## Child Support

### Never Deductible, Never Income
- Child support payments are NOT deductible by the payer and NOT income to the payee. §71(c).
- **Front-end alimony trap**: If payments are reduced upon a contingency relating to a child
  (child reaching age 18, graduating, etc.), the reduced amount is treated as child support
  (not alimony) retroactively. §71(c)(2). Structure agreements carefully to avoid this.

## Retirement Account Division

### QDRO — Qualified Domestic Relations Order
For 401(k)s, pensions, 403(b)s, and other employer-sponsored qualified plans:
- A QDRO directs the plan administrator to pay a portion of the participant's benefits to
  the alternate payee (the non-participant spouse).
- **Transfer is NOT a taxable event** under §414(p). No early withdrawal penalty.
- The alternate payee's account is treated as their own. Distributions to the alternate payee
  are taxable to the alternate payee (not the participant).
- **Special rule**: Distributions from a qualified plan to an alternate payee under a QDRO
  are exempt from the 10% early withdrawal penalty at ANY age (not just after 59 1/2).
  §72(t)(2)(C). *This is one of the few exceptions to the early withdrawal penalty.*
- **Caution**: The QDRO must meet specific legal requirements. Obtain a pre-approval from the
  plan administrator before the QDRO is finalized.

### IRA Division — Transfer Incident to Divorce
For IRAs (traditional and Roth):
- No QDRO needed. The IRA is transferred from one spouse to the other pursuant to a divorce
  decree or separation agreement. §408(d)(6).
- **NOT a taxable event**. No early withdrawal penalty.
- The receiving spouse treats the IRA as their own (can name new beneficiaries, make
  contributions, take distributions under normal rules).
- **Critical**: Must be a direct trustee-to-trustee transfer or a re-titling of the account.
  If the participant spouse withdraws funds and gives cash to the other spouse, it IS taxable
  to the participant (and subject to the 10% penalty if under 59 1/2).

### Pension Valuation
- Pensions with future benefits (defined benefit plans) must be valued for equitable
  distribution. Common methods:
  - **Present value method**: Actuarially calculate the present value of future benefits
    and divide. One spouse buys out the other's share.
  - **Deferred distribution method**: Divide the pension payments as they are received
    (typically via QDRO). Preserves the pension risk for both parties.
- **COLA and survivor benefits**: Must be addressed in the QDRO. The alternate payee may
  elect survivor benefits independently.

## Home Sale in Divorce — Section 121

### Basic Exclusion
- Up to $250,000 of gain ($500,000 if MFJ) on the sale of a principal residence is excluded
  if the taxpayer owned and used the home as a principal residence for 2 of the 5 years
  preceding the sale. §121.

### Divorce-Specific Issues

#### One Spouse Moves Out
If one spouse moves out during the separation and the home is not sold for 3+ years, the
departing spouse may LOSE the §121 exclusion (no longer meets the 2-of-5-year use test).

**Special rule**: §121(d)(3)(B) — Use by a former spouse under a divorce decree counts as
use by the owner-spouse. If the divorce decree awards the home to Wife and Husband retains
ownership, Husband is treated as using the home during any period Wife lives there.

#### Timing Strategies
- **Sell before divorce is final**: Both spouses file MFJ → $500,000 combined exclusion.
- **Sell within 2 years of one spouse moving out**: Both spouses still meet the use test.
- **If holding the home for children**: Ensure the divorce decree grants the occupying spouse
  use under §121(d)(3)(B) to preserve the absent spouse's exclusion.

### Worked Example
```
Home purchased in 2015 for $400,000.
Husband moves out January 2023. Wife stays with children.
Divorce final June 2024. Wife awarded occupancy.
Home sold December 2026 for $900,000.

Husband's ownership: 2015-2026 (11 years) ✓
Husband's use: 2015-January 2023 (8 years) but only 1 of 5 years before sale (2022)
  WITHOUT §121(d)(3)(B): Husband fails 2-of-5 use test → no exclusion on his share
  WITH §121(d)(3)(B) in divorce decree: Wife's use counts as Husband's use → exclusion preserved

Gain: $900,000 - $400,000 = $500,000
Each spouse's share: $250,000
Each spouse's exclusion: $250,000
Tax: $0 (if structured correctly)
```

## Dependency Exemption and Child Tax Credit

### Custodial vs. Non-Custodial Parent
- **Custodial parent**: The parent with whom the child lives for more than half the year.
  Default: custodial parent claims the child for CTC, earned income credit, dependent care
  credit, and HoH status.
- **Non-custodial parent**: Can claim the CTC (but NOT EIC, dependent care credit, or HoH)
  if the custodial parent signs **Form 8332** (Release/Revocation of Release of Claim to
  Exemption for Child by Custodial Parent).
- **Pre-2009 divorce decrees**: Some old decrees allocate the "exemption" to the non-custodial
  parent. Post-TCJA, the personal exemption is $0, but the CTC allocation follows the same
  rules. The decree itself does NOT transfer the CTC — Form 8332 is required.

### Head of Household Requirements (Post-Divorce)
Must meet ALL:
1. Unmarried (or "considered unmarried") on December 31
2. Paid more than half the cost of maintaining the home for the year
3. A qualifying person (child, dependent parent) lived in the home for more than half the year
- **Common dispute**: Both parents claim HoH. Only the parent with whom the child lives for
  more than half the year qualifies. If 50/50 custody, the parent with the higher AGI
  gets the tiebreaker. §152(c)(4)(B).

## Innocent Spouse Relief — Section 6015

### When It Applies
If a joint return was filed and the tax is understated due to an erroneous item of the OTHER
spouse, the requesting spouse may be relieved of liability.

### Three Types of Relief

#### 1. Classic Innocent Spouse (§6015(b))
- Understated tax attributable to erroneous items of the other spouse
- Requesting spouse did not know (and had no reason to know) of the understatement
- Inequitable to hold requesting spouse liable

#### 2. Separation of Liability (§6015(c))
- Available to divorced/separated spouses or those living apart for 12+ months
- Allocates the deficiency between the spouses based on their respective items
- Not available if requesting spouse had actual knowledge of the erroneous item

#### 3. Equitable Relief (§6015(f))
- Catch-all for situations that don't meet (b) or (c)
- IRS considers all facts and circumstances, including:
  - Current marital status
  - Whether requesting spouse received significant benefit from the understatement
  - Whether requesting spouse would suffer economic hardship
  - Whether requesting spouse had knowledge or reason to know
  - The other spouse's legal obligation to pay the tax

### Timing
- Must be requested within 2 years of the first IRS collection activity (for separation of
  liability) or within the statute of limitations period (for innocent spouse and equitable relief).
- **File Form 8857** (Request for Innocent Spouse Relief).

## Community Property States

### The 9 Community Property States
Arizona, California, Idaho, Louisiana, Nevada, New Mexico, Texas, Washington, Wisconsin.

### Income Splitting During Marriage
- In community property states, income earned by either spouse during the marriage is
  community property (owned 50/50).
- **Filing MFS in community property states**: Each spouse reports 50% of all community income,
  regardless of who earned it. This can create surprising results — a stay-at-home spouse
  reports 50% of the working spouse's income on their MFS return.
- **Exception**: §66(a) — If spouses live apart for the entire year, each reports only their
  own earned income (not the community split).

### Basis Step-Up at Divorce
- **Community property basis advantage**: In community property states, when one spouse dies,
  BOTH halves of community property receive a stepped-up basis to FMV. §1014(b)(6). This
  does NOT apply in divorce, but it matters for planning during marriage.
- **At divorce**: Community property is divided under §1041 (carryover basis, not step-up).
  No special basis benefit from community property characterization in divorce.

### Quasi-Community Property
- CA recognizes "quasi-community property" — property acquired by either spouse while
  domiciled in another state that would have been community property if acquired in CA.
  Treated as community property for divorce purposes.

## Legal Fees

### Tax Treatment
- **Tax advice portion**: The portion of legal fees attributable to tax advice in a divorce
  may be deductible. *However*: Post-TCJA, miscellaneous itemized deductions (including tax
  preparation and advice fees) are suspended for federal purposes (2018-2025).
- **Some states allow**: States that did not conform to the TCJA suspension of miscellaneous
  itemized deductions (e.g., CA, NY, MN) may still allow the deduction at the state level.
- **Attorney fees for producing income**: Fees to collect alimony (for pre-2019 agreements
  where alimony is taxable) may be deductible above the line under §212. *Limited application post-TCJA.*
- **Property division fees**: Not deductible (personal expense).

## Tax Return Preparation During Divorce

### Practical Considerations
- **Communication**: Both spouses need access to tax records (W-2s, 1099s, K-1s, prior returns).
  Uncooperative spouses can be compelled by court order.
- **Transcript requests**: Either spouse can request IRS transcripts (Form 4506-T) for joint
  returns. Useful if one spouse controls the records.
- **Estimated payments**: After separation, each spouse is responsible for their own estimated
  tax payments. Overpayments on a prior joint return can be allocated between the spouses
  per agreement or per the IRS (generally 50/50 unless the court orders otherwise).
- **Audit risk**: Returns for years during and immediately after divorce have higher audit
  rates due to inconsistencies between the two returns (both claiming same child, conflicting
  basis claims, etc.).

## Common Mistakes

1. **Dividing assets without calculating after-tax values** — A $500K IRA and $500K cash are
   NOT equal. The IRA holder will owe $185K+ in tax on withdrawal.
2. **Missing the QDRO early-withdrawal exception** — Taking a 401(k) distribution outside of
   a QDRO triggers the 10% penalty. The QDRO exception is lost if funds are first rolled to
   an IRA and then withdrawn.
3. **Failing to change withholding/estimated payments** — After separation, a spouse who was
   previously covered by the other's withholding may owe large estimated payments.
4. **Both parents claiming the same child** — IRS tiebreaker rules (AGI, custodial parent)
   apply. Duplicative claims delay both refunds and may trigger audits.
5. **Not preserving §121 exclusion for the departing spouse** — Ensure the divorce decree
   grants use of the home under §121(d)(3)(B).
6. **Cashing out retirement accounts instead of using QDRO/transfer** — Creates taxable income
   and potential penalties.
7. **Confusing pre-2019 and post-2019 alimony rules** — The tax treatment depends on when the
   agreement was executed, not when the payment is made.
8. **Ignoring community property income splitting on MFS returns** — In community property
   states, MFS returns must split community income 50/50 unless the spouses lived apart all year.
9. **Not filing Form 8332 when needed** — The divorce decree alone does not transfer CTC rights
   to the non-custodial parent.
