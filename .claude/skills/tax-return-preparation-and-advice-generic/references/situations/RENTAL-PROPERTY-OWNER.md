# Rental Property Owner Reference (Tax Year 2025)

## Overview

Rental real estate income and expenses are reported on Schedule E (Form 1040). Rental activities are generally treated as passive activities under Section 469, with specific exceptions for real estate professionals and active participants. Proper classification of expenses, depreciation elections, and understanding passive activity rules are critical to maximizing tax benefits.

## Schedule E Mechanics

### Income Reporting
- Report gross rental income: rent, security deposits applied to final month, advance rent,
  cancellation fees, lease-signing bonuses
- Security deposits held as refundable are NOT income until applied or forfeited
- Report each property separately on Schedule E; use additional pages if more than 3 properties
- Net rental income flows to Form 1040 and is NOT subject to self-employment tax (unless
  RE dealer or providing substantial services like a hotel)
- Rental income/loss is computed per-property: income minus expenses minus depreciation

### Expense Reporting
- Each property's expenses listed separately on Schedule E
- Categories: advertising, auto/travel, cleaning/maintenance, commissions, insurance, legal/professional,
  management fees, mortgage interest, other interest, repairs, supplies, taxes, utilities, depreciation
- If property is partly personal-use: expenses must be allocated (see vacation home rules below)

## Repair vs. Improvement Regulations

### BRA Test (Reg. §1.263(a)-3)
The IRS uses three tests to determine if an expenditure must be capitalized as an improvement:
1. **Betterment**: does the expenditure fix a material condition that existed before acquisition,
   fix a material condition resulting from a defect in the original construction, or result in
   a material addition, increase, or expansion?
2. **Restoration**: does the expenditure return a component to its ordinary operating condition
   after it has deteriorated to a state of disrepair, rebuild the property to like-new condition,
   replace a major component or substantial structural part?
3. **Adaptation**: does the expenditure adapt the property to a new or different use?

If YES to any test: capitalize and depreciate. If NO to all: currently deductible as a repair.

### Unit of Property Rules
- The building structure is ONE unit of property; building systems are each separate units
- **Building systems** (each analyzed separately): HVAC, plumbing, electrical, elevators,
  escalators, fire protection/alarm, security, gas distribution
- Replacing an entire building system = improvement. Replacing a component of a system may be repair.
- Example: replacing one broken furnace in a multi-unit building = repair. Replacing the entire
  HVAC system = improvement.

### De Minimis Safe Harbor (Reg. §1.263(a)-1(f))
- Expense items costing **$2,500 or less** per invoice/item (without applicable financial statement)
- **$5,000** per invoice/item with an applicable financial statement (audited)
- Must elect annually on the tax return (statement attached)
- Applies to tangible property, not to improvements to a unit of property
- Example: $2,200 appliance replacement = expense under de minimis safe harbor

### Routine Maintenance Safe Harbor
- Recurring activities expected to be performed more than once during the property's class life
- Must be consistent with industry standards
- Example: exterior painting every 5 years on a 27.5-year residential building = routine maintenance

### Safe Harbor for Small Taxpayers (Reg. §1.263(a)-3(h))
- Businesses with < $10M average annual gross receipts
- Property with unadjusted basis of $1M or less
- Total annual improvements do not exceed the lesser of $10,000 or 2% of unadjusted basis
- If met: can deduct the amounts as repairs/maintenance

## Depreciation

- **Residential rental property**: 27.5-year straight-line (MACRS, mid-month convention)
- **Commercial property**: 39-year straight-line
- **Land is not depreciable** — must allocate purchase price between land and building
  (use tax assessment ratios, appraisals, or reasonable method)
- Depreciation begins when the property is placed in service (available for rent), not when first rented
- Must claim depreciation even if you choose not to — IRS adjusts basis regardless
  ("allowed or allowable" rule per IRC §1016(a)(2))
- Personal property within rentals (appliances, carpeting): 5-year or 7-year MACRS;
  eligible for Section 179 and bonus depreciation

## Cost Segregation Studies

### What It Does
- Engineering-based study that reclassifies building components into shorter-lived asset classes
  (5, 7, 15-year property instead of 27.5 or 39-year)
- Accelerates depreciation deductions significantly in early years

### Economics
- **When to do one**: generally cost-effective for properties with a cost basis of $500,000+
- **Study cost**: $2,500-$5,000 for single residential properties, $5,000-$15,000 for larger
  commercial properties. Some firms offer contingency-based pricing.
- **Typical ROI**: 5:1 to 15:1 (present value of accelerated deductions vs. study cost)
- **Provider selection**: should be performed by engineers or CPAs with engineering support.
  CPA-only studies without engineering basis are more vulnerable to IRS challenge.

### Components Commonly Reclassified
- **5-year property**: appliances, carpeting, specialty lighting, window treatments, decorative
  fixtures, certain cabinetry
- **7-year property**: furniture, office equipment
- **15-year property**: landscaping, parking lots, sidewalks, fencing, site utilities,
  land improvements

### Retroactive Cost Segregation
- Can be done retroactively using a §481(a) adjustment (Form 3115, Change in Accounting Method)
- Catch-up deduction for all prior-year depreciation that would have been claimed under the
  shorter lives, taken as a single adjustment in the year of change
- No need to amend prior returns — entire catch-up in current year
- Particularly powerful when combined with bonus depreciation (if applicable to placed-in-service year)

## Passive Activity Rules — Detailed (IRC §469)

### General Rule
- Rental activities are **per se passive** regardless of participation level (§469(c)(2))
- Passive losses can only offset passive income; excess losses are suspended and carried forward
- Suspended losses are released when the property is disposed of in a **fully taxable transaction**
  (sale to unrelated party; NOT a §1031 exchange, gift, or transfer at death)

### $25,000 Special Allowance (§469(i))
- Active participants (not merely investors) can deduct up to $25,000 of rental losses against
  non-passive income
- **Phase-out**: reduces by $0.50 for each $1 of MAGI above $100,000
- Fully phased out at $150,000 MAGI
- Active participation: lower bar than material participation — making management decisions
  (approving tenants, setting rent terms, authorizing repairs) suffices
- Must own at least 10% of the activity
- Does NOT apply to limited partners
- The $25,000 is a combined limit across ALL rental activities (not per-property)

### Real Estate Professional Status — Detailed (IRC §469(c)(7))

#### Requirements (Must Meet BOTH)
1. More than 50% of personal services performed during the year in real property trades or
   businesses in which the taxpayer materially participates
2. More than 750 hours of participation in real property trades or businesses during the year

#### Critical Details
- **Spousal hours**: each spouse tested SEPARATELY. One spouse's hours do NOT count toward
  the other spouse's qualification. (IRS position per Reg. §1.469-5T(f)(3); some Tax Court
  cases like Padilla v. Commissioner have allowed combining, but this is a litigating position
  only — do not rely on it for planning.)
- **Real property trades or businesses**: includes development, redevelopment, construction,
  reconstruction, acquisition, conversion, rental, operation, management, leasing, brokerage.
- **Material participation in each rental**: even if RE Pro status is met, must STILL materially
  participate in each rental activity (500+ hours, or other material participation test) for
  losses from that activity to be non-passive. Solution: grouping election.

#### Grouping Election (Reg. §1.469-9(g))
- RE Professional can elect to treat ALL rental real estate interests as a **single activity**
- Must file the election on a timely-filed return (including extensions)
- **Election is irrevocable** (can only be revoked with IRS consent, which is rarely granted)
- Once grouped, need only materially participate in the single grouped activity (easier to meet
  750 hours across all properties combined)
- **Trap**: if you group and later one property is passive (e.g., you stop materially participating
  in total), ALL properties become passive. Grouping is a double-edged sword.

#### Documentation
- Keep **contemporaneous time logs** — IRS heavily scrutinizes RE Professional claims
- Log each activity: property management, tenant interaction, maintenance coordination,
  bookkeeping, property searches, continuing education
- Calendar entries, emails, text messages, and receipts corroborate logs
- Burden of proof is on the taxpayer (Treas. Reg. §1.469-5T(f)(4))

## Short-Term Rental Exception (Reg. §1.469-1T(e)(3))

### Average Customer Use Test
- If the average period of customer use is **7 days or less**: NOT treated as a rental activity
- If average period is **7-30 days AND significant personal services provided**: also not rental
- Result: treated as a regular business activity subject to material participation tests

### Tax Implications
- If materially participating: losses are **non-passive** (can offset W-2 income, interest, etc.)
- This is the "short-term rental loophole" used by high-income taxpayers
- Combined with cost segregation and bonus depreciation: can generate large paper losses in
  year 1 that offset high W-2 income
- Subject to SE tax if significant personal services are provided (cleaning, meals, concierge)
- If NOT materially participating: losses are passive (subject to standard $25K/$150K rules)

## Self-Rental Recharacterization Trap (Reg. §1.469-2(f)(6))

- If you rent property to a business in which you **materially participate**, the rental income
  is recharacterized as **non-passive** income
- But rental losses remain **passive** (cannot offset the recharacterized income)
- **The trap**: a physician rents an office building to their medical practice. The rental income
  is non-passive (cannot be sheltered by other passive losses), but if the rental produces a loss
  (e.g., after depreciation), the loss is passive (cannot offset the physician's active practice income)
- **Planning**: set rental rates to avoid generating losses on the self-rental property, OR
  structure to ensure sufficient passive income from other sources

## Vacation Home Rules (IRC §280A — 14-Day / 10% Test)

### Classification
- **Pure rental** (personal use ≤ 14 days or 10% of rental days, whichever is greater):
  full rental deductions apply, but passive loss rules still limit deductibility
- **Mixed use** (personal use > 14 days or 10% of rental days): expenses allocated by
  rental-day ratio; rental losses limited to rental income (no loss beyond rental income)
- **Pure personal** (rented < 15 days total): rental income is TAX-FREE (IRC §280A(g));
  no rental deductions beyond normal itemized deductions. The "Augusta Rule" or "Masters Rule."

### Allocation of Expenses
- Courts apply two different methods:
  - **IRS method** (Bolton rule): allocate based on rental days / total days used
  - **Tax Court method** (§280A(e)): mortgage interest and property taxes allocated by
    rental days / total days in year (365); other expenses by rental days / total days used
- Tax Court method produces larger deductions; IRS method is more conservative
- Which method to use depends on jurisdiction and risk tolerance

## 1031 Like-Kind Exchange — Mechanics (IRC §1031)

- Defer capital gains and depreciation recapture by exchanging into like-kind property
- Only applies to real property held for investment or business use (not personal residences)
- **Timelines are strict and non-negotiable**:
  - 45 days to identify replacement property (up to 3 properties, or 200% rule, or 95% rule)
  - 180 days to close on replacement property
- Must use a Qualified Intermediary (QI); taxpayer cannot touch the funds
- **Boot**: cash or non-like-kind property received is taxable to the extent of gain
- **Mortgage boot**: if replacement property has less debt, the difference is boot
- Reverse exchanges possible but more complex and expensive
- Cannot exchange with related parties if either party disposes within 2 years (§1031(f))
- Partial exchanges are valid — defer proportionally

### Depreciation After 1031
- Replacement property takes over the depreciation schedule of the relinquished property
  (for the exchanged portion)
- Any boot paid (new money invested) starts a NEW depreciation schedule
- Cost segregation study on the replacement property can accelerate depreciation on the new basis

## Tenant Improvement Allowances

- Landlord pays for improvements to tenant's space: generally capitalized by the landlord
  and depreciated over the building's life (or QIP 15-year life if qualified improvement property)
- **Tenant improvement allowance (TIA)**: cash payment from landlord to tenant for improvements.
  Landlord deducts the payment as a leasing cost amortized over the lease term.
  Tenant capitalizes improvements and depreciates over their own applicable period.
- Under §110: qualified lessee construction allowances are excluded from the tenant's income
  if used for qualified long-lived property improvements

## Optimization Strategies

1. **Maximize current-year deductions**: use cost segregation, de minimis safe harbor, and
   repair regulations to front-load deductions
2. **Group activities**: elect to group rental activities for material participation purposes
   (especially RE Professionals). Remember: grouping is irrevocable.
3. **Offset passive income**: if you have passive income from K-1s or other rentals, use
   suspended passive losses from prior years
4. **Convert to RE Professional**: if feasible, restructure work to qualify (especially useful
   for one-income households where one spouse manages rentals full-time)
5. **Short-term rental strategy**: high-income professionals can generate non-passive losses
   through STR with material participation and cost segregation
6. **Plan 1031 exchanges well in advance**: identify QI, line up replacement properties,
   understand debt replacement requirements
7. **Track improvements meticulously**: every dollar of capital improvement increases basis
   and reduces gain on eventual sale
8. **Watch the self-rental trap**: if renting to your own business, structure rent to avoid
   generating passive losses that cannot be used

## Common Mistakes

1. **Not depreciating the property** — IRS adjusts basis downward regardless; you lose the deduction without the benefit
2. **Deducting improvements as repairs** — IRS scrutinizes large "repair" deductions. Apply BRA test rigorously.
3. **Ignoring passive loss limitation rules** — claiming losses against active income without RE Pro status or $25K active participation exception
4. **Poor basis allocation between land and building** — using arbitrary splits instead of supportable methods (tax assessor ratios, appraisals)
5. **Missing 1031 exchange deadlines** — the 45-day and 180-day windows are absolute; no extensions (even for weekends/holidays, per §1031(a)(3))
6. **Not tracking rental days vs. personal days** — critical for mixed-use property expense allocation under §280A
7. **Failing to recapture depreciation on sale** — Section 1250 unrecaptured gain taxed at max 25% rate; this applies even if you never claimed depreciation (allowed or allowable rule)
8. **RE Professional grouping without understanding irrevocability** — once grouped, cannot ungroup without IRS consent
9. **Spousal hours confusion** — one spouse's RE hours do NOT count for the other spouse's 750-hour test
10. **Self-rental recharacterization** — income recharacterized as non-passive, but losses remain passive. Worst of both worlds.
11. **Not filing de minimis safe harbor election** — must be elected annually on the return. Failure to elect means capitalization rules apply to all items.

## Worked Example: Rental Property with Passive Loss

### Facts
- Purchased rental condo for $350,000 (land: $70K, building: $280K) on Jan 1
- Rental income: $24,000/year ($2,000/month)
- Owner's AGI (W-2 job): $125,000 (within $25K allowance phase-out range)
- Active participant (screens tenants, manages repairs)

### Annual Tax Computation
```
Gross rental income:                          $24,000

Expenses:
  Mortgage interest:                         ($14,000)
  Property taxes:                             ($4,200)
  Insurance:                                  ($1,800)
  Repairs/maintenance:                        ($2,400)
  Property management software:                 ($300)
  Depreciation ($280K / 27.5 years):         ($10,182)
Total expenses:                              ($32,882)

Net rental loss:                              ($8,882)
```

### Passive Loss Analysis
- AGI: $125,000
- $25K allowance phase-out: ($125K - $100K) x $0.50 = $12,500 reduction
- Available allowance: $25,000 - $12,500 = $12,500
- Rental loss of $8,882 is LESS than $12,500 allowance
- **Full $8,882 loss is deductible against W-2 income**
- Tax savings at 22% bracket: $8,882 x 22% = ~$1,954

### If AGI Were $155,000 (Above $150K Threshold)
- $25K allowance fully phased out
- $8,882 loss is SUSPENDED — carried forward until property is sold or passive income offsets it
- Tax savings: $0 in current year

## State-Specific Notes

- **California**: does not conform to §1031 exchanges for state purposes (must recognize gain on CA return); $800 minimum LLC tax if holding rental in LLC; Prop 13 limits reassessment; CA has its own passive loss rules that generally follow federal
- **New York**: rental income sourced to NY if property is located there; non-residents file NY return; NYC RPTT (Real Property Transfer Tax) on sales; NYC has additional transfer taxes
- **Florida, Texas, Nevada, Wyoming**: no state income tax on rental income
- **Hawaii**: GET (General Excise Tax) of 4%-4.5% on gross rental income (in addition to income tax); unique burden on landlords
- **New Jersey**: different passive loss rules; short-term rental registration requirements; state does not conform to federal bonus depreciation
- **Colorado**: local short-term rental regulations (licensing, lodging taxes) vary by municipality
- Some states have landlord registration, rent control, or local business license requirements
  that affect net income
- State passive activity rules may differ from federal
- State depreciation conformity varies — some states (CA, NY, NJ) decouple from federal bonus depreciation, requiring separate depreciation schedules for state purposes
