# Tax Planning by Income Tier (Tax Year 2025)

## Overview

Tax strategy is not one-size-fits-all. The optimal approach depends heavily on income level, because the tax code activates and deactivates provisions at specific thresholds. A strategy that saves thousands at $300K income may be irrelevant at $80K or overly simplistic at $2M. This guide organizes every major planning strategy by the income tier where it first becomes relevant and most impactful, with priority ordering, estimated savings, and practical implementation notes.

All figures assume Married Filing Jointly (MFJ) unless noted. Adjust thresholds for single filers (generally half the MFJ amount for most provisions).

---

## Tier 1: $50,000-$100,000 AGI -- Optimize the Basics

**Philosophy**: At this income level, the tax code is actually working in your favor. Multiple credits and deductions are fully available. The goal is to claim everything you are entitled to and avoid overpaying through ignorance.

**Estimated total tax savings vs doing nothing: $3,000-$12,000/year**

**Professional help level**: DIY with quality software (TurboTax, FreeTaxUSA) or seasonal CPA ($300-$800)

### Priority-Ordered Strategies

#### 1. Maximize Employer 401(k) Match (Savings: $1,000-$5,000)

Every dollar of employer match is a 50-100% instant return. If your employer matches 50% up to 6% of salary, contribute at least 6%.

At $75K salary with 50% match on 6%: contribute $4,500, employer adds $2,250. The tax deduction on your $4,500 saves another $990 (22% bracket). Total first-year benefit: $3,240.

Do not leave match money on the table. This is the single highest-ROI financial action available at any income level.

#### 2. Roth IRA -- Direct Contribution (Savings: $0 now, $100K-$500K+ lifetime)

At this income level, you qualify for direct Roth IRA contributions ($7,000 per person, $8,000 if 50+). Roth grows tax-free and withdrawals are tax-free in retirement.

Why Roth over traditional at this income: you are likely in the 12% or 22% bracket now. If your retirement income (Social Security + RMDs + other) puts you in a similar or higher bracket, Roth wins. Paying 12-22% tax now to avoid 22-32% tax later is an excellent trade.

- Forms: None additional for contributions (report on 5498 by custodian)
- Deadline: April 15 of following year (April 15, 2026 for 2025 contributions)

#### 3. Standard Deduction Optimization (Savings: $0-$3,000)

The standard deduction for 2025 is $31,500 (MFJ). At this income level, most taxpayers should take the standard deduction unless they have unusually high mortgage interest, medical expenses, or charitable contributions.

Quick test: add up SALT (generally capped at $40,000 for TY2025, subject to any income-based limitation) + mortgage interest + charitable contributions. If total exceeds $31,500, itemize. Otherwise, take the standard deduction.

If you are close to the threshold, consider bunching charitable contributions into alternate years using a Donor-Advised Fund (see Tier 3).

#### 4. Saver's Credit (Savings: $200-$2,000)

Often overlooked. The Credit for Qualified Retirement Savings Contributions provides a credit of 10-50% on up to $2,000 of retirement contributions per person ($4,000 MFJ).

| MFJ AGI | Credit Rate | Max Credit (MFJ) |
|---------|-------------|-------------------|
| Up to $47,500 | 50% | $2,000 |
| $47,501-$51,000 | 20% | $800 |
| $51,001-$76,500 | 10% | $400 |
| Above $76,500 | 0% | $0 |

This is a credit, not a deduction -- it directly reduces tax dollar-for-dollar. A couple earning $47K contributing $4,000 total to retirement accounts gets a $2,000 credit PLUS the tax deduction on the contributions. Report on Form 8880.

#### 5. Earned Income Tax Credit (Savings: $0-$7,830)

Available for lower incomes within this tier. Maximum credit for 2025:

| Children | Max Credit | Phase-out Complete (MFJ) |
|----------|-----------|--------------------------|
| 0 | $649 | ~$24,313 |
| 1 | $4,328 | ~$56,004 |
| 2 | $7,152 | ~$62,688 |
| 3+ | $8,046 | ~$66,819 |

Investment income must be $11,600 or less. Report on Schedule EIC.

#### 6. Education Credits (Savings: $0-$2,500)

**American Opportunity Tax Credit (AOTC)**: Up to $2,500/year per student for first 4 years of college. 40% ($1,000) is refundable. Phase-out: $160K-$180K MFJ.

**Lifetime Learning Credit**: Up to $2,000/year per return (not per student). For any post-secondary education, including graduate school and professional development courses. Phase-out: $160K-$180K MFJ. Non-refundable.

- Forms: 8863
- Need: Form 1098-T from institution

#### 7. HSA if on High-Deductible Health Plan (Savings: $1,500-$3,500)

The Health Savings Account is the only triple-tax-advantaged account in the tax code: tax-deductible contribution, tax-free growth, tax-free withdrawal for medical expenses.

2025 limits: $4,300 individual / $8,550 family + $1,000 catch-up if 55+.

**Advanced HSA strategy even at this income**: contribute the maximum, pay medical expenses out of pocket (if you can afford to), let the HSA invest and grow for decades. Withdraw tax-free in retirement for any medical expense incurred after the HSA was established (keep receipts -- no time limit on reimbursement).

- Forms: 8889
- Deadline: April 15 of following year

#### 8. Side Hustle Tax Optimization (Savings: $1,000-$5,000)

If you have self-employment income (1099-NEC, gig work, freelance), keep it on Schedule C until net SE income exceeds $50K-$60K, at which point S-Corp election should be evaluated (see Tier 2).

At this tier, key Schedule C strategies:
- Deduct home office (simplified: $5/sq ft up to 300 sq ft = $1,500)
- Deduct vehicle expenses (standard mileage $0.70/mile for 2025 or actual expenses)
- Deduct health insurance premiums (above-the-line if not eligible for employer plan)
- Deduct 50% of self-employment tax (above-the-line)
- Consider SEP-IRA or Solo 401(k) for retirement contributions (up to 20% of net SE earnings for SEP)

### Key Deadlines for Tier 1
- January 15: Q4 estimated tax payment (if self-employed)
- April 15: Tax return due; IRA/Roth IRA contribution deadline; Q1 estimated payment
- October 15: Extended return deadline

### Common Mistakes at Tier 1
- Missing the Saver's Credit entirely (hugely valuable, rarely claimed)
- Not contributing enough to get full employer 401(k) match
- Filing without checking education credit eligibility
- Paying for tax preparation software when income qualifies for IRS Free File
- Missing EITC due to incorrect filing status or not filing at all
- Choosing traditional IRA over Roth when in the 12% bracket (Roth is almost certainly better)

### Typical Forms Required
1040, W-2, Schedule C (if self-employed), Schedule SE, 8880 (Saver's Credit), 8863 (education credits), 8889 (HSA), Schedule EIC (if EITC)

## Tier 2: $100,000-$250,000 AGI -- Unlock Intermediate Strategies

**Philosophy**: Credits begin phasing out and some deductions disappear. The standard deduction vs itemize decision becomes closer. Retirement plan maximization becomes the primary tax reduction lever. Side hustles with meaningful income should evaluate S-Corp election.

**Estimated total tax savings vs doing nothing: $8,000-$35,000/year**

**Professional help level**: CPA ($500-$2,000) recommended, especially if self-employed or multiple income sources

### Priority-Ordered Strategies

#### 1. Maximize 401(k) Contributions -- $23,500 (Savings: $5,200-$8,700)

At the 22-24% bracket, each dollar deferred saves $0.22-$0.24 in current tax.

Full contribution: $23,500 x 24% = $5,640 tax savings. Add employer match savings on top.

Catch-up if 50+: additional $7,500 ($31,000 total). Super catch-up if 60-63: $11,250 additional ($34,750 total).

#### 2. Backdoor Roth IRA -- $7,000 per Person (Savings: $0 now, $200K-$1M lifetime)

Above $236K MFJ, direct Roth contributions are phased out. Use the backdoor:
1. Contribute $7,000 to non-deductible traditional IRA
2. Convert to Roth IRA (same day or next business day)
3. File Form 8606

**Critical**: Pro-rata rule applies. If you have ANY pre-tax IRA balances (traditional, SEP, SIMPLE), the conversion is partially taxable. Solution: roll pre-tax IRA money into employer 401(k) before year-end (reverse rollover).

Cost: $0 (you are converting non-deductible contributions). Lifetime benefit: tax-free growth on $14,000/year ($7K per spouse) for decades.

#### 3. Itemize vs Standard Deduction Analysis (Savings: $0-$5,000)

The $31,500 standard deduction (MFJ) is the benchmark. At this income level, homeowners in higher-tax states often have:
- SALT: up to $40,000 under current TY2025 law, subject to any income-based limitation
- Mortgage interest: $12,000-$20,000
- Charitable: $3,000-$10,000
- Total: $25,000-$70,000+

If total itemized deductions exceed $30K, itemize. If close, consider bunching strategies (see Tier 3 section on charitable bunching).

#### 4. Home Office Deduction -- If Working From Home (Savings: $1,500-$5,000)

Available ONLY for self-employed individuals (not W-2 employees under TCJA 2018-2025).

- Simplified method: $5/sq ft, max 300 sq ft = $1,500
- Regular method (Form 8829): actual expenses proportional to office square footage / total home square footage. Includes mortgage interest/rent, utilities, insurance, repairs, depreciation. Can exceed $1,500 significantly.
- Must meet "regular and exclusive use" test: dedicated space used only for business

#### 5. HSA + Investment Strategy (Savings: $2,000-$4,000)

If on HDHP, max out HSA ($8,550 family + $1,000 catch-up if 55+).

At this income level, invest HSA funds rather than spending them on current medical costs. Many HSA custodians offer index fund options. Pay medical expenses from checking account, keep HSA receipts, let HSA grow. At 7% return, $8,550/year for 20 years = ~$370K tax-free.

Tax savings: $8,550 x 24% = $2,052 current year deduction + tax-free growth + tax-free withdrawal = triple benefit.

#### 6. 529 Contributions -- State Tax Deduction (Savings: $500-$2,500)

No federal deduction, but 30+ states offer a state income tax deduction or credit:
- New York: up to $10,000 deduction (MFJ) = ~$680 savings
- California: no state deduction
- Illinois: $20,000 deduction (MFJ) = ~$990 savings
- Virginia: $4,000 per account deduction = varies

529 grows tax-free; withdrawals for qualified education expenses are tax-free. Can now transfer up to $35,000 lifetime to beneficiary's Roth IRA (SECURE 2.0, starting 2024, subject to annual Roth contribution limits, account must be 15+ years old).

#### 7. Dependent Care FSA -- $5,000 (Savings: $1,100-$1,850)

Pre-tax contribution up to $5,000/year for dependent care expenses (daycare, before/after school care, summer day camp for children under 13).

Tax savings: $5,000 x 22-37% marginal rate = $1,100-$1,850. Use-it-or-lose-it: must incur expenses within the plan year.

Compare with Child and Dependent Care Credit (Form 2441): for AGI above $43,000, the credit rate is 20% on up to $3,000/$6,000 = max $600/$1,200 credit. The FSA is almost always better at this income level.

#### 8. S-Corp Election for Self-Employment Income (Savings: $5,000-$20,000)

When net self-employment income exceeds $50,000-$60,000, S-Corp election can save significant self-employment tax.

**How it works**: SE tax is 15.3% on all net SE earnings (up to SS wage base) plus 2.9% above. As an S-Corp, you pay yourself a "reasonable salary" (subject to FICA) and take the remaining profit as distributions (not subject to FICA).

**Example**: $120K net SE income
- As sole proprietor: SE tax on $120K = ~$16,956 (before 50% deduction)
- As S-Corp with $60K reasonable salary: FICA on $60K = ~$9,180. Distribution of $60K = no FICA.
- Savings: ~$7,776/year

**Costs**: S-Corp requires separate tax return (Form 1120-S, ~$1,000-$2,500 CPA fee), payroll processing ($500-$2,000/year), state filing fees. Net benefit must exceed these costs.

- Must file Form 2553 for S-Corp election (due March 15 for current year, or within 75 days of formation)
- IRS scrutinizes "reasonable compensation" -- must be comparable to what you would earn as an employee doing similar work

### Key Deadlines for Tier 2
- March 15: S-Corp return (1120-S) due; Form 2553 election deadline for current year
- April 15: Individual return; IRA contributions; Q1 estimated payment
- Quarterly: estimated tax payments if self-employed

### Common Mistakes at Tier 2
- Not doing backdoor Roth due to perceived complexity (it takes 15 minutes)
- Forgetting pro-rata rule on backdoor Roth (pre-tax IRA balances sabotage the conversion)
- Paying SE tax on all self-employment income when S-Corp would save thousands
- Not maximizing HSA (it is a better retirement vehicle than most people realize)
- Taking education credits when income is above phase-out (wasted time, potential audit flag)
- Under-withholding: two-earner couples frequently owe because W-4 does not coordinate between employers

### Typical Forms Required
All Tier 1 forms + Schedule A (if itemizing), 8606 (backdoor Roth), Schedule E (if rental/K-1), 1120-S (if S-Corp), W-2 from S-Corp, 8829 (home office), quarterly 1040-ES

## Tier 3: $250,000-$500,000 AGI -- Active Optimization Required

**Philosophy**: New surtaxes activate (NIIT at $250K, Additional Medicare Tax at $250K). Most credits are fully phased out. QBI deduction phase-out zone for SSTBs. Tax planning shifts from "claim what you are owed" to "actively restructure income and deductions." Without planning, marginal rates (including surtaxes) can reach 40.8%.

**Estimated total tax savings vs doing nothing: $30,000-$80,000/year**

**Professional help level**: CPA with tax planning expertise ($2,000-$5,000), possibly EA or tax attorney for entity structuring

### Priority-Ordered Strategies

#### 1. S-Corp + Solo 401(k) Stacking (Savings: $15,000-$40,000)

The most powerful Tier 3 combination for self-employed taxpayers. S-Corp eliminates FICA on distributions. Solo 401(k) defers income from the highest bracket.

**Example**: $300K net business income via S-Corp
- Reasonable salary: $120K. FICA: ~$16,380
- Employer 401(k) contribution: 25% of $120K = $30,000
- Employee 401(k) deferral: $23,500 (or $31,000 if 50+)
- Total deferred: $53,500. Tax savings at 35%: $18,725
- FICA savings vs sole prop: ~$12,000
- **Combined savings: ~$30,700**

Total with employer plan limit: up to $70,000/year tax-deferred ($77,500 if 50+, $81,250 if 60-63).

#### 2. PTET Election (Savings: $3,000-$15,000)

If you operate through a pass-through entity (S-Corp, LLC, partnership) in a PTET state, elect the entity-level tax. The entity pays state income tax (deductible at the federal level without SALT cap limitation), and you receive a state credit on your personal return.

**Must be done proactively** -- elections have strict deadlines that vary by state. See HIGH-INCOME.md for state-specific deadlines and savings estimates.

Savings = (state tax paid by entity above what the personal SALT cap would otherwise allow) x (federal marginal rate).

#### 3. SALT Cap Management (Savings: built into PTET above)

Without PTET, the personal SALT cap still causes many high-tax-state filers to lose deductions for large amounts of state and local tax.

**Strategies beyond PTET**:
- Property taxes: if total SALT remains below the applicable cap, consider prepaying (limited benefit)
- If considering state migration, quantify annual savings (see HIGH-INCOME.md state migration table)
- For W-2 employees without pass-through entities: no good workaround exists

#### 4. Mega Backdoor Roth (Savings: $0 now, $500K-$2M+ lifetime)

If your employer plan allows after-tax contributions and in-service withdrawals/conversions, contribute after-tax dollars beyond the $23,500 employee limit, up to the $70,000 total annual additions limit (minus employee deferrals and employer contributions).

Potential: $30,000-$46,000/year in additional Roth contributions. Over 20 years at 7%: $1.2M-$1.9M in tax-free money.

Not available in all plans. Check the Summary Plan Description or ask HR.

#### 5. QBI Deduction Optimization (Savings: $5,000-$20,000)

At this income level, SSTB taxpayers are in or entering the phase-out zone ($394,600-$494,600 MFJ).

**If SSTB**: every dollar of taxable income reduction (retirement contributions, charitable bunching, PTET) extends QBI eligibility. At $400K taxable income, you lose about 11% of the QBI deduction versus full availability at $394,600. Strategic deductions to stay below $394,600 can save the entire 20% QBI deduction.

**If non-SSTB**: above $494,600, the deduction is limited to the greater of 50% W-2 wages or 25% W-2 + 2.5% UBIA. Ensure the business pays adequate W-2 compensation and owns qualified property.

**Entity separation**: if you run both SSTB and non-SSTB activities, separate them into distinct entities. The non-SSTB entity retains full QBI eligibility even at high income.

#### 6. Capital Gain/Loss Harvesting (Savings: $2,000-$15,000)

Systematically harvest losses to offset gains, and harvest gains at favorable rates.

- Harvest losses in taxable accounts to offset realized gains
- After harvesting, reinvest in similar (not "substantially identical") investments to maintain market exposure
- Wash sale rule: cannot repurchase substantially identical securities within 30 days before or after the sale
- Net capital losses: up to $3,000/year deductible against ordinary income; excess carries forward indefinitely
- When in a low-income year: harvest gains at 0% or 15% LTCG rate to reset cost basis higher

#### 7. First Rental Property / Build RE Pro Hours (Savings: $5,000-$25,000 in year 1)

At this income, the $25,000 passive rental loss allowance is fully phased out (gone above $150K AGI). Rental losses are suspended unless:
- You or your spouse qualifies as a Real Estate Professional (750+ hours, more than half of services)
- The rental qualifies as a short-term rental (average stay 7 days or less) and you materially participate

Begin tracking real estate hours now if a spouse can dedicate significant time. The 750-hour threshold equals about 15 hours/week.

**Cost segregation**: on a $500K rental property, a cost segregation study ($5,000-$10,000) can accelerate $100K-$150K of depreciation into year 1, creating a large paper loss. Without RE Pro status, this loss is suspended. With RE Pro status, it offsets W-2 and other active income.

#### 8. DAF for Charitable Bunching (Savings: $3,000-$10,000)

At $400K AGI in a lower-tax state, normally giving $12K/year to charity: total itemized deductions might be $32K (barely above the $31.5K standard deduction). Bunch 3 years into one: $36K to DAF + $8K SALT + $12K mortgage = $56K itemized. Take the standard deduction in off years.

Net extra deductions over 3 years: about $23,000. At 32% marginal rate = about $7,400 savings.

Contribute appreciated stock to DAF to avoid capital gains AND get FMV deduction.

#### 9. Estimated Payment Optimization (Savings: avoid $500-$5,000 in penalties)

Above $150K AGI, the estimated tax safe harbor is 110% of prior year tax (not 100%).

If income is variable (large Q4 bonus, seasonal business): use the annualized income installment method (Form 2210 Schedule AI) to avoid penalties for underpaying early quarters.

W-4 trick: increase withholding via W-4 in Q4 -- wage withholding is treated as paid evenly throughout the year, retroactively covering Q1-Q3 shortfalls.

#### 10. NIIT and Additional Medicare Tax Planning (Savings: built into other strategies)

First exposure at $250K MFJ:
- NIIT: 3.8% on the lesser of net investment income or MAGI exceeding $250K
- Additional Medicare Tax: 0.9% on wages + SE income exceeding $250K

Strategies to reduce:
- Maximize pre-tax retirement contributions (reduces MAGI)
- Municipal bonds (interest excluded from NII)
- Material participation in business activities (income exempt from NIIT)
- Roth conversions increase MAGI -- factor NIIT cost into conversion analysis

### Key Deadlines for Tier 3
All Tier 2 deadlines + PTET election deadlines (vary by state), year-end planning meeting with CPA (November)

### Common Mistakes at Tier 3
- Not electing S-Corp when SE income exceeds $50K (thousands in wasted FICA)
- Missing PTET election deadline (no retroactive elections in most states)
- Ignoring QBI phase-out -- a $5K charitable contribution could save $15K in QBI deduction
- Not starting RE Pro hour tracking (spouse needs contemporaneous log -- reconstructed logs are challenged by IRS)
- Steady charitable giving instead of bunching (leaving $5K-$10K on the table every cycle)
- Underestimating NIIT: 3.8% on ALL investment income above threshold adds up fast

### Typical Forms Required
All Tier 2 forms + 8960 (NIIT), 8959 (Additional Medicare Tax), 4562 (depreciation/cost segregation), state PTET forms, 8283 (non-cash charitable contributions over $500)

## Tier 4: $500,000-$1,000,000 AGI -- Structural Strategies

**Philosophy**: At this level, single-strategy optimization is insufficient. You need structural changes: new entities, new plan types, professional-grade charitable vehicles, and real estate strategies. The gap between optimized and unoptimized can exceed $150K/year. Tax planning is not an annual event -- it is an ongoing discipline.

**Estimated total tax savings vs doing nothing: $80,000-$200,000/year**

**Professional help level**: Tax CPA with planning specialization ($5,000-$15,000), possibly tax attorney for entity and estate structuring

### Priority-Ordered Strategies

#### 1. Defined Benefit / Cash Balance Plan (Savings: $40,000-$100,000)

The single most powerful tax deferral tool for high-income self-employed individuals and business owners over age 40.

| Owner Age | Approximate Annual Contribution | Tax Savings at 37% |
|-----------|--------------------------------|---------------------|
| 40 | $100,000-$140,000 | $37,000-$51,800 |
| 50 | $160,000-$220,000 | $59,200-$81,400 |
| 55 | $200,000-$270,000 | $74,000-$99,900 |
| 60 | $250,000-$330,000 | $92,500-$122,100 |

Stack with 401(k): $23,500 employee + employer match + $200K+ Cash Balance = $250K+ total tax-deferred.

Requirements: actuarial design and annual certification ($2,000-$5,000/year), consistent funding for 3-5+ years, works best with stable high income.

- Forms: 5500 series (annual plan filing)
- Setup deadline: December 31 of the plan year
- Contribution deadline: filing deadline including extensions

#### 2. Cost Segregation on Rental Property (Savings: $20,000-$80,000 in year 1)

A cost segregation study reclassifies components of a building from 27.5/39-year property to 5, 7, or 15-year property, accelerating depreciation massively.

On a $1M rental property: a cost seg study might reclassify $250K-$350K to shorter-lived property. With 60% bonus depreciation (2025), that generates $150K-$210K of accelerated depreciation in year 1.

**But**: this depreciation is only deductible against non-passive income if you are a Real Estate Professional or it is a short-term rental with material participation. Otherwise it creates suspended passive losses.

Cost of study: $5,000-$15,000. ROI is typically 10-20x in year 1 tax savings.

#### 3. Real Estate Professional Status -- Spouse Strategy (Savings: $15,000-$60,000)

One spouse dedicates 750+ hours/year to real property trades or businesses (acquisition, development, management, leasing, brokerage). This spouse must spend more than half of their personal services in real property activities.

Once qualified:
- All rental activities can be treated as non-passive (must elect to aggregate)
- Rental losses offset W-2 income, S-Corp income, and other active income
- Rental income is exempt from NIIT (3.8% savings)
- Cost segregation losses become immediately deductible

**Documentation**: maintain a contemporaneous time log. The IRS challenges RE Pro status frequently. Log should include date, hours, activity description, and property. A calendar, app, or spreadsheet maintained throughout the year is far more defensible than a reconstructed log at tax time.

#### 4. Short-Term Rental Loophole (Savings: $10,000-$50,000)

Rentals with average customer stay of 7 days or fewer are classified as a trade or business (not a rental activity) under Reg 1.469-1T(e)(3)(ii)(A). If the taxpayer materially participates (500+ hours/year in the STR business), losses are non-passive and deductible against all income.

This allows high-income W-2 earners to deduct large cost segregation losses from Airbnb-style properties against their salary WITHOUT qualifying as a Real Estate Professional.

Combine with cost segregation: $800K vacation rental property, cost seg produces $150K year-1 depreciation, materially participate for 500+ hours, deduct against $800K W-2 income. Tax savings at 37% + 3.8% NIIT: ~$61,200.

#### 5. Opportunity Zone Investment (Savings: $10,000-$40,000 deferral + permanent exclusion)

Invest capital gains in a Qualified Opportunity Fund (QOF) within 180 days of realization:
- Defer recognition of the original gain until 2026 (or earlier disposition)
- If held 10+ years: NO tax on appreciation of the OZ investment itself

At $500K of capital gains, investing in QOF defers ~$119K in tax (23.8% rate). If the OZ investment doubles in 10 years, the $500K of appreciation is tax-free permanently.

**Caution**: the investment must be in a legitimate opportunity zone business or real estate. Quality of the underlying investment matters more than the tax benefit. A bad investment with good tax treatment is still a bad investment.

- Forms: 8997 (annual), 8949 (election year)
- Deadline: 180 days from gain recognition

#### 6. QSBS Planning -- Section 1202 (Savings: potentially $0 to $10M+)

For startup founders and early-stage investors. Stock in a qualified small business (C-Corp, assets under $50M at issuance, held 5+ years) may qualify for up to 100% exclusion of gain on sale, up to the greater of $10M or 10x basis.

At $5M gain: 100% exclusion = $1.19M tax savings (23.8% rate).

**Planning**: ensure the company is structured as a C-Corp from inception (not LLC converted later -- the holding period restarts). Issue stock early when value is low to minimize gift tax if transferring to family trusts. If the 5-year holding period is not yet met, consider installment sale to defer gain until qualification.

**Stacking with trusts**: each taxpayer gets a separate $10M exclusion. Gift QSBS to family members, trusts, and entities to multiply the exclusion. Gift early when basis is low.

#### 7. Captive Insurance (Savings: $30,000-$100,000 if legitimate)

A business with genuine insurable risks can form a captive insurance company. The operating business pays premiums (deductible) to the captive. The captive may elect under §831(b) to be taxed only on investment income (not premiums) if net written premiums are under $2.8M.

**Extreme IRS scrutiny**: captive insurance arrangements are on the IRS "Dirty Dozen" list. Only viable with genuine, arm's-length risks, actuarially determined premiums, and legitimate insurance operations. Do not pursue as a pure tax play.

#### 8. Augusta Rule -- Section 280A(g) (Savings: $2,000-$5,000)

Rent your personal home to your business for up to 14 days per year. Rental income is excluded from gross income, and the business deducts the rent as a business expense.

Must charge fair market value (get comparable rental rates for your area). Document with a written rental agreement, business purpose for each rental day, and proof of payment.

Modest savings but essentially free money if you have a home and a business.

#### 9. Hire Family Members (Savings: $5,000-$15,000)

Pay children for legitimate work in the family business: social media management, office cleaning, data entry, warehouse help. Wages must be reasonable for the work performed and actually paid.

If sole proprietorship and child under 18: exempt from FICA. Child earns up to $15,750 tax-free (standard deduction). Business deducts the wages at 35-37% marginal rate.

$15,750 deduction at 37% = about $5,830 tax savings. Plus child can contribute to Roth IRA ($7,000/year if earned income supports it) = tax-free compounding.

Pay a spouse to enable their own Solo 401(k) / retirement plan contributions.

#### 10. Estate Planning -- Annual Gifting and 529 Superfunding (Savings: estate tax reduction)

Annual exclusion: $19,000 per recipient ($38,000 with gift-splitting). A couple with 4 children can gift $152,000/year without using any lifetime exemption.

529 superfunding: contribute up to 5 years of annual exclusion ($95,000 per beneficiary, $190,000 if gift-splitting) in a single year. For 4 grandchildren: $760,000 into 529s in one year, all within annual exclusion.

Begin analyzing GRAT/SLAT structures if estate exceeds $7M per person (the projected post-sunset exemption).

#### 11. State Migration Analysis (Savings: $50,000-$120,000/year)

At $750K income in California: state tax is approximately $85,000. Moving to Florida, Texas, Nevada, or Wyoming eliminates this entirely. Even factoring in higher property taxes and cost of living differences, net savings are typically $50K-$100K+/year.

Must establish genuine domicile: driver's license, voter registration, primary home, spend majority of days in new state. California, New York, and New Jersey aggressively audit departures.

### Key Deadlines for Tier 4
All Tier 3 deadlines + Cash Balance plan establishment (Dec 31), opportunity zone investment (180 days from gain), QSBS monitoring (5-year holding period), estate plan annual review

### Common Mistakes at Tier 4
- Not establishing a Defined Benefit plan when income supports it (leaving $50K-$100K in deductions unused)
- Cost segregation without RE Pro status (losses are suspended, not lost, but no current benefit)
- RE Pro spouse not maintaining adequate time log (IRS disallows status on audit)
- Pursuing captive insurance without genuine insurable risk (IRS audit magnet)
- Delaying estate planning until the exemption sunsets (window closing)
- Not evaluating state migration despite six-figure annual savings

### Typical Forms Required
All Tier 3 forms + 5500 (DB plan), 8997 (OZ), cost segregation study report, 709 (gift tax return if gifts exceed annual exclusion), state departure documentation

## Tier 5: $1,000,000-$5,000,000 AGI -- Advanced Structures

**Philosophy**: At seven figures, tax optimization requires a coordinated team (CPA, tax attorney, estate attorney, wealth advisor, insurance specialist). Individual strategies are necessary but insufficient -- the focus shifts to structural architecture: trusts, multi-entity systems, and sophisticated financial instruments. Legislative risk becomes a factor: diversify across strategy types because any single approach may be changed by Congress.

**Estimated total tax savings vs doing nothing: $200,000-$800,000/year**

**Professional help level**: Tax CPA ($10,000-$25,000/year), tax attorney ($5,000-$20,000/year), estate attorney ($10,000-$50,000 for initial planning + annual updates), wealth advisor

### Priority-Ordered Strategies

#### 1. GRAT for Wealth Transfer (Savings: estate tax on transferred wealth)

A Grantor Retained Annuity Trust transfers appreciation out of the estate while the grantor retains an annuity stream.

**Mechanics**: transfer $5M of appreciating assets to GRAT. Retain an annuity based on the §7520 rate (5.0% for April 2025, varies monthly). If assets return more than the §7520 rate, the excess passes to beneficiaries estate-tax-free.

**Zeroed-out GRAT**: set annuity so the present value of the retained annuity equals the full transfer amount. Gift tax = $0. All upside passes free.

**Rolling GRATs**: use short-term (2-year) GRATs in series. If assets appreciate in one GRAT term, that appreciation transfers. If assets decline, the GRAT "fails" harmlessly (assets return to grantor). This captures positive volatility while avoiding downside.

Best for: concentrated stock positions, pre-IPO equity, assets expected to appreciate significantly.

#### 2. SLAT Before Exemption Sunset (Savings: $2M-$5M+ in estate tax avoidance)

Spousal Lifetime Access Trusts allow a married person to make an irrevocable gift into a trust for the spouse's benefit, using the current high exemption ($13.99M per person) before it potentially drops to ~$7M.

**Key benefits**: assets are outside the grantor's estate, but the spouse can access distributions. Income taxed to grantor (not trust), effectively a tax-free gift of the income tax payments.

**Reciprocal trust doctrine**: if both spouses create SLATs, they must differ meaningfully (different trustees, different distribution standards, different beneficiaries, staggered timing) to avoid IRS argument that the trusts are reciprocal and should be collapsed.

**Window**: if the exemption sunsets after 2025, the anti-clawback regulation (Reg 20.2010-1(c)) protects gifts made using the higher exemption. Use it or potentially lose $7M+ of exemption per person.

#### 3. Family Limited Partnership with Valuation Discounts (Savings: 25-40% estate tax discount)

Transfer investment assets to an FLP. Gift limited partnership interests to family members at discounted values:
- Lack of marketability discount: 15-30%
- Minority interest discount: 10-25%
- Combined discount: 25-40%

$5M of assets, discounted by 35% = $3.25M for gift tax purposes. Transfer $1.75M of value using only $3.25M of exemption.

**Requirements**: legitimate non-tax purpose (asset protection, centralized management, keeping family assets together). Maintain formalities (partnership agreement, annual meetings, K-1s). IRS aggressively challenges abusive FLP arrangements lacking economic substance.

#### 4. Private Foundation vs DAF Decision (Savings: depends on charitable intent)

At $1M+ annual giving, a private foundation may be appropriate:
- Full control over investments and grant-making
- Can hire family members (reasonable compensation)
- Can provide scholarships, run programs
- Legacy vehicle (can exist in perpetuity)
- Must distribute 5% of assets annually
- Public filing (Form 990-PF)
- Higher administrative cost ($10K-$50K/year)
- Lower deduction limits (30% AGI cash, 20% AGI property)

For most at this tier, a DAF is still more efficient unless control, legacy, or program operation is the goal.

#### 5. Installment Sales for Business Exit (Savings: $100,000-$500,000+ in tax deferral)

Selling a business for $5M in a lump sum: $5M x 23.8% (LTCG + NIIT) = $1.19M tax in one year.

Installment sale over 10 years: recognize $500K/year, spreading across lower brackets and deferring tax. If the seller moves to a no-income-tax state before payments begin, state tax is also eliminated on future installments.

**Section 453**: gain is recognized proportionally as payments are received. Interest must be charged at AFR (Applicable Federal Rate). Can be combined with opportunity zone reinvestment (invest each year's gain into QOF).

**453A interest charge**: for installment obligations exceeding $5M, interest is charged on the deferred tax. This reduces but does not eliminate the benefit.

#### 6. Split-Dollar Life Insurance (Savings: significant estate tax reduction)

Employer (or trust) and employee share the cost and benefits of a life insurance policy. Used to:
- Transfer wealth outside the estate at minimal gift tax cost
- Provide key-person insurance with tax-efficient structure
- Fund buy-sell agreements

Two types: economic benefit (employer owns policy, employee has term-cost taxable benefit) and loan (employer lends premium, employee-trust owns policy).

**Complex**: requires careful structuring by an attorney specializing in split-dollar arrangements. Missteps create adverse tax consequences.

#### 7. Non-Qualified Deferred Compensation (Savings: timing-dependent)

Defer current compensation to future years (retirement, lower-income period, after state migration).

- Election must be made before the year compensation is earned (generally prior December 31)
- New plan participants: 30-day initial deferral election
- Subject to employer credit risk (unsecured creditor)
- 409A compliance is mandatory -- penalties for violations: immediate income inclusion + 20% penalty + premium interest rate

Best for: executives expecting to retire to a lower bracket or no-income-tax state within 5-10 years.

#### 8. Charitable Remainder Trust for Concentrated Positions (Savings: $50,000-$500,000)

For a taxpayer with $3M in a single appreciated stock position (basis: $300K):

Direct sale: $3M - $300K = $2.7M gain x 23.8% = $642,600 tax.

CRT approach:
1. Transfer $3M of stock to CRT (no immediate tax)
2. CRT sells stock and reinvests (no capital gains at trust level)
3. CRT pays income stream to grantor for life (5-50% of trust value annually)
4. Immediate partial charitable deduction based on remainder value
5. Income is taxed as received, spread over many years across multiple categories (ordinary, capital gain, tax-exempt)
6. Remainder passes to charity at death

Tax savings: immediate charitable deduction ($300K-$600K depending on CRT terms) + deferral of capital gains over life of trust + diversification without immediate tax hit.

#### 9. International Planning (Savings: situation-dependent)

For taxpayers with international business operations, foreign real estate, or foreign financial accounts:
- Treaty-based positions to reduce withholding on foreign income
- Foreign tax credit optimization (Form 1116, general vs passive category)
- PFIC (Passive Foreign Investment Company) planning -- avoid or manage under QEF/MTM elections
- FBAR/FATCA compliance (penalties for non-filing are severe: $10K+ per account per year for FBAR)
- Subpart F and GILTI planning for controlled foreign corporations

#### 10. Multiple Entity Optimization (Savings: $20,000-$100,000)

Separate business lines into distinct entities to:
- Isolate SSTB from non-SSTB (preserve QBI deduction)
- Create captive insurance arrangements
- Enable different retirement plans for different activities
- Facilitate PTET elections
- Provide liability protection

Each entity has separate books, accounts, and tax returns. Management fees and intercompany transactions must be arm's length.

#### 11. Defined Benefit Plan at Maximum (Savings: $80,000-$120,000)

At this income level, maximize the DB plan to the annual benefit limit ($280,000 annual benefit at age 62 for 2025). Required contribution is actuarially determined -- for a 55-year-old targeting maximum benefit, annual contribution can reach $300K+.

Combine with Solo 401(k) for an additional $70K+ in tax-deferred space. Total potential deferral: $350K+/year.

### Key Deadlines for Tier 5
All Tier 4 deadlines + trust establishment (before year-end for current-year gifts), NQDC deferral elections (December 31 for following year), installment sale structuring (before closing), Form 709 (April 15 for prior-year gifts exceeding annual exclusion), FBAR (April 15, auto-extension to October 15)

### Common Mistakes at Tier 5
- Delaying SLAT/GRAT implementation until after the exemption sunsets (potentially losing millions)
- FLP without legitimate non-tax purpose (IRS successfully challenges these)
- Not charging adequate interest on installment sales (AFR requirement)
- CRT with inappropriate payout rate (5% minimum, 50% maximum; remainder must be at least 10% of initial value)
- Ignoring 409A rules for deferred compensation (20% penalty + premium interest is devastating)
- Overlooking international reporting (FBAR/FATCA penalties can exceed the account value)
- Single-strategy concentration: relying entirely on one approach that Congress could change

### Typical Forms Required
All Tier 4 forms + 709 (gift tax), 3520/3520-A (foreign trusts if applicable), 1116 (foreign tax credit), 5471 (CFC), 8865 (foreign partnerships), FBAR (FinCEN 114), trust income tax returns (1041), FLP returns (1065)

## Tier 6: $5,000,000+ AGI -- Comprehensive Wealth Planning

**Philosophy**: Tax planning at this level is inseparable from investment management, estate planning, risk management, and family governance. Every financial decision has tax implications that cascade across multiple jurisdictions, entities, and generations. The team typically includes: tax CPA, tax attorney, estate attorney, wealth advisor, insurance specialist, trust officer, and potentially international tax counsel.

**Estimated total tax savings vs doing nothing: $500,000-$2,000,000+/year**

**Professional help level**: Family office (internal or multi-family office) with dedicated tax team ($50,000-$500,000+/year in advisory fees)

### Priority-Ordered Strategies

#### 1. Dynasty Trusts (Savings: generational wealth preservation)

A dynasty trust is designed to last for multiple generations (or perpetually in favorable jurisdictions). Combined with GST exemption allocation ($13.99M per person, 2025), distributions to descendants avoid estate tax, gift tax, and GST tax at each generational transfer.

**Favorable trust situs states** (no state income tax on trust income, abolished rule against perpetuities):
- South Dakota: no state income tax, perpetual trusts, strong asset protection, trust privacy
- Nevada: no state income tax, 365-year trust duration, strong spendthrift protections
- Alaska: no state income tax, perpetual trusts, self-settled asset protection trusts
- Wyoming: no state income tax, perpetual trusts, low-cost trust administration
- Delaware: no state income tax on out-of-state beneficiaries, 110-year rule against perpetuities

**Structure**: irrevocable trust, funded with $14M-$28M (using both spouses' exemptions), GST-exempt. Trust invests and grows. Distributions to descendants for health, education, maintenance, and support. At death, assets remain in trust for next generation (no estate tax). Repeat indefinitely.

$28M invested at 7% real return, no distributions: $56M in 10 years, $112M in 20 years, $784M in 50 years. All estate-tax-free if properly structured and GST-exempt.

#### 2. Private Placement Life Insurance (PPLI) (Savings: $50,000-$500,000+/year ongoing)

PPLI is a variable universal life insurance policy with a very high premium (typically $1M+ initial), invested in alternative investments (hedge funds, PE, private credit) within the insurance wrapper.

**Tax benefits**: investment income inside the policy grows tax-free. Death benefit passes income-tax-free to beneficiaries. If policy is owned by an irrevocable trust, proceeds are also estate-tax-free.

**Access to cash value**: policy loans against cash value are not taxable events. Effectively tax-free access to investment returns during lifetime.

**Requirements**: must be a legitimate insurance policy (meet the 7-pay test and other insurance requirements). Requires a licensed insurance carrier (typically domiciled in a favorable jurisdiction). Investment options must be managed by independent investment managers (investor control doctrine).

Best for: taxpayers with large allocations to tax-inefficient investments (hedge funds generating short-term gains, high-yield bonds, etc.) where moving them inside PPLI eliminates ongoing tax drag.

#### 3. Family Office Structure (Savings: operational deductions)

For families with $50M+ in investable assets, a dedicated family office may be cost-effective.

**Tax treatment**: post-TCJA, investment advisory fees are not deductible (suspended miscellaneous itemized deductions). But a family office structured as a trade or business (operating through an entity, providing services beyond investment management -- tax planning, estate administration, philanthropic coordination, family governance, bill payment, insurance management) may deduct expenses under Section 162 rather than Section 212.

**Key**: must demonstrate the office is engaged in a trade or business, not merely managing passive investments. Employment of staff, regular business hours, providing services to multiple family entities, and operating from a dedicated office all support trade-or-business characterization.

#### 4. Directed Trustee Structures (Savings: control + tax optimization)

Use directed/delegated trust structures that separate investment authority, distribution authority, and administrative functions:
- **Investment advisor**: directs investments (can be a family member or family office)
- **Distribution advisor**: controls distributions (trust protector or distribution committee)
- **Administrative trustee**: handles paperwork, tax filings, account management (corporate trustee)

This provides family control while maintaining the tax and asset-protection benefits of an irrevocable trust with an independent trustee.

#### 5. Art and Collectibles as Charitable Vehicles (Savings: situation-dependent)

Donating appreciated art (held >1 year) to a public charity (museum) that will use it for its tax-exempt purpose:
- Deduction at full FMV (up to 30% of AGI, 5-year carryforward)
- No capital gains tax on appreciation
- Qualified appraisal required for donations over $5,000 (Form 8283 Section B)

$2M painting with $200K basis: sale would generate $1.8M gain x 31.8% (28% collectibles rate + 3.8% NIIT) = $572,400 tax. Donation: $0 tax + $2M deduction x 37% = $740,000 tax benefit. Total swing: $1.31M.

**Fractional giving**: donate a percentage interest in artwork (must donate the full remaining interest within 10 years or by date of death).

#### 6. Conservation Easements (Savings: potentially large, but EXTREME caution)

Donating a conservation easement (restriction on development of land) can generate a charitable deduction equal to the reduction in property value.

**Syndicated conservation easements** are on the IRS "Dirty Dozen" list of abusive tax schemes. The IRS has won multiple Tax Court cases challenging inflated valuations. Penalties include accuracy-related penalties (40% for gross valuation misstatement) and potential fraud penalties.

**Legitimate easements**: land with genuine conservation value (wetlands, scenic views, historic properties, wildlife habitat), appraised by a qualified appraiser, with a qualified conservation purpose, donated to a qualified organization that will enforce the restriction. Even legitimate easements face heavy IRS scrutiny.

**Bottom line**: only pursue if you own land with genuine conservation value and the easement serves a real conservation purpose. Do not pursue primarily for tax deductions.

#### 7. ESOP for Business Exit (Savings: $1M-$10M+ in tax deferral)

If you own a C-Corporation and want to exit, selling to an Employee Stock Ownership Plan (ESOP) allows:
- Tax-deferred rollover of sale proceeds under Section 1042 (reinvest in "qualified replacement property" -- stocks/bonds of domestic corporations)
- Employees receive ownership (alignment of interests, retention tool)
- Company's ESOP contributions are tax-deductible (deductible dividends on ESOP shares)
- Potential estate planning: qualified replacement property held until death receives step-up in basis

For a $20M business: direct sale at 23.8% = $4.76M tax. ESOP sale with 1042 rollover: $0 tax now. If held until death: $0 tax ever (step-up in basis).

**Requirements**: must be a C-Corp (S-Corps do not qualify for 1042 rollover). ESOP must own 30%+ of company after sale. Complex DOL and ERISA requirements.

#### 8. Section 338(h)(10) Election Planning (Savings: situation-dependent)

In an S-Corp asset sale structured as a stock sale with a 338(h)(10) election, the buyer gets a stepped-up basis in assets (more attractive to buyers, potentially higher sale price) while the seller treats the transaction as an asset sale for tax purposes.

Used to: make S-Corp stock sales more attractive to buyers (who would otherwise get carryover basis), negotiate higher purchase prices (buyer's tax benefit can be shared), and structure deals efficiently.

**Complex**: requires both buyer and seller to jointly elect. Tax consequences flow through to S-Corp shareholders as if assets were sold.

#### 9. International Structures (Savings: situation-dependent, only if genuine business need)

For taxpayers with legitimate international business operations:
- **Treaty planning**: position income in jurisdictions with favorable tax treaty provisions
- **Transfer pricing**: arm's-length pricing between related entities in different jurisdictions
- **Check-the-box elections**: entity classification elections for foreign entities
- **Subpart F and GILTI planning**: structure CFC operations to minimize phantom income inclusion
- **Foreign tax credit planning**: maximize credit utilization, manage separate limitation categories

**Warning**: international tax planning that lacks economic substance is aggressively pursued by the IRS. All structures must have genuine business purpose beyond tax reduction. FATCA and CRS (Common Reporting Standard) have dramatically reduced the ability to hide income offshore.

#### 10. Legislative Risk Hedging (Savings: preservation of existing savings)

At this level of planning, concentration risk applies to tax strategies just as it does to investments. Strategies that work today may be curtailed or eliminated by future legislation.

**Diversification principles**:
- Do not rely entirely on any single strategy (conservation easements, captive insurance, and syndicated transactions have all been targeted)
- Implement strategies with current-year benefits (retirement contributions) alongside long-dated strategies (dynasty trusts)
- Monitor proposed legislation and accelerate planning before adverse changes take effect
- Maintain flexibility: strategies that lock you in for decades are riskier than those with exit options
- Keep detailed documentation: if a strategy is later challenged, contemporaneous records of business purpose are the best defense

**Current legislative risks** (as of 2025):
- Estate tax exemption sunset after 2025 (potential reduction to ~$7M)
- Possible elimination of carried interest preferential treatment
- Possible limitations on dynasty trust planning
- Potential changes to opportunity zone program
- Potential NIIT expansion to cover S-Corp active income
- Possible increase in top capital gains rate

## Quick Decision Framework

**"What should I do first?"**

1. Are you leaving money on the table? (Employer match, Saver's Credit, EITC, education credits) -- grab it.
2. Are you paying tax you do not have to? (No HSA, no S-Corp, no PTET, no retirement plan optimization) -- fix it.
3. Are you paying tax at the wrong time? (No Roth conversion planning, no capital gain timing, no loss harvesting) -- time it.
4. Are you paying tax at the wrong rate? (Ordinary income that could be LTCG, passive income that could be active, NIIT-subject income that could be exempt) -- restructure it.
5. Are you paying tax in the wrong jurisdiction? (High-tax state when relocation is feasible, no PTET election, no trust situs optimization) -- relocate it.
6. Are you paying tax in the wrong generation? (No estate planning, no dynasty trust, no GRAT/SLAT, no QSBS gifting) -- transfer it.

Start at step 1 and work down. Each step is harder and more expensive to implement than the last, but the savings scale accordingly.
