# Expat and Foreign Income Reference (Tax Year 2025)

## Overview

U.S. citizens and resident aliens are taxed on worldwide income regardless of where they live. Americans working or living abroad must navigate the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion, Foreign Tax Credit, FBAR/FATCA reporting requirements, and treaty benefits. Failure to comply with reporting obligations carries severe penalties, even when no tax is owed. The U.S. is one of only two countries (with Eritrea) that taxes citizens on worldwide income regardless of residence.

## Key Tax Considerations

### U.S. Filing Obligation
- U.S. citizens and resident aliens must file a U.S. federal return reporting worldwide income, regardless of country of residence
- Filing threshold is the same as domestic filers (e.g., $15,750 for single filers under 65 in 2025)
- Automatic 2-month extension to June 15 for taxpayers living abroad (attach statement to return); can also request extension to October 15
- Interest accrues from April 15 deadline regardless of extension

### Foreign Earned Income Exclusion (FEIE) -- IRC Section 911

**2025 exclusion amount**: $130,000 (indexed annually)

Applies only to foreign EARNED income (wages, salaries, self-employment income, professional fees). Does NOT apply to: investment income, pensions, Social Security, annuities, capital gains.

#### Physical Presence Test -- Detailed Rules
- Must be physically present in a foreign country for at least **330 full days** during any **12-consecutive-month period**
- The 12-month period does NOT have to be a calendar year -- it can be any consecutive 12-month period that you choose
- A "full day" means midnight to midnight. The day you depart the U.S. and the day you arrive in the U.S. do NOT count as full foreign days.
- Days of transit through the U.S. count as U.S. days
- Travel between foreign countries: days spent in international waters or airspace do NOT count as foreign days

**Worked example**:
- You move to London on February 1, 2025. You want to use the physical presence test.
- 12-month period: February 1, 2025 to January 31, 2026. You need 330 full days in foreign countries.
- That leaves 35 days for U.S. visits during that period.
- If you spend Christmas in the U.S. (Dec 20 - Jan 5 = 16 days) and another trip (July 1-10 = 10 days), you have used 26 of your 35 allowed U.S. days.

#### Bona Fide Residence Test
- Must establish residence in a foreign country for an uninterrupted period that includes at least one FULL tax year (Jan 1 - Dec 31)
- Intent matters: must demonstrate genuine residency -- not merely being present
- Evidence: lease/home purchase, local bank accounts, social ties, visa status (tourist visa generally insufficient), family living with you, local driver's license, membership in local organizations
- Brief trips to the U.S. do not break bona fide residence if intent to return is clear
- **Cannot be used in the first partial year** abroad (because you need a full tax year of foreign residence first)

#### Section 911(d)(4) Waiver for Adverse Conditions
- If you were a bona fide resident or expected to meet the physical presence test but had to leave due to war, civil unrest, or other adverse conditions, the IRS may waive the time requirements
- IRS publishes a list of countries and effective dates for which the waiver applies
- You must have been present in the country for some portion of the qualifying period before the adverse conditions arose

#### Election and Revocation
- Elect on Form 2555; once revoked, cannot re-elect for 5 years without IRS consent
- Election is per taxpayer -- each spouse must independently qualify and elect
- Exclusion reduces taxable income but the excluded income is still used to determine the tax rate on remaining income ("stacking rule")

### Foreign Housing Exclusion / Deduction -- Detailed

**Purpose**: excludes or deducts qualified housing costs above a base amount.

**Base amount**: 16% of FEIE = 16% x $130,000 = $20,800 for 2025

**Standard cap**: 30% of FEIE = $39,000 (maximum housing expenses that count)

**Location-specific limits**: IRS publishes higher limits for high-cost cities:
- Hong Kong: up to ~$114,300 (one of the highest)
- Tokyo: up to ~$80,000+
- London: up to ~$60,000+
- Singapore: up to ~$72,000+
- Zurich, Geneva: up to ~$70,000+

**Qualifying expenses**: rent, utilities (except phone), insurance on dwelling, furniture rental, parking, repairs, occupancy taxes. Does NOT include: mortgage payments on owned home, domestic labor, capital improvements, purchased furniture, cost of meals.

**Calculation**:
- Actual qualifying housing expenses (capped at location limit) MINUS base amount ($20,800)
- Example: London housing costs = $50,000/year. Housing exclusion = $50,000 - $20,800 = $29,200 additional exclusion.
- Total FEIE + housing exclusion cannot exceed foreign earned income.

**Form**: employees claim as exclusion (Form 2555); self-employed claim as deduction (reduces SE income).

### Foreign Tax Credit (FTC) -- Form 1116

- Credit for income taxes paid or accrued to foreign governments
- Generally more beneficial than FEIE for taxpayers in high-tax countries
- **Cannot combine FEIE and FTC on the same income** -- FEIE reduces the FTC limitation proportionally
- Separate limitation categories ("baskets"): general limitation, passive, Section 901(j), foreign branch
- Carryback 1 year, carryforward 10 years for excess credits
- Election: can claim as itemized deduction instead of credit (rarely beneficial; loses carryforward)
- If total foreign taxes paid are $300 or less ($600 MFJ) and all from passive income: can claim credit directly on Form 1040 without Form 1116

### FEIE vs. FTC Decision Framework
| Factor | FEIE Better | FTC Better |
|--------|------------|------------|
| Foreign tax rate | Low-tax country (< ~22%) | High-tax country (> ~22%) |
| Income type | Earned income only | All income types |
| Investment income | No benefit | Can credit foreign taxes on investments |
| Self-employment tax | FEIE does NOT reduce SE tax | FTC does not reduce SE tax either |
| Simplicity | Simpler to calculate | More complex; requires Form 1116 |
| Future flexibility | Revoking FEIE has 5-year lockout | Can change approach annually |
| Income level | Income near exclusion amount | Income far exceeds exclusion |

## FBAR and FATCA Reporting

### FBAR -- FinCEN Report 114
- **Who must file**: U.S. persons with financial interest in or signature authority over foreign financial accounts if aggregate value exceeds $10,000 at any time during the year
- **Accounts include**: bank accounts, brokerage accounts, mutual funds, and certain insurance policies and pension plans
- **Filing**: electronically via BSA E-Filing system (NOT with tax return)
- **Deadline**: April 15; automatic extension to October 15

**FBAR Penalties -- Detailed**:
- **Non-willful**: up to $16,117 per violation (per account per year, adjusted for inflation). IRS has discretion to assess per-account or per-form.
- **Willful**: greater of $100,000 or 50% of the account balance at time of violation. Per account per year.
- **Criminal penalties**: up to $250,000 fine and/or 5 years imprisonment for willful failure to file
- Example: taxpayer has 3 unreported accounts with $200,000 each for 5 years. Willful penalty = 50% x $200,000 x 3 accounts x 5 years = $1,500,000 (potentially exceeding account balances)

### FATCA -- Form 8938 (Statement of Specified Foreign Financial Assets)
- **Thresholds** (total value of specified foreign financial assets):
  - Living in U.S.: > $50,000 end of year / $75,000 at any time (single); $100,000/$150,000 (MFJ)
  - Living abroad: > $200,000 end of year / $300,000 at any time (single); $400,000/$600,000 (MFJ)
- **Assets include**: financial accounts PLUS foreign stocks/securities held directly (not through U.S. broker), interests in foreign entities, foreign financial instruments
- **Filed with**: Form 1040 (not separate like FBAR)
- **Penalties**: $10,000 for failure to file; additional $10,000 for each 30 days of non-filing after IRS notice (max $60,000); 40% penalty on underpayments attributable to undisclosed assets

### FBAR vs. FATCA Comparison Table

| Feature | FBAR (FinCEN 114) | FATCA (Form 8938) |
|---------|-------------------|-------------------|
| Filed with | FinCEN (BSA E-Filing) | IRS (with tax return) |
| Threshold (US resident) | $10,000 aggregate | $50,000/$75,000 (single) |
| Threshold (abroad) | $10,000 aggregate | $200,000/$300,000 (single) |
| Assets covered | Financial accounts only | Accounts + other financial assets |
| Penalties (non-willful) | Up to $16,117/account/year | $10,000-$60,000 |
| Penalties (willful) | Greater of $100K or 50% balance | 40% of underpayment |
| Extension | Automatic to Oct 15 | Follows tax return extension |
| Agency | FinCEN (Treasury) | IRS |

**Both may be required** -- they are not alternatives; many taxpayers must file both.

## CFC, PFIC, and International Business Ownership

### Controlled Foreign Corporation (CFC) -- Form 5471
- A foreign corporation is a CFC if >50% of vote or value is owned by U.S. shareholders (each owning 10%+)
- **Subpart F income** (IRC Sections 951-964): certain types of CFC income (passive, related-party, insurance) are taxed currently to U.S. shareholders even if not distributed
- **GILTI (Global Intangible Low-Taxed Income -- IRC Section 951A)**: enacted by TCJA; requires U.S. shareholders of CFCs to include GILTI in current income. Roughly: CFC income exceeding 10% return on tangible assets. Individual shareholders get no Section 250 deduction (only C-corps do).
- Penalty for failure to file Form 5471: $10,000 per year per CFC

### Passive Foreign Investment Company (PFIC) -- Form 8621
- A foreign corporation is a PFIC if (a) 75%+ of gross income is passive, OR (b) 50%+ of assets produce or are held to produce passive income
- **Common PFIC situations**: foreign mutual funds, foreign ETFs, foreign holding companies
- **Default (Section 1291 excess distribution) regime**: punitive -- gain on sale is spread over holding period, taxed at highest rate for each year, with interest charged. Effectively eliminates long-term capital gain rates.
- **QEF election**: mark-to-market annually; current inclusion of income. Requires information from the PFIC (often unavailable from foreign funds).
- **Mark-to-market election**: recognize gain/loss annually based on FMV. Simpler but creates annual taxable events.
- **Planning**: U.S. persons abroad should generally avoid foreign mutual funds and invest through U.S. brokerages to avoid PFIC complexity.

## Totalization Agreements

- Purpose: prevent double Social Security/social insurance taxation when working abroad
- U.S. has agreements with 30 countries including UK, Canada, Germany, France, Japan, Australia, South Korea
- **How they work**: if you are sent by a U.S. employer to a treaty country for less than 5 years, you remain in the U.S. Social Security system (and exempt from the foreign system). If longer, you switch to the foreign system.
- Also allow combining work credits from both countries to qualify for benefits
- Certificate of Coverage (Form SSA-2032 in the U.S.) proves which country's system applies

## Treaty Benefits

- U.S. has income tax treaties with 60+ countries
- Common benefits: reduced withholding rates on dividends, interest, royalties; exemptions for certain types of income (students, teachers, researchers)
- Must disclose treaty position on **Form 8833** (Treaty-Based Return Position Disclosure)
- **Saving clause**: most treaties preserve the right of the U.S. to tax its citizens as if the treaty did not exist; specific exceptions may apply (usually for pensions, alimony, child support, government service income)
- **Tiebreaker rules for dual residents**: if both countries claim you as a tax resident, the treaty tiebreaker determines which country gets primary taxing rights (based on permanent home, center of vital interests, habitual abode, nationality)

## Expatriation Tax -- IRC Section 877A

For U.S. citizens who renounce citizenship or long-term residents who abandon green cards:

**Covered expatriate**: an individual who (1) has average net income tax of $201,000+ for 5 years prior (2025), OR (2) net worth of $2M+ at expatriation, OR (3) fails to certify 5-year tax compliance.

**Mark-to-market exit tax**:
- All worldwide assets deemed sold at FMV on the day before expatriation
- Gain in excess of exclusion amount ($866,000 for 2025) is taxed
- Deferred compensation and specified tax-deferred accounts have special rules (30% withholding on distributions)
- Does NOT apply if you are not a "covered expatriate"

**Worked example**:
- U.S. citizen renouncing with $5M in assets, cost basis $1M. Deemed gain: $4M. Less $866K exclusion = $3,134,000 taxable at applicable capital gains rates.

## Digital Nomad Considerations

- **Tax home doctrine**: your tax home is your regular place of business, not where you live. If you have no fixed place of business, you may be considered an "itinerant" with no tax home.
- **No tax home = no FEIE**: the Foreign Earned Income Exclusion requires a "tax home" in a foreign country. If you move constantly and have no fixed foreign base, you may not qualify.
- **Physical presence test still works**: if you meet 330 full days in foreign countries, you can use the physical presence test -- but you MUST establish a tax home in one of those countries.
- **State tax risk**: if you do not establish domicile in a no-tax state before leaving, your last U.S. state of domicile may continue to claim you as a tax resident.

## Self-Employment Abroad

- U.S. citizens/residents remain subject to SE tax on foreign self-employment income
- FEIE excludes income from income tax but does NOT reduce SE tax
- **Totalization agreement exception**: if working in a country with a Social Security agreement, may be exempt from U.S. SE tax (and instead pay foreign social insurance)
- File Schedule SE as normal; SE tax calculated on total net SE earnings before FEIE
- May claim FEIE on income tax portion while still paying full SE tax

## State Tax Obligations While Abroad

- **Varies dramatically by state**:
  - Some states (CA, VA, NM, SC) continue to tax former residents on worldwide income unless clear domicile change is established
  - Some states have specific safe harbors for absence (NY: 548-day rule)
  - No-income-tax states: no issue (FL, TX, NV, WY, AK, SD, TN, NH, WA)
- **California**: particularly aggressive; leaving for a foreign country does not automatically break CA residency; must demonstrate intent to permanently leave and sever CA ties
- **Virginia**: continues taxing domiciliaries; must establish domicile elsewhere
- Best practice: establish domicile in a no-income-tax state before moving abroad if possible
- Some states require continued filing even while claiming no state-source income
- **Planning tip**: if you are moving abroad from a high-tax state, consider establishing domicile in a no-tax state (FL, TX, NV) BEFORE departing the country. This is much easier than arguing with CA or NY from abroad.
- **State FEIE treatment**: states that have an income tax generally follow the federal FEIE, but some have additional requirements or limitations. Check specific state rules.

## Dual-Status Aliens (First Year in U.S. / Departure Year)

- If you are a nonresident alien who becomes a resident alien during the year (or vice versa), you are a "dual-status" alien
- File a dual-status return: nonresident portion uses Form 1040-NR rules; resident portion uses Form 1040 rules
- Cannot file jointly (unless making the first-year choice election under IRC Section 6013(g))
- Cannot claim standard deduction for the nonresident portion
- Dual-status taxpayers face complex rules about which income is taxable in each portion of the year

## Optimization Strategies

1. **Choose FEIE vs. FTC based on foreign tax rate**: in low-tax jurisdictions, FEIE is typically better; in high-tax jurisdictions, FTC preserves excess credits for carryforward
2. **Plan the Physical Presence Test around moves**: the 330-day test uses a rolling 12-month period; time departures to maximize qualification. Choose the 12-month period that gives you the best coverage.
3. **Establish domicile in a no-tax state before going abroad**: avoids state tax complications entirely
4. **Maximize housing exclusion**: in high-cost cities, the housing exclusion can add $20,000-$90,000+ of additional tax-free income
5. **Use Totalization Agreements**: avoid double social security taxation; can save 15.3% of income
6. **Consider Roth conversions while abroad**: if using FEIE and foreign-sourced income is excluded, other income may be in lower brackets -- good time for Roth conversions
7. **File all information returns on time**: penalties for FBAR, FATCA, 5471, 3520 are draconian and disproportionate to the tax involved
8. **Avoid foreign mutual funds**: the PFIC regime is punitive. Invest through U.S.-based brokerages.
9. **Coordinate FEIE and FTC across spouses**: if both work abroad, each spouse can independently elect FEIE or FTC based on their circumstances

## Common Mistakes

1. **Not filing U.S. returns at all** -- U.S. citizens must file regardless of where they live
2. **Forgetting FBAR** -- this is not part of the tax return and is often overlooked; penalties are severe
3. **Double-dipping FEIE and FTC** -- cannot claim both on the same income
4. **Ignoring SE tax** -- FEIE does not reduce self-employment tax
5. **Not filing Form 8938** -- FATCA reporting is separate from FBAR and has different thresholds
6. **Failing to disclose foreign trusts or corporate interests** -- Forms 3520, 5471 carry $10,000+ penalties per form per year
7. **Revoking FEIE without understanding the 5-year lockout** -- switching from FEIE to FTC is a one-way door for 5 years
8. **Assuming a treaty eliminates all U.S. tax** -- the saving clause in most treaties preserves U.S. taxing rights for citizens
9. **Not filing state returns** -- some states aggressively pursue former residents who move abroad
10. **Investing in foreign mutual funds without understanding PFIC** -- triggers punitive tax regime that eliminates capital gains benefits
11. **Digital nomads claiming FEIE without a foreign tax home** -- no fixed abode = no FEIE. Must establish a tax home.
12. **Not considering expatriation tax implications** -- covered expatriates face mark-to-market exit tax that can be hundreds of thousands of dollars

## Foreign Bank Account and Financial Planning

### Banking Challenges for Americans Abroad
- Many foreign banks refuse to open accounts for U.S. citizens due to FATCA compliance burden
- U.S. banks may close accounts of expats who no longer have a U.S. address
- Solutions: Charles Schwab International, Interactive Brokers, HSBC Expat, maintaining a U.S. address (family member)
- Never fail to report accounts due to banking difficulties -- penalties are assessed regardless

### Investment Strategy for Expats
- **Avoid**: foreign mutual funds (PFIC), foreign life insurance with investment component (taxable in U.S.), foreign pension contributions (may not be deductible in U.S.)
- **Prefer**: U.S.-based brokerage (Schwab, Fidelity, Interactive Brokers for expats), U.S. ETFs, individual stocks
- **Tax-advantaged accounts**: continue maximizing IRA/401(k) contributions if you have earned income (FEIE-excluded income still counts as earned income for IRA contribution purposes)

### Foreign Pension Plans
- Contributions to foreign pension plans are generally NOT deductible for U.S. tax purposes (unless a treaty provides otherwise)
- Growth in foreign pension may be currently taxable in the U.S. (depends on classification as a grantor trust, employee trust, etc.)
- UK/Canada pensions have favorable treaty provisions; most other countries do not
- Distributions are taxable in the U.S.; foreign tax credit available for foreign taxes paid on the distribution

## Worked Example -- FEIE vs. FTC Comparison

**Scenario**: U.S. citizen living in Germany, earning EUR 150,000 (~$165,000). Germany imposes ~42% income tax = ~$69,300.

**Option A -- FEIE**:
- Exclude $130,000 under FEIE. Remaining $35,000 taxed at U.S. rates (stacking rule applies).
- U.S. tax on $35,000 (at rates starting where $130,000 left off): ~$8,400.
- Cannot claim FTC on the excluded $130,000 of income.
- Can claim FTC on $35,000 portion: limited to U.S. tax on that income (~$8,400).
- Foreign taxes allocable to $35,000: $69,300 x ($35,000/$165,000) = ~$14,700.
- FTC used: $8,400 (limited). Excess: $6,300 carried forward.
- **Net U.S. tax: $0** (FTC covers remaining U.S. tax).
- **Total tax (Germany + U.S.): $69,300**.

**Option B -- FTC only (no FEIE)**:
- All $165,000 reported as taxable. U.S. tax before credits: ~$32,000.
- FTC for $69,300 foreign taxes: limited to $32,000 (U.S. tax on foreign-source income).
- FTC used: $32,000. Excess: $37,300 carried forward 10 years.
- **Net U.S. tax: $0**.
- **Total tax: $69,300** (same), BUT $37,300 in FTC carryforward for future use.

**Conclusion**: in high-tax countries, FTC is often better because it generates carryforward credits. FEIE is better in low-tax countries where FTC would not fully offset U.S. tax.

## Streamlined Filing Compliance Procedures

- For taxpayers who are non-willfully out of compliance with U.S. filing obligations
- **Streamlined Foreign Offshore Procedures**: for those living abroad; file 3 years of returns + 6 years of FBARs; no penalties
- **Streamlined Domestic Offshore Procedures**: for those living in U.S.; 5% miscellaneous offshore penalty
- Must certify non-willfulness
- Alternative: Delinquent FBAR submission procedures, Delinquent international information return procedures
- Voluntary Disclosure Practice: for willful violations (involves IRS Criminal Investigation)
- **Timing matters**: streamlined procedures are available only while IRS offers them. If IRS contacts you first (audit letter, notice), you may no longer qualify.
