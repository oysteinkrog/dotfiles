# Business Exit Planning -- Comprehensive Reference (TY 2025)

## Overview

Business exit planning is where tax strategy, deal design, entity law, state residency, estate
planning, compensation design, and negotiation leverage all collide. The highest-value mistake is
usually not a math error. It is a structural error made too early to undo or too late to fix:
- agreeing to an asset sale without pricing the tax cost,
- failing to prove basis or QSBS eligibility,
- letting purchase-price allocation be dictated entirely by the buyer,
- confusing compensation with purchase price,
- implementing charitable or trust planning after the sale is effectively locked,
- ignoring state tax while focusing only on federal rates.

This guide is the planning layer. It complements the more transaction-focused
[BUSINESS-EXIT.md](BUSINESS-EXIT.md) and the event-oriented
[BUSINESS-EXIT-SALE.md](../life-events/BUSINESS-EXIT-SALE.md).

## 1. Define the Exit Objective Before Modeling Tax

### Possible Objectives
- Maximize after-tax cash at close.
- Preserve upside through rollover equity.
- Protect employees through an ESOP or internal transition.
- Spread tax over time through installment treatment.
- Minimize state tax exposure.
- Pair the exit with estate or charitable planning.

### Why This Matters
- The "best" tax structure is not always the best business structure.
- A seller who needs liquidity, certainty, and simplicity may rationally reject a structure that
  looks better on paper but adds buyer-credit risk, contingent payments, or litigation exposure.

## 2. The Minimum Data Room for Tax Planning

### Ownership and Equity
- Cap table and all historical issuances.
- Shareholder and partner basis schedules.
- Debt basis and shareholder loans.
- Stock-option, warrant, SAFE, and convertible-note history.
- Section 83(b) records.

### Entity and Tax History
- Prior federal and state returns.
- NOLs, credit carryforwards, suspended losses, PTET history.
- Built-in gains history for post-conversion S-Corps.
- Payroll and reasonable-compensation history.

### Asset Base
- Fixed-asset ledger.
- Depreciation schedules.
- Inventory and receivable aging.
- IP ownership and transfer history.
- Real-estate basis, cost-seg studies, and depreciation recapture exposure.

### Personal Overlay
- Seller residency history.
- Trust and estate structure.
- Charitable intent.
- Expected post-close working role.

## 3. Structure Decision Tree

### Stock / Equity Sale
- Usually best for seller from a federal rate perspective.
- Often worst for buyer without a step-up.
- Requires clean equity records and manageable liability profile.

### Asset Sale
- Often best for buyer.
- May be dramatically worse for seller depending on entity type and asset mix.
- Needs deep modeling of inventory, receivables, depreciation recapture, and goodwill.

### Deemed Asset Sale Election
- 338(h)(10), 336(e), or related structures can bridge buyer and seller objectives in the right
  fact pattern.
- Never assume the election is neutral. It changes character and sometimes state outcomes.

### Redemption / Internal Buyout
- Sale vs dividend treatment can materially change the result.
- Family, partner, and related-party transactions need special scrutiny.

## 4. The Five Planning Levers With the Biggest Potential Dollar Impact

### 1. Price Allocation
- Often the single most important tax exhibit in the deal.
- Inventory, receivables, recapture assets, covenant-not-to-compete, customer lists, software,
  real estate, and goodwill each push the seller into different tax buckets.

### 2. QSBS / Section 1202
- Can be transformational when valid.
- Requires a documentary case, not a vibe.
- Must be evaluated before the exit is functionally locked.

### 3. Charitable Planning Before Liquidity
- Public charity, DAF, or CRT planning before a binding sale can convert a taxable exit into a
  partially or materially untaxed one on the donated slice.
- The timing analysis must be disciplined and documented.

### 4. State Residency and Sourcing
- State tax on an exit can be enormous.
- Real planning requires facts to change, not just intent language.

### 5. Compensation vs Purchase Price Separation
- Earnouts, stay bonuses, consulting, and noncompetes are not interchangeable.
- What looks like "more price" may actually be ordinary income or wages.

## 5. Planning Windows

### 12-24 Months Before Exit
- Clean up books and cap table.
- Confirm basis support.
- Evaluate QSBS and trust architecture.
- Review state-residency feasibility.
- Simplify entities where legally and commercially possible.

### 6-12 Months Before Exit
- Build the tax model for realistic structures.
- Identify buyer/seller tax tradeoffs to use in price negotiation.
- Clarify who owns IP, goodwill, and customer relationships.

### LOI to Signing
- Lock down price-allocation objectives.
- Separate labor economics from capital economics.
- Confirm what planning is still available and what is already too late.

### Closing Through Filing
- Track escrow, holdback, earnouts, indemnity recoveries, and true-ups.
- Make estimated-tax and liquidity decisions immediately.

## 6. Special Situations

### C-Corp Exit
- Double-tax modeling on asset sale is mandatory.
- Personal goodwill, ESOP / Section 1042, and stock-sale alternatives deserve early review.

### S-Corp Exit
- Need shareholder basis, debt basis, hot-asset style analysis, and built-in gains history.
- Buyers may push for deemed asset treatment; sellers need a quantified answer, not a reflex.

### Partnership / LLC Exit
- Section 751 and debt-allocation effects can create ordinary income where the owner expected
  capital gain.
- Installment and earnout terms need especially careful modeling.

### Founder With Rollover Equity
- Cash-out and rollover basis mapping matters.
- Future liquidity events depend on how the rollover is structured and documented now.

---

## 7. The Amendment and Cleanup Layer

Before exit, ask:
- Are prior-year basis schedules wrong or incomplete?
- Were prior PTET elections missed?
- Are depreciation schedules broken?
- Is there unresolved shareholder-loan or capital-account confusion?

A clean exit often requires fixing old tax history before the closing-year return is filed.

---

## 8. Common Mistakes

1. Starting tax analysis after the economics are already fixed.
2. Treating the buyer's draft allocation as market truth.
3. Failing to build a basis file before diligence.
4. Letting rollover equity, earnout, consulting, and noncompete provisions blur together.
5. Ignoring state tax until the final tax estimate.
6. Forgetting that escrow and holdback mechanics change timing.
7. Assuming a founder can "just move states" right before close and win.
8. Implementing charitable planning after the sale is already effectively certain.

---

## 9. What the Skill Should Produce for a Real Exit

### Core Deliverables
- structure comparison memo,
- seller after-tax proceeds model,
- state-tax overlay,
- price-allocation issue list,
- pre-close planning calendar,
- closing-year filing checklist,
- earnout / installment monitoring template.

### Risk Grading
Each proposed strategy should be labeled:
- conservative,
- moderate,
- aggressive,
- too late / not viable.
