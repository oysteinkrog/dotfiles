# Retiree Reference (Tax Year 2025)

## Overview

Retired taxpayers face unique tax considerations including Social Security taxation, Required Minimum Distributions, pension income, Roth conversion strategies, and managing tax-efficient withdrawals from multiple account types. Proper planning can significantly reduce the lifetime tax burden on retirement assets and maximize after-tax income. Many retirees are surprised to learn that retirement is not tax-free -- and that strategic withdrawal sequencing matters enormously.

## Key Tax Considerations

### Social Security Taxation -- Deep Dive

Up to 85% of Social Security benefits may be taxable (never 100%). Taxation is based on "provisional income."

**Provisional income formula**: AGI (excluding SS) + tax-exempt interest + 50% of Social Security benefits

| Filing Status | Provisional Income | Taxable Portion |
|--------------|-------------------|-----------------|
| Single | < $25,000 | 0% |
| Single | $25,000 - $34,000 | Up to 50% |
| Single | > $34,000 | Up to 85% |
| MFJ | < $32,000 | 0% |
| MFJ | $32,000 - $44,000 | Up to 50% |
| MFJ | > $44,000 | Up to 85% |
| MFS (living with spouse) | $0+ | Up to 85% always |

These thresholds have NEVER been adjusted for inflation since 1993. As a result, the vast majority of retirees with any other income now pay tax on their Social Security benefits.

**The "Tax Torpedo" -- Understanding the Marginal Impact**

In the phase-in range where Social Security goes from 0% taxable to 85% taxable, each additional dollar of outside income can cause up to $0.85 of Social Security to become newly taxable. This creates an effective marginal rate much higher than the statutory bracket.

**Worked example -- single filer**:
- Social Security: $30,000/year
- Other income: $28,000 (pension)
- Provisional income: $28,000 + $15,000 (50% of SS) = $43,000
- This is above the $34,000 threshold, so up to 85% of SS is taxable
- Taxable SS = lesser of: 85% x $30,000 = $25,500 OR a formula-based calculation
- Total taxable income: $28,000 + $25,500 = $53,500 (before standard deduction)

**The torpedo effect**: If this filer earns $1,000 more in pension income:
- The $1,000 is taxable itself
- It also causes an additional $850 of Social Security to become taxable
- Total additional taxable income: $1,850
- At 22% bracket: additional tax = $407 on $1,000 of extra income -- an effective 40.7% marginal rate
- This "torpedo" effect occurs in the phase-in zone and can push effective marginal rates to 40%+ even for modest-income retirees

**Strategies to minimize Social Security tax**:
- Roth withdrawals are NOT included in provisional income
- Tax-exempt municipal bond interest IS included in provisional income (unusual -- affects SS calculation even though not directly taxed)
- Strategy: take distributions from Roth accounts to supplement SS rather than traditional IRA/401(k)
- Strategy: do Roth conversions BEFORE claiming Social Security (reduces future provisional income)
- Strategy: QCDs reduce AGI, which reduces provisional income

### Required Minimum Distributions (RMDs) -- Detailed

#### Age Requirements
- **Age 73**: RMDs begin for those born 1951-1959 (SECURE 2.0)
- **Age 75**: RMDs begin for those born 1960 or later (SECURE 2.0)
- First RMD: can be delayed until April 1 of the year following the year you turn 73/75 (but then you take TWO RMDs in that year)

#### RMD Computation -- Step by Step

**Step 1**: Determine account balance as of December 31 of the prior year for EACH account subject to RMDs.

**Step 2**: Look up the life expectancy factor:
- **Uniform Lifetime Table** (default): used by most account owners

| Age | Factor | Age | Factor | Age | Factor |
|-----|--------|-----|--------|-----|--------|
| 73 | 26.5 | 78 | 22.0 | 83 | 17.7 |
| 74 | 25.5 | 79 | 21.1 | 84 | 16.8 |
| 75 | 24.6 | 80 | 20.2 | 85 | 16.0 |
| 76 | 23.7 | 81 | 19.4 | 86 | 15.2 |
| 77 | 22.9 | 82 | 18.5 | 87 | 14.4 |

- **Joint Life Expectancy Table**: used ONLY when the sole beneficiary is a spouse who is more than 10 years younger. Produces a smaller RMD (larger divisor).

**Step 3**: RMD = Account balance / Factor

**Worked example**:
- Age 75. Traditional IRA balance on Dec 31, 2024 = $800,000.
- Factor at age 75 = 24.6
- RMD = $800,000 / 24.6 = $32,520
- This $32,520 MUST be distributed by December 31, 2025 (or April 1, 2026 if this is the first RMD year, but that triggers a double-RMD year).

**Aggregation rules**:
- **IRAs**: Calculate RMD separately for EACH traditional IRA, BUT can aggregate and take the total from any one or combination of IRAs
- **401(k)/403(b)**: RMDs must be taken separately from EACH plan (cannot aggregate across employer plans)
- **403(b) plans**: can aggregate across 403(b) plans (exception to the employer plan rule)

#### Penalties for Missed RMDs
- **25% excise tax** on the amount not distributed (reduced from 50% by SECURE 2.0)
- **Reduced to 10%** if corrected within the "correction window" (generally by end of second year following the year of the missed RMD)
- File Form 5329 to report and pay penalty; request waiver for reasonable cause

#### Roth IRAs and RMDs
- NO RMDs for original Roth IRA owners during their lifetime
- Roth 401(k): previously had RMDs, but SECURE 2.0 eliminated Roth 401(k) RMDs starting in 2024
- Inherited Roths: subject to 10-year distribution rule (but distributions are generally tax-free)

### Qualified Charitable Distribution (QCD) -- Strategy Guide

**Eligibility**: age 70.5 or older (NOT 73 -- QCDs are available earlier than RMDs)

**Amount**: up to $108,000 per person per year (2025, indexed for inflation) directly from IRA to qualified charity

**Key benefits**:
- Counts toward RMD (satisfies all or part of the distribution requirement)
- **Excluded from gross income** -- this is the key advantage over taking RMD and donating separately
- Reduces AGI, which has cascading benefits:
  - Reduces taxable Social Security (lower provisional income)
  - Reduces Medicare IRMAA premiums (lower MAGI)
  - Reduces Net Investment Income Tax exposure
  - Reduces ACA premium tax credit clawback (for pre-Medicare retirees)
  - May help stay below SALT/itemization thresholds

**QCD vs. Deduction comparison**:
- Retiree age 76, RMD = $30,000. Wants to donate $10,000 to charity. Takes standard deduction ($16,550 single, 65+).
- **Without QCD**: Takes $30,000 RMD. AGI increases by $30,000. Donates $10,000 cash. Gets NO deduction (takes standard deduction). Net income increase: $30,000.
- **With QCD**: Takes $20,000 RMD + $10,000 QCD. AGI increases by only $20,000. QCD counts toward RMD. Net income increase: $20,000. Saves tax on $10,000 of income.

**Rules and restrictions**:
- Cannot go to a Donor Advised Fund (DAF) or private foundation
- Must be a direct transfer from IRA custodian to charity (not a withdrawal followed by a donation)
- Must be from an IRA (not 401(k) -- roll to IRA first if needed)
- SECURE 2.0 addition: one-time QCD of up to $54,000 to a charitable remainder trust or charitable gift annuity (inflation-adjusted)

### Roth Conversion Ladder Strategy

#### The Opportunity Window
Between retirement and age 73 (when RMDs start), many retirees have unusually low taxable income. This creates a prime window for Roth conversions.

**Strategy**: convert Traditional IRA to Roth each year, "filling up" lower tax brackets:
- Identify your current taxable income (pension, part-time work, investment income)
- Determine how much room remains in your current bracket (or the next one you are willing to fill)
- Convert that amount from Traditional to Roth
- Pay the tax now at a lower rate; future Roth distributions are tax-free

**Worked example -- filling the 22% bracket (MFJ)**:
- Couple age 64, both retired. Pension income: $40,000. Standard deduction: $33,200 (both 65+).
- Taxable income before conversion: $40,000 - $33,200 = $6,800 (in 10% bracket)
- Top of 22% bracket (MFJ 2025): $96,950 of taxable income
- Room to fill: $96,950 - $6,800 = $90,150
- Convert $90,150 from Traditional to Roth. Tax on conversion: ~$16,680 (blended rate across 12% and 22% brackets).
- That $90,150 now grows tax-free in the Roth forever. If withdrawn at age 80 when RMDs would push them into the 32% bracket, the savings are substantial.

**Over 10 years of conversions**: $900,000+ moved to Roth, reducing future RMDs by ~$36,000/year and eliminating tax on those distributions permanently.

#### Conversion Guardrails
- **IRMAA impact**: conversion increases MAGI, which triggers IRMAA surcharges on Medicare premiums 2 years later. Plan conversions to stay below IRMAA thresholds when possible.
- **Social Security taxation**: if converting before claiming SS, this is not a concern. If already claiming, conversion income increases provisional income.
- **ACA premiums**: for retirees under 65 on ACA marketplace plans, conversion income can reduce or eliminate Premium Tax Credits
- **No 10% early withdrawal penalty** on conversions (IRC Section 408A(d)(3))
- **5-year rule**: each conversion has its own 5-year holding period for penalty-free withdrawal of the conversion amount (only matters if under 59.5)

### Medicare IRMAA Planning

**Income-Related Monthly Adjustment Amount**: higher Medicare Part B and D premiums based on MAGI from 2 years prior.

**2025 IRMAA brackets (based on 2023 MAGI)**:

| Single MAGI | MFJ MAGI | Monthly Part B Surcharge |
|-------------|----------|--------------------------|
| $106,000 or less | $212,000 or less | $0 (standard premium) |
| $106,001-$133,500 | $212,001-$267,000 | +$70/month |
| $133,501-$167,000 | $267,001-$334,000 | +$175/month |
| $167,001-$200,000 | $334,001-$400,000 | +$280/month |
| $200,001-$500,000 | $400,001-$750,000 | +$384.30/month |
| $500,000+ | $750,000+ | +$419.30/month |

**Key planning points**:
- IRMAA uses a **2-year lookback** (2025 premiums based on 2023 MAGI)
- Large Roth conversions or one-time income events can trigger surcharges 2 years later
- **Life-changing event exception (Form SSA-44)**: can request IRMAA recalculation for marriage, divorce, death of spouse, work stoppage, work reduction, loss of income-producing property, loss of pension income. One-time income events (like selling a house) do NOT qualify.
- Part D surcharges follow the same brackets but are smaller amounts

### Pension Income
- Employer pensions (defined benefit plans): fully or partially taxable depending on whether you made after-tax contributions
- If you contributed after-tax dollars: use the Simplified Method (Form 1040 pension/annuity line) to recover your basis tax-free over expected payments
- Report on Form 1099-R; taxable amount in Box 2a
- Lump-sum distribution options: may qualify for special 10-year averaging (if born before 1936) or rollover to IRA
- **Government pensions**: may affect Social Security benefits:
  - **Historical note:** WEP and GPO used to reduce benefits for some retirees with non-SS-covered government pensions
  - **Current law:** the Social Security Fairness Act repealed both WEP and GPO for benefits payable after December 2023, so current planning should use actual SSA notices and the post-repeal rules

### Withdrawal Sequencing Optimization

#### General Order (tax-efficient)
1. **Taxable accounts first**: withdrawals are return of basis (tax-free) plus capital gains (preferential rates)
2. **Tax-deferred accounts (traditional IRA/401k)**: withdrawals are ordinary income
3. **Tax-free accounts (Roth)**: preserve for last; grows tax-free; no RMDs; best for heirs

#### Nuanced Approach (Dynamic Sequencing)
- Withdraw from taxable accounts to cover spending needs
- Convert traditional IRA to Roth in the "gap" between current income and the top of your desired bracket
- Use Roth funds strategically in high-income years to avoid bracket jumps or IRMAA triggers
- Consider "bracket bumping" analysis: sometimes paying slightly more tax now saves significantly more later

**Year-by-year optimization example**:
- Year 1 (age 62): income = $20K pension. Fill 12% bracket with $50K Roth conversion. Spend from taxable accounts.
- Year 2-4: similar strategy. Move $150K+ to Roth.
- Year 5 (age 67): start Social Security. Provisional income now includes 50% of SS. Reduce conversion amount to stay below IRMAA threshold.
- Year 6-10: smaller conversions, QCDs for charitable giving.
- Year 11 (age 73): RMDs begin. Having moved $300K+ to Roth, RMDs are smaller. Use QCDs for charitable portions.

## Tax Credits for Retirees

### Credit for the Elderly or Disabled (Schedule R)
- Age 65+ OR permanently/totally disabled under 65
- Very restrictive income limits: AGI < $17,500 (single) / $25,000 (MFJ, both 65+)
- Non-taxable Social Security must also be below threshold
- Maximum credit: $1,125 (single) / $1,687.50 (MFJ both 65+)
- Rarely applicable due to low income thresholds

### Medical Expense Deduction
- Deductible portion: medical expenses exceeding 7.5% of AGI
- Includes: Medicare premiums (Part B, D, supplemental), dental, vision, hearing aids, long-term care insurance (age-based limits), home modifications for medical reasons, nursing home costs (if primarily for medical care)
- Long-term care insurance deduction limits (2025, approximate): age 61-70 = $4,900; age 71+ = $6,200
- Many retirees exceed the 7.5% threshold due to high medical costs

### Medicare Premium Deduction
- Self-employed retirees with net SE income: deduct Medicare premiums as self-employed health insurance (above-the-line)
- Others: include as medical expense on Schedule A (subject to 7.5% AGI threshold)
- Includes Part B, Part D, and Medicare Advantage premiums
- Does NOT include Part A premiums unless you voluntarily enrolled and pay for Part A

## Retired Military Pay

- Military retirement pay is fully taxable as ordinary income (reported on Form 1099-R)
- Combat-related special compensation (CRSC) and concurrent retirement and disability payments (CRDP): tax treatment varies
- Disability retirement pay: may be partially or fully excludable depending on VA disability rating
- Survivor Benefit Plan (SBP) payments to surviving spouse: taxable as pension income
- Many states exempt military retirement pay from state taxation

## Common Mistakes

1. **Not taking RMDs on time** -- 25% penalty on the amount not distributed (10% if corrected promptly)
2. **Forgetting RMDs from multiple accounts** -- each 401(k) requires separate RMD; IRAs can be aggregated but must calculate for each
3. **Over-converting to Roth** -- pushing into higher brackets or triggering IRMAA unnecessarily. Converting $200K in one year when $50K/year over 4 years would be far more tax-efficient.
4. **Not considering the Social Security tax torpedo** -- the range where each additional dollar of income causes $0.50-$0.85 of Social Security to become taxable, creating effective marginal rates of 40%+
5. **Missing QCD opportunity** -- taking RMD then donating cash instead of doing a QCD. The QCD reduces AGI; the deduction (if even available) merely reduces taxable income after AGI.
6. **Not tracking IRA basis** -- if you ever made nondeductible contributions, Form 8606 must be filed to avoid double taxation
7. **Taking Social Security too early for tax reasons** -- delaying to 70 increases benefits by ~8%/year and may be more tax-efficient long-term
8. **Ignoring state tax treatment** -- some states fully exempt retirement income; others fully tax it
9. **Not filing Form SSA-44 after a life-changing event** -- overpaying IRMAA when eligible for a reset
10. **Delaying first RMD and getting hit with a double-RMD year** -- two RMDs in one year can push you into a much higher bracket

## State-Specific Notes

### States That Do Not Tax Retirement Income
- **No income tax at all**: AK, FL, NV, SD, TX, WA, WY
- **No tax on any retirement income**: IL, IA, MS, PA (specific types)

### States Exempting Social Security (13 states fully exempt)
- Most states do not tax Social Security benefits at all
- States that DO tax Social Security (fully or partially, often with income thresholds): CO, CT, KS, MN, MO, MT, NE, NM, RI, UT, VT, WV
- Several of these states have recently enacted or expanded exemptions, trending toward full exemption

### States Exempting Pensions
- **Fully exempt all retirement income**: IL, IA (phasing in), MS, PA
- **Exempt government pensions only**: AL, HI, KS, LA, MA, MI (public pensions before certain dates), NY (state/local/federal pensions)
- **Partial exemptions**: many states offer exemptions up to a dollar limit (e.g., GA $65,000 per person if 62+)

### States Exempting Military Retirement Pay
- Fully exempt in 30+ states including: AL, AR, CT, HI, IL, IA, KS, KY, LA, MA, MI, MN, MO (if under income limit), MS, NJ, NY, NC (if vested before Aug 12, 1989), ND, OH, PA, WI, and others
- Partial exemptions in several other states

### States With Favorable Retiree Tax Treatment
- **Pennsylvania**: does not tax retirement plan distributions, Social Security, or investment income for retirees
- **Mississippi**: does not tax any retirement income
- **Illinois**: exempts retirement income, Social Security
- **Iowa**: phasing out retirement income taxation
- **Florida/Texas/Nevada/Wyoming/Alaska/South Dakota/Washington**: no income tax at all

### IRMAA Planning
- IRMAA is based on federal MAGI regardless of state; moving to a no-tax state does not affect IRMAA
- All state tax planning is separate from IRMAA bracket management
- However, state taxes paid reduce after-tax wealth, so state planning and IRMAA planning are both important components of overall retirement tax optimization

## Worked Example -- Comprehensive Retirement Tax Plan

**Couple**: both age 65, MFJ. Pension: $40,000. Social Security (combined): $50,000. Traditional IRA (combined): $1,200,000. Roth IRA: $200,000. Taxable brokerage: $300,000.

**Year 1 (age 65, before RMDs)**:
- Pension income: $40,000
- Standard deduction: $33,200 (both 65+)
- Taxable income before conversion: $6,800 (10% bracket)
- Room in 22% bracket: $96,950 - $6,800 = $90,150
- **Action**: convert $90,150 from Traditional IRA to Roth. Tax: ~$16,700.
- **Spend from**: taxable brokerage account ($30,000 withdrawal, mostly return of basis)

**Year 5 (age 69, start Social Security)**:
- Pension: $40,000. SS: $50,000.
- Provisional income: $40,000 + $25,000 (50% of SS) = $65,000 (well above $44K MFJ threshold)
- Taxable SS: ~$42,500 (85%)
- Taxable income: $40,000 + $42,500 - $33,200 = $49,300 (22% bracket)
- Room for Roth conversion: $96,950 - $49,300 = $47,650 (smaller due to SS taxation)
- **Action**: convert $47,650 to Roth. Charitable giving via QCD ($10,000) to reduce AGI.

**Year 9 (age 73, RMDs start)**:
- Traditional IRA now: ~$700,000 (after 8 years of conversions)
- RMD: $700,000 / 26.5 = $26,415
- Previous IRA without conversions: ~$1,500,000 (with growth)
- RMD without conversions: $1,500,000 / 26.5 = $56,604
- **RMD reduction**: $30,189/year permanently, saving ~$7,200/year in tax at 24% bracket
- **Roth balance**: ~$600,000+ (with growth on conversions), generating tax-free income

**Lifetime tax savings from this strategy**: approximately $150,000-$250,000 over a 25-year retirement, depending on returns and tax rates.
