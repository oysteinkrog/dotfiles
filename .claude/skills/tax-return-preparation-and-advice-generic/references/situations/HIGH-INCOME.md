# High-Income Taxpayer Reference (Tax Year 2025)

## Overview

High-income taxpayers face a layered system of surtaxes, phase-outs, and limitations that compound at progressively higher income levels. A taxpayer earning $500K faces a fundamentally different tax landscape than one earning $150K -- not merely higher rates, but entirely new taxes (NIIT, Additional Medicare Tax), lost deductions and credits (child tax credit, education credits, IRA deductions), and alternative tax regimes (AMT). Strategic planning requires understanding all of these simultaneously and coordinating strategies across income tax, employment tax, investment tax, and estate/gift tax systems.

This reference covers taxpayers with AGI generally above $200,000 (single) or $250,000 (MFJ). For strategy prioritization by specific income tier, see TAX-PLANNING-BY-INCOME-TIER.md.

## Alternative Minimum Tax (AMT) -- Deep Dive

### 2025 AMT Parameters

| Parameter | Single | MFJ |
|-----------|--------|-----|
| AMT exemption | $88,100 | $137,000 |
| Exemption phase-out begins | $626,350 | $1,252,700 |
| Exemption fully eliminated | $978,750 | $1,800,700 |
| 26% AMT rate applies up to | $239,100 | $239,100 |
| 28% AMT rate above | $239,100 | $239,100 |

### AMT Exposure Analysis by Income Level

**$200K-$400K AGI (MFJ)**: AMT risk is usually lower than in the pre-TCJA world because the
personal SALT deduction is still limited and exemptions are high. Main trigger: large ISO exercises.

**$400K-$700K AGI (MFJ)**: Moderate risk. ISO exercises, private activity bond interest, or large miscellaneous adjustments can push into AMT. Run parallel calculations.

**$700K-$1.2M AGI (MFJ)**: Highest marginal AMT risk zone. Regular tax rate (35-37%) often exceeds AMT rate (26-28%), so AMT rarely bites from income alone. But ISO exercises at this level can create massive AMT liability.

**Above $1.25M AMTI (MFJ)**: AMT exemption begins phasing out at 25 cents per dollar of AMTI above $1,252,700. This creates an effective 35% AMT rate in the phase-out zone (28% + 25% of 28%). Exemption fully gone at $1,800,700.

### Most Dangerous AMT Triggers

1. **ISO exercise spread**: The difference between FMV and exercise price is an AMT preference item. A $500K ISO spread can create $140K+ of AMT liability even if no shares are sold. This is the single most common cause of unexpected six-figure tax bills.
2. **Large personal SALT deductions**: For 2025, the Schedule A SALT cap is generally $40,000 ($20,000 MFS), subject to MAGI-based reduction. Large allowed SALT deductions can still increase AMTI materially even though the exemption shields many taxpayers.
3. **Private activity bond interest**: Tax-exempt for regular tax but included in AMTI. High-income investors in muni bond funds should check for PAB exposure.
4. **Accelerated depreciation**: MACRS depreciation exceeding ADS straight-line can still create AMT adjustments in the right fact pattern. Less relevant for property eligible for 100% bonus depreciation after January 19, 2025, but still important for ADS property, pre-2018 property, and assets outside bonus rules.
5. **Large long-term capital gains**: While LTCG rates are the same under both systems, large gains can push AMTI into the exemption phase-out zone.

### AMT Credit Carryforward (Form 8801)

If you paid AMT in a prior year due to timing differences (ISO exercise, depreciation), you generated a minimum tax credit. This credit can offset regular tax (but not below tentative minimum tax) in future years. After selling ISO shares, run Form 8801 to recover prior AMT paid. This is frequently overlooked and can be worth tens of thousands.

## Net Investment Income Tax (NIIT) -- Section 1411 Deep Dive

### What Counts as Net Investment Income

**INCLUDED** (subject to 3.8% NIIT):
- Interest income (taxable)
- Dividend income (qualified and non-qualified)
- Capital gains (short-term and long-term, including gain on sale of home exceeding $500K MFJ exclusion)
- Rental income (unless taxpayer qualifies as Real Estate Professional)
- Royalty income
- Passive business income (K-1 income from partnerships/S-Corps where taxpayer does not materially participate)
- Income from trading financial instruments or commodities
- Gain on sale of partnership interest (to extent of NII)

**EXCLUDED** (not subject to 3.8% NIIT):
- Wages and salaries
- Self-employment income (already subject to Medicare tax)
- Social Security benefits
- Tax-exempt bond interest
- Distributions from qualified retirement plans (401(k), IRA, pension)
- Veterans' benefits
- Active business income from S-Corps/partnerships where taxpayer materially participates
- Income from businesses in which taxpayer materially participates (under Reg 1.1411-4)
- Alaska Permanent Fund dividends (per IRS ruling)

### Material Participation Exception -- The Key NIIT Planning Lever

Income from a trade or business is exempt from NIIT if the taxpayer materially participates. The seven tests under Reg 1.469-5T:
1. **500-hour test**: Participate 500+ hours during the year
2. **Substantially all test**: Your participation constitutes substantially all participation
3. **100-hour/most test**: 100+ hours AND no other person participates more
4. **Significant participation activities**: 100+ hours each in multiple activities totaling 500+
5. **Prior 5-of-10 year test**: Materially participated in 5 of the prior 10 years
6. **Personal service activity**: 3-of-prior-10-year test for personal service activities
7. **Facts and circumstances**: 100+ hours and regular, continuous, substantial participation

**Grouping elections (Reg 1.469-4)**: Taxpayers can elect to group multiple activities as a single activity for material participation purposes. Once made, the election is binding unless facts materially change. This can convert several activities that individually fail the 500-hour test into a single grouped activity that passes.

### S-Corp Distributions vs K-1 Income for NIIT

S-Corp distributions themselves are not subject to NIIT (return of basis). The K-1 income flowing through to the shareholder is what matters. If the S-Corp shareholder materially participates, the K-1 ordinary business income is exempt from NIIT. This creates a powerful advantage over passive investments:

- S-Corp active income: No FICA above reasonable salary, no NIIT = saves up to 3.8%
- Passive partnership income: Subject to NIIT = 3.8% surtax applies
- Portfolio income: Always subject to NIIT regardless of participation

### Real Estate Professional Exemption

A qualifying Real Estate Professional can treat rental income as non-passive, thereby exempting it from NIIT. Requirements:
1. More than 750 hours in real property trades or businesses
2. More than half of personal services performed in real property trades or businesses
3. Material participation in each rental activity (or elect to aggregate all rental activities as one)

**Spouse strategy**: Only one spouse needs to qualify. Hours cannot be combined between spouses for the 750-hour test. Common approach: one spouse (often non-working or part-time) dedicates time to real estate management to qualify as RE Pro, converting all rental losses from passive to active (deductible against wages) and all rental income from NIIT-subject to NIIT-exempt.

## Additional Medicare Tax -- Section 3101(b)(2)

- 0.9% surtax on wages + SE income exceeding $200,000 (single) / $250,000 (MFJ)
- Employer withholds 0.9% on individual wages exceeding $200,000 regardless of filing status
- MFJ trap: if each spouse earns $175K, neither triggers employer withholding, but combined $350K exceeds $250K threshold by $100K. Owe $900 at filing ($100K x 0.9%).
- Planning: unlike NIIT, no participation exception. Can only be managed by reducing wages/SE income (S-Corp reasonable compensation optimization, retirement plan contributions)
- Report on Form 8959

## Passive Activity Limitations -- Section 469

### General Rules

Passive activity losses can only offset passive activity income. Suspended losses carry forward until:
1. There is passive income to offset, OR
2. The entire activity is disposed of in a fully taxable transaction (complete disposition releases all suspended losses)

### $25,000 Rental Exception

Active participants (not the same as material participation) in rental real estate can deduct up to $25,000 of rental losses against non-passive income. But:
- Phase-out begins at $100,000 AGI
- Fully phased out at $150,000 AGI
- Rate: $1 reduction for every $2 of AGI above $100K
- For most readers of this document: this exception is GONE (AGI well above $150K)

### Converting Passive to Non-Passive Income

**Material participation** (see 7 tests above): if you can meet any test, the activity becomes non-passive. Most practical for high-income taxpayers: the 500-hour test or the significant participation activity aggregation.

**Real Estate Professional status**: converts rental activities from per-se passive to non-passive (if also materially participating in each rental).

**Self-rental recharacterization trap (Reg 1.469-2(f)(6))**: If you rent property to a business in which you materially participate, the rental income is recharacterized as non-passive. This is a TRAP because:
- Rental income becomes non-passive (cannot be offset by other passive losses)
- But rental losses remain passive (cannot offset non-passive income unless RE Pro)
- Net effect: heads IRS wins, tails you lose
- Planning: be aware of this asymmetry when renting property to your own business

**Short-term rental loophole**: Rentals with average customer use of 7 days or fewer are NOT treated as rental activities under Reg 1.469-1T(e)(3)(ii)(A). They are treated as regular trade or business activities. If you materially participate (500+ hours), income/losses are non-passive. This is how Airbnb-style short-term rentals can generate deductible losses against W-2 income for high-income taxpayers without RE Pro status.

### Grouping Strategies

Taxpayers can group activities under Reg 1.469-4 to:
1. Combine hours across related businesses to meet material participation
2. Aggregate rental activities (RE Pro must elect this to avoid per-property material participation)
3. Group a rental activity with a trade or business (limited to certain situations per Reg 1.469-4(d))

Grouping elections are made by filing a statement with the return. Once made, they are generally binding unless facts change materially.

## S-Corp Basis and Loss Limitations -- The Three Gates

S-Corp losses flowing through on K-1 must pass three sequential gates before becoming deductible. Each gate is independent, and a loss blocked at any gate is suspended at that gate.

### Gate 1: Stock and Debt Basis -- Section 1366(d)

Losses are limited to the shareholder's adjusted basis in S-Corp stock PLUS direct loans from the shareholder to the S-Corp.

**Stock basis** includes: initial investment + additional contributions + income items - distributions - loss items (in a specific ordering under Reg 1.1367-1).

**Debt basis** includes ONLY direct loans from shareholder to the S-Corp. Does NOT include:
- Bank loans guaranteed by the shareholder
- Loans from related parties
- Loans from other shareholders
- Entity-level debt (unlike partnerships, where entity debt increases partner basis)

This is a critical difference from partnerships. An S-Corp shareholder who personally guarantees a $500K bank loan to the S-Corp gets ZERO basis increase. A partner in a partnership would get basis for that same guarantee.

**Restoring basis**: Additional capital contributions or direct shareholder loans create basis to absorb suspended losses. Common year-end strategy: shareholder loans cash to S-Corp before Dec 31 to unlock suspended losses.

### Gate 2: At-Risk Rules -- Section 465

After passing the basis test, losses must also not exceed the amount the taxpayer is "at risk." At-risk amount generally includes:
- Cash contributed
- Adjusted basis of property contributed
- Amounts borrowed for which taxpayer is personally liable
- Qualified nonrecourse financing (real estate only -- certain institutional lending)

NOT at risk for: nonrecourse debt (except qualified real estate financing), amounts protected by stop-loss agreements, guarantees, or similar arrangements.

### Gate 3: Passive Activity Rules -- Section 469

After passing both basis and at-risk tests, losses must clear passive activity rules. If the shareholder does not materially participate, losses are passive and can only offset passive income.

**Order matters**: A $100K loss with only $60K of stock/debt basis means $40K is suspended at Gate 1 (carried forward under 1366(d)). The $60K that passes Gate 1 is then tested at Gate 2, then Gate 3. Suspended amounts at each gate are tracked separately.

## Qualified Business Income (QBI) Deduction -- Section 199A

### Phase-Out Mechanics for SSTBs

Specified Service Trades or Businesses (health, law, accounting, consulting, financial services, performing arts, athletics, and "any trade or business where the principal asset is the reputation or skill of its employees"):

| MFJ Taxable Income | QBI Deduction Available |
|---------------------|------------------------|
| Below $394,600 | Full 20% of QBI |
| $394,600-$494,600 | Reduced (phase-out zone) |
| Above $494,600 | ZERO -- completely eliminated |

### Non-SSTB Limitation Above Threshold

For non-SSTB businesses above $494,600 (MFJ), the deduction is limited to the GREATER of:
- 50% of W-2 wages paid by the business, OR
- 25% of W-2 wages + 2.5% of the unadjusted basis immediately after acquisition (UBIA) of qualified property

**Planning**: ensure the business pays sufficient W-2 wages. For S-Corps, the reasonable compensation paid to the owner counts as W-2 wages for this calculation. Hiring employees also increases the W-2 wage component.

**Entity structuring**: separate SSTB and non-SSTB activities into different entities. The SSTB taint does not spread to a separate entity conducting non-SSTB activities (but beware the "cracking and packing" anti-abuse rule in Reg 1.199A-5(c)(2)).

### Mega Backdoor Roth

After-tax contributions to 401(k) above the $23,500 employee limit, up to the $70,000 total annual additions limit minus employee deferrals and employer match. Immediately convert to Roth (in-plan conversion or in-service distribution to Roth IRA).

Requirements: plan must allow (a) after-tax contributions and (b) in-service withdrawals or in-plan Roth conversions. Not all plans permit this -- check plan document.

At $70K total limit minus $23,500 employee deferral minus (say) $10,000 employer match = $36,500 potential mega backdoor Roth per year. For a 35-year-old, 25 years of this at 7% growth = ~$2.5M in tax-free Roth by age 60.

### Backdoor Roth IRA

1. Contribute $7,000 ($8,000 if 50+) to non-deductible traditional IRA
2. Convert entire balance to Roth IRA (ideally same day or next business day to minimize gains)
3. Report on Form 8606 Parts I and II

**Pro-rata rule**: If you have ANY pre-tax IRA balances (traditional, SEP, SIMPLE), the conversion is partially taxable. The taxable percentage = total pre-tax IRA balance / total of all IRA balances. ALL IRAs are aggregated.

**Solution**: Roll all pre-tax IRA money into employer 401(k) before year-end (reverse rollover). This leaves only the non-deductible contribution in the traditional IRA, making the conversion nearly tax-free.

### Cash Balance / Defined Benefit Plans

For self-employed individuals or business owners with consistent high income:

| Owner Age | Approximate Annual Contribution Limit |
|-----------|--------------------------------------|
| 35 | $100,000-$130,000 |
| 45 | $150,000-$200,000 |
| 55 | $220,000-$280,000 |
| 62 | $280,000-$350,000 |

Can be stacked with a 401(k): contribute $23,500 (employee) + $70,000 (employer/total 401(k) limit) + $200,000+ (defined benefit) = $270,000+ total tax-deferred savings in a single year.

Setup and actuarial costs: $2,000-$5,000/year. Only justified with consistent annual income of $300K+ from the business. Must fund for minimum 3-5 years to avoid IRS scrutiny.

## SALT Cap Strategies by State

### Pass-Through Entity Tax (PTET)

Over 30 states now offer PTET elections allowing entity-level state tax payment that bypasses the
personal SALT cap. IRS Notice 2020-75 confirmed federal deductibility.

**PTET Decision Framework Under the 2025 Personal SALT Cap**:

| State | State Rate | PTET EV Once the Personal SALT Cap Is Already Saturated | Practical Takeaway |
|-------|-----------|-------------------------------------------|-------------------|
| California | 13.3% | High | Even mid-six-figure pass-through owners should usually model PTET because state tax is large and property taxes often consume much of the personal cap already |
| New York | 10.9% | High | Very attractive for high earners, especially in NYC or high-property-tax counties |
| New Jersey | 10.75% | High | Strong candidate once the household would otherwise lose most of the state income tax deduction |
| Connecticut | 6.99% | Moderate | Still valuable, but benefit is less dramatic than CA/NY/NJ unless income and property tax are both high |
| Illinois | 4.95% | Situational | Often worth modeling, but the flatter rate means the federal benefit is more modest unless pass-through income is large |

PTET value is now much more fact-specific than it was under the old $10,000 personal SALT cap. The highest-value scenarios are the ones where:
- Property taxes and other personal SALT already consume most or all of the $40,000 cap
- The taxpayer has substantial pass-through income in a high-tax state
- The state PTET credit mechanics and payment timing are clean

**Key PTET Deadlines (examples -- verify current year)**:
- California (PTET): election due on original return; tax due June 15 (first installment) and by original due date
- New York (PTET): election due March 15; estimated payments due quarterly
- New Jersey (BAIT): election due with return; estimated payments due quarterly
- Connecticut: mandatory for eligible entities (not elective)

### State Migration Analysis

Moving from a high-tax state to a no-income-tax state produces permanent annual savings:

| From State | At $500K Income | At $1M Income | At $3M Income |
|------------|----------------|---------------|---------------|
| California | ~$45,000/yr | ~$110,000/yr | ~$370,000/yr |
| New York (+ NYC) | ~$55,000/yr | ~$120,000/yr | ~$380,000/yr |
| New Jersey | ~$40,000/yr | ~$95,000/yr | ~$310,000/yr |

Must establish genuine domicile. High-audit-risk factors: maintaining home in prior state, keeping same doctors/club memberships, children in school in prior state, more days spent in prior state than new state. California is particularly aggressive about departure audits.

## Medicare IRMAA Thresholds and Planning

### 2025 IRMAA Brackets (Based on MAGI from 2 Years Prior -- 2023 Return)

Medicare Part B and Part D premiums increase based on income:

| MAGI (MFJ) | Part B Monthly Surcharge | Part D Monthly Surcharge | Total Annual Extra Cost (Both Spouses) |
|-------------|--------------------------|--------------------------|---------------------------------------|
| Up to $206,000 | $0 (standard premium) | $0 | $0 |
| $206,001-$258,000 | +$70.00 | +$13.70 | ~$2,009 |
| $258,001-$322,000 | +$175.10 | +$35.50 | ~$5,054 |
| $322,001-$386,000 | +$280.10 | +$57.30 | ~$8,098 |
| $386,001-$750,000 | +$385.10 | +$79.00 | ~$11,138 |
| Above $750,000 | +$419.30 | +$85.80 | ~$12,122 |

### IRMAA Planning Strategies

**Two-year lookback**: 2025 premiums are based on 2023 MAGI. Plan two years ahead.

**Life-changing event exception (Form SSA-44)**: If income dropped due to retirement, divorce, death of spouse, work reduction, or loss of income-producing property, you can request IRMAA redetermination based on current year income.

**Roth conversions before age 63**: Convert traditional IRA to Roth before Medicare enrollment (age 65) to reduce future RMDs that inflate MAGI. The conversion itself increases MAGI in the conversion year, but if done before age 63, it will not affect IRMAA premiums (which start at 65 using 2-year lookback).

**Bracket management**: A couple at $210K MAGI pays ~$2,009/year extra. Reducing MAGI by $5K through additional retirement contributions or charitable deductions could save that entire surcharge.

**QCD strategy**: At 70.5+, Qualified Charitable Distributions from IRA reduce MAGI (unlike regular charitable deductions which only reduce taxable income, not AGI).

## Kiddie Tax -- Section 1(g)

### Rules

Children under 19 (or under 24 if full-time students) with unearned income above $2,500 (2025) are taxed at the parent's marginal rate on the excess.

- First $1,250 of unearned income: tax-free (child's standard deduction)
- Next $1,250: taxed at child's rate (10%)
- Above $2,500: taxed at parent's marginal rate (up to 37% + 3.8% NIIT)

### Planning Implications

- Custodial accounts (UTMA/UGMA): investment growth above $2,500 is taxed at parent's rate -- largely eliminates the income-shifting benefit
- 529 plans: growth is tax-free if used for education -- far more efficient than custodial accounts for education savings
- Series I/EE bonds in child's name: interest deferred until redemption; if redeemed when child is no longer subject to kiddie tax, taxed at child's low rate
- Roth IRA for child: if child has earned income (summer job, modeling, family business), contribute to child's Roth IRA -- grows tax-free forever
- Municipal bonds in custodial accounts: interest exempt from kiddie tax (and income tax generally)

## Bunching Strategies

### Concept

In years when total itemized deductions are near the standard deduction ($31,500 MFJ for 2025), alternate between:
- **Bunching year**: Accelerate deductions into one year to substantially exceed standard deduction
- **Off year**: Take standard deduction (no need to maximize deductions)

### Deductions Amenable to Bunching

**Charitable contributions**: Most flexible. Use a Donor-Advised Fund (DAF) to bunch 2-3 years of giving into one year.
- Example: normally give $12,000/year. Instead, contribute $36,000 to DAF every 3 years. Distribute grants from DAF to charities annually.
- Contribute appreciated stock to DAF: avoid capital gains AND get FMV deduction
- Cash to public charities: deductible up to 60% of AGI
- Appreciated property to public charities: deductible up to 30% of AGI

**Medical expenses**: Time elective procedures (LASIK, orthodontics, joint replacement) into a single year to exceed the 7.5% AGI floor.
- At $500K AGI, the floor is $37,500. Medical bunching only works if you have extraordinary expenses.

**Property taxes**: Limited utility when the personal SALT cap is already saturated. Most useful
when total personal SALT is below the cap.

**Mortgage interest**: Not typically bunchable, but making January payment in December pulls one extra month into current year.

### Two-Year Bunching Example (MFJ, $400K AGI)

| Category | Year 1 (Bunch) | Year 2 (Standard) |
|----------|----------------|-------------------|
| Charitable (DAF) | $40,000 | $0 |
| SALT | $20,000 | $20,000 |
| Mortgage interest | $8,000 | $8,000 |
| **Total itemized** | **$68,000** | **$28,000** |
| Deduction used | $68,000 (itemize) | $31,500 (standard) |
| **Two-year total deductions** | **$99,500** | vs $63,000 (standard both years) or $56,000 (itemize both at $28K) |

Net benefit of bunching: roughly $36,500 additional deductions over two years. At a 35% marginal rate, that is about $12,775 of federal tax savings.

## Family Income Splitting Strategies

### Employ Family Members

**Children**: Pay children for legitimate work in the family business. If sole proprietorship or spousal partnership and child is under 18: wages exempt from Social Security and Medicare tax (FICA), and exempt from FUTA. Child can earn up to the single standard deduction ($15,750 for 2025) with no regular income tax, contribute to Roth IRA, and the business deducts the wages.

**S-Corp caveat**: The FICA exemption for children under 18 does NOT apply to S-Corps or C-Corps. Only applies to sole proprietorships and partnerships where both partners are parents.

**Spouse**: Employing a spouse in the business allows them to participate in the business's retirement plan, health plan (potentially deducting family medical expenses as business expense if structured correctly), and dependent care assistance program.

### Family LLC / FLP for Investment Income

Transfer investment assets to a family LLC or Family Limited Partnership (FLP). Gift limited partnership interests to lower-bracket family members over time (using annual exclusion and lifetime exemption). Income is allocated per ownership percentages.

**Constraints**: assignment of income doctrine, kiddie tax (children under 19/24), valuation requirements for gifted interests, and potential gift tax issues. Must have legitimate non-tax business purpose (asset protection, management training, centralized management).

### Trust Structures

- **Grantor trusts** (IDGT, SLAT): income taxed to grantor, but assets are outside estate. No income splitting benefit but powerful estate planning.
- **Non-grantor trusts**: separate taxpayer, but compressed brackets (37% at $15,450 in 2025) make this tax-inefficient for income retention. Better to distribute income to lower-bracket beneficiaries.

## Estimated Tax Penalty Avoidance

### Safe Harbors

| Prior Year AGI | Safe Harbor |
|---------------|-------------|
| $150,000 or less (MFJ) | 100% of prior year tax liability |
| Above $150,000 (MFJ) | 110% of prior year tax liability |
| Alternative | 90% of current year tax liability |

Meet EITHER safe harbor to avoid penalties completely, regardless of balance owed at filing.

### Annualized Income Installment Method (Form 2210, Schedule AI)

For taxpayers with variable income (large Q4 bonus, year-end business income, concentrated capital gains), the standard quarterly payment schedule penalizes front-loading. The annualized method calculates required payments based on income actually earned in each period:
- Period 1: Jan 1 - Mar 31 (payment due Apr 15)
- Period 2: Jan 1 - May 31 (payment due Jun 15)
- Period 3: Jan 1 - Aug 31 (payment due Sep 15)
- Period 4: Jan 1 - Dec 31 (payment due Jan 15)

Particularly valuable when: large capital gains realized in Q4, year-end bonus, business with seasonal income, Roth conversion done in December.

### W-4 vs Estimated Payments

Withholding from wages is treated as paid evenly throughout the year (even if increased in December). Estimated tax payments are credited when actually paid. Strategy for variable income: increase W-4 withholding in Q4 rather than making large Q4 estimated payment -- withholding gets credit as if paid quarterly, potentially avoiding underpayment penalty for earlier quarters.

## Tax Bracket Management

### Roth Conversion Bracket Filling

In low-income years (sabbatical, early retirement, between jobs, business downturn), convert traditional IRA to Roth up to the top of a lower bracket.

Example: MFJ couple with $200K taxable income in a low year. The 24% bracket extends to $394,600. Convert $194,600 from traditional IRA to Roth, paying 24% on the conversion. In normal years, this income would be taxed at 32-37%.

Factor in: state income tax on conversion, NIIT if conversion pushes MAGI above $250K, IRMAA impact if age 63+, impact on ACA premium subsidies if on marketplace plan.

### Capital Gain Harvesting at 0% Rate

The 0% LTCG rate applies up to $96,700 taxable income (MFJ). In years with low ordinary income, sell appreciated assets to recognize gains taxed at 0%.

This primarily benefits: early retirees before Social Security/RMDs begin, taxpayers between jobs, sabbatical years, or years with large business losses offsetting ordinary income.

### Timing of Income Recognition

**Accelerate income** when: you expect to be in a higher bracket next year, tax rates are scheduled to increase (sunset provisions), or you have large deductions/losses to offset.

**Defer income** when: you expect lower income next year (retirement, sabbatical), moving to a no-income-tax state, or large deductions will be available next year.

Methods: defer billing/invoicing (cash basis), installment sales, deferred compensation elections (before year of service), exercising options timing.

## Charitable Strategies for High-Income Taxpayers

### Donor-Advised Fund (DAF)

- Immediate deduction, distribute grants to charities over time
- Contribute appreciated stock: avoid capital gains AND get FMV deduction (if held >1 year)
- Minimum initial contribution: typically $5,000-$25,000 depending on sponsor
- Fund grows tax-free; no required distribution timeline
- Cash: deductible up to 60% AGI; appreciated property: up to 30% AGI

### Qualified Charitable Distribution (QCD)

- Age 70.5+ only: direct IRA distribution to charity, up to $108,000 for 2025
- Excluded from gross income -- reduces AGI (unlike regular charitable deduction which reduces taxable income)
- Satisfies Required Minimum Distribution
- Cannot go to DAF or private foundation
- Particularly valuable for those who take standard deduction (get charitable benefit without itemizing)
- Also reduces MAGI for NIIT, IRMAA, and other AGI-based thresholds

### Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT)

For concentrated appreciated positions ($1M+):
1. Transfer appreciated asset to CRT (no immediate capital gains tax)
2. CRT sells asset and reinvests proceeds (diversification without tax)
3. Income stream to grantor for life or term of years
4. Remainder passes to charity
5. Immediate partial charitable deduction based on present value of remainder

### Private Foundation vs DAF

| Feature | Private Foundation | DAF |
|---------|-------------------|-----|
| Control | Full board control | Advisory only (sponsor has legal control) |
| Required distributions | 5% of assets annually | No minimum |
| Startup cost | $5,000-$25,000 legal fees | $0-$5,000 |
| Annual admin | $5,000-$50,000+ | $0 (fees embedded in fund) |
| Cash deduction limit | 30% AGI | 60% AGI |
| Property deduction limit | 20% AGI (cost basis for non-publicly-traded) | 30% AGI (FMV for publicly traded) |
| Excise tax | 1.39% on investment income | None |
| Public disclosure | Form 990-PF is public | No public disclosure |
| Minimum funding | ~$1M+ to justify costs | $5,000-$25,000 |

## Estate Planning Tax Considerations

### Current Exemptions (2025)

- Federal estate tax exemption: $13,990,000 per person
- Portability: surviving spouse can use deceased spouse's unused exemption (DSUE) -- must file estate return (706) even if not required
- Married couple effective exemption: $27,980,000 (with portability)
- Annual gift tax exclusion: $19,000 per recipient ($38,000 with gift-splitting)
- Generation-skipping transfer (GST) tax exemption: $13,990,000

### TCJA Sunset -- Critical Planning Issue

The doubled exemption is scheduled to revert to approximately $7,000,000 (indexed) after 2025 unless Congress extends it. For taxpayers with estates between $7M and $14M per person, there is a limited window to use the higher exemption through:
- Irrevocable gifts to trusts (SLAT, IDGT, dynasty trust)
- IRS has confirmed: gifts made using the higher exemption will NOT be "clawed back" if the exemption later decreases (anti-clawback rule in Reg 20.2010-1(c))
- GRATs to transfer appreciation out of estate
- Family limited partnership discounted gifts

### Advanced Estate Structures (Brief Overview)

**GRAT (Grantor Retained Annuity Trust)**: Transfer appreciating assets, retain annuity stream. If assets outperform the §7520 rate, excess passes to beneficiaries transfer-tax-free. Zeroed-out GRAT: annuity equals initial value, so gift tax is near zero.

**SLAT (Spousal Lifetime Access Trust)**: Irrevocable trust for benefit of spouse (and descendants). Removes assets from grantor's estate but spouse retains access. Use the higher exemption before sunset. Reciprocal trust doctrine risk: if both spouses create SLATs, must be substantially different.

**IDGT (Intentionally Defective Grantor Trust)**: Grantor pays income tax on trust income (effectively a tax-free gift of the tax payment), but assets are outside the estate. Sale to IDGT: sell appreciated asset to trust for a note; no capital gain recognized (grantor trust is disregarded for income tax); future appreciation is outside the estate.

**Dynasty Trust**: Trust designed to last for multiple generations (or perpetually in states that have abolished the rule against perpetuities: SD, NV, AK, DE, NH). Uses GST exemption to shield distributions from generation-skipping tax. Combined with state trust law advantages (no state income tax in trust situs states like SD, NV, WY, AK).

## Common Mistakes at High Income Levels

1. **Not running AMT calculations proactively** -- ISO exercises can create six-figure unexpected AMT
2. **Ignoring the pro-rata rule on backdoor Roth** -- existing IRA balances create taxable conversion
3. **Missing PTET election deadlines** -- state deadlines are strict and vary
4. **Failing to bunch charitable contributions** -- steady giving when near the standard deduction threshold
5. **Underestimating NIIT impact** -- 3.8% surtax on investment income above $250K is perpetual (not indexed)
6. **Not maximizing all retirement plan options** -- especially Cash Balance plans for high earners over 45
7. **Over-contributing to Roth IRA directly** -- income above threshold requires backdoor method
8. **Missing estate planning sunset** -- TCJA exemption halves after 2025 without Congressional action
9. **S-Corp loss without basis** -- losses suspended at Gate 1 if no stock/debt basis; shareholder guarantees do NOT create basis
10. **Self-rental trap** -- renting to own business recharacterizes income as non-passive but keeps losses passive
11. **Failing to elect activity grouping** -- missing the 469 grouping election that could convert passive to non-passive
12. **Ignoring IRMAA** -- large income event two years before Medicare enrollment permanently increases premiums for that year
13. **Kiddie tax surprise** -- custodial account income above $2,500 taxed at parent's rate
14. **Underpayment penalty with variable income** -- not using annualized installment method when income is concentrated in later quarters
15. **State departure audit failure** -- claiming no-income-tax-state residency while maintaining ties to prior state

## State-Specific Notes

- **California**: Top rate 13.3% on income above $1M; 1% mental health surcharge above $1M; no LTCG preference; aggressive departure audits; PTET available
- **New York**: Top rate 10.9% on income above $25M; NYC adds up to 3.876%; PTET available; state estate tax has $6.94M cliff (exceeding by 5% = entire estate taxable)
- **New Jersey**: Top rate 10.75% above $1M; BAIT (entity tax) available; estate tax with $2M exemption
- **Connecticut**: Top rate 6.99%; mandatory pass-through entity tax; state estate tax with $13.99M exemption
- **Massachusetts**: Flat 5% + 4% surtax on income above $1M (effective 9% above $1M); PTET available
- **Illinois**: Flat 4.95%; PTET available
- **No income tax states**: FL, TX, NV, WY, WA (but 7% capital gains tax above $270K), TN, AK, SD, NH (interest/dividends only through 2026)
- **States with estate taxes below federal**: MA ($2M), OR ($1M), NY ($6.94M cliff), CT ($13.99M), MN ($3M), WA ($2.193M), IL ($4M), VT ($5M), ME ($6.8M), MD ($5M + separate inheritance tax), HI ($5.49M), DC ($4.71M), RI ($1.77M)
