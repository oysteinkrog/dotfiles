**Purpose:** Talk to your family about the plan during your life. Helps prevent the surprise-at-funeral dynamic that often drives the ugliest later fights.

## Pre-Meeting

- [ ] Decide who attends (often: all adult heirs, sometimes their spouses)
- [ ] Decide where (your home? attorney's conference room? neutral location?)
- [ ] Decide format (formal sit-down or casual conversation)
- [ ] Decide whether attorney is present (often helpful for technical questions, but not required)
- [ ] Send a brief invite explaining purpose and what to expect
- [ ] Set duration (90-120 minutes for first meeting)

## Suggested Agenda (90 minutes)

### Opening (10 min)

- Why we're having this conversation
- What I want everyone to take away
- What this is NOT (not a negotiation, not a surprise reveal of dollar amounts)

### Big Picture (15 min)

- My values and what I hope for the family
- The structure I've chosen and why (high-level)
- Who I've named in key roles (executor, trustee, guardian)
- Where the documents are

### Asset Big Picture (20 min)

(You don't need to disclose specific dollar amounts. Discuss structure and intent.)

- The house: [intent — sell? keep? for whom?]
- The business: [intent — sell? family successor? equalization plan?]
- Investment accounts: [general allocation philosophy]
- Real estate: [vacation home plans]
- Charitable intent: [if applicable]

### Specific Concerns (20 min)

If applicable:
- Why blended-family structure (QTIP) protects everyone
- Why one heir's share is in trust (without using stigmatizing language)
- Why an adult child has been deliberately excluded (only if you've decided to discuss this in person)
- Concerns about how the family handles money
- Concerns about communication

### Practical Logistics (15 min)

- Where the documents are stored
- Who has copies
- How to find the attorney
- The "If I die tomorrow" packet
- Pet care plan
- Funeral wishes (high-level)
- Long-term care plan if applicable

### Q&A (15 min)

Open floor. Be prepared for hard questions:
- "Why does X get more than Y?"
- "Why is [vulnerable heir]'s share in trust?"
- "What if you remarry?"
- "What happens to the house?"
- "Have you thought about [scenario]?"

### Closing (5 min)

- Thank them for participating
- Confirm next steps (if any)
- Encourage individual follow-up conversations
- Reaffirm love

## Tone Guidance

- This is a conversation, not a presentation
- Acknowledge that the topic is hard
- Make space for emotion
- Don't rush — let silences happen
- Avoid defensiveness if challenged
- "I understand it's hard to hear..." goes a long way

## Multiple Meetings

For complex situations, consider:
- Meeting 1: spouse alone
- Meeting 2: with children from a prior relationship or the adult children most affected by the plan
- Meeting 3: with stepchildren
- Meeting 4: combined family (only if dynamics permit)

## Follow-Up

- Send a written summary of decisions discussed
- Address any specific questions raised
- Update the plan if family input changes anything
- Schedule next family meeting (often coincides with annual plan review)

## When NOT to Hold a Family Meeting

- Genuinely high conflict where the meeting will inflame rather than resolve
- Estranged family member where contact is unwise
- Vulnerable heir whose presence could destabilize them
- During acute crisis (divorce in progress, recent loss, etc.)

For these cases: write the letter of explanation, but don't force the meeting.

## Common Mistakes

- Surprising heirs with documents they've never heard of
- Using stigmatizing language about vulnerable heirs in their presence
- Discussing specific dollar amounts that create resentment
- Letting one heir dominate
- Not allowing time for emotional reactions
- Failing to follow up after the meeting

## The Goal

The goal is **not consensus**. It's **transparency**. Heirs may not all agree with your choices. But they will accept choices they understand far more readily than choices that feel like ambushes.
