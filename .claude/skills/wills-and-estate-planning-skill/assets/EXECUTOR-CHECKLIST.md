## First 24-72 Hours

- [ ] Secure residence, pets, vehicles, phones, and physical documents
- [ ] Notify the most critical family / fiduciary people per the Letter of Instruction
- [ ] Confirm funeral-home and remains decisions
- [ ] Order death certificates
- [ ] Locate original will, trust, POA, and Letter of Instruction
- [ ] Freeze obvious fraud vectors (mail, email, exposed credit cards, phones)

## Week 1-2

- [ ] Obtain 15 certified death certificate copies (funeral home or vital records)
- [ ] Locate original will + trust documents
- [ ] Secure decedent's residence (change locks if estranged relatives might access)
- [ ] Secure vehicles, safe deposit box, mail
- [ ] Notify Social Security (1-800-772-1213)
- [ ] Notify Medicare/Medicaid if applicable
- [ ] Notify three credit bureaus to flag against ghost fraud
- [ ] Forward mail to executor's address
- [ ] Take pets — arrange care
- [ ] Notify employer, pension administrator
- [ ] Notify close family per Letter of Instruction
- [ ] Pay funeral from decedent's funds if possible (becomes estate expense)
- [ ] Begin contacting attorney listed in Letter of Instruction

## First 30 Days

- [ ] File will with probate court in decedent's county
- [ ] Petition for letters testamentary (or "letters of administration" if no will)
- [ ] Attend first probate hearing
- [ ] Open estate checking account (need EIN from IRS — apply online, free)
- [ ] Notify named beneficiaries
- [ ] Begin notifying known creditors
- [ ] Publish creditor notice in local paper if state requires
- [ ] Cancel: cell phone, streaming, gym, credit cards, subscriptions, utilities (or transfer)
- [ ] Inventory assets — coordinate with appraiser
- [ ] Confirm date-of-death valuations for all assets

## If The Decedent Owned An Operating Business

- [ ] Identify who can run payroll and approve wires immediately
- [ ] Secure accounting, payroll, banking, and email access
- [ ] Review buy-sell / operating / shareholder agreements
- [ ] Coordinate with business counsel and CPA before making ownership representations
- [ ] Preserve customer, vendor, and employee confidence

## Months 2-4

- [ ] Notify banks, brokerages, insurance, retirement plan administrators
- [ ] Direct beneficiary-designated assets to named beneficiaries
- [ ] Coordinate margin debt payoff if applicable
- [ ] File final personal income tax returns (Form 1040) for year of death
- [ ] Begin fiduciary income tax (Form 1041) for estate
- [ ] Engage CPA for Form 706 if estate exceeds federal exemption OR if portability election
- [ ] Document every transaction in estate ledger

## Months 4-9

- [ ] Resolve creditor claims (priority order)
- [ ] Sell assets as needed for liquidity
- [ ] Continue managing ongoing assets (rents, real estate maintenance)
- [ ] File Form 706 (federal estate tax) — due 9 months after death; 6-month extension available
- [ ] **Evaluate portability election** even if no tax is otherwise due; if DSUE preservation matters, timely Form 706 filing is required
- [ ] Allocate GST exemption if applicable
- [ ] Consider qualified disclaimer window (also 9 months)
- [ ] Consider §6166 election for closely held business
- [ ] Consider §754 election for partnership interests
- [ ] If Form 8971 applies, file it on the current timetable: generally 30 days after the earlier of the Form 706 due date (including extensions) or the actual filing date

## Months 9-18+

- [ ] Make interim distributions to beneficiaries (with attorney guidance)
- [ ] If Form 706 was filed, determine whether an IRS estate tax closing letter or account transcript is needed; handle state clearance separately
- [ ] Prepare final accounting
- [ ] Obtain beneficiary receipts and releases
- [ ] Make final distributions
- [ ] Petition court for executor discharge
- [ ] Close estate account
- [ ] File final fiduciary income tax return

## Critical Don'ts

- Don't distribute before debts and taxes paid (personal liability)
- Don't commingle estate and personal funds
- Don't miss federal tax deadline (9 months)
- Don't overlook portability election
- Don't make casual promises to beneficiaries
- Don't undervalue assets to reduce estate tax (basis consequences)
- Don't pay illegal claims to avoid family conflict
- Don't take "advances" against expected fees

## Document Everything

Every decision, every transaction, every communication. The executor's defense against future claims is the paper trail.
