# Durable Financial Power of Attorney

The legal document that authorizes someone to manage your money if you're incapacitated. Without it, families often end up seeking a conservatorship or similar court-supervised substitute — slow, expensive, public, and often unwanted.

## Why It Is a Strong Default for Most Adults

- Stroke, dementia, severe mental illness, coma, terminal illness — any of these can leave you unable to manage finances long before death
- No POA = court-appointed conservator (3-12 months, $5K-$50K, ongoing court oversight)
- POA can be activated immediately or upon a defined trigger
- Costs $200-$1,000 to draft; saves tens of thousands in conservatorship costs

## Key Drafting Decisions

### Springing vs. Immediate (Critical Choice)

**Springing POA**: takes effect ONLY upon incapacity (typically certified by 1-2 physicians).

- Sounds prudent but is often a brittle default in practice
- Banks reject ("we don't recognize this; doctor's letter is too old")
- Doctors won't certify (liability concern, no clear standard)
- Agent paralyzed when most needed
- Often the wrong default unless the abuse-risk facts, state law, and the actual institutions involved justify the extra friction

**Immediate POA**: takes effect on signing.

- Agent can act immediately (you must trust them)
- Banks accept readily
- No certification logistics
- Risk: agent could act prematurely (mitigation: choose trusted agent)
- Often the practical default when the agent is highly trusted and the priority is smooth usability during a real crisis

### Powers to Include

State statutory POA forms typically have a checklist. **Do not reflexively check every box.** Map powers to the user's actual assets, institutions, likely incapacity scenarios, and abuse risk. Powers often worth considering include:

- Real property transactions (buying, selling, renting, encumbering)
- Tangible personal property
- Banking and financial transactions (open accounts, withdraw, sign checks)
- Investment management (buy/sell securities, manage brokerage)
- Business operations (continue business, sign contracts)
- Insurance transactions (buy, change, claim)
- Estate planning (CRITICAL: see "gifting authority" below)
- Tax matters (file returns, sign 2848, deal with IRS)
- Government benefits (Social Security, Medicare, VA)
- Retirement accounts (rollover, distributions, possibly beneficiary changes — controversial; verify state and plan rules)
- Healthcare expenses (pay bills; doesn't authorize medical decisions — that's separate POA)
- Litigation (initiate, defend, settle)
- Personal and family maintenance
- Digital assets (RUFADAA authorization)

**Omitting a power** means the agent may be blocked. But over-granting power can create self-dealing, elder-abuse, or family-conflict risk. Red-flag powers that deserve explicit discussion instead of boilerplate include:

- gifting authority
- beneficiary-designation changes
- trust creation, amendment, decanting, or funding powers
- survivorship / TOD / deed changes
- delegation to third parties
- any power that lets the agent benefit themselves directly

### Gifting Authority (CRITICAL)

Without explicit gifting authority, the agent typically CANNOT continue an annual gifting program during incapacity — significant planning loss in some estates.

**Do not copy a gifting clause blindly.** If gifting power is appropriate, draft parameters on purpose:
- Annual exclusion gifting to any descendants (or specified persons)
- Direct payment of medical/educational expenses
- Charitable gifts up to specified amount
- Gifts to spouse for tax purposes
- Whether self-gifts are prohibited or narrowly authorized
- Whether beneficiary changes, trust funding, or entity transactions need separate limits

### Successor Agents (Strong Default)

- Primary agent
- First successor (if primary unavailable)
- Second successor

Agents can die, become incompetent, resign, or themselves become inappropriate. Without successors, court appoints conservator anyway.

### Joint or Several

- **Acting jointly**: two agents must agree (more checks; can deadlock)
- **Acting severally**: either agent can act alone (more flexibility; more risk)
- Many plans prefer several authority, but joint authority can be deliberate in high-abuse-risk or high-conflict families

### Compensation

- Agent typically entitled to "reasonable compensation"
- Family-member agents often waive
- Document the choice

### Bank Acceptance Issues

Many banks refuse POAs they consider "stale" (more than a few years old) or that aren't on their specific form.

**Mitigations:**
- Refresh POA every 3-5 years
- Notify banks in advance and provide POA on file
- Some states have "good faith reliance" statutes requiring banks to accept properly executed POAs (penalties for refusal)

## Execution Formalities

- Signed by principal
- Notarized
- Some states require witnesses (1-2)
- Some states require specific statutory form

Verify state requirements; don't use generic online forms without confirming validity.

## Coordination With Other Documents

- **Healthcare POA**: separate document for medical decisions
- **Living will**: end-of-life preferences
- **HIPAA authorization**: medical records access
- **Revocable trust**: successor trustee handles trust assets (POA is for non-trust assets)

The POA + healthcare POA + HIPAA + revocable trust + living will = comprehensive incapacity package.

## What POA Does NOT Cover

- Healthcare decisions (need healthcare POA)
- HIPAA medical records (need HIPAA authorization)
- Trust assets (need successor trustee)
- Decisions after death (need will/executor)
- Decisions during incapacity if POA is springing and trigger fails

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Springing POA that requires physician certification** — agent paralyzed
2. **No gifting authority** — annual exclusion program halts
3. **No successor agents named** — primary dies, court appoints conservator
4. **Stale POA** — banks reject
5. **Wrong state's form** — invalidated by court
6. **Non-statutory powers omitted** — agent can't act in critical situations
7. **Agent has incentives adverse to principal** — abuse risk
8. **POA used as if it gave healthcare authority** — different document needed
9. **Joint agents deadlock** — court must intervene
10. **No RUFADAA authorization** — digital assets locked

## State-Specific Notes

- Many states use modern POA statutes influenced by uniform-act concepts, but do not assume a state adopted the Uniform Power of Attorney Act verbatim.
- States with especially important state-specific formality or form issues include NY and CA; FL and TX also have their own detailed statutory frameworks and acceptance quirks
- Verify state-specific execution requirements
