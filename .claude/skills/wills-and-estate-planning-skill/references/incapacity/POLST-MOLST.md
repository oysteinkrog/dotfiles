# POLST/MOLST (Physician/Medical Orders for Life-Sustaining Treatment)

A **medical order**, not a planning document. For seriously ill or significantly frail patients, it translates advance-directive wishes into actionable physician orders that EMTs and hospital staff can often follow in real time when the order is valid, available, and recognized.

## What POLST Is (and Isn't)

### POLST IS
- An **actionable physician order** signed by a physician
- Bright-colored paper (often pink, green, or fluorescent yellow depending on state)
- Travels with the patient to ER, ICU, nursing home
- Specifies: CPR / no CPR; comfort vs. aggressive treatment; antibiotics; artificial nutrition
- Often honored by EMTs and hospital staff in real time when valid, available, and recognized under local practice

### POLST IS NOT
- A planning document for healthy people (use living will / advance directive instead)
- A replacement for advance directive (use both for seriously ill patients)
- A general expression of preferences (it's specific orders)
- A set-it-and-forget-it document (it should be reviewed periodically)

## When to Get One

Common heuristic: physicians ask "Would you be surprised if this patient died within a year?" If no, POLST may be appropriate. But the operational question is whether the patient needs current bedside medical orders, not whether a single prognosis formula is met.

Specific scenarios:
- Advanced cancer
- End-stage heart, lung, kidney, or liver disease
- Late-stage dementia
- Frailty with significant decline
- Hospice eligibility
- Multiple advanced chronic conditions

**Not for healthy patients** of any age — POLST is for end-of-life context.

## Standard POLST Sections

### Section A: CPR / Resuscitation
- "Attempt CPR" (full code)
- "Do Not Attempt Resuscitation" (DNAR/DNR)

### Section B: Medical Interventions
- "Full Treatment" (all life-sustaining treatments including ICU)
- "Selective Treatment" (treat with antibiotics, IV fluids; no intubation/ICU)
- "Comfort-Focused Treatment" (relief from pain only; no aggressive treatment)

### Section C: Artificial Nutrition
- Long-term feeding tube
- Trial period
- No artificial nutrition

### Section D: Authorized Decision-Maker
- Patient (if has capacity)
- Healthcare agent
- Surrogate per state hierarchy

## How to Get One

1. **Discuss with primary care physician** or hospital palliative care team
2. **Complete the form together** — you (or healthcare agent) plus physician
3. **Both sign** — physician signature makes it an order
4. **Distribute copies**:
   - Patient (carry with them)
   - Refrigerator (EMTs check there)
   - Primary care physician (chart)
   - Hospital (when admitted)
   - Healthcare agent
   - Nursing home (if applicable)
5. **Review periodically** — annually, on hospitalization, on condition change

## Coordination With Other Documents

POLST is the **operationalization** of the living will for current illness:

- **Living will** = standing wishes for hypothetical future scenarios
- **POLST** = current orders for current illness; in practice bedside teams often follow it over more general advance-directive language while it remains valid and applicable
- **Healthcare POA** = designates who decides; can sign POLST on patient's behalf

A patient with terminal cancer should have:
- Healthcare POA (designating agent)
- Living will (general wishes)
- POLST (specific current orders)
- HIPAA authorization

## Common Issues

### POLST Goes Missing
- EMTs need it visible (refrigerator, wallet, bedside)
- If no visible order is available, emergency teams may treat under ordinary emergency protocols
- Mitigation: copies in multiple locations + medical alert bracelet

### POLST Inconsistent With Living Will
- For current illness, the bedside team often follows the POLST order set while it remains valid and applicable
- But contradiction may cause confusion at the bedside
- Update both consistently

### POLST Made Too Early
- A 70-year-old in good health doesn't need POLST
- Premature POLST can cause undertreatment
- Use only when seriously ill

### POLST Made Without Patient Capacity
- Healthcare agent can sign POLST on patient's behalf
- But agent must follow patient's known wishes (from living will, prior conversations)
- Document the basis for each decision

### POLST Honored in Different State
- Many states honor out-of-state POLSTs
- Some require new in-state POLST
- Verify before relocation

## Common Failure Modes

1. **POLST in healthy patient** — premature; default scenarios may be wrong
2. **POLST not accessible** at hospital admission — providers may treat under ordinary emergency protocols until an order is located
3. **POLST not updated** when condition or wishes change
4. **POLST without underlying advance directive** — no context for unusual scenarios
5. **POLST signed by physician without conversation with patient/agent** — not patient-centered
6. **DNR misunderstood** as "do not treat" (it means "do not resuscitate"; treatment continues)
7. **POLST destroyed accidentally** during home cleaning
8. **Family conflict at bedside** can still create delay or confusion even with a POLST — documentation and care-team communication matter
