# Psychiatric Advance Directives and Ulysses Clauses

For people with serious mental illness (bipolar disorder, schizophrenia, severe depression, PTSD with dissociation) who have experienced episodes requiring hospitalization or involuntary treatment.

## What a Psychiatric Advance Directive Does

While competent, you specify in advance:
- Hospitals and treatment providers preferred or refused
- Medications that have worked or failed, with dosing preferences
- People authorized (and not authorized) to be contacted during a crisis
- Consent to treatment during a future episode (the **Ulysses clause**)

When in crisis, the document guides treatment teams and family — and may be legally enforceable.

## The Ulysses Clause

Named after Odysseus binding himself to the mast to resist the Sirens.

> "When I am well, I direct that during future episodes I be admitted even over my objection and given [specific treatments/medications], for a period not to exceed [X] days, with the consent of [my designated agent]."

Effect: pre-commits the person to treatment during a future episode, even if the person in crisis objects.

### Enforceability Varies

- Some states (NY, OR, MA, others) have specific psychiatric advance directive statutes
- Others rely on general advance directive law
- Federal: Patient Self-Determination Act recognizes them
- Practical: even where not strictly enforceable, they provide powerful guidance to treatment teams

## When to Create

**Strong indicators:**
- History of psychiatric hospitalization
- Bipolar I or II with documented manic episodes
- Schizophrenia or schizoaffective disorder
- Severe depression with prior suicide attempts
- PTSD with dissociative episodes
- Postpartum psychosis history (planning future pregnancies)

**Best timing:** when stable, not during or immediately after an episode.

## Drafting Components

### Treatment Preferences
- Specific medications that have worked (with dosages)
- Medications that have failed or caused side effects (avoid)
- Specific therapies preferred (CBT, DBT, ECT consent/refusal)
- Hospitalization vs. intensive outpatient preferences

### Provider Preferences
- Treating psychiatrist (current name)
- Preferred hospital(s)
- Insurance information for verification
- Refused providers (if any)

### Contact Preferences
- People to notify during crisis
- People NOT to contact (often estranged family)
- Healthcare agent (specific to psychiatric decisions, may differ from general healthcare agent)

### Crisis Recognition
- Early-warning signs you've identified ("if I'm doing X, that's the start")
- Authorization for designated person to initiate evaluation
- Specific behaviors that indicate need for hospitalization

### Ulysses Clause (Optional)
- Specific consent in advance to treatment during future episodes
- Time limits
- Required involvement of designated agent
- Specific treatments allowed even over objection

## Coordination With Other Documents

- **Healthcare POA** typically covers psychiatric decisions; specify whether designated psychiatric agent differs from general healthcare agent
- **Living will** generally covers end-of-life; psychiatric directive covers psychiatric crisis
- **Trust planning**: self-settled special-purpose trust can become irrevocable on evidence of episode (financial parallel)

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Created during episode** — capacity questioned later
2. **Not shared with treatment team** — providers don't know it exists
3. **Designated agent not informed** — agent doesn't know role
4. **Stale document** — preferences out of date
5. **Refused provider list outdated** — recommended provider listed instead of refused
6. **Ulysses clause too broad** — invalidated as overreaching
7. **No coordination with insurance** — preferred provider out of network
8. **Family conflict** — family overrides directive at crisis
