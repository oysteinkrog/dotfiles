# Medicaid Planning

Medicaid pays for long-term care (nursing home, in some states home care) for those who qualify. Qualification requires low income AND low countable assets. Medicaid planning legally restructures assets to qualify while preserving wealth for spouse and heirs.

## Who Needs Medicaid Planning

- Net worth under $1M and expected need for long-term care
- Wealthy couples where one spouse needs nursing care (preserves community spouse's assets)
- Families wanting to leave a legacy despite catastrophic care costs
- Parents with disabled adult children (preserves child's benefits)

Not needed for:
- Net worth >$3M (self-funding typically preferred unless family legacy a priority)
- No projected need for institutional care

## Eligibility Basics (2026, varies by state)

### Income Cap (Nursing Home Medicaid)
- Monthly income cap: ~$2,982/month in 2026 for the standard 300%-of-SSI framework (varies by program and state)
- "Income Cap states" (e.g., TX, FL) use Qualified Income Trust (Miller Trust) to qualify above cap
- "Medically Needy states" allow spend-down of income to eligibility level

### Countable Asset Limit
- **Individual:** $2,000 in countable assets (some states $3,000 or $4,000)
- **Community spouse resource allowance (CSRA):** up to **$162,660** in 2026, with a federal minimum of **$32,532**
- **Home equity exemption:** roughly **$752,000 to $1,130,000** in 2026, depending on the state standard adopted

### Countable vs. Exempt Assets

**Countable** (count toward limit):
- Cash, checking, savings
- CDs, stocks, bonds, mutual funds
- Second home, vacation home
- Investment real estate
- Cash value life insurance (some)
- Retirement accounts (state-dependent — exempt in some states if in payout status)

**Exempt** (don't count):
- Primary residence (subject to equity cap)
- One vehicle (no value limit in most states)
- Personal belongings, household furnishings
- Irrevocable burial trust (up to ~$15K)
- Term life insurance (no cash value)
- Prepaid funeral plan (irrevocable)

## The Transfer Lookback (Often 5 Years, but Verify State Exceptions)

In many states, when you apply for Medicaid they review the past **5 years (60 months)** of asset transfers. Gifts and below-market transfers during the lookback period create a **penalty period** of Medicaid ineligibility. But do not universalize that rule without checking the state-specific long-term-care program; some states now use materially different transfer-review frameworks.

### Penalty Calculation
- Divide amount transferred by state's average monthly nursing home cost
- Result = months of ineligibility, starting from application date (not transfer date)

Example: Transferred $240K in 2024, apply in 2026, state average cost $12K/month:
- Penalty: $240K / $12K = 20 months of ineligibility
- Family pays for 20 months of care before Medicaid kicks in

### Exempt Transfers (NO Penalty)

- To spouse (unlimited)
- To disabled child (any age, any amount)
- To child under 21 (any amount)
- To caretaker child who lived in home 2+ years providing care that delayed institutionalization
- To sibling with equity interest in home who lived there 1+ year
- Transfers for "purpose other than to qualify for Medicaid" (high burden of proof)

## Core Medicaid-Planning Strategies

### Strategy 1: Early Gifting (For States Using the Traditional 5-Year Plan)

Transfer assets to children 5+ years before applying for Medicaid. Under the traditional 60-month framework, transfers outside the lookback generally are not penalized.

**Pros:**
- Full protection after 5 years
- Simple
- Children control the funds, which can be helpful or dangerous depending on the family

**Cons:**
- Lose control during 5 years (and after)
- Children's divorce/creditor exposes transferred assets
- Lose step-up in basis (kids inherit parent's basis; capital-gains tax on sale)
- If need care within 5 years, major penalty
- Kids may use funds (or refuse to give back)

### Strategy 2: Medicaid Asset Protection Trust (MAPT)

Irrevocable trust that owns assets. Parent retains income interest but no access to principal.

**Structure:**
- Parent transfers assets to irrevocable trust
- Child (or trusted person) is trustee
- Parent retains right to income (interest, dividends, rental income)
- Principal distributions only to beneficiaries (not parent)
- Upon death, principal passes to beneficiaries

**Pros:**
- After 5-year lookback, trust assets not counted
- Retains income for parent during life
- Protects from children's creditors/divorces
- May preserve step-up in basis (with careful drafting — grantor trust status)
- Parent can live in house if trust owns residence

**Cons:**
- Irrevocable (can't undo)
- Works best when funded well before application under a state's traditional 60-month lookback; verify newer state exceptions and implementation quirks
- Trustee must cooperate
- Setup cost ($3K-$8K)

### Strategy 3: Spousal Protections (Community Spouse)

When one spouse in nursing home and one at home ("institutionalized" vs. "community" spouse):

**Snapshot Date:** Date institutionalized spouse enters facility. Assets counted as of that date.

**Division:**
- Community spouse keeps CSRA: federal minimum **$32,532** / federal maximum **$162,660** for 2026, subject to state implementation
- Half of total countable assets up to max
- Some states use more spouse-friendly approaches; New York is the classic example, but its own DOH materials still describe the allowance as a greater-of-minimum-or-half-share-up-to-maximum framework rather than a literal "always full CSRA" rule

**Income:**
- Minimum Monthly Maintenance Needs Allowance (MMMNA): **$2,643.75** minimum / **$4,066.50** maximum in 2026, with housing adjustments
- If community spouse's income below MMMNA, can take from institutionalized spouse's income
- Can even increase CSRA via fair hearing to generate required income

**Spousal Refusal / related state-specific tactics:**
- In New York, spousal-refusal-style planning can preserve Medicaid eligibility in some cases, but it is not just a one-page magic form
- Information-sharing rules, assignment-of-support mechanics, and later contribution / recovery actions still matter
- New York is the classic reference point, and any other state should be verified before it is presented as available

### Strategy 4: Annuities (Half-a-Loaf)

**"Half-a-Loaf"** (sophisticated):
- Transfer half of assets to children (creates partial penalty)
- Other half into Medicaid-compliant immediate annuity
- Annuity income pays nursing home during penalty period
- If structured correctly, the annuity can fund the penalty period while the transferred portion is not treated the same way as an outright countable reserve; bad drafting or the wrong annuity product can destroy the strategy

**Medicaid-Compliant Annuity requirements:**
- Not every annuity is even in the risky bucket; some retirement-account annuities are treated differently under the federal statute
- Irrevocable
- Non-assignable
- Actuarially sound (pays out within life expectancy)
- Pays in equal amounts with no deferral or balloon payment
- State beneficiary designation has position rules: usually first position for the institutionalized individual, or second position behind a community spouse / minor / disabled child with a step-up-to-first-position rule if that protected remainder is disposed of below fair market value

### Strategy 5: Personal Care Contract

Parent pays adult child (or others) for legitimate caregiving services under contract.

**Requirements:**
- Written contract BEFORE services provided
- Market-rate hourly (comparable to home health aide)
- Caregiver reports income on taxes
- Services documented (time logs)

**Effect:**
- Converts inheritance to compensated services (legitimate)
- Not treated as uncompensated transfer
- But: child pays income tax on wages

### Strategy 6: Spending Down

Legitimately spend assets on:
- Home improvements (new roof, HVAC, accessibility modifications)
- New car (for community spouse)
- Prepaid funeral (irrevocable)
- Paying off mortgages and debts
- Medical/dental work
- Hearing aids, dental implants, medical equipment

Exempt assets grow, countable assets shrink.

### Strategy 7: Spousal Transfer + New Will

Institutionalized spouse transfers assets to community spouse (unlimited spousal transfer). Community spouse then executes new will leaving assets to children, not back to institutionalized spouse.

This works because if community spouse dies first, institutionalized spouse could inherit back to Medicaid disqualification. New will prevents that.

**Complication:** Elective-share or augmented-estate law. In some states, the institutionalized spouse may retain a statutory claim against the community spouse's estate. If that claim is not waived or planned around, Medicaid may treat the failure to pursue it as an available resource or transfer issue.

**Mitigation:** Elective share waiver (via prenuptial or postnuptial) or structuring community spouse's estate to bypass elective share.

### Strategy 8: Reverse Half-a-Loaf

Transfer all assets to children → apply for Medicaid → get penalty period → children gift back enough to cover penalty period → apply again at end.

More aggressive; state approaches vary.

## Medicaid Estate Recovery (MERP)

After the Medicaid recipient dies, state estate recovery may apply. This is a real risk in many plans, but the scope of recoverable assets varies sharply by state and by whether the state uses a narrow probate-estate definition or a broader recovery regime.

### Subject to Recovery
- Assets in probate estate (passing through will)
- In some states: all "estate" assets, including non-probate (joint accounts, life estates, revocable trusts) — see "expanded recovery"
- Typically from surviving spouse's estate (deferred until spouse dies)

### Exempt from Recovery
- Surviving spouse alive (deferred)
- Surviving child under 21
- Disabled child (any age)
- Hardship waiver (heir needs home to live in; substantial hardship)

### Avoiding MERP

- MAPT (assets not in estate)
- In some states, enhanced-life-estate or other carefully structured deed approaches can keep the home outside the recoverable estate, but the state-specific recovery definition must be verified before relying on that result
- Transfer home to caretaker child (exempt transfer)
- Irrevocable burial trust (exempt)
- Move to state with limited MERP

## Medicaid Planning Timeline

### 10+ Years Before Need
- Start considering MAPT
- Begin gifting program (if financially feasible)
- Purchase LTC insurance (eliminates need for Medicaid)

### 5+ Years Before Need (Ideal)
- Create and fund MAPT
- Transfer home using the state-appropriate deed / trust structure
- Document all transfers carefully
- Revise estate plan to coordinate

### 3-5 Years Before Need (Possible)
- Accelerated gifting (but penalty risk)
- "Half-a-loaf" or reverse half-a-loaf
- Asset conversion (countable → exempt)

### Crisis Planning (<1 Year Before)
- Focus on exempt assets and spend-down
- Spousal protections
- Personal care contract
- Annuity conversion
- Immediate disqualification expected; plan to cover penalty

## State Variation

Medicaid is federal-state partnership; significant state variation:

### More-Generous States
- **California (Medi-Cal):** rules changed materially again in 2026; DHCS now says asset limits returned for certain Non-MAGI populations and LTC transfer-review / penalty rules are back, but phased in rather than fully switching on all at once. Verify current DHCS guidance before relying on older "no asset test / no lookback" summaries
- Some states administer community-spouse protections more generously than the federal floor, but verify the live state manual instead of repeating planner folklore
- **New York:** comparatively favorable spousal-impoverishment and spousal-refusal terrain, but do not collapse it into an "always full max CSRA" shortcut

### Less-Generous States
- **Texas, Florida:** income cap states (Miller Trust needed)
- **States with expanded MERP:** aggressive estate recovery
- **Annuity state-beneficiary rules:** the federal Medicaid annuity framework makes this a nationwide baseline issue, but state manuals, forms, and enforcement posture still vary

### Spousal Refusal and Similar Doctrines
- New York is the clearest and best-known example
- Do not assume a similar strategy is available elsewhere without live elder-law verification

## Coordination With Other Planning

- **Estate plan:** must coordinate MAPT with overall plan
- **Special-needs planning:** if beneficiary disabled, coordinate SNT
- **Tax planning:** gifts may use gift tax exemption (rarely triggers tax under $15M exemption)
- **Retirement accounts:** exempt in some states if in RMD status; not in others
- **Life insurance:** surrender cash-value policies to reduce countable assets

## Ethical and Moral Considerations

Medicaid planning is legal but politically controversial. Arguments:

**Pro:** Middle-class families spending $100K+/year on care deplete everything; family legacy eliminated; Medicaid is the safety net.

**Con:** Medicaid is means-tested public benefit; planning diverts resources from those truly in need.

Legal. Each family decides own ethics.

## Who Should NOT Plan for Medicaid

- Wealthy families (self-fund)
- Those with good LTC insurance
- Those without 5-year horizon and healthy now (can't plan ahead)
- Those with no heirs to benefit (only benefits the individual, not worthwhile vs. self-pay)

## Professional Help Required

Medicaid planning is **highly state-specific** and **frequently changing**. DIY fails routinely.

- **Certified Elder Law Attorney (CELA)** — highest credential
- **NAELA membership** — National Academy of Elder Law Attorneys
- **State Bar Elder Law Section**
- **Geriatric care manager** — coordinates with attorney

Cost: $3K-$15K for comprehensive planning; $10K-$50K+ for crisis planning.

## Common Failure Modes

1. **5-year lookback ignored** — transfers penalize when care needed
2. **Revocable trust thought to protect** — revocable trust assets are countable
3. **Parent gives house to child directly** — creditor exposure, tax basis lost, child divorce
4. **Personal care contract without documentation** — treated as uncompensated transfer
5. **Community spouse doesn't update will** — asset returns to institutionalized spouse at death
6. **Income above cap in cap state** — need Miller Trust
7. **Ignoring MERP** — heir thinks they'll inherit house; state claims it
8. **DIY MAPT** — drafting errors render it revocable in state's view
9. **Transferring assets to spouse, then spouse dies first** — assets return to institutionalized spouse
10. **Annuity not Medicaid-compliant** — treated as transfer, penalty
11. **Moving states mid-planning** — different rules disrupt strategy
12. **Wrong asset used for spend-down** — spend countable (not exempt) assets
