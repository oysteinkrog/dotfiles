# Long-Term Care Planning

ACL says someone turning age 65 today has **almost a 70% chance** of needing some form of long-term care support, and Medicare generally does **not** pay for most long-term care services. Planning for this is central to protecting a household and an estate.

Treat the cost figures below as directional planning ranges, not quoting-grade numbers. Local labor markets, facility type, and level of care matter more than any generic national average.

## The Coverage Gap

### What Medicare Covers
- Acute care (hospitalization)
- Post-hospitalization skilled nursing (up to 100 days, with co-pays after day 20)
- Home health for skilled nursing needs (limited)
- Hospice for terminal illness

### What Medicare Does NOT Cover
- Custodial care (help with bathing, dressing, eating, toileting) — the main cost
- Long-term nursing home care (beyond the 100-day skilled period)
- Assisted living
- Most home care (if it's custodial rather than skilled)

### Recent National Cost Baseline

| Setting | Monthly | Annual |
|---------|---------|--------|
| In-home care / home health aide (44 hrs/wk) | ~$6,500 | ~$77.8K |
| Assisted living (private) | ~$5,900 | ~$70.8K |
| Memory care | Often assisted-living baseline plus a meaningful dementia-care premium | Varies widely |
| Nursing home (semi-private) | ~$9,300 | ~$111.3K |
| Nursing home (private room) | ~$10,650 | ~$127.8K |

Use local quotes before finalizing any recommendation. HCOL markets can run materially above these nationwide medians.

### Duration
- ACL's baseline: about one-third of today's 65-year-olds may never need long-term care support, but about 20% will need it for more than 5 years
- Average duration of needing any long-term care is about 3 years overall; ACL's planning figures are roughly 3.7 years for women and 2.2 years for men
- Alzheimer's/dementia cases often run materially longer than the headline averages

## Four Funding Strategies

### 1. Self-Funding

Pay out of savings/portfolio. Best for:
- Families whose balance sheet can absorb a multi-year care burn without destabilizing the survivor
- Situations where preserving optionality matters more than paying premiums forever
- Households willing to earmark real liquidity rather than assuming illiquid wealth solves the problem

**Risk:** depletes estate; surviving spouse impoverished.

### 2. Long-Term Care Insurance (Traditional)

Traditional LTCi: monthly premium, benefits when qualifying event (can't do 2+ activities of daily living, or cognitive impairment).

**Pros:**
- Leverages premium dollars into benefits
- Usually includes home care, assisted living, nursing home

**Cons:**
- Premiums can increase (regulated rate hikes have happened repeatedly)
- "Use it or lose it" — if you don't need care, you've paid for nothing
- Underwriting strict — many people declined after age 65
- Industry has struggled; few traditional carriers remain

**Common shopping window:** often the 50s to early 60s. Earlier can be expensive deadweight; later can mean tougher underwriting and materially higher premiums.

### 3. Hybrid / Life Insurance with LTC Rider

A growing alternative. You buy permanent life insurance with long-term care rider.

**Structure:**
- Single premium (e.g., $100K) or annual premium
- Death benefit (e.g., $200K) payable if you don't need care
- LTC benefit (e.g., $400K pool, 200% of death benefit) accessible if you need care

**Pros:**
- No "use it or lose it" — heirs get death benefit if unused
- Many products market level or contractually fixed premiums, but verify the actual policy terms, rider charges, and lapse assumptions before presenting that as a feature
- Tax-advantaged (LTC benefits tax-free; death benefit income-tax-free)
- Easier underwriting than traditional LTCi

**Cons:**
- Opportunity cost of premium capital
- Benefit ratio less generous than pure LTCi (you're paying for insurance + life)
- Complex products — shop carefully

Market leaders and product names change. Use a current broker comparison rather than hard-coding a specific carrier list into the plan.

### 4. Medicaid Planning

Medicaid covers long-term care for those who qualify (low income + low assets). Most nursing home residents eventually on Medicaid. See MEDICAID-PLANNING.md for detailed strategies.

- Involves divesting assets under 5-year lookback
- Protects assets for spouse and heirs
- Requires careful 5+ year advance planning

## Choosing a Strategy by Wealth Tier

| Tier | Useful starting posture |
|------|---------------------|
| Under $500K net worth | Benefits-readiness, caregiver planning, and crisis-proof paperwork usually matter more than product complexity |
| $500K-$2M | Often a mix of self-funding, insurance evaluation, and Medicaid-backup planning; spouse needs and legacy goals decide the mix |
| $2M-$10M | Frequently a self-funding discussion, but insurance can still be useful for spouse protection, behavioral discipline, or equalization |
| $10M+ | Usually a flexibility / liquidity / family-governance question rather than a pure affordability question |

## Care Setting Preferences

### Aging in Place (most common preference)
- Home modifications (grab bars, ramps, stairlifts, walk-in showers): $5K-$50K
- Home care agency or private-hire caregivers
- Technology: medical alert, fall detection, remote monitoring
- Family coordination (often an unpaid family member becomes primary caregiver)

### Continuing Care Retirement Community (CCRC)
- Entrance fee ($250K-$1M+) plus monthly fee ($3K-$8K)
- Three levels: independent, assisted, skilled nursing (on one campus)
- Predictability of care; residents move between levels as needed
- Risk: entrance fee refund policies vary; financial health of facility matters

### Assisted Living
- For those who need help with some ADLs but not 24/7 skilled nursing
- $5K-$10K/month typically
- Medicaid / waiver coverage varies widely; do not assume either "never covered" or "easily covered"

### Nursing Home (Skilled Nursing Facility)
- 24/7 medical supervision
- Most expensive
- Medicaid accepted in most facilities

### Memory Care (specialized dementia facilities)
- Higher cost than general assisted living
- Secured units, specialized training, structured programming

## Family Caregiving

Often a family member (typically an adult daughter) becomes primary caregiver. Key considerations:

- **Compensation:** Use "personal care contract" to formally pay a family caregiver. Documents the services and transfer (avoiding Medicaid penalty later) and lets the caregiver earn wages.
- **Respite care:** Caregivers burn out. Budget for professional respite.
- **Lost wages:** If caregiver leaves job, impacts their Social Security, retirement. Consider family compensation.
- **Sibling coordination:** One child often becomes primary caregiver while siblings contribute financially. Document the arrangement to avoid estate disputes.

## Planning Timeline

### 10+ Years Before Need
- Evaluate long-term care insurance (best if bought early)
- Start Medicaid-planning gifting if low-wealth path
- Consider hybrid life insurance with LTC rider
- Start regular health maintenance (genuinely reduces LTC needs)

### 5 Years Before Need
- Medicaid lookback begins (asset transfers start clock)
- Review POA / trust powers to see whether explicitly scoped gifting, trust, or benefits-planning authority is needed; do not casually add sweeping self-dealing powers
- Assess home for aging in place
- Talk with adult children about preferences

### Year of Diagnosis / Need
- Assess care setting options
- Activate healthcare POA if incapacity
- Coordinate with estate plan (trust funding, beneficiary review)
- Consider state-specific community-spouse strategies only after verifying current local law and practice

### During Care
- Monitor quality
- Manage finances (especially if cognitive decline)
- Track medical expenses (deductible)
- Update estate plan as circumstances change

## Tax Aspects

- **Medical expense deduction:** LTC costs deductible as medical expenses (over 7.5% of AGI)
- **LTCi premiums:** deductible up to age-based limits (small but real)
- **HSA funds:** can pay LTCi premiums up to age-based limits
- **Tax-qualified LTC benefits:** non-taxable up to per-diem limit
- **ABLE accounts** (for disabled): tax-free for qualifying disability expenses

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Assuming Medicare covers nursing home** — it doesn't (major misconception)
2. **Waiting too long to buy insurance** — declined due to health changes
3. **Not starting Medicaid planning 5 years early** — lookback penalty
4. **Spousal impoverishment** — not using spousal protections under Medicaid
5. **Traditional LTCi with rate increases** — premium spike forces lapse
6. **Not including home modifications** in aging-in-place plan
7. **Family caregiver burnout** — single caregiver with no respite
8. **No personal care contract** for family caregiver — Medicaid transfer penalty
9. **No healthcare POA** — family paralyzed at crisis
10. **Moving to nursing home without trial** — settle for first available; better planning possible
11. **CCRC with weak finances** — facility bankrupt, entrance fee gone
12. **Stale LTC policy** — low benefit relative to current care costs (inflation)
