# Anti-Patterns — The Catalog

Failure modes seen across thousands of estates. Each is a specific failure mode you should pattern-match for in any plan.

## Document & Coordination Anti-Patterns

1. **Treating the will as the plan.** For many households, a large share of wealth passes outside probate by contract and title. The will is one document among many.

2. **Empty revocable trust.** Trust created but never funded in any meaningful way — major assets still titled in individual name. Pour-over cleanup can still leave those assets in probate.

3. **Ex-spouse still on 401(k).** On ERISA-governed workplace plans especially, do not assume divorce statutes clean this up for you; the ex can still take if the form was never updated.

4. **"Estate" named as life-insurance or retirement-account beneficiary without a deliberate reason.** Forces probate, adds friction and creditor exposure, and for retirement accounts often accelerates taxable payout.

5. **Minor children as direct beneficiaries.** Often pushes the asset into court-supervised property management, and direct control can arrive at the applicable age of majority or custodial-release age.

6. **No contingent beneficiary.** Primary predeceases; asset goes through probate.

7. **No successor agent named.** Primary agent dies/declines; court appoints stranger.

8. **Document inconsistency.** Will, trust, beneficiary forms, deeds tell different stories.

9. **Stale plan never reviewed.** Plan reflects a dead spouse, divorced ex, moved-from state.

10. **Original document missing.** Family knows there was a will; can't find it.

## Family-Structure Anti-Patterns

11. **"Everything to spouse" in blended family.** Without a protected-remainder design, the surviving spouse can later redirect the property and first-marriage children can be cut out.

12. **Verbal agreement that surviving spouse will leave to first-marriage children.** Unenforceable; routinely broken.

13. **Stepchildren assumed included as "my children."** They aren't unless adopted.

14. **Disinheritance by silent omission.** Pretermitted-heir statutes may give them a share anyway.

15. **No-contest clause with $1 bequest.** Disinherited person has nothing to lose by suing.

16. **Last-minute amendment** under suspicious circumstances. Undue-influence challenge invited.

17. **Disinheriting spouse without valid waiver.** Elective share defeats the disinheritance.

18. **No protected-remainder design in a blended family.** Spouse takes outright, rewrites plan later, and kids from the first marriage can be cut out. Often the right fix is a QTIP or similar structure.

19. **No prenup with separate property merged into joint accounts.** Separate property became marital.

20. **Outright distribution to vulnerable beneficiary.** Disqualifies SSI/Medicaid; funds addiction; lost to creditors.

## Vulnerable-Heir Anti-Patterns

21. **Family member trustee for addicted beneficiary.** Manipulation; cash distributions "just this once."

22. **First-party SNT when third-party would have worked.** Medicaid payback unnecessary.

23. **Cash distribution from spendthrift trust.** Beneficiary spends on substances/predatory partner.

24. **Distribution at "mature" ages without testing.** 25/30/35-year-old former addict gets $500K outright.

25. **No trust protector.** Decades-old trust becomes unworkable as beneficiary's life changes.

## Incapacity Anti-Patterns

26. **Springing POA requiring physician certification.** Banks reject; doctors won't certify; agent paralyzed.

27. **No gifting authority in POA.** Annual exclusion gifting program halts during incapacity.

28. **No healthcare proxy.** Default decisions go to next-of-kin under statute (often unintended).

29. **No HIPAA authorization.** Family locked out of medical records.

30. **No dementia-specific directive.** Aggressive treatment continues against intent.

31. **No mental-health Ulysses clause** despite history. Future episode treats can't be enforced.

32. **Firearms in home of person with cognitive decline** without surrender directive.

## Asset-Specific Anti-Patterns

33. **Joint tenancy with one child.** Disinherits other children.

34. **Vacation home to "all my kids equally."** Partition-action risk is extremely high if there is no governance wrapper or sale plan.

35. **Crypto seed phrase in the will.** Will becomes public at probate; theft.

36. **Crypto with no backup.** Single safe burns down; wealth gone.

37. **Margin loan triggers force-liquidation during probate.** Liquidity crisis forces sales on a bad timetable and can create avoidable post-death tax friction.

38. **Concentrated stock with no diversification plan.** Estate value crashes with single stock.

39. **NFA item to ineligible heir.** Federal felony for executor.

40. **Out-of-state real estate in own name.** Triggers ancillary probate in second state.

41. **Reverse mortgage hidden from heirs.** Surprise + crisis at death.

42. **Old whole-life policy still owned by insured** when ILIT would be appropriate.

43. **HSA to non-spouse beneficiary.** Full balance taxable in year of death.

44. **Pension joint-and-survivor not elected.** Surviving spouse loses lifetime income.

45. **529 plan with no successor-owner review.** Family assumes one death-transfer path, but the program's actual default rule points somewhere else.

46. **Trust as IRA beneficiary** without see-through drafting. It can lose designated-beneficiary treatment and fall into a worse payout regime, often the 5-year rule or the decedent's remaining-life-expectancy regime depending on the facts.

47. **Charitable bequest to "United Way" without specifying chapter.** Dispute over recipient.

48. **Foreign real estate not addressed in plan.** Forced heirship overrides U.S. will.

## Tax Anti-Patterns

49. **Lifetime gifting of appreciated assets below the federal exemption.** Often gives up step-up for transfer-tax savings that the estate was unlikely to need after federal and state modeling.

50. **Failure to file Form 706 for portability** after first spouse's death. DSUE can be lost or preserved only through costly relief work.

51. **GST exemption not allocated** during life. Lost at death (not portable).

52. **No state estate tax planning** for residents of NY/MA/OR/IL etc. Six- and seven-figure surprise.

53. **Domicile change without clean break.** Old state asserts continued domicile, taxes the estate.

54. **Reciprocal SLATs.** IRS uncrosses, includes both in each grantor's estate.

55. **3-year lookback failure on ILIT.** Existing policy transferred; included in estate if death within 3 years.

56. **No tax apportionment clause.** Residuary beneficiary pays full estate tax while specific-bequest takers walk away tax-free.

57. **No ILIT for illiquid estate when there is real transfer-tax or first-death-liquidity exposure.** Family may be pushed toward an avoidable distressed sale or expensive financing scramble.

58. **§6166 not elected** for closely held business. Cash-flow crisis 9 months after death.

59. **Forgetting §754 election** on partnership interests. Heir inherits awkward inside basis.

60. **No basis records for crypto.** Basis becomes hard to substantiate and the family may be pushed into unfavorable tax reporting positions.

## Process Anti-Patterns

61. **Family conversation never held.** Surprise at funeral; litigation.

62. **Attorney + CPA + financial advisor never coordinated.** Plan that "makes sense on paper" doesn't work in execution.

63. **DIY plan from online templates** for non-trivial estate. State-specific provisions missing.

64. **Plan executed but never tested.** Family has no idea where documents are at death.

65. **Estate plan signed but witnesses not properly arranged.** Will invalid for execution defect.

66. **No "if I die tomorrow" package.** Spouse with no idea what to do.

67. **Joint signing of married couple's documents** without independent counsel for each. Conflict of interest issue.

68. **Single attorney representing multiple beneficiaries.** Conflict of interest.

69. **Co-executors in conflicted family.** Gridlock.

70. **No review cadence.** Plan from 2010 still in force 15 years later.

## Post-Death Anti-Patterns (Executor)

71. **Distributing too early.** Executor personally liable if creditors emerge.

72. **Commingling estate and personal funds.** Executor liability.

73. **Missing federal estate tax deadline** (9 months). Penalties.

74. **Missing portability election** on Form 706.

75. **Missing qualified disclaimer window** (9 months). Better tax outcome lost.

76. **Force-sale of margin account during probate.** Tax consequences.

77. **No notification to credit bureaus.** Identity theft / ghost fraud.

78. **Continuing zombie subscriptions** for 18 months of administration.

79. **Failure to file Form 8971 when required** under the current timing rules. Penalties.

80. **Trust funded but never administered properly.** Crummey notices missing on ILIT.

## The Meta Anti-Pattern

81. **"I'll do it later."** The cost of good planning is a rounding error compared to the cost of its absence. Procrastination is the most expensive estate planning decision possible.
