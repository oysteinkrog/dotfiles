# Non-U.S.-Citizen Spouse

This is a **high-alert special-routing case**. Do not treat it as ordinary spousal estate planning with one or two tweaks.

## Core Federal Problem

For a U.S.-citizen surviving spouse, the federal marital deduction is generally unlimited.

For a surviving spouse who is **not** a U.S. citizen, the federal marital deduction is generally available only through a **Qualified Domestic Trust (QDOT)** or another specifically qualifying path under current federal law.

The practical result is that:

- first-death planning can change materially,
- portability analysis becomes more technical,
- and citizenship / residency / treaty posture can be outcome-determinative.

The following were verified against official IRS materials on **2026-04-16**:

## Do Not Overstate Portability

The old shortcut "portability does not apply with a non-citizen spouse" is too blunt and can be wrong in edge cases.

The safer rule for this skill is:

- **Do not present blanket portability conclusions** in non-citizen-spouse cases.
- Instead, verify current Form 706 instructions and treaty posture before finalizing the recommendation.
- If the plan depends on DSUE, QDOT treatment, or citizenship timing, log the exact source in `analyses/official-source-log.md`.

Important current IRS signal:

- the Form 706 instructions explicitly tie the marital-deduction treatment for a non-U.S.-citizen surviving spouse to QDOT rules, and
- portability in nonresident / noncitizen situations can be limited or unavailable absent specific qualifying circumstances.

## QDOT — What the Skill Must Remember

A QDOT is not just "a trust for a spouse." It is a federal estate-tax mechanism with strict requirements.

The current Form 706 instructions indicate that:

- at least one trustee must be a U.S. citizen or domestic corporation,
- the trust must satisfy QDOT regulatory requirements,
- and the election is made on the federal estate tax return.

Planning consequences:

- do not recommend a generic revocable trust clause and assume that solves the issue,
- do not assume the family can retrofit this casually after death,
- and do not separate QDOT planning from the attorney handoff.

## Naturalization Can Change the Outcome

Citizenship timing can materially affect the federal result. That does **not** mean the skill should make broad promises. The right posture is:

- identify whether the spouse is a green-card holder, long-term resident, temporary visa holder, or nonresident,
- identify whether naturalization is realistically expected,
- route the timing question to current IRS instructions and counsel,
- and avoid any claim that "naturalization will definitely fix it" without a live-law check.

## Lifetime Gift Strategy

For 2026, the annual exclusion for gifts to a non-citizen spouse is **$194,000**.

That makes annual gifting an important planning lever for some couples, especially where:

- one spouse needs a separate liquidity cushion,
- asset ownership is lopsided,
- pre-death rebalancing helps the overall plan,
- or eventual citizenship/residency changes are expected.

But:

- gifts above the special annual exclusion still require regular gift-tax analysis,
- basis tradeoffs still matter,
- and gifting is not automatically superior to retaining appreciation for a later basis adjustment.

## Joint Ownership Issues

Joint ownership with a non-citizen spouse deserves separate scrutiny.

The skill should specifically ask:

- who actually funded the account or property,
- whether the transfer into joint form was itself a taxable gift,
- and what percentage of value is expected to be included at death.

Do not assume ordinary citizen-spouse joint-ownership intuitions carry over cleanly.

## Nonresident Noncitizen (NRA) Exposure

If the spouse is not a U.S. resident and not a U.S. citizen, the problem becomes even more technical because U.S.-situs rules matter.

Common U.S.-situs categories include:

- U.S. real estate,
- stock of U.S. corporations,
- and tangible personal property physically located in the United States.

This file should route any NRA-owned U.S.-asset case into a separate live verification step. The stakes are too high for memorized heuristics.

## State-Law Overlay

Federal non-citizen-spouse planning is only half the problem.

Also verify:

- state elective-share rules,
- state estate or inheritance tax treatment,
- state marital-property regime,
- execution formalities for wills, trusts, and powers,
- and whether state practice departs from the federal default in any material way.

## Cross-Border Overlay

Ask these questions early:

- Does either spouse have foreign citizenship only, dual citizenship, or permanent residence abroad?
- Are there foreign bank, trust, company, or real-estate interests?
- Is there a prenup or marital agreement tied to another legal regime?
- Does forced-heirship law apply to foreign property?
- Is there an estate or gift tax treaty in play?

If yes, this is no longer a standard domestic estate-planning workflow. Escalate early.

## Common Failure Modes

1. Assuming the unlimited marital deduction applies automatically.
2. Using the wrong annual-exclusion number for gifts to the non-citizen spouse.
3. Treating portability as automatic or categorically unavailable without checking the actual posture.
4. Missing QDOT design and election requirements.
5. Ignoring contribution history on jointly held assets.
6. Allowing an NRA spouse to hold U.S.-situs assets directly without dedicated analysis.
7. Missing treaty or foreign-law interactions entirely.

## The Intake Language To Use

> "Because your spouse is not a U.S. citizen, we need to route this into the non-citizen-spouse branch immediately. The federal marital-deduction and portability analysis can change a lot here. We need to verify current IRS rules, determine whether a QDOT path matters, and understand the citizenship, residency, and treaty posture before we finalize any recommendation."
