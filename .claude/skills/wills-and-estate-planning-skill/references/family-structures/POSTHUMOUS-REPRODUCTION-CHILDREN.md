# Posthumous Reproduction and Assisted-Reproduction Children

## Frozen Embryos, Sperm, and Eggs

If the user has reproductive material in cryostorage:

- **Disposition directive** with the fertility clinic — what happens at death? (destroyed, donated to research, donated to other families, preserved for surviving spouse, used for posthumous conception)
- **Will/trust language** addressing posthumous-conception scenarios
- **Coordinate with spouse/partner** on shared embryos — both parties must typically agree
- **Cost considerations** — annual storage fees may continue post-death; trust-funded continuation often appropriate

## Posthumous Conception — The Inheritance Question

If the surviving spouse uses the deceased spouse's frozen embryo or sperm to conceive a child **after** the spouse's death, does that child legally inherit?

State law diverges:

- **Some states** (UPC-influenced) allow posthumously conceived children to inherit if conceived within a specified period (typically 36-45 months) and the deceased parent consented in writing
- **Other states** require explicit pre-death documentation
- **Federal law** (Social Security survivor benefits) follows state law for purposes of inheritance — Astrue v. Capato (2012)

**Will language to address explicitly:**

```
For purposes of this Will, my "issue" or "descendants" includes
[shall include / shall not include] any child conceived from my
genetic material after my death, provided that:
  (a) my [spouse / former spouse / partner] is the gestational carrier
      or donor recipient,
  (b) the child is born within [36] months of my death, and
  (c) [other conditions].
```

OR, to exclude:

```
For purposes of this Will, my "issue" and "descendants" shall NOT
include any child conceived from my genetic material after my death.
```

## Assisted Reproductive Technology (ART)

For families formed through:
- **IVF** (in vitro fertilization)
- **Donor gametes** (sperm donor, egg donor)
- **Gestational surrogacy**
- **Embryo donation**

### Legal Parentage

State law determines legal parentage. Some states have adopted the Uniform Parentage Act and provide clear rules; others are inconsistent.

For the user as **intended parent**:
- Pre-birth parentage order from the court (often required for surrogacy)
- Adoption (sometimes required even for biological parent of intended-parent couple)
- Birth certificate listing both intended parents

For the user as **donor**:
- Sperm/egg donation contracts typically waive parental rights
- Biological children conceived from donation typically have no inheritance claim against donor
- But state law varies; emerging law on disclosure of donor identity (Maine, Washington and others now allow donor-conceived adults to access identity)

### Donor-Conceived Children Discovering the Donor

Consumer DNA testing (AncestryDNA, 23andMe) has exposed many sperm/egg donors to contact from biological children they fathered/mothered decades earlier.

If the user was a sperm or egg donor:
- Pre-existing donation contracts typically waive parental rights and inheritance obligations
- Biological children conceived from donation typically have no inheritance claim
- But explicit will language eliminates ambiguity:

```
I have previously donated sperm/eggs to [Clinic] under contracts
waiving parental rights. Any biological child conceived from such
donations shall NOT be considered my "child" or "issue" for any
purpose under this Will.
```

## Pretermitted-Heir Coordination

State pretermitted-heir statutes typically apply to children "born or adopted after the will was signed." Posthumously conceived children may or may not be covered, depending on state law and the clarity of the will.

**Usually include explicit "after-born" language unless state-specific counsel prefers a narrower formulation:**

```
Any child born to or adopted by me after the date of this Will shall
share equally with my other children in any provision benefiting my
children, except as otherwise specifically provided. This Will
contemplates that I may have additional children after this date and
intentionally provides for them on the same terms as my currently
named children.
```

OR, if not desired:

```
I have intentionally not provided for any child born to or adopted
by me after the date of this Will. Any such child shall take nothing
under this Will.
```

## Surrogacy Arrangements

If the user (alive) used a gestational surrogate to have a child:
- Pre-birth or post-birth parentage order documented
- Birth certificate listing user as parent
- Will treats child as user's child — no special language needed if parentage was established

If the user is a surrogate carrying for someone else's intended parents:
- Surrogacy contract should specify that user has no parental rights
- User should NOT name the carried child in their will (avoid creating a parentage claim)

## Donor as Beneficiary (Reverse Issue)

A user who donated eggs or sperm and later wants to provide for biological children they may not have known existed — possible but requires explicit naming:

```
I leave $X to [biological child's full name], born [date], with
whom I established contact in [year] following DNA-testing results.
This bequest is voluntary and not in recognition of any legal
parental obligation.
```

## Coordination With Family Conversation

This is delicate. A user with frozen embryos may not have shared this with all family members. A donor's biological children discovered via DNA testing may not be known to existing family.

The will is a public document at probate. If sensitive information is included (paternity, donor history), consider:
- Trust structure to keep details out of the public probate record
- Letter of explanation in the file (not in the will itself)
- Family conversation in advance to prevent surprise

## The Conversation to Have

For users with cryopreserved material:

> "You have frozen [embryos/sperm/eggs] at [Clinic]. We need to address: (1) what happens to the material at your death — destroyed, donated, preserved for [spouse]'s use? (2) If [spouse] uses it to have a child after your death, does that child legally inherit from you? (3) If you've designated cost obligations for storage, who pays? Let's draft an explicit policy and coordinate with the clinic."

For donors:

> "Because you donated [sperm/eggs] in the past, there's a chance biological children may exist or may discover you through DNA testing. Your will should explicitly address whether those children — including any not yet known — are your 'children' for inheritance purposes. The default in most states is that they aren't, but explicit language eliminates uncertainty."
