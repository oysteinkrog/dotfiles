# Incarcerated or Foreign Heirs

## Incarcerated Heirs

A beneficiary serving a prison sentence can technically inherit, but receiving and managing property while incarcerated is impractical:

- **Prison commissary accounts** have low deposit limits
- **Property cannot be possessed** in the facility
- **Restitution obligations** may have liens on inherited assets
- **Civil judgments** may attach
- **Child support arrears** may attach
- **Parole conditions** may include financial restrictions

### The Solution: Holding Trust

Set up a trust structure for an incarcerated heir's inheritance:
- **Discretionary trust** — trustee holds the assets
- **Spendthrift provision** — protects from creditors and restitution liens (state-specific protection)
- **Funding distributions during incarceration** — trustee pays for commissary deposits, legal representation, communication costs, family support
- **Re-entry funding** — at release, trustee may distribute for housing, transportation, employment-search support
- **Continued trust post-release** if heir's circumstances suggest it (substance abuse history, mental illness)

### Restitution and Civil Judgments

In many states, court-ordered restitution and civil judgments **can be collected from inherited assets**, sometimes even from spendthrift trusts (varies by state). Coordinate with criminal-defense counsel before structuring.

### Visitation, Communication, and Support

The trustee may need to:
- Send commissary funds via approved channels
- Pay attorney fees for ongoing legal matters
- Maintain contact information
- Coordinate with the heir's family
- Plan for re-entry support

### Letter of Wishes for an Incarcerated Heir

The grantor's letter to the trustee should address:
- The grantor's understanding of the conviction
- Hopes for rehabilitation
- Specific concerns (substance abuse, mental health)
- Family relationships
- Goals for re-entry support

## Foreign Heirs (Non-U.S. Citizens, Non-U.S. Residents)

Beneficiaries who are neither U.S. citizens nor U.S. residents create several layers of complications.

### Tax Withholding

Distributions from U.S. trusts or retirement accounts to foreign beneficiaries are subject to U.S. **withholding tax**:

- 30% standard withholding on most U.S.-source income
- May be reduced by tax treaty (UK, Germany, France, Canada, etc. have treaties)
- Trustee responsible for withholding and remitting to IRS

### Estate Tax Treaty Considerations

The U.S. has estate-tax treaties with fewer than 20 countries. For treaty countries, double taxation is reduced. For non-treaty countries, both U.S. estate tax (on U.S.-situs assets) and foreign inheritance tax may apply — full double taxation.

### Foreign Probate of U.S. Real Estate

If a foreign beneficiary inherits U.S. real estate and later transfers it, foreign probate may be required at death:
- Most countries recognize a U.S. court's authority over U.S.-situs property at U.S. owner's death
- But foreign country's tax authority may still tax the inheritance under their rules
- Subsequent transfer at foreign owner's death triggers foreign probate

### Currency and Banking

Large transfers to foreign beneficiaries may trigger:
- **U.S. reporting requirements** — Form 1041 distributions, FATCA reporting
- **Foreign country reporting** — many countries require disclosure of large foreign inheritances
- **Banking limitations** — some countries restrict foreign currency receipts; some banks refuse large international wires
- **Sanctions screening** — distributions to recipients in sanctioned countries (Russia, Iran, Cuba, North Korea, etc.) may be blocked

### Foreign Tax Treatment of Inheritance

A bequest that is **tax-free in the U.S.** may be **taxable in the beneficiary's country**. Examples:

- **France** — taxes inheritances by relationship; close relatives have exemptions, more distant relatives heavily taxed
- **Germany** — similar progressive structure
- **Japan** — high inheritance tax up to 55%
- **UK** — inheritance tax of 40% above threshold
- **Australia, Canada, New Zealand, Sweden, Norway** — no inheritance tax but capital gains may apply
- **Many countries** — no inheritance tax

Heir should consult a tax professional in their country **before accepting** the inheritance.

### Forced Heirship in Foreign Civil-Law Jurisdictions

Many civil-law countries (France, Spain, Italy, Latin America, Saudi Arabia, much of Africa) have **forced heirship** rules: certain heirs (children, sometimes spouses) are entitled to a "reserved portion" of the estate that cannot be devised away.

If a U.S. testator owns property abroad in a forced-heirship jurisdiction:
- The U.S. will may be unenforceable as to that property
- Local heirs can claim their reserved portion
- Solution: separate "situs will" drafted under foreign law, coordinated with U.S. plan

### Practical Strategies

1. **Lifetime gifts** to foreign heirs may be more tax-efficient than bequests (annual exclusion plus, in some treaty countries, more favorable treatment of gifts vs. inheritance)

2. **Trust structure that distributes outside the foreign tax base** — discretionary trust where beneficiary doesn't have an enforceable right may not trigger inheritance tax in some countries

3. **Bequest structure that minimizes foreign tax** — a charitable bequest in the foreign country (for a foreign-resident beneficiary) may be deductible under their tax law

4. **Currency considerations** — leave the asset in U.S. dollars rather than the heir's local currency; let them control conversion timing

5. **Asset protection from foreign creditors** — the trust structure should consider that beneficiary may face very different creditor dynamics in their country

### Reporting Requirements for U.S. Beneficiary Inheriting from Foreign Decedent

The reverse situation: a U.S. person inheriting from a foreign person triggers:
- **Form 3520** — Annual Return To Report Transactions With Foreign Trusts and Receipt of Certain Foreign Gifts
- Threshold: foreign inheritance > $100K in a tax year
- Penalties for non-filing: 5% per month, max 25%
- Foreign trust beneficiary status triggers ongoing 3520 and 3520-A filings

This is a separate issue but worth flagging if the user has a foreign relative whose estate they may inherit.

### The Conversation to Have

For a user with a foreign heir:

> "Leaving assets to [Foreign Heir] in [Country] is more complex than leaving the same to a U.S. resident. There will be U.S. withholding, possibly inheritance tax in [Country], potentially currency conversion issues, and reporting on both sides. Let's structure the bequest to minimize the friction. We may want to consider whether a lifetime gift, a different vehicle, or a different kind of trust would work better than a simple will provision."
