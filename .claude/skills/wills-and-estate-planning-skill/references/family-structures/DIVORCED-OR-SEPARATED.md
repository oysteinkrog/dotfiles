# Divorced or Currently Separated

## Divorce: Update Everything Affirmatively

In most states, divorce automatically revokes provisions in favor of the ex-spouse under the will and many beneficiary designations — but **not all**. Critical exceptions:

### ERISA Retirement Plans

Many **private-employer** workplace retirement plans are governed by **ERISA**, federal law, but do not assume every 403(b) or public/church plan fits that bucket. For plans that are actually subject to ERISA's beneficiary and survivor-rights regime, **ERISA preempts many state divorce-revocation rules.** The named beneficiary on file can still control unless the form is affirmatively updated or a valid federal-law path, such as a QDRO or required spousal-consent framework, changes the result.

**Required action:** File new beneficiary form personally with the plan administrator. Do not rely on automatic revocation.

### Life Insurance

Most state revocation statutes apply to life insurance, but **divorce decrees frequently require maintaining life insurance** for the benefit of children or alimony recipients. Don't remove those beneficiaries without checking the decree.

### Trust Beneficiary Designations

State revocation statutes may not apply to ex-spouse's status as trust beneficiary if the trust was created during marriage. Update the trust.

### Children From the Ex-Spouse

Children from the ex-spouse still have full inheritance rights from you. The ex-spouse does not, but the children with the ex-spouse remain heirs.

## The Post-Divorce Update Project

Treat as a single coordinated project:

- [ ] Will — remove all provisions favoring ex-spouse
- [ ] Trust — same
- [ ] All retirement account beneficiary forms
- [ ] All life insurance beneficiary forms
- [ ] All annuities
- [ ] HSA beneficiary
- [ ] 529 plans (especially successor owner / account successor if ex-spouse was named)
- [ ] TOD/POD on bank and brokerage
- [ ] Real estate titling — quitclaim from joint tenancy
- [ ] Healthcare proxy
- [ ] Living will
- [ ] HIPAA authorization
- [ ] Durable financial POA
- [ ] Disposition of remains
- [ ] Letter of instruction
- [ ] Emergency contacts
- [ ] Hospital visitation authorization
- [ ] Email / social-media legacy contacts
- [ ] Employer-benefit beneficiary and emergency-contact records

## Divorce Decree Coordination

Many divorce decrees require:
- Maintaining specific life insurance for children
- Maintaining health insurance for ex-spouse for a period
- Specific division of retirement accounts (QDRO)
- Continued payment of mortgage, child support, alimony

Do NOT remove these beneficiaries or stop these obligations without coordinating with the divorce decree. Estate planning that ignores existing legal obligations creates a separate liability for the estate.

## Children's Inheritance Through Both Parents

Children typically inherit from both biological parents independently. After divorce:
- Children remain primary beneficiaries on most plans
- May inherit through both parents' estates
- Trust structures help if children are minors (avoid the ex-spouse becoming guardian of the property by default)

**Important:** A trust for minor children that names a successor trustee should consider that the ex-spouse may be the surviving parent and natural guardian — but you may not want them administering the children's inheritance from your side. Use a non-spouse trustee.

## Ex-Spouses You Want to Provide For

Some divorced couples remain on good terms. The ex-spouse may be:
- The mother of your children
- A long-time companion before formal separation
- Someone you have moral obligations to

Nothing prevents you from naming an ex-spouse — but **do so deliberately and explicitly**. A will that accidentally still mentions an ex-spouse can be challenged as outdated. An explicit reference ("I leave $100,000 to my former wife Jane Smith, in recognition of our long partnership") is unambiguous.

## Separated But Not Divorced

This is legally hazardous. The "separated spouse" often still has major default rights:

- Spousal inheritance rights
- Elective-share or similar spouse-protection rights
- ERISA beneficiary rights if forms remain unchanged
- Default healthcare decision-making rights if no proxy exists
- Other default marital rights depending on state law and account structure

If you've been separated for years from a spouse whose interests you no longer share, **either:**

1. **Complete the divorce** — provides clean break
2. **Build belt-and-suspenders estate plan:**
   - Separation agreement with spousal-rights waiver (some states)
   - Healthcare proxy explicitly naming someone other than spouse
   - Financial POA explicitly naming someone other than spouse
   - Beneficiary designations updated (within ERISA constraints)
   - Will explicitly addressing the spouse's role and any waivers from separation agreement

The half-separated state where nothing is updated is often among the worst positions legally — the spouse may still retain major default rights, but neither party feels obligated to act in the other's interest.

## Common Law Marriage Note

If you separated from a partner in a jurisdiction that may recognize common-law marriage without ever formalizing the marriage or the breakup, the state may still consider you married for inheritance purposes. Verify the specific jurisdiction rather than relying on any canned state list.

## Remarriage After Divorce

If you remarry after divorce (becoming a blended family), see [BLENDED-FAMILY.md](BLENDED-FAMILY.md). Critical: update ALL plans before remarriage, and use prenup if separate property exists from prior marriage.

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Ex-spouse still on 401(k)** — on ERISA-governed workplace plans especially, the old beneficiary form can still control unless it is affirmatively updated or another valid federal-law path changes the result
2. **Ex-spouse named as healthcare proxy never updated** — the ex may still be the person making life-or-death decisions
3. **Ex-spouse named as POA agent** — ex has access to financial accounts during incapacity
4. **Real estate still in joint tenancy with ex** — ex inherits at death
5. **Forgetting divorce decree obligations** — estate sued by ex for failure to maintain insurance
6. **Children's trust naming ex as trustee** — ex controls children's inheritance
7. **Separated but no plan changes** — spouse may retain major default rights that the user assumed were gone
8. **Common-law-marriage state separation without formal divorce** — partner retains spousal rights

## The Conversation to Have

For someone recently divorced:

> "Divorce doesn't automatically clean up your estate plan. Some things may auto-revoke under state law, but workplace retirement plans are where people get burned if they assume the paperwork fixed itself. I want to walk through every single account, every document, every default — and update them all. This will take a few hours, and it is usually among the highest-priority pieces of post-divorce financial cleanup. Let's start with the 401(k)."

For someone separated:

> "Separation is a tough legal limbo. Your spouse may still have many of the rights they had when you were together, depending on the state, the account, and what paperwork has or has not been updated. We can build belt-and-suspenders protections — healthcare proxy, financial POA, beneficiary updates within what ERISA allows — but the cleanest fix is usually finalizing the divorce. Where are you on that timeline?"
