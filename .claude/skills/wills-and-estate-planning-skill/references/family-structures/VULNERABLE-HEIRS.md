# Vulnerable Heirs (Disability, Addiction, Mental Illness, Creditor Risk, Predatory Spouse)

Not every heir is ready for an outright inheritance. The wrong distribution structure can disqualify them from benefits, fund a relapse, fall to creditors, end up with a predatory spouse, or simply be wasted.

## The Universal Tool: Discretionary Spendthrift Trust

For any vulnerable beneficiary, the baseline is a **third-party discretionary spendthrift trust** with these features:

- **Beneficiary cannot assign or pledge** the interest (spendthrift)
- **Creditors cannot reach the assets** (spendthrift)
- **Trustee has full discretion** over distributions (discretionary — no enforceable right to compel)
- **Independent trustee** (not a family member with emotional involvement)
- **Letter of wishes** guides discretion without binding the trustee
- **Trust protector** with power to remove and replace trustee, modify administrative provisions, change situs

Distributions are made by the trustee, often **directly to providers** (landlord, grocery store, school, treatment facility) rather than to the beneficiary in cash.

## Vulnerability Profiles and Specific Tools

### Disability — Special Needs Trust (SNT)

For beneficiaries receiving means-tested government benefits (SSI, Medicaid, Section 8, SNAP, VA Aid & Attendance):

**Direct inheritance can disqualify or suspend means-tested benefits.** SSI resource limit: $2,000 individual / $3,000 couple. An inheritance that pushes the beneficiary over the resource limit can disrupt benefits until resources are properly spent down or moved into an appropriate structure, and it can create parallel Medicaid / housing / support-program problems.

**Two SNT types:**

#### Third-Party SNT (Strongly Preferred When Possible)

- Funded by parents or others (not the beneficiary's own money)
- Assets do not count as the beneficiary's resources
- **No Medicaid payback** at the beneficiary's death — remainder passes to whomever the grantor named
- Used for inheritance from parents, grandparents

#### First-Party (Self-Settled) SNT

- Funded with the beneficiary's own assets (e.g., personal injury settlement, an unexpected inheritance that came outright)
- Special "d4A" or "pooled" trust under 42 USC 1396p(d)(4)
- **Medicaid payback required** at death — Medicaid recoups what it paid before remainder distributes
- Trustee restrictions: typically a parent, grandparent, court, guardian, or pooled trust

When planning ahead, usually favor the third-party route: the parent leaves the disability beneficiary's share via a third-party SNT created in the will or trust, rather than relying on an outright bequest that later has to be repaired.

#### ABLE Account (Coordination)

For disability onset before age 26, with the threshold expanding to age 46 starting in 2026, subject to current-law verification:

- Tax-advantaged savings account (like 529)
- Up to the **current annual gift-tax exclusion amount** as the base annual limit (**$19K in 2026**), with possible additional employed-beneficiary contribution
- Up to $100K not counted for SSI
- Used for qualified disability expenses
- Coordinates with SNT (SNT pays for non-qualified items)

#### What SNT Distributions Can Cover

Trustee can pay for:
- Medical care not covered by Medicaid
- Therapies (physical, occupational, speech, behavioral)
- Education and tutoring
- Vocational training, supported employment
- Transportation (specially equipped vehicle, accessibility modifications)
- Recreation and entertainment (vacations, hobbies, events)
- Personal services (caregivers above what Medicaid covers)
- Home accessibility improvements
- Better housing than Medicaid alone provides
- Personal assistant or home health aide

Trustee cannot pay for:
- Cash directly to beneficiary (counts as income, may disqualify)
- Items already covered by Medicaid in a way that creates duplicate benefit

### Addiction — Behavioral / Incentive Trust

**Discretionary spendthrift trust is the foundation.** Add:

- Distributions paid **directly to providers** or otherwise tightly controlled, rather than handed over as unrestricted cash
- Trustee may condition distributions on:
  - Sustained sobriety verified by independent testing
  - Active participation in approved treatment program
  - Maintained contact with sponsor / support group
  - Compliance with terms set by treatment professional
- Trustee may pay directly for:
  - Treatment programs
  - Sober-living housing
  - Medical care
  - Basic needs (rent, utilities, groceries)
  - Phone, transportation
  - Therapy
- Incentive provisions:
  - Match earned income (encourage employment)
  - Bonus for completing treatment / education milestones
  - Reduce support if substance abuse resumes
- Professional or corporate trustee — not a family member

**Family-member-only trustee often struggles over time.** Emotional compromise, manipulation, and "just this once" exceptions are common enough that a sole family-member trustee should be chosen only with eyes open.

**Letter of wishes** explaining the addiction history and goals helps the trustee navigate gray areas.

### Mental Illness — Discretionary Trust + Psychiatric Coordination

For beneficiaries with serious mental illness (bipolar, schizophrenia, severe depression):

- Discretionary spendthrift trust as base
- **Psychiatric advance directive** (separate document) with Ulysses clause for treatment during episodes
- Trustee coordinates with treatment team during stable periods
- Self-settled special-purpose trust if beneficiary currently competent and wants to pre-commit (revocable trust that becomes irrevocable on evidence of episode)
- Trust protector authorized to suspend distributions during medical crisis

### Creditor Risk — Spendthrift + Asset Protection

For beneficiaries facing:
- Bankruptcy
- Pending lawsuits
- Tax liens
- Judgment creditors
- High-liability profession (surgeon, developer)

**Spendthrift trust + discretionary trust** prevents creditors from reaching assets in most states (state-law variation). Trust assets cannot be assigned, pledged, or attached.

For the most vulnerable beneficiaries (or for self-settled protection), consider:
- Domestic Asset Protection Trust (DAPT) — Nevada, South Dakota, Alaska, Delaware, Wyoming, etc.
- Offshore trust — Cook Islands, Nevis, Belize (with full FBAR/FATCA compliance)

### Predatory Spouse — Lifetime Trust

For beneficiaries in marriages the parent doesn't trust:

- Lifetime trust (not terminating at any age)
- Distributions controlled by trustee
- Spendthrift provision (creditor and divorce protection in most states)
- Beneficiary as co-trustee at later age (giving authority without ownership)
- Optional: distribute to beneficiary's descendants rather than to beneficiary's spouse

Properly drafted lifetime trusts often keep inherited wealth out of the marital balance sheet and make direct division much harder, but do not present that as absolute immunity. Divorce courts in some states can still consider trust interests, distributions, or access patterns indirectly. State-specific advice required.

### Financial Immaturity — Staged Distributions

For young or simply immature beneficiaries:

- Trust distributes principal in stages:
  - 1/3 at 25
  - 1/3 at 30
  - 1/3 at 35
- Or all-life trust with HEMS distributions
- Trustee discretion before each milestone

**Staged distribution gives the beneficiary the chance to mature** between stages. If they handle the first distribution well, the second is unconcerning. If they don't, the trustee has time to reconsider.

### Gambling Addiction

Like substance addiction:
- Discretionary trust
- Direct-pay distributions
- Independent trustee
- Specific awareness in letter of wishes
- Possibly bar from trust positions (some structures)

## Trustee Selection (Critical)

For vulnerable-heir trusts, trustee selection is one of the most consequential decisions in the whole structure.

**Family-member trustee:**
- Knows the beneficiary
- Emotionally compromised
- Subject to guilt, manipulation, family dynamics
- Hard to say no
- Often fails over years

**Professional / corporate trustee:**
- Objective, consistent, expert
- Lacks personal knowledge of beneficiary
- Costs ($5,000–$25,000+/year)
- Rules-bound

**Co-trustee structure (often best):**
- Corporate trustee for administration, investment, objective discretion
- Family member for context, beneficiary relationship
- Both must agree on distributions OR each handles different decisions

**Trust Protector:**
- Third party (often attorney) with power to:
  - Remove and replace trustee
  - Change trust situs
  - Modify administrative provisions
  - Adapt to changed law or family circumstances
- Crucial for long-term trusts where today's plan may not fit 30 years from now

## Letter of Wishes (Non-Binding, Powerful)

For every vulnerable-heir trust, a **letter of wishes** to the trustee:

- The beneficiary's history (in the parent's words)
- What worked and what failed in the past
- Specific concerns
- Goals for the trust
- Whose advice to consult (treatment professional, family doctor, social worker, sibling)
- What kinds of expenses to favor or disfavor
- Specific scenarios and how the parent would handle them

Not legally binding. The trustee retains discretion. But the letter informs every decision and protects the trustee from claims of mismanagement.

## Coordination Issues

### With Government Benefits

- Verify means-tested benefit eligibility before any distribution
- Coordinate with SSI/Medicaid representative
- Watch state-specific Medicaid asset and income limits (varies)
- Consider impact on Section 8 housing, SNAP, LIHEAP

### With Other Heirs

- Unequal treatment is common — disabled child gets a trust, others get outright
- Document reasoning clearly
- Avoid resentment by transparent communication during life

### With ABLE Account

- Coordinate annual ABLE contribution with SNT distributions
- ABLE used for qualified expenses; SNT for everything else

## Common Mistakes

1. **Outright inheritance to a beneficiary on SSI** — benefits disruption and forced spend-down / restructuring instead of smooth long-term support
2. **Family-member trustee for an addicted beneficiary** — manipulation risk is often extremely high
3. **No trust protector** — decades-old trust becomes unworkable as beneficiary's life changes
4. **First-party SNT when third-party would have worked** — Medicaid payback unnecessary
5. **Distribution age too early** — 25-year-old former addict doesn't get $500K outright
6. **No coordination with treatment team** — trustee makes decisions in a vacuum
7. **Cash distributions to a vulnerable beneficiary** — diverted to substances, manipulated by predatory spouse, taken by creditors

## The Conversation to Have

This is a hard conversation, especially when the vulnerable heir is the user's own child. Frame it carefully:

> "I love [Heir]. I want to make sure their inheritance actually helps them, not hurts them. Given what they're going through, an outright distribution would create real problems — disqualification from their benefits / risk of relapse / risk to creditors / vulnerability to a partner. Instead, I'm going to set up a trust where a professional trustee can pay directly for their housing, treatment, medical care, and other needs throughout their life. They'll be supported, but the assets will be protected. Does that fit how you see [Heir]'s needs?"

For users with concerns about an heir but no clear "vulnerability," consider the **discretionary spendthrift trust as a strong default** rather than an outright distribution. It imposes some structural cost, but it often buys a large amount of protection and flexibility.
