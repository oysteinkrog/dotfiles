# Disinheritance — Excluding a Child, Sibling, or Other Relative

People sometimes want to disinherit a child, parent, sibling, or other expected heir — because of abuse, addiction, profound estrangement, or irreconcilable values. The legal rules are clear; the human stakes are high.

## What You Can and Cannot Disinherit

### Spouses — Generally Cannot Be Fully Disinherited

Without a written, knowing waiver (prenup or postnup with valid spousal-rights waiver), many common-law states give the surviving spouse an **elective share** or augmented-estate claim regardless of what the will says. The size of the share and what assets are pulled into the calculation vary sharply by state.

Community-property states handle this through marital-property rules — half is already legally the spouse's.

To validly disinherit a spouse:
- Spouse must execute a **valid waiver** under state law (usually with clear disclosure, formal waiver language, and careful execution; some states strongly prefer or practically require independent counsel, but do not assume the rule is identical everywhere)
- Or spouse must consent in writing during life

Without that, the will may be effective as written but the surviving spouse can elect against it and claim the statutory share.

### Children — Generally Can Be Disinherited (Adults)

Most states allow full disinheritance of adult children. Notable exception:

**Louisiana** retains a small remnant of French forced-heirship law: children with disabilities OR under age 24 must receive a "forced portion" of the estate. Other states have abolished forced heirship.

For minor children, courts may impose a duty of support during minority (separate from inheritance), but most states allow disinheritance.

### Parents, Siblings, Other Relatives — Yes

No state requires that you provide for parents, siblings, nieces/nephews, etc. They are heirs only by intestacy default. A will overrides intestacy.

### Stepchildren — Usually Excluded by Default

As a general rule, unadopted stepchildren do not inherit through intestacy. If you want them included, name them explicitly. If you want them excluded, do not assume vague class terms like "children" or "issue" will always answer the question cleanly in a blended-family dispute.

## Explicit vs. Implicit

**Explicit disinheritance is dramatically safer than implicit.**

A will that simply omits a child creates ambiguity:
- Was the omission intentional?
- Was the child a pretermitted heir (born after will signed)?
- Was there undue influence?
- Was there mistake?

Explicit disinheritance removes the argument:

```
"I have intentionally not made provision for my son ROBERT JOHN SMITH 
in this Will. This omission is intentional and not the result of 
oversight or mistake. I have considered my relationship with him 
in making this Will and have determined that he should not benefit 
from my estate."
```

Some people fear this language is cruel. Soften only if the family will see the will:

```
"For reasons known to my family, I have intentionally not provided 
for my son ROBERT JOHN SMITH in this Will. This is intentional."
```

But do not leave it ambiguous. Ambiguity is the breeding ground for litigation.

## The "$1 Bequest" Question

Some attorneys recommend a small bequest plus a no-contest clause:

```
"I leave $10,000 to my son ROBERT JOHN SMITH. If he contests any 
provision of this Will, this gift shall be forfeited and pass under 
the residuary clause."
```

This gives the no-contest clause something to bite on. Receiving $10,000 is meaningful enough that the disinherited child might decide not to risk losing it through contest.

A pure "$1" bequest is easily forfeited and doesn't deter litigation. **Either nothing (with explicit disinheritance language) or something meaningful (with a no-contest clause).** Avoid the symbolic $1.

## No-Contest Clause (In Terrorem Clause)

Provides that anyone who contests the will forfeits their bequest.

**Enforceability varies by state:**
- **Florida** does NOT enforce no-contest clauses
- Some states enforce them strongly
- Many states have a **probable cause exception** or other carve-outs
- Effective only if the contesting party has something meaningful to lose

Combine with mediation clause for a softer first step:

```
"Any dispute arising from this Will shall first be submitted to 
mediation through [forum]. Only after mediation has been attempted 
in good faith may a beneficiary file a contest. Any beneficiary who 
contests this Will without first attempting mediation shall forfeit 
their interest."
```

## Documentation Strategy (Critical)

If a disinherited person contests the will after death, your contemporaneous documentation is the defense:

1. **Memo to file** — a private memo (typically held by the attorney) explaining:
   - The history of the relationship with the disinherited person
   - The specific reasons for disinheritance
   - Acknowledgment that the testator considered providing for the person and chose not to
   - Date and signature

2. **Capacity letter** — if you're elderly, ill, or recently changed your will under any unusual circumstances, get a contemporaneous letter from your physician confirming capacity.

3. **Independent legal counsel** — the testator's attorney represents only the testator. Beneficiaries should not be in the meetings or have access.

4. **Avoid last-minute changes** — amending the will a week before death when a new caregiver has moved in is begging for an undue-influence challenge.

5. **Videotape the signing** (in states that permit) — visual evidence of capacity and free choice.

6. **Keep hostile or interested people away from the signing.** Do not let the disinherited person's spouse, child, business partner, caregiver ally, or other interested person witness the will or hover in the room.

## Should the Disinherited Person's Descendants Also Be Excluded?

A common question. If you disinherit your son Robert:
- Does Robert's daughter (your granddaughter) also get nothing?
- Do Robert's biological siblings get more (proportional shifting)?

State default rules vary. Be explicit:

```
"For purposes of this Will, my son ROBERT JOHN SMITH and his 
descendants shall be deemed to have predeceased me. The share that 
would otherwise have passed to him shall instead pass to my other 
children and their descendants in equal shares per stirpes."
```

OR

```
"While I have intentionally not provided for my son ROBERT JOHN SMITH, 
this disinheritance does not extend to his descendants. The share 
that would have passed to Robert had he predeceased me shall pass 
to his descendants per stirpes."
```

Grandchildren are often innocent of the conflict. Decide deliberately.

## State-Specific Pretermitted-Heir Statutes

Most states protect children **born or adopted after the will was signed** with default share statutes. To override:

```
"I have considered the possibility of after-born or after-adopted 
children. Any such child shall share in my estate on the same terms 
as my currently named children."
```

Or, to exclude:

```
"I have intentionally not provided for any child born to or adopted 
by me after the date of this Will."
```

## Disinheritance Under Attack

Common contests:

- **Lack of capacity** — testator didn't understand
- **Undue influence** — manipulated by another beneficiary
- **Fraud** — deceived about contents
- **Improper execution** — witnesses, signatures
- **Newer will** — purported subsequent will revoking this one
- **Mistake** — drafting error misrepresenting intent

Defenses (built in advance):
- Capacity documentation
- Video signing
- Independent counsel for testator
- Self-proving affidavit
- Memo to file
- No-contest clause (in states that enforce)
- Family meeting / explanation during life — eliminates surprise

## "Soft" Alternatives to Full Disinheritance

If full disinheritance feels too harsh:

- **Smaller share** with explanation
- **Trust with discretionary distributions** — disinheritance via "no distributions to be made"
- **Charitable bequest** in lieu — share goes to a cause both you and the disinherited person care about
- **Conditional bequest** — share contingent on specific behavior (often legally problematic but achievable in some forms)

## The Conversation to Have (or Not Have)

Sometimes the family conversation about disinheritance is appropriate; sometimes it's not. Considerations:

- **If the disinherited person already knows the relationship is broken** — the conversation may add nothing
- **If the disinherited person assumes inheritance** — the surprise at the funeral is the worst case; advance notice may be kinder
- **If you fear retaliation** (legal, emotional, physical) — silence may be safer

For other family members (those still inheriting), a separate conversation explains the reasoning and prevents them from being blindsided.

## The Most Common Disinheritance Failures

1. **Silent omission** instead of explicit language — invites pretermitted-heir or undue-influence claims
2. **No memo to file** — no defense if contested
3. **Last-minute amendment** without capacity documentation
4. **Disinherited person's family present at signing**
5. **No-contest clause but $1 bequest** — disinherited person has nothing to lose
6. **Forgetting that descendants of disinherited person may take** — clarify either way
7. **Forgetting spouse-protection statutes** — elective-share, augmented-estate, or community-property rights can override the intended disinheritance
