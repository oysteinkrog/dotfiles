# Unmarried Partners (Long-Term, Same-Sex Pre-Marriage, Polyamorous)

## The Default Reality

Outside of formal marriage and certain specific state arrangements, **there is usually no "partner" category in intestacy law**. Without explicit planning, an unmarried partner — no matter how long the relationship — usually has:

- Usually no default inheritance rights absent marriage or another recognized legal status
- Usually no automatic right to make medical decisions
- Usually no automatic right to control disposition of remains
- No HIPAA access to medical records
- Usually no automatic right to receive Social Security survivor benefits as a spouse
- Usually no automatic right to assert wrongful death claims
- No estate-tax marital deduction
- Status as a "stranger" to inheritance/inheritance tax in many states

Most protections have to be created affirmatively and documented clearly.

## The Core Document Stack for Unmarried Partners

Both partners should execute, at a minimum:

1. **Will** explicitly naming partner as beneficiary (and contingent — what if partner predeceases?)
2. **Healthcare proxy** explicitly naming partner as healthcare agent (and backup)
3. **Living will / advance directive** stating treatment preferences; do not confuse this with the healthcare-proxy document that names the decisionmaker
4. **HIPAA authorization** explicitly listing partner
5. **Durable financial POA** naming partner
6. **Disposition-of-remains** designation naming partner
7. **Beneficiary designations** on every retirement, life insurance, brokerage updated to partner
8. **Real estate titling** that reflects intent (joint tenancy if survivorship desired; or trust)
9. **Hospital visitation authorization** (some hospitals require this even for legal partners)
10. **Cohabitation or domestic partnership agreement** documenting financial arrangements

## Property Contributions Over Time

A common scenario: one partner owns the house. The other has paid half the mortgage, all the renovations, and most of the upkeep for 20 years. There is no paper trail.

If the homeowner dies without a will:
- House passes to homeowner's blood family (parents, siblings)
- Surviving partner is a tenant in their own home
- Surviving partner has no equitable claim absent state-specific cohabitation doctrines

**Fixes:**

- **Add partner to deed** (creates joint tenancy or tenancy in common) — but consider gift tax implications and creditor risk
- **Cohabitation agreement** documenting contributions and ownership intentions
- **Testamentary bequest** in the will of an appropriate share to surviving partner
- **Life estate** in the will (partner stays in house for life, then to homeowner's family)
- **Trust ownership** with rights for partner

## Same-Sex Couples Whose Marriage Postdates Long Relationship

A couple together since 1985, married in 2015 after Obergefell, has:

- 10 years of legal marriage
- 30 years of total relationship

The "start of relationship" matters for:

- **Social Security survivor benefits** — marriage of 9+ months required for survivor benefits in most cases (with exceptions for accidental death)
- **Community property accounting** — only marital assets count
- **Basis questions** for assets acquired before the marriage
- **Tax-history reconstruction** — pre-marriage years they couldn't file jointly

Document the relationship history carefully. Holiday cards, joint accounts, lease/deed history — all evidence of relationship duration. May matter for federal benefit appeals.

## Polyamorous and Multi-Partner Households

No U.S. state recognizes more-than-two-adult marriages. Protection for additional partners usually has to be **built affirmatively**:

- Individual wills with explicit specific bequests to each partner
- Beneficiary designations to each partner (where multiple beneficiaries are allowed; not always)
- Trusts with each partner as named beneficiary
- Healthcare and financial POAs naming specific partners (with backup hierarchy)
- Cohabitation contracts among multiple parties
- Co-parenting arrangements (if children involved) — most states only recognize 2 legal parents

**Practical issues:**

- **Hospital pushback** is common when more than one partner shows up claiming to be a partner — written authorization helps, but confusion and delay still happen in the real world
- **Insurance** — most policies allow only one named beneficiary or a spouse + dependents structure
- **Custody battles** if a partnership ends and children are involved — non-legal-parent partners typically lose

## Healthcare and Funeral Authority

This is where unmarried-partner failures hurt most.

Without a healthcare proxy:
- Default authority goes to the next-of-kin under state statute
- That's usually parents, then adult children, then siblings
- Long-term partner is **not** in the default chain
- A blood relative who hasn't seen the patient in 10 years can lock the partner out of the ICU

**Solution:** Healthcare proxy + HIPAA + hospital visitation authorization. Execute them cleanly and make them easy to produce when needed.

For disposition of remains, similar default rules apply. A **disposition-of-remains directive** naming the partner usually strengthens the partner's authority materially, but the exact effect is state-specific and the document still has to be available when needed.

## Joint Accounts and Titling

Used deliberately, joint titling creates automatic survivorship. But:

- **Gift-tax issue** — putting a partner on a deed or large account is a gift if they didn't contribute
- **Creditor exposure** — joint account is reachable by either party's creditors
- **Loss of control** — joint owner can withdraw, encumber, sell

For most unmarried partners with substantial separate assets, **trust-based planning** offers more flexibility than joint titling.

## Beneficiary Designation Checklist for Unmarried Partners

- [ ] Retirement accounts — partner as primary, contingent named
- [ ] Life insurance — partner as primary
- [ ] Annuities — partner as primary
- [ ] HSA — partner as primary (note: non-spouse HSA distributions are taxable)
- [ ] TOD/POD on bank/brokerage — partner named
- [ ] 529 plans — partner as successor owner or plan-designated successor if they'll continue education funding for children
- [ ] Pension survivor election — many pensions don't allow non-spouse survivor benefits; check

## Estate and Income Tax Differences (vs. Married)

| Issue | Married | Unmarried |
|-------|---------|-----------|
| Marital deduction | Often unlimited for citizen-spouse transfers; noncitizen-spouse cases differ | None — fully taxable transfer |
| Annual exclusion | $19K (2026) per recipient | Same — but no marital deduction means more eats into exemption |
| Gift-splitting | Available in ordinary married-taxpayer cases, subject to the actual federal requirements | Not available |
| Spousal IRA rollover | Yes — usually the most flexible spousal treatment | No spousal rollover unless the survivor is recognized as a spouse; many partners face a year-10 outside deadline, but some close-in-age beneficiaries may qualify as eligible designated beneficiaries |
| Filing jointly for income tax | Yes | No |
| Basis step-up for appreciated inherited assets | Yes; community-property rules can produce especially favorable results for married couples in some states | Often yes for assets included in the decedent's estate, but without marital-deduction planning or community-property spousal rules |
| Social Security survivor | Yes (with marriage tenure) | Generally no spousal survivor benefit absent marriage or another recognized legal status |
| Health insurance coverage | Yes — spouse | Often domestic partner offered, often imputed taxable income |

## Special Considerations

### Health Insurance

If the deceased partner provided health insurance, the surviving partner loses coverage immediately (unlike spouse who often has continuation rights). COBRA may not extend to non-spouse partners. Plan for transition.

### Retirement Account Inheritance

Non-spouse beneficiaries of retirement accounts:
- Often face a year-10 outside deadline on IRA/401(k) accounts inherited from someone who died after 2019, though beneficiary status, plan terms, and required-beginning-date timing still matter
- But a partner who is not more than 10 years younger than the decedent may qualify as an eligible designated beneficiary with life-expectancy treatment
- Cannot roll over to their own IRA unless they qualify as a surviving spouse under the governing rules
- Need account-by-account tax modeling; do not assume every unmarried partner gets the same payout pattern

### Federal Estate Tax

For estates under the available federal exclusion amount: usually not a live issue absent prior taxable gifts or other transfer-tax complexity.

For estates above the available exclusion amount: **serious problem**. No marital deduction means transfers to the partner can trigger federal estate tax immediately rather than deferring tax until the survivor's death.

Mitigation:
- Lifetime use of exemption (gifts during life)
- ILIT-funded life insurance to partner
- Charitable remainder trust with partner as income beneficiary
- Structured planning with partnership to optimize basis

### State Estate Tax — The Stranger Problem

Some states' inheritance taxes apply higher rates to "non-relatives." A long-term partner is a non-relative under most inheritance-tax statutes. PA, KY, NJ in particular:

- Spouse: 0% (exempt)
- Children: low rates
- Friend / non-relative partner: high rates (up to 16% in PA)

Mitigation: Trust planning, lifetime gifting within annual exclusion, life insurance owned by ILIT.

## The Marriage Question

If both partners are willing to marry, marriage resolves **many** of these default-rights problems quickly:

- Marital deduction
- Spousal IRA rollover
- Social Security survivor
- Healthcare and funeral default rights
- Filing status
- State-tax treatment

But marriage does **not** instantly clean up old beneficiary forms, cohabitation economics, separate-property tracing, blended-family conflicts, or prior bad titling. For couples who choose not to marry (philosophical, financial, immigration, family pressure), the trade-off is far more legal complexity and often meaningful tax cost. Every plan should at least surface the question: **why not marry, and what are the consequences of not doing so?**

## The Conversation to Have

> "Without legal marriage, every protection between us has to be explicitly created. If something happens to one of us tonight, the law treats us as strangers. I want to walk through every account, every document, every default — and make sure they all reflect us as partners. This is going to take a few sessions. It's emotional and detailed. Are you up for it?"
