# Single Individuals (No Spouse, No Children)

Conventional wisdom often treats singles as low-priority for estate planning. In practice, many single people need an especially intentional plan because intestacy rarely produces the result they actually want.

## Why Singles Often Need Especially Intentional Plans

Without a will, intestacy passes assets to:
1. Parents (if living)
2. Siblings (if no parents)
3. Nieces/nephews (if no siblings)
4. More remote relatives
5. Eventually escheats to the state

This typically excludes:
- Long-term partners (no marriage = no inheritance)
- Friends, godchildren, mentees
- Charities they cared about
- Specific heirs they might prefer over default ones (e.g., one favored niece, not all 8)

## The Core Plan

### 1. Designate Heirs Deliberately

Pick beneficiaries based on actual relationships and wishes:
- Specific siblings, not "all my siblings equally"
- Specific nieces/nephews, not by default
- Friends, godchildren, mentees by name
- Charities — by full legal name with EIN

### 2. Comprehensive Incapacity Plan

Without a spouse, incapacity defaults often produce a result the user would not have chosen:
- Healthcare decisions may follow a state-specific surrogate hierarchy that privileges relatives over the trusted friend, partner, or chosen family member who actually knows the user's wishes
- Financial decisions may end up in a guardianship / conservatorship process if no valid POA or trust-management structure exists

**For many single people, the default incapacity package should include:**
- Healthcare proxy with explicit primary + multiple backups
- Durable financial POA with explicit primary + backups
- Living will
- HIPAA authorization listing trusted friends, possibly chosen family
- Hospital visitation authorization

Don't default to "parent" for healthcare proxy if parent is elderly or unfit. Choose based on capability, not default.

### 3. Emergency Reachability Plan

Single people without resident family need explicit emergency contacts:
- A nearby friend with a spare key and the user's schedule
- A neighbor who can check on welfare
- A coworker who would notice absence
- An emergency contact card in wallet
- File of medical conditions / medications stored where it can be found

### 4. Pet Planning

Singles often have pets without backup caregivers. Build an explicit pet-care plan, often with a named caregiver plus dedicated funding; in some cases that means a formal pet trust, and in others a simpler structure is enough (see [PETS-AND-ANIMALS.md](../assets/PETS-AND-ANIMALS.md)).

### 5. Beneficiary Designations

Without a spouse:
- Retirement accounts: name specific person or trust
- Life insurance: same
- 401(k) does NOT have automatic spouse rule for unmarried individuals
- HSA: name specific person (note: non-spouse HSA distributions taxable)
- TOD/POD on bank/brokerage

### 6. Fiduciary Selection

For singles, fiduciary choice is harder because there's no obvious "spouse" default. Considerations:

- **Trusted friend** — if reliable and willing
- **Sibling or niece/nephew** — if good relationship
- **Professional or corporate fiduciary** — neutral but expensive (especially for smaller estates where corporate trustee fees become a meaningful percentage)
- **Co-fiduciaries** — friend + corporate trustee combination

**Name backups.** A 60-year-old single person naming a 65-year-old college friend as executor usually needs a meaningfully younger or otherwise more durable backup.

### 7. Disposition of Remains

Without a spouse, default disposition authority falls to next-of-kin (parent, sibling). If you want a specific friend or partner to handle this, an explicit disposition-of-remains document is essential.

### 8. Beneficiary of Last Resort

Common scenario: single person leaves entire estate to one sibling. Sibling predeceases. Without a contingent provision, the estate passes by default — often not where intended.

Usually specify:
- Primary beneficiary
- Contingent beneficiary
- Beneficiary if all named beneficiaries predecease (often charity)

### 9. Charitable Planning

Singles without children often choose to leave significant portions to charity. Vehicles:
- Direct bequest in will
- Beneficiary designation on retirement account (charity tax-efficient — pays no income tax)
- Donor-Advised Fund with successor advisor (someone you trust to direct grants)
- Private foundation only when philanthropic scale, governance ambitions, and tolerance for administration justify it
- Charitable Remainder Trust (income to self for life, remainder to charity)

For singles with no family heirs, a planned philanthropy strategy can transform the legacy.

### 10. Life Story / Ethical Will

Singles who leave the estate to charities or to nieces/nephews who didn't grow up close to them often have rich life stories that would otherwise die with them. The ethical will is especially valuable: stories, photos, recipes, life lessons, advice for the next generation in the family.

## Special Categories

### Single Parent

A single parent with minor children has:
- Sole responsibility for guardianship designation (the other parent may be deceased, absent, or the user explicitly doesn't want them to have custody)
- Should document any reasons for excluding the other biological parent (helpful if other parent later asserts custody claims)
- Often higher life insurance need (single income supporting children)
- Trust planning for children is usually the default, with outright distributions only in unusually simple or low-dollar situations

### Widowed

Specific concerns:
- Update plan after spouse's death — old plan reflects two-spouse structure
- File Form 706 if late spouse's estate hasn't (DSUE preservation)
- Likely need new healthcare proxy, POA agent
- Reconsider beneficiary designations
- Consider whether prior estate plan needs full overhaul under new circumstances

### Recently Divorced

- Plan often deeply intertwined with ex-spouse — extract immediately
- Update beneficiary forms (especially ERISA — not auto-revoked)
- Update healthcare proxy
- Update financial POA
- Reconsider executor and guardian if previously named ex-spouse
- Coordinate with divorce decree obligations (life insurance maintenance often required)

### Long-term Single by Choice

Some people are single by choice and have rich chosen-family networks. The plan should reflect this:
- Chosen family may be more important than blood family
- Document relationships explicitly to avoid challenges from biological relatives
- Consider letters of explanation for unusual choices

### LGBTQ+ Single (Without a Partner)

Family-of-origin estrangement is more common in LGBTQ+ populations. Consider:
- Explicit exclusion of unsupportive family members
- Chosen-family beneficiaries
- Trusted friend as healthcare proxy (in some hospitals, blood family historically tried to override partner/friend authority — explicit documentation matters)

## Common Failure Modes

- **No plan at all.** Defaults rarely produce desired outcome.
- **Naming an aging parent as beneficiary** without a contingent — parent predeceases or assets disrupt parent's Medicaid.
- **Naming a sibling as healthcare agent who lives 2000 miles away.**
- **Letting employer-provided life insurance default to "estate"** — forces probate and exposes to creditors.
- **No pet plan.**
- **Forgetting digital legacy** — singles often have substantial digital footprints (creator accounts, online business, crypto) without anyone knowing.
- **Failing to file portability for late spouse** if widowed.

## The Conversation to Have

There's often no conversation partner for a single person. The plan often involves the single user thinking aloud with the planner about what matters and who matters. This makes the **letter of wishes and ethical will** unusually important — they capture intent and reasoning that a future family member might need to honor.

## A Single Person's Plan in Brief

1. Will naming specific beneficiaries with contingencies
2. Comprehensive incapacity package (POAs, healthcare proxy, living will, HIPAA)
3. Beneficiary designations on every account
4. Pet trust if applicable
5. Disposition-of-remains directive
6. Letter of instruction with emergency contacts
7. Digital asset inventory + password access plan
8. Funeral/disposition pre-arranged or funded
9. Charitable plan if intent exists
10. Letter of wishes / ethical will
