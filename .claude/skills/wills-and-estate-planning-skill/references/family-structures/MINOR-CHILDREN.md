# Minor Children

If you have children under 18, this is one of the highest-stakes sections of your plan.

## The Two Decisions

### 1. Guardianship of the Person

Who raises the children if both parents die?

This is a court-approved decision. Your nomination in the will is given strong weight but the court ultimately decides what's in the child's best interest.

### 2. Guardianship of the Estate / Trust

Who manages the children's money?

Often a different person than the guardian. Separating these creates **healthy checks and balances**.

## Choosing the Guardian (of the Person)

Core considerations to weigh:

1. **Existing relationship.** The guardian should already be someone the children love and trust. Not "the most responsible relative" — the relative the kids actually want to be with.
2. **Values alignment.** Religion, education philosophy, discipline, lifestyle. These matter for daily life.
3. **Geographic disruption.** Moving children across the country after losing both parents piles trauma on trauma. A nearby aunt may be a better choice than a distant grandparent.
4. **Age and health.** Grandparents are emotional choices but may not live long enough to finish raising a toddler.
5. **Their family situation.** A childless sibling may be different from one with four kids of their own.
6. **Stability** — relationship, employment, location.
7. **Willingness.** Ask first. Do not surprise someone with custody of your kids.
8. **Marriage stability** — if the proposed guardian is married, both spouses must agree; consider what happens if they later divorce.

### Name backups, usually more than one if the facts justify it

The first-choice guardian may have died, divorced, become ill, or said no. Without backups, the court chooses.

## Choosing the Trustee (of the Money)

Different criteria:

- **Financial competence** — are they good with money?
- **Discipline** — will they say no to expensive demands?
- **Independence** — are they emotionally separable from the kids' immediate desires?
- **Longevity** — will they be around for the trust's full term?
- **Reasonable fees** — corporate trustees are professional but expensive

**Common structure:**
- **Family member as guardian of person** (loves the kids, raises them)
- **Different family member or corporate trustee as financial trustee** (manages the money objectively)
- **The two coordinate** — guardian submits requests, trustee evaluates

This prevents:
- Guardian using kids' inheritance for their own household expenses
- Conflict between "I'm doing my best" and "you're spending too much"
- Burnout — being both raising the kids AND managing the money is too much

## Trust for Minor Children

**As a rule, do not leave significant assets directly to minors.** Two reasons:

1. **Court-appointed guardian of property** — formal court process, ongoing oversight, fees, restrictions. Trustee fees and court costs eat into the inheritance.
2. **Outright distribution at age of majority (18 in most states, 21 in some)** — an 18-year-old with $400K is often a bad outcome.

### Minor-Children Trust Structures

**Pot Trust ("Common Trust") for All Children:**

- Single trust for all kids until youngest reaches a specified age (often 25)
- Trustee distributes for HEMS as needed, regardless of which child
- Reflects how parents would treat kids — the one with greater need at any given moment gets more
- At pot termination, divides equally (or per terms) among children
- Continues as separate trusts for each child or distributes outright

Best when:
- Children are similar ages
- Parents want flexibility
- Equal-treatment-by-need is the philosophy

**Separate Trusts for Each Child:**

- Each child has their own trust from death
- Each child has their own trustee or co-trustee
- Distributions on each child's own timeline

Best when:
- Children are very different ages
- Parents want clear individual treatment
- Conflict between children is a concern

### Distribution Schedule

Typical pattern:

- **Until age 18-25**: Pot trust or separate trust; HEMS distributions; trustee covers college, vehicles, summer programs, etc.
- **Age 25**: Optional partial distribution (e.g., 1/3 of principal)
- **Age 30**: Optional partial distribution (1/3 of remainder)
- **Age 35**: Optional balance distribution OR continuing trust

**Alternative — Lifetime / Long-Duration Trust:**

Some families prefer trusts that continue for life or for a very long time, providing creditor protection, divorce protection, and continued professional management. Beneficiary may become co-trustee at a later age, or the trust may remain professionally administered. This is often most valuable for:
- Higher-wealth families
- Beneficiaries in litigation-prone professions
- Beneficiaries who married later or had unstable marriages

### HEMS Standard

Most discretionary trusts use the **Health, Education, Maintenance, and Support** standard:

- **Health** — medical, dental, mental health, fitness, wellness
- **Education** — tuition, books, room and board through graduate school
- **Maintenance** — clothing, food, transportation, household
- **Support** — accustomed standard of living

Trustee uses judgment within HEMS to approve or deny requests.

## Life Insurance for Young Parents

**Usually one of the highest-leverage Tier-1/2 financial decisions** for parents of minor children.

Start with the real economic problem, not a canned multiple:
- Income replacement during the years the children still depend on that parent
- Mortgage or rent runway
- Childcare / household-management replacement if one parent dies
- Education funding if that is a core family goal
- Debt payoff and emergency reserve for the surviving household
- Any special-needs or long-term-support obligation for a child

**Quick heuristics like "10x income" are only a smell test, not a plan.** A stay-at-home parent may need major coverage despite little or no earned income; a high earner with a financially independent spouse, no mortgage, and grown children may need less than the heuristic suggests.

**Both parents usually need coverage** — including a parent whose labor is primarily childcare, scheduling, transportation, and household management.

**For many families, level term insurance is the default starting point.** Permanent insurance becomes more interesting when there are estate-tax, special-needs, business-succession, asset-protection, or long-horizon liquidity goals that justify the cost and complexity.

## 529 Plans for Minor Children

If education funding is a real goal, set up at birth (or soon after) and usually:
- **Name a successor owner or other plan-designated successor** so control continuity is explicit
- **Coordinate with trust** — at parent's death, trust may continue funding
- **Update beneficiary** as children's needs change

Without a named successor, the fallback rule depends on the specific 529 program. In some plans the beneficiary becomes owner; in others the estate or another default mechanism applies. Often not what was intended.

## UTMA/UGMA Caution

Uniform Transfers to Minors Act / Uniform Gifts to Minors Act accounts:

- Become the **child's outright property at age of majority** (18 or 21 depending on state)
- Cannot be reclaimed
- A completed gift is generally not reversible

An 18-year-old with $200,000 in a UTMA is common and sometimes disastrous. **For substantial gifts to minors, use a 2503(c) trust or other trust structure** that allows extended control.

## Pretermitted-Heir Statutes

Most states protect children **born or adopted after the will was signed** with default share statutes. Without explicit handling, an after-born child can claim a statutory share that disrupts the plan.

**Standard clause:**

> "Any child born to or adopted by me after the date of this Will shall share equally with my other children in any provision benefiting my children."

Or, if not desired:

> "I have intentionally not provided for any after-born or after-adopted children."

Be explicit either way.

## Special Needs / Disability in a Child

If any child has a disability or support profile that may require ongoing supplemental support, **pause before recommending an outright inheritance**. A third-party Special Needs Trust often deserves serious consideration when preserving SSI, Medicaid, or other means-tested benefits may matter.

This is not automatic for every diagnosis. Mild ADHD or ordinary learning issues usually do **not** make an SNT necessary by themselves. Autism, Down syndrome, severe mental illness, traumatic brain injury, cognitive impairment, or any realistic future-benefits scenario should trigger a deeper benefits-aware review.

See [VULNERABLE-HEIRS.md](VULNERABLE-HEIRS.md).

## Adult Children Still Considered "Minors" Functionally

A child who is 19 but in the middle of a substance-abuse crisis, an 18-year-old who hasn't yet graduated high school, a 21-year-old still in college — legally adults but practically not ready for unrestricted inheritance. Discretionary trust with HEMS for these scenarios.

## Coordination With Beneficiary Designations

The most common error: parents set up a great trust for minors in the will, but the **life insurance still names the kids directly**. At death, the policy pays cash to the kids, triggering court-appointed guardian of property.

**Fix for life insurance and similar non-retirement assets:** if the intended beneficiary is a minor, route the asset to the trust or trustee structure rather than directly to the child.

Apply that logic carefully to:
- Life insurance
- TOD/POD accounts
- Annuities

For retirement accounts, **do not automatically point the account at the minors trust** just because the beneficiary is underage. Often a spouse-primary / child-or-trust-contingent structure is better. If an IRA or 401(k) is going to a trust, that trust has to be drafted and coordinated specifically for retirement-account rules.

## The Letter for the Guardian

Beyond the legal nomination, write a **letter to your chosen guardian** covering:

- What you want them to know about each child
- Educational philosophy
- Religious upbringing preferences
- Health considerations
- Personality nuances ("Sarah needs extra time to warm up to people")
- Important relationships (godparents, close friends, schools)
- Financial expectations — the trustee will fund what
- Final messages from you to your children, to be shared at appropriate ages

Not legally binding. Profoundly meaningful.

## The Conversation to Have

> "If something happens to both of us, [Name] and [Backup Name] would be the kids' guardians. We want to talk to you about it because you can't surprise someone with that role. We've also set up a trust so that all the financial stuff is managed separately — that's [Trustee Name]'s job, not yours. They handle the money, you focus on the kids. We've also written down what we want the kids to know. Are you in?"
