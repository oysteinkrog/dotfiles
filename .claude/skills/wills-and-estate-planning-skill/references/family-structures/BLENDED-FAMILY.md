# Blended Families (Second Marriages, Stepchildren)

One of the most litigation-prone categories in estate planning. Get this wrong and the result can be years or decades of family damage.

## The Core Tension

- **The new spouse** has legitimate expectations of continued lifestyle and the marital home.
- **The decedent's children from a prior relationship** have legitimate expectations of inheriting from their parent.
- **The default plan ("everything to spouse") is often too dangerous to use as the default.** The surviving spouse can later rewrite their will, remarry, spend down, or favor their own children. The first spouse's children may get little or nothing.

## The Default-Plan Failure Mode (Concrete)

- John, age 60, second marriage to Carol (age 55). Two kids from first marriage: Mike (35) and Sarah (32).
- John's will: "Everything to Carol; if Carol predeceases, equally to Mike and Sarah."
- John dies at 70.
- Carol inherits the house, the brokerage, the IRA, the life insurance — about $4M total.
- Carol writes a new will leaving everything to her own daughter from her first marriage.
- Carol dies at 85.
- Mike and Sarah receive nothing. In this outright-to-spouse fact pattern, they often have little or no practical recourse against Carol's later rewrite. The "verbal understanding" is unenforceable.

This is a very common and expensive blended-family failure pattern.

## The Workhorse Solution That Often Deserves First Look: QTIP Trust

**Qualified Terminable Interest Property trust:**

- On the first spouse's death, marital assets flow into the QTIP.
- Surviving spouse receives **all income for life** and may receive principal under a HEMS standard.
- When the surviving spouse dies, the **remaining principal passes to the first spouse's chosen beneficiaries** — often children from a prior relationship.
- For a U.S.-citizen surviving spouse, a properly structured and elected QTIP generally qualifies for the **unlimited marital deduction**, so federal estate tax is usually deferred at the first death. Noncitizen-spouse cases need separate QDOT analysis, and some states require separate state QTIP handling.
- QTIP assets are included in the surviving spouse's taxable estate at second death, which often preserves a later basis adjustment for includible appreciated assets. Do not summarize that as a universal "second step-up" for every asset class, because IRD and similar exceptions still exist.

Required elements:

1. Spouse must be entitled to all income at least annually
2. No one can have power to direct property to anyone other than spouse during spouse's lifetime
3. Election made on Form 706

### QTIP Variants

- **Reverse QTIP** — election to use GST exemption on QTIP for grandchildren-friendly remainder planning
- **Marital QTIP** — standard
- **QTIP-able marital trust** — created at first death, election made later (allows post-mortem flexibility)

### Trustee Selection in QTIP

**Default caution:** the surviving spouse usually should not be the sole trustee in a hard blended-family fact pattern (creates HEMS administration conflict and emotional friction with first-marriage children).

Often a strong structure in conflict-heavy blended-family facts:
- Independent corporate or professional trustee for administration and discretion
- Spouse as co-trustee for limited purposes (e.g., requesting distributions for HEMS)
- Trust protector with power to remove trustee, change situs, modify

## Other Blended-Family Tools

### Separate Trusts for Pre-Marriage Property

If each spouse came into the marriage with assets, fund separate revocable trusts before or early in the marriage. Document origins. Keep separate property separate.

### Life Insurance / ILIT for First-Marriage Children

The QTIP gives spouse income for life — but first-marriage children may wait decades to receive principal. Address with **ILIT-owned life insurance payable to first-marriage children** at first spouse's death:

- Provides immediate inheritance for first-marriage kids
- Surviving spouse keeps marital lifestyle through QTIP
- Equalization without forcing immediate sale of marital assets

### Prenuptial / Postnuptial Agreement

Specifies what each spouse waives. Some states require specific waiver language for a spouse to waive the elective share. Coordinate with the will and trust to avoid contradictions.

### Elective Share Awareness

Many common-law states give the surviving spouse a **statutory share** or augmented-estate claim regardless of the will. Without a valid waiver, attempts to disinherit a spouse often fail in whole or in part.

Community-property states use marital-property rules instead — the surviving spouse already owns half by operation of law.

### Stepchildren Inclusion

As a general rule, unadopted stepchildren do **not** inherit by default. If you want stepchildren included:
- Name them explicitly with full legal name
- Use specific bequests or shares
- Don't rely on "my children" language — that may exclude unadopted stepchildren

### Beneficiary Designations Coordination

Most common blended-family blunder: 401(k) still names "my wife" — but "wife" was the first wife from 1992. Update every beneficiary form on remarriage. **And** consider non-spouse contingent beneficiary structure for first-marriage children's protection.

### Disposition of Remains

In blended families, the surviving spouse and the adult children frequently disagree about funeral arrangements. Execute an explicit disposition-of-remains document naming a specific person (often the surviving spouse, but not always). Avoids hospital-side disputes.

### Family Meetings

Particularly important in blended families. Hold separate meetings:
1. With spouse alone
2. With children from the prior relationship
3. Possibly together (if family dynamics permit)

The goal is **transparency**: the children from the prior relationship should know the protective structure exists, and the surviving spouse should know they are being provided for.

## Step-by-Step Decision Process

1. **Identify the assets to flow to spouse vs. prior-relationship children at first death.**
2. **For assets to spouse:** outright? In QTIP for income+protection?
3. **For prior-relationship children at first death:** specific bequests outside QTIP? Life insurance via ILIT? Specific account?
4. **For QTIP at second death:** principal distributes to prior-relationship children — outright or in trust?
5. **Trustee selection.** Independent trustee for QTIP. Family trustee may serve elsewhere.
6. **Trust protector** — to handle changed circumstances, especially decades-long horizon.
7. **Coordination with prenup/postnup if any.**
8. **Beneficiary designations** updated.
9. **Disposition-of-remains** explicit.
10. **Family meeting** held or scheduled.

## Common Mistakes

- **No conflict-management plan for the second marriage.** Separate counsel often deserves serious consideration, especially when each spouse has different children or materially different goals.
- **QTIP without independent trustee or other real governance constraint.** Surviving spouse + first-marriage children can become an adversarial dynamic, so many files need a more objective administrator.
- **Forgetting to do anything for the first-marriage children at first death.** They wait decades for QTIP principal — life insurance to them at first death helps.
- **Stepchildren raised but not adopted, assumed included.** Usually wrong unless the documents name them clearly.
- **Forgetting elective share.** Disinheriting a spouse without a valid waiver is generally not possible.
- **No discussion with stepchildren.** The new spouse's children may have their own expectations; ignoring them creates conflict.

## When the Spouse Has Children Too

Symmetrical structure. Each spouse:
- Funds their own QTIP at their own death for the survivor
- Names their own children as remainder beneficiaries
- Allows the surviving spouse to use QTIP income/principal under HEMS
- Coordinates pre/postnup to clarify what's separate, what's marital, what's covered

Result: each spouse's children get their parent's wealth eventually; survivor is provided for during life.

## The Conversation to Have

> "I've thought about how to make sure both you [spouse] and my children from my prior relationship are protected. One structure that may fit is a marital trust — sometimes called a QTIP. When I die, the assets would go into a trust where you receive the income for life, and principal access would be defined in the document. When you eventually pass away, what's left would go to my children. That can protect everyone: you have what you need, and my children aren't unintentionally disinherited if circumstances change later. We may also look at life insurance or other first-death equalization so they do not have to wait for everything. Does that fit how you see it?"

This conversation, held during life, can materially reduce avoidable conflict later.
