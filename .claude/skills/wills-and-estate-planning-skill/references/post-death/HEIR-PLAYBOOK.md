# Heir Playbook — Receiving an Inheritance

Companion to the executor playbook, from the recipient's side.

## Immediate (First 48 hours)

1. **Don't make any major decisions yet.** Grief impairs judgment.
2. **Contact the executor.** They will reach out, but check in.
3. **Find out how to access the probate filings.** In many states beneficiaries and heirs can obtain the will once it is filed, but timing and access mechanics vary.
4. **Don't make promises** to other heirs about how you'll handle your share.

## First 30 Days

- Identify what you may receive (will, beneficiary designations on accounts)
- Engage independent counsel if substantial interests are at stake (don't rely on executor's attorney)
- Engage your own CPA / financial advisor
- Don't accept distributions yet — review qualified disclaimer option before taking benefits or exercising control

## Critical Decision: Should You Disclaim?

A **qualified disclaimer** within 9 months of death lets you refuse the inheritance, causing it to pass as if you predeceased.

Reasons to consider:
- You're already wealthy; adding to your estate creates more eventual estate tax
- You're in bankruptcy or facing creditors
- The asset is a burden (timeshare, contaminated land)
- Disclaiming lets the asset pass to next-in-line at no tax cost (e.g., generation-skipping)
- Strategic estate-tax planning

Requirements:
- In writing
- Within 9 months of death
- No acceptance of any benefit beforehand
- Cannot direct where the asset goes (it follows the will's contingent provisions)

Consult attorney before disclaiming.

## Inheriting Specific Assets

### Cash / Checking

- Wait for executor distribution
- Will arrive after taxes and creditors paid
- Consider parking in money market until you decide what to do

### Brokerage Account

- Stepped-up basis at date of death
- Can sell soon after death with minimal capital gains
- Or hold for further appreciation

### Real Estate

- Stepped-up basis
- Options: live in it, rent it, sell it
- §121 personal residence exclusion if you live in 2 of 5 years before sale
- Coordinate with siblings if co-inherited

### Inherited IRA (Traditional)

- Many adult non-spouse designated beneficiaries are subject to a year-10 outside deadline, rather than having unlimited lifetime stretch treatment
- If the original owner died on or after their required beginning date, annual distributions during years 1-9 may also apply
- Spouses and other eligible designated beneficiaries can have materially different rules
- Subject to ordinary income tax on each taxable withdrawal
- Strategy is usually bracket management, not reflexive lump-sum withdrawal
- Watch IRMAA (Medicare surcharges) and state tax

### Inherited IRA (Roth)

- Many non-spouse beneficiaries still face a year-10 outside deadline
- Distributions are generally tax-free if the Roth satisfied the 5-year rule; if not, earnings can be taxable until the rule is met
- Spouses and other eligible designated beneficiaries can have different timing options
- Strategy: maximize tax-free compounding where the rules and cash-flow picture justify it, but don't assume every case should wait until year 10

### Inherited 401(k)

- Similar tax framework, but plan terms are often less flexible than IRA rules
- Some plans force earlier payout, limit investment choices, or restrict how long the account can stay in-plan
- A direct transfer to an inherited IRA often improves flexibility if the plan permits and the transfer is done correctly

### Life Insurance Proceeds

- Generally tax-free to beneficiary
- Lump sum or installments per policy
- Don't rush to spend

### Business Interest

- Coordinate with attorney
- Buy-sell agreement may apply
- §754 election for partnership interests
- Successor planning urgent
- Often: structured sale rather than active operation

### Specific Bequests of Personal Property

- Receive when executor distributes
- Sentimental value often exceeds monetary value
- Photograph, document
- Insurance

## Sudden Wealth Syndrome — A Real Phenomenon

Receiving substantial inheritance, especially unexpectedly, can produce:
- Identity confusion ("am I still the same person?")
- Damaged relationships ("now everyone wants something")
- Poor financial decisions ("I should buy the thing I always wanted")
- Depression and guilt ("my parent had to die for this")
- Paralysis ("afraid to touch it")

**Practical advice:**

1. **Wait.** Do nothing major for 6-12 months.
2. **Park in safe assets** (high-yield savings, treasuries) while you decompress.
3. **Hire fee-only fiduciary advisor** (not commission-driven salesperson).
4. **Don't tell people.** Inheritance attracts predators (financial, romantic, family).
5. **Keep your job and lifestyle stable** for at least the first year.
6. **Therapy.** Financial therapist, grief counselor, or both.
7. **Document your intentions.** Write down what you want to accomplish before you decide what to do.

## Managing Sibling Dynamics

If you co-inherit with siblings:
- Don't treat every disagreement as purely personal; it is often grief, fear, or old family dynamics showing up as conflict
- Communicate in writing
- Get independent appraisals for disputed assets
- Use a mediator at first sign of real conflict
- Don't share an attorney with adverse siblings
- Separate the emotional dispute from the financial one

## Common Heir Mistakes

1. **Quitting a job** based on inheritance windfall
2. **Making large purchases** in first year
3. **Lending to friends/family** without contracts
4. **Investing in someone's business idea** without due diligence
5. **Buying a vacation home** that becomes a money pit
6. **Disclosing inheritance** widely (predator magnet)
7. **Taking IRA lump sum** without tax planning
8. **Selling appreciated assets** without basis-step-up planning
9. **Trusting the executor's attorney** as your own
10. **Not updating your own estate plan** after receiving inheritance
