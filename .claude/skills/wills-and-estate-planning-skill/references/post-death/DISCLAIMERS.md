# Qualified Disclaimers

## What It Is

A **qualified disclaimer** lets a beneficiary refuse an inheritance, causing it to pass as if the disclaimant had predeceased the decedent.

Powerful post-death planning tool. The federal timing anchor is usually a **9-month window** from death, but state mechanics still need to be checked.

## Federal Requirements (IRC §2518)

1. **In writing**
2. **Within 9 months** of decedent's death (or if disclaimant is minor, within 9 months of reaching majority)
3. **Disclaimant has not accepted any benefit** of the property
4. **Disclaimant cannot direct** where the property goes — it passes per the decedent's plan as if disclaimant predeceased
5. **Filed with appropriate party** (executor, trustee, custodian — depends on asset type)

## State Requirements

State law also imposes requirements that may add to or modify federal requirements. Most state laws are coordinated with federal but verify.

## When to Use

### To Skip a Generation

A wealthy adult child disclaims, allowing the asset to pass to grandchildren without an additional layer of estate tax at the adult child's eventual death.

**Example:**
- Father dies with $1M IRA, leaves to son (age 60, already wealthy)
- Son disclaims; IRA passes to son's children (grandchildren)
- Asset bypasses son's eventual taxable estate

### To Avoid Creditors

If the disclaimant is in bankruptcy or facing creditor claims, disclaiming may protect the asset from being taken (state-specific; federal bankruptcy generally voids disclaimers made within 90 days of filing).

### To Refuse a Burdensome Asset

- Underwater mortgaged property
- Contaminated land (CERCLA exposure)
- Timeshare with ongoing obligations
- Business interest with personal guarantees

### To Optimize Tax Outcome

- Disclaimer redirects from high-tax-bracket heir to low-tax-bracket heir (e.g., charity, lower-bracket beneficiary)
- Disclaimer fixes a mistake in the will (where contingent provision is more favorable)

### To Re-Allocate Among Family

If the will provides for contingent beneficiaries who would have produced a better outcome (e.g., the surviving spouse disclaims to allow assets into the credit-shelter trust they should have been in originally), disclaimer creates a "post-mortem fix."

## When NOT to Use

- **Disclaimant has already accepted** any benefit (taken income, used the property)
- **Disclaimant wants to direct** where it goes (can't — only follows existing terms)
- **Asset is needed by the disclaimant**
- **Disclaiming worsens the tax outcome** (e.g., contingent recipient is in higher bracket)

## Partial Disclaimers

Allowed. Disclaim some but not all of a bequest. For example, disclaim 30% of an IRA, accept 70%.

## Ramifications by Asset Type

### IRA / Retirement

- Disclaim within 9 months (federal) and complies with state requirements
- Asset passes to contingent beneficiary per the form
- Be careful: disclaimer cannot redirect — verify the contingent beneficiary is the desired recipient

### Life Insurance

- Disclaim with insurance company per their procedures
- Verify contingent beneficiary

### Trust Distributions

- Disclaim per trust terms; coordinate with trustee

### Real Estate

- Recordable disclaimer in county records
- Coordinate with executor and title insurance

### Joint Tenancy with Right of Survivorship

- Limited circumstances allow disclaimer of survivorship interest
- Highly state-specific
- Specialized advice required

## Procedure

1. **Consult attorney early** — well before the 9-month window
2. **Confirm the contingent beneficiary** is the desired recipient
3. **Confirm no benefit accepted** by potential disclaimant
4. **Draft disclaimer** per state and federal requirements
5. **Sign and notarize**
6. **File with appropriate party** (executor, plan administrator, etc.)
7. **Document filing** for future reference

## Common Mistakes

1. **Missing the 9-month deadline** (rare relief available; expensive)
2. **Accepting partial benefit** before disclaiming (disqualifies disclaimer)
3. **Disclaiming without verifying contingent beneficiary**
4. **Forgetting state-specific requirements**
5. **Forgetting that disclaimer is irrevocable**

## Tax Treatment

- Disclaimed asset is treated for transfer-tax purposes as if decedent transferred directly to contingent beneficiary
- No additional gift-tax consequence to the disclaimant
- Income tax: disclaimant did not receive the asset, so no income tax to disclaimant
- Contingent beneficiary takes with same basis (stepped-up at decedent's death)
