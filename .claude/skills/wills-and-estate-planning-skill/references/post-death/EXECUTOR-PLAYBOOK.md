# Executor Playbook (Detailed)

Companion to [EXECUTOR-CHECKLIST.md](../../assets/EXECUTOR-CHECKLIST.md). This is the deeper guidance.

## Pre-Death (If Possible)

If terminal illness is confirmed and the executor is known:
- Sit down with the testator and review the asset inventory
- Confirm location of all original documents
- Understand pre-existing arrangements (funeral pre-paid? grave plot?)
- Get attorney contact info
- Get CPA contact info
- Understand any pending business matters

## Day 1-7

### Immediate Tasks (within 48 hours)

1. **Pronouncement and death certificate.** Funeral home arranges. Order a working stack of certified copies, often around 10-15, scaled to the likely institution count.
2. **Funeral arrangements.** Per Letter of Instruction or family discussion.
3. **Notify Social Security** (1-800-772-1213). Funeral home often does this.
4. **Find and review original will + trust.** Locked in attorney's office? Safe deposit box?
5. **Secure home.** Lock doors. Change locks if estranged relatives might enter. Take pets in.
6. **Refrigerator.** Throw out perishables.
7. **Mail handling.** Set up controlled forwarding, hold, or monitored access consistent with occupancy, fraud, and privacy concerns.

### Within Week 1

- Take photos and video of the home and contents (insurance, dispute defense)
- Inventory rooms — note items per Personal Property Memorandum
- Notify three credit bureaus to flag against ghost fraud
- Notify employer (final paycheck, benefits, group life)
- Notify pension administrator
- Notify Veterans Affairs if applicable
- Cancel: cell phone, streaming, gym, club memberships
- Secure vehicles (gather all keys)
- Open up communication channel with attorney
- Identify location of safe deposit box and key
- Identify cryptocurrency seed phrase locations (per Digital Inventory)

## Week 2-6: Probate Initiation

### File the Will

- Original will to probate court in decedent's county of domicile
- Petition for letters testamentary
- File any required notices (heirs, creditors)
- Pay court filing fees
- Bond (if required by will or court)
- Attend first hearing (if required) — many states allow paper-only

### Get the Letters

- Letters testamentary (or "letters of administration" if no will / no qualified executor)
- This is the magic document — needed by every bank, brokerage, etc.
- Order multiple certified copies (5-10)

### EIN and Estate Account

- Apply for estate EIN online at IRS (free, instant)
- Open estate checking account (most banks have specific process)
- Use ONLY this account for estate transactions

## Week 6 - Month 4: Inventory and Notification

### Notify Beneficiaries

- Send copies of relevant will provisions or notices as required by local law or counsel
- Provide rough timeline expectations (typically 9-18 months)
- Establish communication cadence (monthly updates work well)
- Don't make specific promises about timing or amounts

### Inventory Assets

- Real estate — appraisals
- Investments — date-of-death statements
- Retirement — date-of-death values, beneficiary status
- Life insurance — claims process
- Personal property — photographs, descriptions, estimates
- Business interests — formal valuation if estate-tax filing required
- Crypto — date-of-death values; coordinate with exchange or custodian
- Out-of-state real property — engage local counsel for ancillary probate

### Direct Beneficiary-Designated Assets

- Retirement accounts → directly to named beneficiaries (not through estate)
- Life insurance → directly to named beneficiaries
- TOD/POD accounts → directly to named beneficiaries
- These don't go through probate — handle in parallel

### Income Tax — Final 1040

- File decedent's final income tax return (Form 1040) for year of death
- Cover period from January 1 to date of death
- Surviving spouse often can file MFJ for that year, but not if the survivor remarried before year-end and subject to the usual personal-representative coordination rules
- Indicate "deceased" on signature line

### Fiduciary Income Tax — Form 1041

- Estate is its own taxpayer for income earned during administration
- File Form 1041 annually until estate closes
- Use estate EIN

## Month 4-9: Tax and Creditor Resolution

### Form 706 (if required)

- Required if gross estate exceeds federal exemption ($15M in 2026), OR
- Surviving spouse wants to elect portability (DSUE preservation), OR
- State estate tax requires it
- **Due 9 months from date of death** — apply for 6-month extension if needed
- Engage CPA experienced with 706
- Coordinate with appraisers
- Document every asset, every valuation
- Consider alternate valuation date election (6 months later) if values fell
- Allocate GST exemption if applicable
- File Form 8971 on time using the current rule: generally 30 days after the earlier of the Form 706 due date (including extensions) or the actual filing date

### Creditor Claims

- Publish notice in local paper (state-specific requirement)
- Send direct notice to known creditors
- Wait the state-specified claim period (typically 4-6 months)
- Pay valid claims in priority order:
  1. Administration expenses
  2. Funeral and burial
  3. Taxes (federal, state, income)
  4. Secured creditors (mortgages)
  5. Unsecured creditors
- Dispute invalid claims
- Document everything

### Ongoing Asset Management

- Pay mortgages
- Pay property taxes
- Pay utilities
- Maintain insurance
- Manage rental properties
- Handle capital calls on private fund interests
- Coordinate margin debt resolution

### Liquidity

- Sell assets as needed to fund tax / debt / specific bequests
- Coordinate with financial advisor on which assets to sell
- For appreciated assets: consider step-up basis (sale near death = minimal gain)
- For margin accounts: address debt to prevent force-liquidation

## Month 9-18+: Distribution and Closure

### Interim Distributions

- May make interim distributions to beneficiaries with attorney guidance
- Withhold reserves for unresolved tax / creditor issues
- Document what's distributed and to whom

### Final Tax Returns and Clearances

- If Form 706 was filed, decide whether you need an IRS estate tax closing letter or an account transcript; transfer certificates are a separate nonresident-estate tool, not the same thing
- Receive state tax clearance if applicable
- Final fiduciary income tax return (Form 1041, marked "final")

### Final Accounting

- Comprehensive ledger of every transaction
- Inventory at start and end
- All income and expenses
- All distributions
- Submit to court for approval (formal probate) or to beneficiaries (informal)

### Get Releases

- Each beneficiary signs receipt for what they received
- Each beneficiary signs release of executor from further liability
- Attorney drafts these documents

### Close the Estate

- Petition court for executor discharge (formal probate)
- Close estate bank account
- Cancel EIN (optional — IRS leaves it open generally)
- File final Form 1041 marked "final"

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Distributing too early.** Personal liability if creditors emerge.
2. **Commingling estate and personal funds.** Liability + tax confusion.
3. **Missing 9-month federal estate tax deadline.** Penalties.
4. **Forgetting portability election.** DSUE can be lost, or the family may be pushed into late-relief procedures that should have been avoided.
5. **Missing qualified disclaimer window** (also 9 months). Better tax outcome lost.
6. **Failure to file Form 8971 when required** under the current timing rules. Penalties.
7. **Force-sale of margin account during probate.** Tax consequences.
8. **No notification to credit bureaus.** Identity theft.
9. **Continuing zombie subscriptions** for 18 months. Money wasted.
10. **Pets neglected** because no one took responsibility.
11. **Documents lost** because they weren't where the executor expected.
12. **Family conflict not managed.** Beneficiaries who feel ignored sue.

## Executor Compensation

- Statutory in most states (often percentage of estate value)
- Family-member executors often waive
- Compensation is taxable income to executor
- Document hours worked even if planning to waive

## Risk Management for Executor

- Maintain detailed contemporaneous records
- Communicate frequently with beneficiaries (even boring updates)
- Consult attorney on every meaningful decision
- Get written approval for unusual actions
- Carry executor errors-and-omissions insurance for complex estates
- Get court approval (or unanimous beneficiary consent) for risky decisions

## When to Hire a Co-Executor

- Estate complexity exceeds executor's expertise
- Executor lives far from decedent's residence
- Executor has personal conflicts with beneficiaries
- Concerns about contests
- Family wants professional oversight

Corporate trustees / bank trust departments can serve as co-executor for $5K-$25K+ depending on estate size.

## When to Resign

- Conflict of interest emerges
- Health prevents proper service
- Beneficiaries lose confidence
- Personal liability risk too high

Resignation requires court approval and typically a successor executor.
