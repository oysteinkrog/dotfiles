# Digital Legacy Planning

Everyone has a digital estate — email, photos, financial accounts, social media, crypto wallets, subscriptions, cloud storage. Without proper planning, heirs face frustrating access barriers, identity theft risk, and lost digital assets.

## Why Digital Planning Matters

- **Access:** family often locked out of email, photos, documents
- **Value:** crypto, domain names, creator accounts (YouTube, Substack, etc.) can be substantial
- **Security:** dormant accounts get breached, used for fraud
- **Identity theft:** deceased identities actively targeted
- **Grief:** social media accounts becoming memorials or haunting
- **Sentimental:** photos, messages, creative work

## RUFADAA (Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act)

Widely adopted across the U.S. (verify the user's state if this becomes outcome-determinative). Creates a three-tier hierarchy for digital asset access:

### Tier 1: Online Tool Settings
If the platform offers a "legacy" feature, that governs:
- **Google Inactive Account Manager** — designate someone; choose what to share
- **Facebook Legacy Contact** — memorialize or delete
- **Apple Digital Legacy** — contact receives access key to photos, contacts, etc.
- **Instagram Memorialization** — request memorialization or deletion
- **LinkedIn Memorialization**

**Use these first.** They override will/trust provisions.

### Tier 2: Will / Trust Provisions
If no online tool exists or was never configured, the will or trust can authorize a fiduciary to access digital assets. Use jurisdiction-appropriate authorization language rather than assuming one generic clause is enough everywhere.

### Tier 3: Terms of Service
Default is governed by each platform's TOS. Many TOS prohibit account sharing and may restrict access, transfer, or continued use after death.

## Categories of Digital Assets

### 1. Financial Accounts (High Value, High Priority)
- Online banking and brokerage
- Retirement accounts (401(k), IRA)
- PayPal, Venmo, Cash App, Zelle balances
- Cryptocurrency wallets and exchange accounts
- Robinhood, Coinbase, etc.
- Online-only banks (Ally, Chime, Sofi)

### 2. Communication
- Email (Gmail, Outlook, Yahoo, iCloud)
- Text messages
- Messaging apps (WhatsApp, Signal, Telegram)
- Video conferencing (Zoom, FaceTime)
- Social media DMs

### 3. Social Media
- Facebook, Instagram, Twitter/X, LinkedIn
- TikTok, Snapchat, YouTube
- Reddit, Mastodon, Bluesky
- Dating apps (often high-priority because of privacy, impersonation, and reputational concerns)

### 4. Cloud Storage / Photos
- Google Drive, Dropbox, OneDrive, iCloud
- Google Photos, Apple Photos, Amazon Photos
- Smugmug, Shutterfly, mixed photo services

### 5. Digital Media Libraries
- iTunes / Apple Music
- Kindle / audible (generally licensed, not owned — extinguishes at death)
- Steam, PlayStation, Xbox gaming libraries
- Spotify playlists (licensed)
- Netflix, streaming (subscription ends)

### 6. Creative / Intellectual Property
- YouTube channels (can be lucrative)
- Substack / Medium publications
- Patreon / OnlyFans creator accounts
- Blog / website ownership and domains
- Podcast channels
- Digital art / NFTs
- Original manuscripts / photography

### 7. Subscriptions and Services
- Netflix, Hulu, Disney+, HBO, etc.
- Amazon Prime
- Microsoft 365, Adobe Creative Cloud
- Dropbox, Evernote, 1Password
- Gym memberships, meditation apps
- Physical subscription boxes (Harry's, Birchbox)

### 8. Utility / Account Access
- Utility accounts (electric, gas, water, internet)
- Mobile phone account
- Streaming TV services
- Home automation (Ring, Nest, Sonos)
- Loyalty / rewards programs (airline miles, hotel points)

### 9. Cryptocurrency (Special Category)
- Exchange accounts (Coinbase, Kraken, Binance)
- Self-custody wallets (hardware wallets, MetaMask)
- Seed phrases and private keys
- NFT holdings
- DeFi positions
- Staking and yield positions

**Critical:** cryptocurrency held in self-custody wallet is permanently lost without seed phrase. More has been lost to poor key management than any other way.

## Digital Asset Inventory

Create and maintain a comprehensive inventory with:

### Per Account
- Platform / service name
- URL
- Username
- Credential location or access path (for example: "in password manager," "recovery process in sealed envelope")
- Two-factor authentication method
- Recovery email
- Recovery phone
- Security-question recovery location if relevant
- Account value / importance
- Desired action (memorialize, delete, transfer, download)
- Legacy contact / designated successor

### Per Device
- Device name
- PIN / password / biometric recovery path
- Find My iPhone / Android recovery
- Encryption key / FileVault / BitLocker
- Backup encryption key

## Password Management

### Password Manager (RECOMMENDED)
- 1Password, Bitwarden, Dashlane, or another password manager the user will actually maintain and update
- Single master password unlocks everything
- **Emergency Access feature:** trusted person receives access after waiting period
- Backup master password in secure location (safe, attorney, sealed envelope)

### Critical: Pass the Master Password to Fiduciary
- Use a controlled recovery path, not casual duplication
- Options can include a sealed envelope, attorney-controlled storage, home safe, or the password manager's emergency-access workflow
- Do **not** put the master password in the will or in a broad-access spreadsheet
- Document who can retrieve it, under what circumstances, and how the fiduciary will know the process exists

### Split-Key Strategies
- Use Shamir Secret Sharing (advanced)
- Split password: half to executor, half to backup
- Use 2-of-3 multisig for crypto

## Key Documents to Include

### RUFADAA Authorization in Will / Trust
Language granting fiduciary specific authority to access, copy, transfer, or delete digital assets.

Sample: "I grant my Executor/Trustee the same rights, powers, and authorities with respect to my digital assets, as defined under RUFADAA (or any successor law), as I would have personally, including the authority to access, transfer, control, modify, copy, archive, remove, or delete such digital assets."

### Digital Asset Inventory (separate from will)
Keep separate from will because:
- Password changes would otherwise require will amendment
- Not desirable in public probate record
- Keep in secure location, reference in will

### Online Tool Pre-Designations
- Google Inactive Account Manager (set up 5-min task)
- Facebook Legacy Contact
- Apple Digital Legacy
- Instagram memorial preferences

### Letter of Instruction
- Desired handling of each major account
- Sentimental / logistical notes
- Preferred memorialization

## Crypto-Specific Planning

Requires special attention because of irreversibility:

### Self-Custody Considerations
- **Hardware wallet** (Ledger, Trezor): device location + PIN + seed phrase
- **Seed phrase** (12-24 words): CRITICAL; without it, funds gone forever
- **Backup strategies:**
  - Steel plates (Cryptosteel, Billfodl) — fire/water resistant
  - Split phrase via Shamir's Secret Sharing
  - Multiple geographic locations

### Multi-Sig Wallets
- Require M-of-N signatures
- E.g., 2-of-3: you + spouse + attorney; 2 can spend
- Casa, Unchained Capital, Sparrow

### Exchange Accounts
- Often require a death certificate, identity verification, executor or heir documentation, and sometimes court papers
- Some exchanges (Coinbase, Kraken, Gemini, others) publish estate processes
- Time-consuming, but usually more recoverable than self-custody with a missing seed phrase

### Specific Instructions Needed

These instructions should usually live in a secure credential and key-management system, with the estate plan pointing to the recovery path rather than duplicating every secret in general planning documents.

- Private keys and their locations
- Seed phrases (encoded or split)
- Hardware wallet PINs
- Multi-sig quorum instructions
- Exchange account access and recovery path
- NFT wallets (separate from primary)

### Guidance for Fiduciary
- "What to do with crypto" — hold, sell, transfer
- Tax basis information (cost basis)
- Valuation challenge (pricing at date of death)

## Platform-Specific Notes

### Google
- Inactive Account Manager: designate up to 10 contacts; choose data to share; set inactivity trigger
- Google account holds: Gmail, Photos, Drive, YouTube, Android, Google Pay

### Apple
- Digital Legacy: designate contact; provide access key; contact generally must be age 13+ (country/region rules can vary)
- Access: photos, messages, notes, contacts, backups (not purchased media/apps/subscriptions)

### Facebook / Instagram
- Legacy contact can memorialize
- Page continues as memorial
- No one gets login
- Can optionally allow legacy contact to manage certain features

### Microsoft
- OneDrive now has a Digital Legacy feature for pre-authorized read-only access
- Broader Microsoft account content still often requires legal process and supporting documentation if no legacy setup exists
- Do not assume the executor can simply log in or transfer the account

### Amazon
- Estate team processes via document submission
- Account closed; digital purchases (Kindle books, etc.) not transferable

### Twitter / X
- Account can be deleted or memorialized (limited)
- Cannot be transferred

### LinkedIn
- Memorialization request
- Cannot be transferred

### Cryptocurrency Exchanges
- Many require executor or heir documentation, and some require court documentation such as letters testamentary
- Coinbase, Kraken, Gemini have estate processes
- Binance notoriously difficult

### Cloud Photo Services
- Apple Photos: via Digital Legacy
- Google Photos: via Inactive Account Manager
- Apple, Amazon, Google: purchased content typically non-transferable

## Common Failure Modes

1. **No password manager or key access** — family locked out entirely
2. **Crypto seed phrase lost** — $100K+ gone permanently
3. **Hardware wallet location unknown** — device lost with crypto
4. **No digital-access authorization or online-tool setup** — platform may refuse or sharply narrow cooperation
5. **Two-factor authentication without recovery** — authenticator app on dead phone
6. **Digital inventory in will** — public record, identity theft exposure
7. **Password list stored in password-protected file** — new layer of lockout
8. **Beloved YouTube channel** lost to TOS rather than captured for family
9. **Dating app account** remains active, awkwardly
10. **Auto-renewing subscriptions** continue billing estate for years
11. **Identity used for fraud** after death (Facebook, SSN, credit)
12. **Domain name registration lapses** and professional identity lost
