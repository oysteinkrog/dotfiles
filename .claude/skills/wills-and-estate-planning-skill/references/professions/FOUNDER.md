# Startup Founder Estate Planning

Founders of VC-backed or successful companies have unique estate-planning opportunities and pitfalls. Much of the highest-impact planning must happen BEFORE liquidity events.

## The Founder's Timing Problem

Estate planning leverage is highest when:
- Shares are cheap (pre-IPO)
- Valuation is uncertain
- Liquidity is limited

This is paradoxically the time founders have least cash and least attention for planning. But waiting until post-liquidity means missing the window.

**Key insight:** Plan BEFORE liquidity. The same shares gifted now at $1M FMV may be worth $100M later — and that $99M growth escapes estate tax.

## Pre-Liquidity Planning

### Gift Shares to Trust (Early is Better)

- Transfer founder shares to irrevocable grantor trust (IDGT) or dynasty trust
- Uses gift-tax exemption at current valuation
- All future growth escapes estate
- Spouse can remain beneficiary (SLAT)
- Children / grandchildren beneficiaries via dynasty structure

### 83(b) Election (Within 30 Days of Grant)

- Critical for restricted founder stock
- Election to pay tax on current FMV (usually minimal)
- Starts capital-gains clock
- Without 83(b): taxed on vesting at then-FMV (could be huge)

### QSBS (Section 1202) Planning

For C-corporation founders:
- Section 1202 is **not one rule for all founder stock anymore**. Post-enactment stock can follow a phased **3 / 4 / 5-year** regime with a higher cap, while older stock can remain on the older timeline and cap.
- Active trade/business
- C-corporation (not S or LLC)
- **Multiple taxpayer "stacking":** Gift shares to children / trusts before sale only after checking original-issue, holding-period, and trust-transfer mechanics
- Can multiply benefit 5-10x

### Grantor Trust Status

- Grantor (founder) pays trust's income tax
- Effectively additional gift to trust (not subject to gift tax)
- Trust grows tax-free from trust's perspective

### Valuation Discounts on Gifts

- Minority interest discount (15-30%)
- Lack of marketability discount (15-35%)
- Total: 30-50% discount on transferred value
- Enables transferring more shares within exemption

## Special Considerations

### Founder Stock Vesting

- Typical 4-year vest with 1-year cliff
- Unvested shares may not be transferable
- 83(b) on full grant addresses this (usual recommendation)

### Acceleration Clauses

- Single-trigger: shares vest on sale of company
- Double-trigger: accelerate on sale + post-sale termination
- Estate planning: shares typically accelerate on death (check plan)

### Non-Compete / Non-Solicitation

- Founders often signed strict non-competes
- Affects post-exit activities
- Affects family members continuing operations

### Secondary Sales (Pre-IPO Liquidity)

- Private liquidity via tender offers, secondary markets
- Partial diversification before IPO
- Tax: ordinary or capital gains
- Shares sold may hurt QSBS status (verify)

## At Liquidity Event

### Strategic Timing

- 10b5-1 plan for post-IPO phased selling
- Lockup expiration timing
- Tax-year management

### Charitable Strategies

**Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT):**
- Contribute appreciated shares
- CRT sells without immediate gain
- Income stream to family
- Remainder to charity

**Direct gift of appreciated stock:**
- Deduction at FMV
- No capital gains

**Private foundation:**
- For founders with $50M+ charitable intent
- Lasting family legacy vehicle

**Donor-advised fund:**
- Easier, faster
- Immediate deduction
- Grant-making flexibility

### IPO-Driven Planning

Post-IPO often triggers:
- Revisit estate documents (far more at stake)
- SLAT using exemption
- Dynasty trust funding
- ILIT for large life insurance
- Revocable trust expansion
- CPA strategy for tax optimization
- Domicile planning if moving

## Entity Structure Considerations

### C-Corp vs LLC vs S-Corp

- **C-Corp:** QSBS eligible; VC standard; double taxation offset by QSBS
- **S-Corp:** single taxation; not QSBS-eligible
- **LLC:** flexible but not QSBS-eligible; K-1 complications

Most startups should be C-corps to preserve QSBS.

### Founders Agreement

- Vesting
- Good leaver / bad leaver provisions
- Transfer restrictions
- Tag/drag-along rights
- Death: shares repurchased? buy-sell with other founders?

## Founders' Post-Exit Situation

Many first-time founders post-exit have:
- Lots of cash
- No business to run
- Time for deep estate planning
- Often no existing plan (was too busy before)

### Immediate (Within 30 Days)
- Revisit all estate documents
- Update beneficiaries
- Tax planning for year of sale

### Within 6 Months
- Comprehensive estate plan
- SLAT or other exemption-use trust
- Charitable planning
- Asset protection
- Family governance for new wealth

### Within 1 Year
- Philanthropic structure
- Next-gen education
- Next venture planning
- Real estate structuring

## Common Founder Scenarios

### Scenario A: Pre-IPO, Fully-Vested
- 32, engaged, $200K salary, 5% of $100M company, 83(b) elected early
- Plan: gift partial shares to grantor trust (valuation while still "uncertain"); retain majority for control; consider QSBS stacking; ILIT for small term

### Scenario B: Series B Founder
- 35, married, 2 kids, $300K salary, $100M valuation company, 15% ownership
- Plan: gift shares to dynasty trust NOW (valuation pre-Series C); SLAT funded with shares; ILIT; term life $2-5M

### Scenario C: Post-IPO, Locked Up
- 38, married, $50M liquid (post-lockup sale), $200M still locked
- Plan: 10b5-1 plan; charitable strategy (CRT or DAF); SLAT for remaining exemption; real estate diversification; establish family office

### Scenario D: Acquired Founder
- 42, cashed out $80M in acquisition, non-compete 3 years
- Plan: full HNW planning; dynasty trust; domicile consideration (often move to FL/TX/NV); philanthropic structure; asset protection; next-venture entity structure

### Scenario E: Serial Founder
- 50, multiple exits, $500M net worth, 3 operating investments
- Plan: full UHNW planning; private foundation; family office; multi-entity structure; venture-investment structure

## Founder-Specific Asset Classes

### Founder Stock
- Most valuable asset
- High concentration risk
- 83(b), QSBS, transferability rules

### Founder IP (pre-transfer to company)
- Personal IP vs. company IP
- Founder agreements usually assign
- Check for any retained IP

### Founder Loans to Company
- Often pre-VC
- May be forgiven or repaid
- Documentation matters

### Board Advisor Positions
- Usually non-transferable
- Fees taxable income
- Small equity possible

## Common Founder Failure Modes

1. **83(b) missed** — taxed on full vesting value (can be millions)
2. **Gift to trust post-valuation** — higher gift tax or exemption use
3. **Shares not QSBS-eligible** due to entity choice
4. **Non-QSBS entity** (LLC, S-corp) — missed 1202 opportunity
5. **Pre-IPO planning too late** — all growth in estate
6. **Exit without tax planning** — huge one-year tax
7. **Concentrated position post-IPO** — company troubles hit
8. **Lockup violation** via planned gifting
9. **No ILIT** for significant term life
10. **Founder ex-spouse issues** — equity division in divorce
11. **Blended family not addressed** — prior-relationship kids missed
12. **Domicile not established** pre-exit — state tax on full gain
13. **Post-exit no plan** — $80M suddenly and no structure

- Startup / corporate lawyer
- Tax CPA with QSBS / equity comp expertise
- Venture advisor
