# Corporate Executive Estate Planning

Senior corporate executives face distinct estate-planning considerations driven by concentrated employer stock, complex compensation, Rule 10b-5 restrictions, deferred comp plans, and public visibility.

## Compensation Components

### Base Salary
Straightforward W-2. Usually funds lifestyle.

### Annual Bonus
Cash + deferred. Vesting conditions.

### Restricted Stock Units (RSU)
- Vest over time (typically 3-5 years)
- Taxed as ordinary income at vesting
- Then capital gains on appreciation
- Estate planning: RSUs in estate at fair market value; unvested may or may not be in estate depending on plan terms

### Incentive Stock Options (ISO)
- Favorable tax treatment
- AMT trigger at exercise
- Capital gains if held 2 years from grant + 1 year from exercise
- Estate planning: ISOs die with holder (most plans); executor typically has limited time to exercise

### Non-Qualified Stock Options (NQSO)
- Taxable at exercise (spread as ordinary income)
- Transferable under some plan terms (valuable for estate planning)
- Can sometimes gift to grantor trust

### Employee Stock Purchase Plan (ESPP)
- Payroll deduction purchases
- Discount creates tax complexity
- Often underutilized asset

### Performance Shares / PSUs
- Vest based on performance metrics
- Estate inclusion depends on plan

### Deferred Compensation (409A Plans)
- Tax deferral; distribution per schedule
- **Highly problematic in estates:** distribution often accelerated at death; beneficiary faces large one-year tax hit
- Can't roll over like qualified plans
- Named beneficiary governs

### Supplemental Executive Retirement Plans (SERPs)
- Non-qualified; creditor of company if bankrupt
- Taxation at distribution per plan terms

### Golden Parachute
- Severance triggered by change of control
- Section 280G tax implications
- Often negotiable at hire

## Concentrated Stock Position

Most common executive issue: large percentage of wealth in employer stock.

### Risks
- Single-stock risk (company-specific)
- Industry risk
- Regulatory risk (Enron-style collapse)
- Correlation with job (lose job + lose wealth simultaneously)

### 10b5-1 Plan
- SEC Rule 10b5-1
- Adopted in advance (when not in possession of material non-public info)
- Pre-scheduled sales (trailing window)
- Safe harbor from insider trading claims
- **Current SEC rule set (adopted in 2023):** 90-120 day cooling-off period; only one plan active; overlapping-period restrictions

Use 10b5-1 for systematic diversification.

### Exchange Funds
- Private pool: contribute appreciated stock; receive diversified interest
- 7-year lockup
- Tax-deferred until exit
- Access for qualified purchasers ($5M+ investable)

### Charitable Remainder Trust
- Contribute appreciated stock to CRT
- Trust sells without immediate gain recognition
- Income stream to you / spouse for term or life
- Remainder to charity

### Direct Charitable Donation
- Appreciated stock → charity: deduction at FMV, no gain recognized
- Best for already-charitable executive

### Gifts to Family
- Use annual exclusion ($19K/person 2026)
- Use lifetime exemption ($15M 2026)
- Gift to grantor trust / SLAT for broader planning
- Value low, growth escapes estate

## Estate Planning Mechanics

### Stock Option Transferability
Check plan document:
- Most ISOs not transferable
- Some NQSOs transferable (to spouse, trust)
- If transferable: gift to grantor trust for growth outside estate
- Estate tax valuation methods

### Beneficiary Designations
- 401(k)/IRA beneficiary
- Deferred comp beneficiary
- Life insurance (often large group term through employer; plus individual)
- RSU beneficiary designation
- ESPP designation

### Spousal Consent
- Qualified plans require spousal consent for non-spouse beneficiary
- Executives with complex plans must document

### ILIT for Life Insurance
- Often own large life insurance from employer + personal
- ILIT removes death benefit from estate
- Executives paying for permanent policies often candidates

### Non-Compete / Non-Solicitation
- Executive agreements often restrict post-employment activities
- Affects estate plan for family continuing executive's business interests
- Survives death in some jurisdictions

## Domicile Considerations

- Many executives relocate for jobs
- Retirement: strategic domicile choice
- **Popular HNW retirement states:** FL, TX, NV, WY, TN, SD
- Document move thoroughly

## Pre-IPO / Post-IPO Distinctions

### Pre-IPO
- Shares illiquid, valuations uncertain
- **Early exercise of options** (83(b) election) starts capital-gains clock
- **Gift to grantor trust** before IPO captures growth
- **QSBS planning** if Section 1202 qualifies (C-corp, active trade/business, and acquisition-date-specific holding/cap rules verified)

### At IPO
- Lockup (180-day typical)
- Post-lockup, stagger sales
- 10b5-1 plan
- Charitable strategy

### Post-IPO
- Liquidity opportunity for estate planning
- SLAT, dynasty trust funding
- Consider QSBS if timing allows

## Retirement Planning

### Maxing Retirement Plans
- 401(k) / 403(b) / governmental 457 elective deferral: use the current IRS-indexed annual limit ($24,500 in 2026)
- Catch-up contributions: use the current IRS-indexed age-based catch-up limit ($8,000 in 2026 for most 401(k) / 403(b) / governmental 457 plans; a higher age-60-to-63 catch-up may apply)
- Mega backdoor Roth: only if plan design permits after-tax contributions and in-plan Roth conversion / rollover, and only after checking the current annual-additions limit plus employer-match interaction
- Deferred comp plans (non-qualified)
- Stock options in tax-efficient sequencing

### Post-Employment
- 401(k) rollover decisions
- Net Unrealized Appreciation (NUA) for employer stock in 401(k) — one-time tax opportunity
- Roth conversion in gap years between retirement and RMDs

## Common Scenarios

### Scenario A: Mid-Career VP, $3M Net Worth
- 45, married, 2 kids, $800K base + $500K bonus + $1M RSU annual vesting
- Concentrated: $1.5M employer stock
- Plan: 10b5-1 plan; term life; ILIT for permanent; joint revocable trust

### Scenario B: Senior VP, $10M Net Worth
- 55, married, adult kids, $500K base + $1M bonus + $2M equity vests annually
- Deferred comp plan $1M balance
- Plan: credit-shelter / QTIP; ILIT; SLAT using exemption; charitable strategy; deferred comp beneficiary planning

### Scenario C: C-Suite, $30M Net Worth
- 58, married, $100M company, own 1%, unvested options $10M
- Plan: comprehensive UHNW planning; DAPT for liability; family limited partnership; dynasty trust

### Scenario D: CEO, $200M Net Worth
- 62, founder-CEO, family office territory
- Plan: full Tier 4/5 planning; dynasty + GST; private foundation; 10b5-1 for diversification; gift to dynasty trust using the current-law exemption intentionally

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Over-concentration in employer stock** — Enron/WeWork scenarios
2. **Deferred comp at death** — large bunched tax hit
3. **10b5-1 plan violated** or inadequate
4. **ISO incentive lost** (sold too early, lost LTCG treatment)
5. **Golden parachute tax consequences** (280G) unmanaged
6. **Non-compete blocks estate from continuing business relationships**
7. **Spousal consent for beneficiary missed**
8. **NUA opportunity missed** at retirement
9. **Group term life bigger than estate** — inclusion issue
10. **Post-IPO lockup planning not optimized**
11. **Pre-IPO trust gift too late** — missed growth outside estate
12. **Equity compensation in grantor trust with tax issues**

## Professional Team

- Estate attorney with executive-comp experience
- CPA with AMT / ISO / NQSO expertise
- Financial advisor with concentrated-stock experience
- Insurance broker for ILIT + exec disability
- Tax attorney (for 409A, 83(b), 280G issues)
