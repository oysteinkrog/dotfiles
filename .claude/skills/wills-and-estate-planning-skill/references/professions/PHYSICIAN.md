# Physician Estate Planning

Physicians face specific estate-planning needs driven by high liability exposure, distinct compensation and retirement vehicles, disability risk, and practice succession.

## Key Features of Physician Planning

### Malpractice Liability Exposure
- Claims made years after incident
- Judgments can exceed coverage
- Personal assets at risk
- Reputation damage even from non-meritorious suits

### Compensation Complexity
- Salary (W-2 for employed, K-1 for partner)
- Production bonuses
- RVU-based incentives
- Practice ownership distributions
- Hospital executive compensation
- Research grants
- Consulting income
- Locum tenens work
- Expert witness fees
- Speaking / teaching fees
- Publishing royalties (rare but significant for academics)

### Retirement Vehicles
- 401(k) / 403(b)
- 457(b) non-qualified plans (specific to hospital / non-profit employment)
- Defined benefit / cash balance plans (high earners, sometimes with $200K+ annual contribution)
- SEP-IRA or solo 401(k) (practice owners)
- IRAs
- Deferred comp arrangements

### Disability Risk
- Most physicians disable before dying
- Own-occupation disability insurance critical
- Residency and specialty matter (surgeons higher rates)

## Asset Protection (Priority #1 for Physicians)

### Malpractice + Umbrella Insurance
- Malpractice: specialty-appropriate limits ($1M/$3M typical; $3M/$5M for high-risk)
- Personal umbrella: $5M-$10M
- Doesn't cover intentional acts or certain HR claims
- Evaluate carrier solvency (Golden Door, MLMIC, state carriers)

### State Exemptions
- **Florida:** unlimited homestead; tenancies by entirety; strong asset protection
- **Texas:** unlimited homestead; tenancies by entirety
- **States with DAPT:** NV, SD, DE, AK, WY
- **Retirement plans:** generally unlimited (ERISA)
- **Life insurance cash value:** varies by state
- **Annuities:** varies by state

### Domestic Asset Protection Trust (DAPT)
- Self-settled spendthrift trust in favorable state
- Transfer assets while solvent; 5-10 year statute of repose
- Protects from subsequent claims
- Costs: $10K-$30K to establish; ongoing trustee fees

### Entity Structuring
- **Practice in professional corporation (PC/PLLC):** limits vicarious liability
- **Holding company** for passive assets (real estate, etc.)
- **Family limited partnership** for family investments
- **Charging-order protection** (LLCs) for equity in investment real estate

### Titling for Married Physicians
- **Tenants by entirety** (non-community-property states)
- Home, bank accounts, brokerage as TBE
- Protected from creditors of one spouse alone
- Lost if divorce or move to non-TBE state

### Non-Citizen Spouse Considerations
- QDOT planning (estate tax)
- Citizenship planning (long-term)

## Practice Succession

### Solo Practitioner
- Selling practice to junior / PA at retirement
- Valuation: multiples of revenue or EBITDA
- Transition period (2-5 years)
- Seller financing common
- Estate plan: buy-sell funded by life insurance for surviving family

### Partnership Practice
- **Buy-sell agreement** between partners
- Funded by life + disability insurance on each partner
- Valuation formula (multiple of earnings, book value, or appraisal)
- Trigger events: death, disability, retirement, withdrawal
- **Pro-rata buyout** of departed partner's equity

### Group Practice
- More complex; often with hospital-affiliated structures
- Deferred comp and pension vesting
- Non-compete clauses affect transition

### Academic / Hospital Employment
- Usually no practice ownership
- Deferred comp and pensions are main "equity"
- Vesting schedules matter

## Retirement Optimization

### Defined Benefit / Cash Balance Plans
- High-earning partners can contribute $200K-$300K+/year
- Combined with 401(k): total $300K-$400K/year
- Significant tax deferral
- Creditor protected (generally)

### Mega Backdoor Roth
- After-tax contributions + in-plan conversion
- Allows much larger Roth accumulation

### Roth Conversions
- Consider in lower-income years
- Before Medicare age (for IRMAA planning)
- Pre-tax to post-tax for heirs (avoids 10-year rule distribution tax bunching)

### QDRO (Qualified Domestic Relations Order) Awareness
- Divorce splits of qualified retirement plans
- Post-divorce: re-verify beneficiaries

## Disability Insurance

- **Own-occupation, specialty-specific:** essential
- **Individual policy** (portable) vs. group (tied to job)
- Benefit period to age 65-67
- Monthly benefit approximating income
- Premium is large (~1-3% of income) but worth it

### Estate Planning Integration
- Disability income if triggered: funds living while alive
- Should complement, not replace, life insurance
- Separate from long-term care insurance

## Long-Term Care

- Higher than average physician net worth often means self-funding viable
- But consider hybrid life + LTC for asset protection
- Decision depends on wealth tier

## Unique to Physicians

### Student Loan Consideration (Earlier Career)
- Public Service Loan Forgiveness for non-profit/hospital work
- Income-driven repayment strategies
- Refinancing considerations
- Loans die with debtor (federal) — may affect estate plan priorities

### Research Grants / IP
- NIH / NSF grants: what happens at death?
- Patents on inventions
- Publication royalties
- Named chairs

### International Medical Graduates
- Visa / green card considerations
- Foreign family complications
- Non-US assets
- Pre-immigration trust planning

### Locum / Independent Work
- Self-employment tax
- Malpractice between engagements (tail coverage)
- Solo 401(k) or SEP-IRA

### Telemedicine
- Multi-state license considerations
- Liability across states

### Non-Competes
- Typical 1-3 years, 10-50 mile radius
- Affects transition planning
- Can be violated with legal action

## Life Insurance for Physicians

- Young physician: term life $2M-$5M typical
- Established partner: may add permanent for ILIT (estate tax) and buy-sell funding
- Disability insurance separately

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Insufficient malpractice limits** — lawsuit exceeds coverage, personal assets at risk
2. **No umbrella** — gaps between professional and personal
3. **No personal liability protection** — titling, DAPT not used
4. **Defined benefit plan not maxed out** — leaving significant tax deferral
5. **Non-citizen spouse handled like a standard citizen-spouse case** — marital-deduction, treaty, and portability issues missed
6. **Student loans paid off but no will** — estate has no debt but no plan
7. **Buy-sell not funded** — partner dies, practice splits up
8. **Disability insurance not "own-occupation"** — can't get full benefit
9. **Concentrated practice equity** — no diversification; illiquid at death
10. **Tail coverage missed** on malpractice switch — uncovered gap
11. **457(b) plan beneficiary not updated** after divorce
12. **Research IP rights unaddressed** — institution claims ownership
