# Becoming a Caregiver

Caregiving changes both the older generation’s planning and the caregiver’s own risk profile.

## Immediate Priorities

- Clarify authority: POA, healthcare proxy, caregiver agreement, reimbursement rights
- Avoid accidental unequal-inheritance assumptions among siblings
- Review the caregiver’s own plan, burnout risk, and asset exposure

## Questions To Ask

- Is the caregiver being paid, reimbursed, or just expected to "handle it"?
- Are siblings aligned on the arrangement?
- Is the caregiving parent / relative still competent?
- Are Medicaid lookback or transfer concerns in play?

## Planning Adaptations

- Use written caregiver agreements when compensation or housing is involved
- Document unequal-support expectations explicitly
- Review the caregiver’s employment, housing, and inheritance assumptions
- Add conflict-prevention planning early

## Documents To Collect

- Existing POA and healthcare documents
- Bank-access / bill-pay records
- Care contracts or informal family notes
- Medicaid / LTC records if relevant

## Common Mistakes

- Letting one child shoulder care while the estate plan still says "equal and no questions asked"
- Treating reimbursement as an informal promise
- Delaying authority documents until crisis
