# Life Event: Marriage

Marriage dramatically changes the estate-planning landscape. Many states' default rules now apply to spouse; many automatic rights accrue; many documents need updating.

## Automatic Legal Changes on Marriage

Without any planning:
- **Intestate succession** now favors spouse (varies by state)
- **Elective share** allows spouse to claim against will (common-law states)
- **Community property** applies in 9 states (AZ, CA, ID, LA, NV, NM, TX, WA, WI)
- **Social Security survivor benefits** available
- **Unlimited marital deduction** for federal estate tax (between US citizens)
- **Joint filing status** for income tax
- **ERISA plan rights** (spouse default beneficiary)
- **Family & Medical Leave Act** applies
- **Health insurance portability**
- **Legal next-of-kin status** for medical decisions

## Documents to Update / Create

### Urgent (First 30 Days)
- **Beneficiary designations** (401(k), IRA, life insurance, brokerage)
- **Emergency contacts** at work, medical providers
- **Will** (if prior will named ex-spouse or didn't include new spouse)

### Important (First 6 Months)
- **Will** (comprehensive update)
- **Healthcare POA** (decide deliberately whether spouse should serve as agent, and name backups)
- **HIPAA authorization** (include spouse and any other people who realistically need access)
- **Financial POA** (decide deliberately whether spouse should serve, and what backup / guardrail structure fits)
- **Revocable trust review** (joint trust, coordinated separate trusts, or a simpler approach if a trust is not actually justified)
- **Postnuptial review** if premarital-asset, waiver, or separate-property questions remain unresolved after marriage; a true prenup must be signed before marriage

### If Applicable
- **Prenuptial agreement** (before marriage, ideally 30+ days ahead)
- **Postnuptial agreement** (if no prenup)
- **Property titling** (tenants by entirety, community property, etc.)
- **Domicile planning** (if interstate marriage)
- **Immigration / citizenship** planning (if non-citizen spouse)
- **Child's plan** (if spouse has kids)

## Prenuptial Agreement

### When to Consider
- Either party has significant assets (over $500K)
- Either party has children from prior relationship
- Business ownership
- Expected inheritance
- Age gap
- Second (or later) marriage
- Significant income disparity
- International or interstate considerations

### What Prenup Addresses
- Division of assets in divorce
- Division of assets at death (who inherits what)
- Debt responsibility
- Spousal support terms
- Specific assets excluded from marital property

### What Prenup CANNOT Override
- Child support (courts decide)
- Certain elective share waivers (varies by state)
- Rights in qualified retirement plans (without separate spousal consent)

### Execution Requirements
- **Written**
- **Voluntary** (no duress; time to review)
- **Full financial disclosure**
- **Each party with independent counsel** (strongly recommended)
- **Signed before marriage** (ideally 30+ days)

### Enforcement
Highly state-dependent. Some states (CA, TX) strong enforcement. Others (NY) can invalidate if unconscionable.

## Non-Citizen Spouse Considerations

### Federal Estate Tax
- Unlimited marital deduction requires US citizen spouse
- Non-citizen spouse: route immediately to **QDOT / marital-deduction analysis**, not generic spouse planning
- If the transfer depends on the marital deduction and exceeds the otherwise available exemption, a **QDOT** or other qualifying path may be required under current federal law

### Gift Tax
- Annual exclusion to non-citizen spouse: current IRS-indexed amount ($194,000 in 2026)
- Lower than citizen (unlimited)
- Plan annual gifts accordingly, and verify the current-year figure if the recommendation depends on it

### Immigration Status
- Citizenship timeline
- Naturalization
- Impact on estate plan

### Foreign Assets
- Both spouses' foreign assets
- Tax treaties
- Reporting requirements

## Community Property States

If marrying into or within community-property state:

### Default
Property acquired during marriage is community (half each spouse).

### Implications
- **Both spouses' wages** are community (even if one earner)
- **Business acquired during marriage** is community
- **Inheritance / gifts** remain separate (unless commingled)
- **Double step-up in basis** at first death

### Transmutation
Can convert:
- Separate → community
- Community → separate
- Specific written transmutation document

### Pre-Commingling Separate Property
If coming into marriage with separate assets:
- Keep in separate accounts
- Document separate origin
- Avoid using for marital purchases
- Keep records for years

## Blended Family Complications

Marriage where either partner has kids from prior relationships:

### Who Inherits If You Die First
- Without planning: new spouse may get everything, your kids get nothing
- Your kids from prior may be surprised / angry
- Ex-spouse may try to claim on kids' behalf

### Who Inherits When Second Spouse Dies
- Your kids rely on second spouse passing to them
- Second spouse's family may get it all instead
- No legal obligation to your kids absent specific provisions

### Solutions
- **QTIP or similar protected-remainder trust** — income/support path for spouse; remainder preserved for your kids
- **Life insurance to kids** — separate from spousal inheritance
- **Pre-marital agreement** on estate provisions
- **Explicit will** — children from prior relationship named
- **Conversation** with all parties about expectations

## Retirement Plan Implications

### 401(k), 403(b), Pensions
- ERISA: spouse is automatic primary beneficiary unless spousal consent
- Must sign spousal consent to name someone else
- Retitled accounts, updated beneficiaries
- **Pre-marriage beneficiary can be overridden by marriage** in some cases

### IRAs
- Not ERISA; beneficiary designation governs
- Must update post-marriage

### Pensions
- Spouse often has survivor benefit rights
- Waiver requires spousal consent

## Property Titling Choices

### Joint Tenancy with Right of Survivorship (JTWROS)
- Passes to survivor outside probate
- Simple but loses flexibility
- No step-up on survivor's half at first death (in most states)

### Tenants by the Entirety (TBE)
- Available in ~25 states for married couples only
- Passes to survivor
- Creditor protection from creditors of one spouse alone
- Strong planning tool where available

### Community Property with Right of Survivorship
- 9 community property states + some hybrids
- Passes to survivor + can preserve the community-property basis adjustment when the asset is actually community property
- Best of both worlds

### Tenants in Common
- Each owns separate interest
- Passes per will (through probate)
- More flexible but more complex

### In Separate Ownership
- Each spouse owns separately
- Allows separate estate planning
- Consider for pre-marital assets

## Domicile Considerations (Interstate Marriages)

If spouses from different states:
- Choose domicile state carefully
- May have different implications for community property, estate tax, probate
- Document the domicile choice
- Revise estate plan to reflect new state

## Post-Marriage Timeline

### First 30 Days
- Beneficiary updates (401(k), IRA, life insurance)
- Emergency contact updates (work, medical)
- Joint bank account (optional but common)

### First 6 Months
- Will/trust update
- POA / Healthcare POA
- HIPAA
- Joint tax filing preparation
- Health insurance choice (employer vs. spouse's vs. ACA)

### First Year
- Comprehensive estate plan execution
- Property titling review
- Business interest / buy-sell review
- Philosophical conversation about money, children, aging parents

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Prior beneficiary designations not updated** — ex or parent inherits instead of spouse
2. **No prenup when one is wise** — pre-marital assets at risk in divorce
3. **Blended family not addressed** — your kids get nothing; spouse's everything
4. **Non-citizen spouse handled like an ordinary citizen-spouse case** — marital-deduction, portability, and treaty/QDOT issues missed
5. **Inheritance commingled** — loses separate-property status
6. **Elective share not considered** — spouse can override your will
7. **ERISA spousal consent skipped** — beneficiary designation invalid
8. **Moving to community property state** without addressing separate property
9. **Name change delays** causing document identity issues
10. **Assuming marriage "automatically fixes"** prior estate plan — it doesn't
