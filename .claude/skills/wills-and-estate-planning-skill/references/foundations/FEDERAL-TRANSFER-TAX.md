## Current-Law Framing

The old "use it before the 2026 sunset" framing is stale under current law.

- Congress changed the federal baseline through P.L. 119-21 (commonly referred to as the One Big Beautiful Bill Act / OBBBA).
- The 2026 federal estate / gift basic exclusion amount is **$15,000,000 per person**, with inflation indexing thereafter under current law.
- That does **not** mean the numbers are permanent in an absolute sense. A future Congress can still change them again.

Planning implication:

- do not use outdated sunset panic as a substitute for analysis, and
- do not assume today's higher exclusion makes exemption deployment irrelevant for large or fast-growing estates.

## What Actually Matters in Planning

### 1. File Form 706 aggressively when portability could matter

For married couples, the default question is not "is federal tax due right now?" but "could portability or downstream exemption use matter enough to justify a timely 706 analysis?":

- If there is any realistic chance the surviving spouse could benefit from deceased-spousal-unused exclusion (DSUE), **analyze Form 706 filing early**.
- Do not assume "no federal tax due" means "nothing to file."
- Track federal and state deadlines separately. Some states require separate state estate tax returns even when federal tax is not due.

### 2. Below the exemption, basis usually dominates transfer tax

For estates materially below the federal exclusion amount:

- Preserving step-up in basis often creates more family wealth than moving appreciated assets out of the estate.
- Aggressive gifting of appreciated assets can be a net negative if there was no realistic federal transfer-tax problem to solve.
- State estate tax, creditor protection, remarriage risk, and vulnerable-heir issues can still justify trust planning even where federal tax does not.

### 3. Above the exemption, the problem changes

Once the estate is meaningfully above the federal exclusion amount, the planning center of gravity shifts toward:

- removing post-transfer appreciation from the taxable estate,
- using exemption intentionally,
- preserving GST leverage where multigenerational planning matters,
- and solving liquidity so the estate is not forced into a bad sale.

### 4. GST is not a casual afterthought

When planning involves dynasty trusts, skip-person beneficiaries, or direct transfers to grandchildren:

- treat GST allocation as a separate design step,
- do not assume portability,
- and confirm the current exemption/allocation mechanics directly from current IRS materials before finalizing recommendations.

## Estate Tax

Federal estate tax is imposed on the taxable estate at death.

- Form: `Form 706`
- General due date: **9 months after death**
- Extension: generally **6 months to file**, but extensions to file do not automatically extend time to pay

### Typical federal triggers

- Gross estate above the federal filing threshold
- Estate tax actually due
- Portability analysis for a surviving spouse
- GST allocation questions
- State return package requiring a pro forma or full federal return

## Gift Tax

Federal gift tax is imposed on lifetime taxable gifts by the donor.

- Form: `Form 709`
- General due date: with the donor's income tax return, including extensions

### Core operational rules

- The first **$19,000** per donee per year is excluded for 2026.
- Direct tuition payments to the school and direct medical payments to the provider are generally outside the annual-exclusion framework and are often high-value planning tools.
- Gifts above the annual exclusion generally require 709 analysis even where no out-of-pocket tax is due.

## Portability and DSUE

Portability is powerful, but the details are not optional.

- Do not assume portability is available in every citizenship or residency posture.
- Do not assume state portability tracks federal portability.
- If the surviving spouse is not a U.S. citizen, QDOT and citizenship timing can materially affect the marital-deduction and DSUE analysis.

### Important noncitizen / nonresident caution

Current Form 706 instructions make clear that:

- property passing to a surviving spouse who is not a U.S. citizen generally qualifies for the marital deduction only through a **QDOT** or equivalent qualifying path, and
- portability for nonresident noncitizen situations is limited and can depend on treaty and QDOT rules.

This skill should **not** present blanket portability conclusions in noncitizen-spouse cases. Route those cases through:

- [NON-CITIZEN-SPOUSE.md](../family-structures/NON-CITIZEN-SPOUSE.md)
- current Form 706 instructions
- state-specific counsel where relevant

## QDOT Reminder

The current Form 706 instructions state that the marital deduction for transfers to a non-U.S.-citizen surviving spouse generally requires a **Qualified Domestic Trust (QDOT)** or a qualifying transfer into a QDOT before the estate tax return is filed.

Practical consequence:

- noncitizen-spouse planning is not an ordinary spousal-planning variant,
- it is a separate routing branch that should be surfaced early in intake,
- and it requires live-law verification before final recommendations.

## Step-Up in Basis

The skill should keep repeating this because it is where many plans go wrong:

- Assets included in the estate may receive a basis adjustment at death.
- Assets removed from the estate by lifetime gifts often carry over basis instead.
- Retirement accounts and other IRD assets do **not** get a conventional step-up just because the owner died.

When the estate is under the federal exclusion amount, this income-tax effect often overwhelms the transfer-tax effect.

## Planning Heuristics by Estate Size

| Estate posture | Federal tax posture | Common planning emphasis |
|----------------|--------------------|--------------------------|
| Below exclusion | Usually no federal transfer tax | Basis, beneficiary coherence, incapacity, probate avoidance, state-tax check |
| Near exclusion | Sensitive to valuation/appreciation drift | Portability discipline, state-tax modeling, liquidity, selective trust planning |
| Above exclusion | Real federal transfer-tax issue | Exemption deployment, appreciation transfer, GST design, liquidity engineering |

## Anti-Error Rules

1. Never hard-code federal numbers into a client-facing conclusion without re-checking the current IRS source.
2. Never discuss portability casually in noncitizen-spouse or nonresident scenarios.
3. Never recommend major lifetime gifting of appreciated assets below the exemption without explicitly comparing lost basis step-up versus transfer-tax savings.
4. Never treat GST as "same as estate tax" without verifying current allocation consequences for the actual structure.
