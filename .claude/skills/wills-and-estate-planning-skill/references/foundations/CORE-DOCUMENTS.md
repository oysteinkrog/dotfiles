# Core Documents — What Every Plan Needs

The "estate plan" is not a single document. For most American adults, a complete plan is usually built from some mix of the coordinated documents below:

## The Core 8 (Common Starting Set for Many Adults)

1. **Will** — names executor, guardians for minor children, residuary beneficiaries; controls probate-estate distribution
2. **Durable financial power of attorney** — authorizes someone to manage money during incapacity
3. **Healthcare power of attorney / proxy** — authorizes someone to make medical decisions
4. **Living will / advance directive** — written end-of-life preferences
5. **HIPAA authorization** — lets named people access medical records (not automatic)
6. **Beneficiary designations** — on every retirement account, life insurance, annuity, TOD/POD account
7. **Letter of instruction** — non-binding but invaluable: where documents are, account inventory, funeral wishes
8. **Digital asset inventory** — accounts, wallets, seed-phrase locations, password manager pointer

## The Common Add-Ons

9. **Revocable living trust** — for probate avoidance, multi-state real estate, incapacity continuity
10. **Pour-over will** — backup for assets not titled to the trust
11. **Personal property memorandum** — state-specific list of tangible items, separately updatable
12. **Disposition-of-remains document** — funeral wishes that survive faster than the will
13. **Ethical will / legacy letter** — non-legal values document
14. **Pre/postnup** — coordinated with estate plan
15. **Buy-sell agreement** — for any closely held business, often funded with life insurance or other liquidity planning

## Specialty Documents (When Indicated)

16. **POLST/MOLST** — portable medical orders for patients with serious illness or advanced frailty when current bedside treatment preferences need to travel across settings
17. **Psychiatric advance directive (with Ulysses clause)** — for serious mental-illness history
18. **Voluntary firearm surrender directive** — for gun owners with depression/cognitive risk
19. **Special Needs Trust** — for disabled heirs on means-tested benefits
20. **ILIT** — for life insurance to be excluded from taxable estate
21. **Pet trust** — for pets requiring lifetime funding
22. **Gun trust (NFA)** — for suppressors, SBRs, machine guns
23. **Conservation easement** — for land protection
24. **Letter of wishes to trustees** — non-binding guidance for discretionary trust distributions

## Document Hierarchy at Death

When a person dies, here is the order of operations:

```
DEATH
  │
  ├─→ NON-PROBATE TRANSFERS (immediate / contractual)
  │     • Beneficiary designations (life insurance, retirement, annuities)
  │     • TOD/POD accounts
  │     • Joint tenancy with right of survivorship
  │     • Tenancy by the entirety
  │     • Community property with right of survivorship
  │     • Trust-owned assets
  │     • Assets subject to buy-sell agreement
  │
  ├─→ PROBATE (court process)
  │     • Anything not above
  │     • Pour-over will assets if revocable trust used
  │     • Administered per the WILL (or intestacy if no will)
  │
  └─→ ANCILLARY PROBATE
        • Out-of-state real estate not in trust or with TOD deed
```

The will controls only the third box. Most wealth in most American estates flows through the first two.

## Execution Formalities

State law varies. Treat the bullets below as **planning defaults to verify**, not a national signing recipe:

- **Will** — usually writing + signature + attesting witnesses, but the witness count, presence rule, harmless-error doctrine, and self-proving practice are all state-specific
- **Trust** — often notarized and usually easier to execute than a will, but do not assume witness rules or trustee-signature practice are identical everywhere
- **POA** — often notarized; some states also require witnesses or statutory forms
- **Healthcare proxy / living will** — witnessing, notary rules, and registry practice are state-specific
- **Disposition-of-remains** — authority and formalities vary sharply by state; verify the local statute before calling it binding

**Holographic (handwritten) wills** are valid only in a minority of states and even there with strict requirements. Avoid them.

**Electronic wills and remote signing** — legal in a small but growing number of states; verify state-by-state before relying on a Zoom signing.

**Self-proving affidavit** is usually worth using when the state provides one and counsel is preparing a formal will package. It often saves later witness-proof hassle, but the exact form and mechanics are state-specific.

## Storage

- **Originals**: attorney's office, a fireproof home safe, or another location the executor can actually reach. If using a safe-deposit box, confirm the local access rules and do not make the only original practically unreachable.
- **Copies**: executor, spouse, trusted adult child, possibly attorney.
- **Digital scans**: in the password manager or encrypted backup. Useful for reference but not legal originals.

**Tell the executor where the original is.** Plans fail more often because nobody could find the document than because the document was bad.

## Coordination Audit

After every plan update, run the coherence audit:

- [ ] Will, trust, beneficiary forms, POAs, deeds — all reference the same agents and beneficiaries
- [ ] Successor agents named at every level
- [ ] Trust funded — every asset that should be in the trust actually is
- [ ] Beneficiary designations updated post-life-event (marriage, divorce, birth, death, move)
- [ ] State of execution matches current state of domicile (or attorney has confirmed cross-state validity)
- [ ] Originals located, copies distributed, executor informed
- [ ] Letter of instruction reflects current account list
- [ ] Digital inventory current
