# Probate and Intestacy

## What Probate Is

Probate is the **court-supervised process** of validating a will, inventorying assets, paying debts and taxes, and distributing what remains. Three flavors:

- **Formal probate** — court-supervised throughout, with hearings, accountings, and judicial approval at each stage. Slowest and most expensive.
- **Informal / unsupervised probate** — court issues letters testamentary; executor administers without ongoing supervision; available in many states.
- **Small-estate procedures** — abbreviated process for estates below a state-specific threshold (often $50,000–$200,000); some states allow affidavit-based collection without any court filing.

## Why People Avoid It

- **Cost** — statutory fees, attorney fees, bond premiums, court costs, appraisal fees. California probate on a $1M estate can run $46,000+ in statutory fees alone. Texas independent administration is much cheaper.
- **Time** — typically 6–18 months; longer for contested estates or those with real estate.
- **Privacy** — court files are often public or semi-public. Even where sensitive data is redacted, probate usually reduces privacy compared with a funded trust or contract-based transfer.
- **Complexity** — particularly for ancillary probate (out-of-state real estate requiring separate probate in each state).

## Why People Sometimes Want It

- **Creditor cutoff** — if the estate uses the state's notice-and-claims process correctly, many creditor claims can be cut off after a state-specific window (often 4–6 months from notice), but timing rules, notice requirements, and exceptions vary by state and claim type. This is a real benefit when there's any concern about unknown creditors.
- **Court oversight** — beneficial when family conflict is likely; the court provides external checks on the executor.
- **Title certainty** — a probate decree quiets title in a way some title insurers prefer.

## Standard Probate-Avoidance Tools

| Tool | Coverage | Cost | Trade-offs |
|------|----------|------|-----------|
| **Funded revocable trust** | Most assets re-titled to trust | Higher up-front ($2K–$7K legal + retitling) | Must actually fund the trust |
| **TOD/POD designations** | Bank, brokerage, vehicle (some states) | Free | Per-account; doesn't avoid for unnamed contingencies |
| **Beneficiary designations** | Retirement, life insurance, annuities | Free | Pre-existing |
| **Joint tenancy with ROS** | Real estate, accounts | Low | Survivor takes all; outside community-property states the basis adjustment may be only partial; gift-tax concern on funding |
| **Tenancy by the entirety** | Real estate (married couples in some states) | Low | Same as JTROS plus creditor protection from one-spouse creditors |
| **Community property w/ ROS** | Real estate, accounts (community-property states) | Low | Can produce a major basis benefit if the asset is actually community property under local law |
| **Transfer-on-death deed** | Real estate (in growing list of states) | Low (~$50–$200 to record) | State-specific availability; revocable |
| **Small-estate affidavit** | Personal property under state threshold | Free | Only if total estate is small enough |

## Intestacy — The Default if No Will

Every state has intestacy statutes. They are blunt instruments. Typical patterns (highly simplified; verify the actual state's statute before teaching from this table):

| Decedent's situation | Typical default distribution |
|---------------------|------------------------------|
| Married, no descendants, parents dead | Spouse usually takes all |
| Married, only mutual descendants | Spouse often takes all, but not uniformly |
| Married, kids from prior relationship | Spouse usually gets a large share, but children may also receive an immediate share or later claim; family-home pressure depends heavily on state law and asset structure |
| Unmarried, descendants | Descendants usually take by representation (for example, per stirpes / by representation under the local statute) |
| Unmarried, no descendants, parents alive | Parents usually take all or the next priority share |
| Unmarried, no descendants or parents | Siblings or descendants of siblings are usually next, then more remote relatives |
| Truly no relatives | Escheat to state (rare) |

**What intestacy ignores:**

- Unmarried partners (generally no inheritance right absent marriage or another recognized legal status)
- Friends, godchildren, mentees
- Stepchildren (raised but not adopted)
- Long-term employees, caregivers
- Charities the decedent supported

**Plus:** intestacy usually means some probate or administration process, may require a bond, and can trigger appointment of an administrator the family would not have chosen.

## Common-Law Marriage Note

A small and shrinking group of states still recognize common-law marriage under specific conditions, but the current list and the exact doctrinal tests are volatile enough that you should verify the relevant jurisdiction rather than rely on memory. Couples are occasionally surprised at death to learn they were legally married — affects spousal rights, intestacy, Social Security, and beneficiary defaults. If long-term cohabitation may implicate common-law marriage, confirm status while alive.

## Will Contests — How They Happen

Common grounds:

- **Lack of capacity** — testator didn't understand what they were signing
- **Undue influence** — testator was manipulated by someone with access (caregiver, recent partner, child who isolated others)
- **Fraud** — testator was deceived about contents
- **Improper execution** — witnesses weren't present, signature missing, etc.
- **Revocation** — a later document supersedes
- **Mistake** — drafting error

Defenses (build into the plan):

- **Capacity letter** from physician at signing if elderly or ill
- **Videotape signing** (in states that permit) to rebut later capacity challenges
- **Independent counsel** — testator represented separately from any beneficiary
- **Self-proving affidavit** — eliminates witness-availability issues
- **No-contest (in terrorem) clause** — beneficiary who challenges loses their bequest (enforceability varies; FL doesn't enforce; many states have probable-cause exceptions)
- **Mediation clause** — softer alternative; often more effective
- **Family meeting / explanation during life** — often one of the most effective contest-prevention tools when the family can handle it safely
- **Avoid last-minute changes** under suspicious circumstances (new caregiver, deathbed amendment)

## Probate Cost & Time by State (Rough)

| State | Cost Profile | Time | Notes |
|-------|--------------|------|-------|
| TX | Low | 6–9 mo | Independent administration is fast |
| FL | Higher (paperwork-heavy) | 9–18 mo | Required attorney representation |
| CA | High (statutory fees) | 12–24 mo | Statutory fees are % of gross estate |
| NY | Moderate | 9–18 mo | Surrogate's Court manageable but not free |
| IL | Moderate | 9–18 mo | Independent administration available |
| Most others | Moderate | 9–15 mo | Wide variation |

For wealthy estates or estates with multiple states or contested heirs, all numbers escalate.

## Practical Heuristic

- **Modest estate, simple family, no real estate in multiple states:** simple will + beneficiary designations + small-estate procedures may be sufficient.
- **Substantial estate, multi-state real estate, privacy concerns, management-continuity needs, or meaningful complexity:** a funded revocable living trust often deserves serious consideration, but do not treat it as the automatic answer to every complication. Compare it against beneficiary-designation cleanup, deed strategy, and the actual probate posture of the relevant states.
- **High-conflict family or known potential contests:** trust + supplemental protections (no-contest clause, capacity documentation, video signing, independent counsel).
