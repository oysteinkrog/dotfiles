# Domicile, Residency, and the Domicile Trap

## Domicile vs. Residence

These are **not** the same.

- **Residence** — where you live (you can have multiple residences simultaneously).
- **Domicile** — your legal home state for many tax and estate purposes, generally the place you intend to treat as your permanent base. In most analyses, you are treated as having one domicile at a time.

Domicile determines:
- Which state's law governs your estate
- Which state's income tax applies to you (and to your trusts)
- Which state's estate tax applies at death
- Which state's elective share, community property, and intestacy rules apply
- Which state's probate court has jurisdiction

## Establishing or Changing Domicile

Domicile requires **physical presence + intent to remain indefinitely**. Courts and tax auditors look at a long list of factors. None alone is dispositive; all together paint the picture.

### The "Bring Your Life" Test (Strong Evidence)

- **Days present** in each state per year (aggressive states may reconstruct this from records such as phone, toll, card, and travel data)
- **Driver's license** issued by which state
- **Voter registration** and where you actually vote
- **Vehicle registration**
- **Homestead declaration** filed in the new state
- **Primary physician, dentist, accountant, attorney, financial advisor** all in new state
- **Bank where you do daily banking**
- **Religious congregation, clubs, professional associations**
- **Where your spouse and minor children live**
- **Where your pets live**
- **Address on tax returns, insurance policies, investment statements, passports**
- **Burial plot** in the new state
- **Will and important papers** kept in new state

### The "Lifestyle" Test (Supporting Evidence)

- **Where you would want to die** (literally — relevant to auditors)
- **Where your "near and dear" personal items are** (family photos, wedding album, jewelry of sentimental value, daily-use clothing)
- **Hometown of friends and social network**
- **Where children visit on holidays**
- **Where you keep your art collection, your wine cellar, your gun safe**
- **Where you renew passports**

### Negative Evidence (What Hurts a Domicile Claim)

- Returning to the old state for medical treatment when you could get it locally
- Keeping a residence in the old state that's the same size or larger than the new one
- Spending more than 183 days/year in the old state
- Old-state professional team unchanged
- Filing tax returns at the old-state address
- Continuing to vote in the old state
- Keeping a homestead exemption claim active in the old state

## A Clean Break Checklist (Moving from High-Tax to No-Tax)

For someone moving from NY/NJ/CT/CA/MA/IL to FL/TX/TN/NV/WY/SD:

### Within 30 days of move

- [ ] Apply for new state driver's license; surrender old one
- [ ] Register to vote in new state; cancel old registration
- [ ] Register vehicles in new state
- [ ] File homestead declaration in new state (if available)
- [ ] Update address on bank accounts, brokerage, retirement, insurance
- [ ] Update address on tax returns
- [ ] Update address on passport
- [ ] Update employer/HR records

### Within 90 days

- [ ] Establish primary care physician in new state
- [ ] Move primary banking relationship
- [ ] Change accountant if old one is in old state
- [ ] Update will, trust, POA, healthcare proxy under new state's law
- [ ] Update beneficiary forms
- [ ] Sell or significantly downsize old-state residence
- [ ] Cancel old state's professional licenses if not needed

### Within 1 year

- [ ] Track day counts carefully and avoid old-state statutory-residency traps; if the old state uses a 183-day test, do not drift near it while also keeping an old-state abode available
- [ ] Change religious congregation, club memberships, professional associations
- [ ] Move "near and dear" personal items
- [ ] Establish dentist, attorney, financial advisor in new state
- [ ] Burial plot in new state if appropriate

### Document Everything

Maintain a **domicile binder**:

- Physical move date and confirmation (movers' invoice, U-Haul receipt)
- Driver's license issue date
- Homestead declaration filing
- Voter registration date and voting history
- Day-by-day calendar of where you slept (apps like TaxBird, Monaeo)
- Photos of new home with personal items
- Lease or deed for new residence

This binder is your defense if the old state audits.

## The "Statutory Resident" Trap

**Even if you're not domiciled in NY, NY can tax you as a statutory resident** if both:

1. You spend more than 183 days in NY in the tax year, AND
2. You maintain a "permanent place of abode" in NY (any housing available for your year-round use, even rented to a relative)

This is **separate from domicile**. NY can pursue former residents who claim to have moved if they keep a NYC apartment available and spend even half the year there.

California is aggressive too, but it uses its own residency/contact analysis rather than New York's exact statutory-resident formula.

## The Audit Risk

NY, NJ, CT, CA, MA — these states **actively audit** former residents who claim to have moved. Audits can reach back multiple years and argue that the move was incomplete.

Common audit triggers:

- High income or large estate that left after a known liquidity event (IPO, business sale, inheritance)
- Continued real-estate ownership in the old state
- Continued voter registration or driver's license
- Returns filed showing income from old-state sources

## Multi-State Strategy

For people who legitimately split time between states:

- **Snowbirds** (FL winter, NY summer): meticulous day-counting, FL homestead, NY apartment kept available — high audit risk; consider sale of NY apartment or making the FL home strictly larger and more "lived in"
- **Bicoastal** (CA + NY): nightmare scenario; both states aggressive; often need to make a clear primary choice

## Domicile and Estate Tax

At death, the **decedent's domicile state** taxes the entire estate (subject to its rules and exemptions). **States where the decedent owned real property** can additionally tax that real property regardless of domicile.

**Strategy implications:**
- Establishing FL/TX domicile saves state estate tax on the entire estate
- But out-of-state real property is still exposed to that state's estate tax
- Hold out-of-state property in LLC or revocable trust to potentially avoid (depends on state rules)

## Practical Recommendations

1. **If the move is being timed around a liquidity, sale, or inheritance event, start early.** A rushed pre-event move is much easier for an old state to challenge than a long-settled relocation.
2. **Document carefully for the first several years post-move,** especially if the old state is known to audit residency changes aggressively.
3. **If feasible, sell or truly lease out the old-state residence** rather than keeping it casually available for your own use.
4. **Half-moves create the worst facts.** If you are claiming a new domicile, your documents, time allocation, professional relationships, and lived reality should all point in the same direction.
5. **Update the estate plan under new-state law** within the first year — old-state documents may still work, but they may miss state-specific issues such as homestead, community-property treatment, or elective-share waivers.
