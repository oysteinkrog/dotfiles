# State Estate and Inheritance Tax — Verification-First Snapshot

This file is a **routing tool**, not the last word. State death-tax law drifts faster than most general estate-planning references. For any live recommendation, verify the user's domicile state, every state where they own real estate or tangible personal property, and any inheritance-tax state relevant to intended beneficiaries. Record those checks in `analyses/official-source-log.md`.

## Inheritance Tax States — Current Practical Snapshot

Inheritance tax is imposed on the **recipient**, and relationship classification matters more than most families realize.

| State | Current practical summary | Official-source posture |
|-------|----------------------------|-------------------------|
| Kentucky | Class A beneficiaries are exempt; Class B generally 4% to 16% after exemption; Class C generally 6% to 16% after exemption | Official Kentucky DOR inheritance-tax page |
| Maryland | 10% inheritance tax on many non-exempt transfers; close relatives are generally exempt | Official Maryland tax guidance and estate/inheritance materials |
| Nebraska | County-level inheritance tax; post-2023 rules generally use 1% / 11% / 15% rates with larger exemptions than before | Official Nebraska DOR legislative summary and inheritance-tax materials |
| New Jersey | Estate tax gone for deaths on/after Jan. 1, 2018; inheritance tax still applies to some beneficiary classes | Official NJ Division of Taxation page |
| Pennsylvania | 0% spouse; 4.5% lineal heirs; 12% siblings; 15% other heirs | Official Pennsylvania Department of Revenue page |

Iowa's inheritance tax has been repealed.

## What Matters Most in Real Planning

### 1. State planning often matters before federal planning

A family can be comfortably below the federal exclusion amount and still face meaningful state death tax.

Examples:

- Massachusetts at $2M
- Oregon at $1M
- Rhode Island at roughly $1.8M
- Minnesota at $3M
- Washington with materially more aggressive current tax rates than many older summaries show

### 2. State portability is the exception, not the rule

Federal portability cannot be assumed to solve the state problem.

That means:

- first-death credit-shelter planning can still be critical even when federal tax is not,
- state QTIP elections may matter,
- and "everything to spouse" can waste a state exclusion.

### 3. Date-of-death ranges matter

Do not answer "What is Washington's estate-tax threshold?" or "What is New York's exclusion?" without anchoring the date of death.

Recent examples:

- Washington changed materially in mid-2025 and again for 2026.
- New York updates annually and the 105% cliff remains central.
- DC and Connecticut pages may lag the calendar year, so you may have only the latest published official amount when planning during the next year.

### 4. Real-estate situs is a recurring trap

A state can tax real estate physically located there even if the decedent was domiciled elsewhere.

At minimum ask:

- Where is the person domiciled?
- Where is the real estate?
- Is the property held directly, by trust, or through an entity?
- Is the recommendation relying on the property being treated as an intangible instead of situs real property? If yes, escalate and verify.

### 5. Inheritance-tax states punish loose family assumptions

Pennsylvania, Kentucky, Nebraska, Maryland, and New Jersey can produce surprising results when property goes to:

- siblings,
- nieces/nephews,
- in-laws,
- unmarried partners,
- friends,
- or more remote relatives.

Never summarize inheritance tax as "the estate pays some tax" without classifying the beneficiaries.

## Working Heuristics

1. If the estate is below federal but above a state threshold, state planning may dominate.
2. If the family owns out-of-state real estate, treat each property state as part of the analysis.
3. If there is a blended family, do not waste the first spouse's state exclusion casually.
4. If the plan changes domicile, document the move aggressively. Death-tax states audit this.
5. If the conclusion depends on a number from this file, verify it again before finalizing the deliverable.
