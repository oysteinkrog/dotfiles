# Beneficiary Designation Coordination

One of the most common causes of serious estate-plan failure is a beneficiary designation that contradicts the will. This file is the playbook for getting it right.

## The Core Rule

**Beneficiary designations override the will and trust** for assets that pass by contract:

- Retirement accounts (401(k), IRA, Roth, SEP, SIMPLE, 403(b), 457)
- Life insurance (term, whole, universal, variable)
- Annuities (immediate, deferred, fixed, variable)
- HSAs
- 529 plans
- TOD (Transfer-on-Death) brokerage and bank accounts
- POD (Payable-on-Death) bank accounts and CDs
- Series EE / I bonds with beneficiary designation
- TreasuryDirect accounts with co-owner or beneficiary

**Title overrides the will and trust** for assets that pass by titling:

- Real estate held joint tenancy with right of survivorship
- Real estate held tenancy by the entirety
- Real estate held community property with right of survivorship
- Vehicles with TOD title (where state allows)
- Real estate with TOD deed (where state allows)
- Trust-owned assets

The will controls only what's left.

## ERISA Preemption — Powerful, But Not Universal

Many **private-employer** workplace retirement plans are governed by **ERISA**, federal law. But do not treat "workplace plan" as automatically synonymous with ERISA: governmental plans, many church plans, and some 403(b) arrangements can sit outside Title I. Start by identifying the actual plan type.

For plans that are actually subject to the relevant ERISA survivor-benefit / beneficiary-designation rules, the named beneficiary on the plan form is usually the controlling starting point, regardless of:

- A will saying otherwise
- A divorce decree saying otherwise
- A state divorce-revocation statute
- General estate-plan intent that never made it onto the plan's own paperwork

**Key exceptions / overlays:** QDROs, plan-specific survivor-benefit rules, and any required spousal consent can change the result. Do not summarize this as "the form always wins no matter what" without checking those overlays.

**Spousal-rights rule:** Many plans require the spouse to receive the default survivor benefit or be the primary beneficiary unless the spouse signs a valid waiver, but the exact rule depends on plan type and whether the plan is subject to the QJSA / QPSA framework.

Qualified disclaimers can still matter after death, but they are cleanup tools, not a substitute for correct beneficiary designations during life.

**Update the form personally after every major life event.**

## The Audit Process

Step 1: List every account/policy/asset that passes by beneficiary designation or titling.

Step 2: For each, capture:
- Current named beneficiary (primary AND contingent)
- Date of last update
- Any spousal consent issues
- Account number / policy number
- Where the form is filed

Step 3: Compare to the user's intent. Identify deltas.

Step 4: For each delta, plan the fix:
- Online beneficiary update (most retirement, brokerage)
- Form submission (life insurance, annuities)
- Deed change (real estate)
- Title transfer (vehicles)

Step 5: Confirm completion. Get the updated form/deed in writing.

## Critical Designations

### 401(k) and Similar Workplace Plans

- **Spousal protection is common, but not identical across all plans** — many plans require spouse-first treatment or spousal consent for a different beneficiary, but check the actual plan and its survivor-benefit rules
- Update after marriage, divorce, death of spouse
- For plans actually governed by ERISA, federal law generally preempts contrary state revocation statutes
- For assets that truly pass under the plan's beneficiary-designation regime, the will does not override the form
- Naming a trust as beneficiary requires careful drafting (see-through trust under SECURE Act)

### IRA (Traditional and Roth)

- Not ERISA, but state law often automatically revokes ex-spouse designations after divorce — but **don't rely on it; update the form**
- Contingent beneficiary critical
- Naming a trust requires careful drafting
- Roth: many non-spouse beneficiaries face a year-10 outside deadline, but the tax treatment and payout pattern still depend on beneficiary type and account facts

### Life Insurance

- Insurance contract controls
- Naming "the estate" as beneficiary forces probate and can expose proceeds to creditor and administrative friction
- Naming a minor directly often pushes the asset into court-supervised property management unless a deliberate custodial or trust structure was already set up
- Naming an ILIT as beneficiary keeps proceeds out of the taxable estate (if ILIT properly structured and 3-year lookback satisfied)

### Annuities

- Beneficiary designation controls
- Death-benefit options vary heavily by contract terms, payout election, and whether the annuity is qualified or nonqualified
- Surviving spouses often have materially more continuation or rollover flexibility than non-spouse beneficiaries, but confirm the carrier's actual rules before assuming the best outcome
- Non-spouse beneficiaries often face tighter payout timing or less favorable continuation options, but there is no single universal "accelerated schedule" that fits every annuity
- Some annuities have "joint and survivor" features built in — review carefully

### HSAs

- Spouse: continues as spouse's own HSA (best outcome)
- Non-spouse: HSA terminates; full balance becomes taxable income to beneficiary in year of death (often a significant tax hit)
- Estate: balance taxable on decedent's final 1040
- **Strategy:** Often worth evaluating a deliberate HSA spend-down during life if the likely heir is a non-spouse, especially after age 65

### 529 Plans

- **Owner controls, not beneficiary**
- At owner's death, any **successor owner / account-successor designation** usually controls, but terminology and mechanics vary by program
- If no successor is named, do **not** assume the account simply follows the will; some programs default control to the beneficiary, some to the estate, and some use other plan-specific succession rules
- Usually review successor-owner setup when opening or revising a 529, and confirm the plan's actual default rule in writing

### TOD/POD Accounts

- Beneficiary takes outright at death
- Multiple beneficiaries take in shares (often equal unless specified)
- No probate
- No restrictions on use post-receipt — if a minor is named directly, control usually ends at the applicable age of majority or custodial-release age

### Joint Tenancy

- Survivor takes all
- No probate
- In non-community-property states, only the decedent's fractional interest typically receives a basis adjustment
- Adding a child to title is a gift (gift tax issue if amount exceeds annual exclusion)
- Loss of control during life (joint owner can sell, encumber, expose to their creditors)

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Ex-spouse still on 401(k).** On ERISA-governed workplace plans especially, do not assume divorce cleaned up the form.
2. **Naming "estate" as life insurance beneficiary.** Forces probate and adds avoidable friction and creditor exposure.
3. **Naming minor children directly.** Usually pushes the asset into court-supervised property management or an outright handoff at the applicable age of majority or custodial-release age.
4. **No contingent beneficiary.** If primary predeceases, asset goes through probate or to the estate.
5. **Spouse named but spouse waiver not done for retirement plan.** Plan defaults override.
6. **Trust named but trust not properly drafted for retirement-account rules** — can trigger a materially worse payout schedule.
7. **Joint tenancy with one child** (informal estate planning) — disinherits other children.
8. **TOD form completed but never submitted.** Many TOD forms sit in a desk drawer.
9. **529 successor-owner field blank and no plan-default review.** Control may land somewhere the family did not expect.
10. **Old beneficiary form on a paper file the institution lost.** Confirm in writing the institution has the current form on file.

## Trust as Beneficiary — Special Considerations

Naming a trust as IRA/401(k) beneficiary is appropriate in some fact patterns (control, asset protection, special needs) but requires careful drafting:

- **See-through trust** — must satisfy IRS regulations so the trust beneficiaries, rather than the trust itself, can drive the payout regime
- **Conduit trust vs. accumulation trust** — conduit passes RMDs straight through (simple but gives beneficiary direct receipt); accumulation trust holds (creditor protection but trust pays high tax rates on retained income)
- **Eligible Designated Beneficiary status** — for a spouse, disabled beneficiary, certain minor-child planning, or other qualifying beneficiary, can preserve more favorable timing
- **Post-SECURE Act regulations finalized in 2024** — get current advice; pre-2024 trusts may need revision

## Update Triggers

Update beneficiary designations after:

- **Marriage** — re-check whether spouse-first beneficiary treatment still makes sense across the account set, or whether trust/children/asset-protection considerations call for a different design
- **Divorce** — remove ex-spouse from everything
- **Birth/adoption** — add as contingent (or to trust for minors)
- **Death of primary beneficiary** — update primary; add new contingent
- **Death of contingent beneficiary** — update contingent
- **Major change in relationship** with named beneficiary
- **Move to new state** — confirm state rules don't affect designations
- **Any change in plan structure** (creating trust, ILIT, etc.) — coordinate

## The Beneficiary Map (Deliverable)

Every plan produces a Beneficiary Map (see [BENEFICIARY-MAP.md](../../assets/BENEFICIARY-MAP.md)) — a single-page table covering every asset, current designation, intended designation, and pending action. This is the most actionable document a user takes away from the engagement.

```
| Asset                           | Current Designation        | Intended           | Action Required          |
|---------------------------------|---------------------------|--------------------|-------------------------|
| 401(k) (Fidelity, Acme Corp)    | Ex-spouse Sarah (2008)    | Spouse Jane        | File new form by [date] |
| Term Life ($500K, AIG)          | Estate                    | Spouse 100% / kids | Update online           |
| House (123 Main, JTROS w/Mom)   | Mother (auto on death)    | Trust              | Quitclaim + retitle     |
| Vanguard Brokerage              | None (no TOD)             | Trust              | Add TOD or retitle      |
| Roth IRA (Schwab)               | Spouse only               | Spouse 100%        | OK; verify contingent   |
| HSA (HealthEquity)              | None                      | Spouse             | Add online              |
| 529 (Vanguard, Daughter)        | Owner only, no successor  | Spouse as successor| Add successor-owner designation and confirm plan default |
```

For many users, this map is among the highest-leverage deliverables. It often surfaces major misrouting problems that would not have been obvious from the will alone.
