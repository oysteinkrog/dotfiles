# Tier 2: Middle-Class / Upper-Middle-Class

## Profile

- **Net worth:** ~$500K to ~$3M (with some high-complexity households spilling upward from Tier 1)
- **Typical profile:** homeowner(s), possibly vacation home, dual-income household, established careers, kids in school or college, meaningful 401(k)/IRA balances, term life insurance, maybe small investment portfolio
- **Primary concerns:** probate avoidance, coordinated beneficiary planning, incapacity planning, 529 integration, SECURE Act 10-year rule, possibly blended-family or vulnerable-heir issues

## Core Document Checklist

**Default starting package for many Tier 2 households:**
- [x] Revocable living trust when probate, privacy, incapacity-continuity, or multi-property concerns justify it; many Tier 2 households should analyze it seriously, but it is not an automatic answer
- [x] Pour-over will
- [x] Durable financial POA (immediate)
- [x] Healthcare POA
- [x] Living will / advance directive
- [x] HIPAA authorization
- [x] Dementia-specific directive (recommended)
- [x] Guardian nominations for minor children
- [x] Beneficiary designations audited and coordinated with trust/will
- [x] Trust funding (deeds retitled, accounts retitled)
- [x] Letter of instruction
- [x] Digital asset plan with RUFADAA authorization
- [x] Disposition of remains document

**Conditional:**
- [x] Special-needs trust if vulnerable heir
- [x] ILIT only when estate-tax, liquidity, or equalization modeling justifies irrevocability, usually because of state estate-tax exposure, business/liquidity needs, or upper-tier complexity
- [x] Non-citizen-spouse federal analysis (QDOT / treaty / portability issues when relevant)
- [x] 529 plan as part of plan (not a will/trust document but estate-adjacent)

## Decisions to Make at This Tier

### 1. Revocable trust vs. will-only
**Revocable trust is often worth strong consideration if:**
- Live in state with expensive/slow probate (CA, FL, NY metros)
- Own out-of-state property (avoid ancillary probate)
- Want privacy
- Want continuity if incapacitated
- Have second marriage with distinct family branches

**Will-only may be fine if:**
- Live in simple-probate state (WI, ND, small-population)
- Modest estate mostly in TOD/POD form
- POA, beneficiary designations, and titling already solve most of the continuity goals
- Want to minimize cost

### 2. Trustee / successor trustee
- Who serves during your life? (usually you)
- Successor trustee upon incapacity or death? (spouse, then adult child, then corporate trustee?)
- Co-trustee for checks and balances?

### 3. Distribution to children
- Outright at age X?
- Staggered ages (e.g., 1/3 at 25, 1/3 at 30, 1/3 at 35)?
- Held in trust for life with independent trustee for creditor/divorce protection?
- HEMS (Health, Education, Maintenance, Support) standard?

### 4. SECURE Act / retirement planning
- Many non-spouse beneficiaries will face 10-year distribution pressure on large IRAs
- Conduit vs. accumulation trust?
- Consider Roth conversions to avoid heirs facing large taxable distributions
- Charitable remainder trust as IRA beneficiary (for charitable intent)

### 5. State estate tax planning
- If in NY, MA, CT, OR, WA, MN, IL, MD, HI, DC, VT, ME, RI, or another applicable state death-tax jurisdiction: state-level thresholds can be much lower than federal
- NY threshold ~$7M (2026), MA $2M
- May need planning even if federal exemption not triggered

### 6. Insurance adequacy
- Term life: enough to replace income + pay off mortgage + fund kids' education?
- Disability insurance: long-term disability?
- Umbrella liability: $1M-$5M typical for homeowners with assets
- LTC insurance: if age 55-65, evaluate

## Common Profiles at This Tier

### Profile A: Dual-Income Professional Couple, Young Kids
- 42/40, married, two kids ages 8 and 5, $750K home ($400K equity), $600K combined 401(k)s, $100K taxable, $1M+ term life each, $150K 529s
- **Plan:** joint revocable trust (or separate trusts), pour-over wills, POA/HCPOA, testamentary trust for kids to age 25/30, beneficiary audit, 529 continuity, digital assets, letter of instruction
- **Total cost:** $3,000-$6,000

### Profile B: Empty Nesters, $2M Net Worth
- 62/60, married, adult kids (independent), $900K home, $800K 401(k)/IRA, $300K taxable, second home $400K, wholesaling to retirement
- **Plan:** joint trust with credit shelter portion (if state tax threshold matters), POA/HCPOA/living will updated, dementia directive, Roth conversion consideration, LTC insurance evaluation, review beneficiaries after kids' divorces/remarriages
- **Total cost:** $4,000-$8,000

### Profile C: Blended Family, $1.5M Net Worth
- 50/48 second marriage, each has kids from prior marriage, $600K home jointly titled, $500K 401(k) each, life insurance, some taxable investments
- **Plan:** QTIP or similar protected-remainder trust for spouse/children tension, prenup review, beneficiary coordination, healthcare POA may specify current spouse, guardian nominations still for minors
- **Total cost:** $5,000-$10,000 (blended family complexity)

### Profile D: Single, Late 50s, Professional
- 58, never married, no kids, parents deceased, $1.2M net worth, $400K home, siblings and nieces/nephews
- **Plan:** revocable trust, will, POA (choose carefully — sibling? friend? professional fiduciary?), HCPOA, charitable bequests maybe, disposition of pets, letter of instruction, beneficiaries to nieces/nephews
- **Total cost:** $2,500-$5,000

### Profile E: Recently Divorced
- 45, one kid 12, $800K net worth post-divorce, $350K home, $400K retirement, life insurance per divorce decree
- **Plan:** high-priority cleanup — update will and beneficiaries to remove ex-spouse; new guardian nomination; trust for child; coordinate with child support obligations and life insurance required by decree
- **Total cost:** $2,000-$4,000

## Cost Range

- **Online document packages:** $500-$1,500 — workable for simpler cases, risky for complexity
- **Flat-fee attorney (basic package):** $2,000-$4,000 — recommended baseline
- **Flat-fee attorney (with revocable trust and trust funding):** $4,000-$8,000
- **Blended family or complex trust structure:** $6,000-$12,000
- **Add: trust funding (deed retitling):** $200-$500 per property
- **Ongoing reviews:** $500-$1,500 every 3-5 years

## Attorney Engagement

**Look for:**
- Estate planning as primary practice (not a side practice)
- Flat-fee for standard packages
- Reviews trust funding (some attorneys just draft, don't ensure funding)
- Offers 3-5 year review service

**Questions to ask:**
- What happens if I'm in another state when I die?
- Do you coordinate with my financial advisor and accountant?
- Can you draft a dementia directive in addition to living will?
- What's your approach to beneficiary audits?
- Will you review my beneficiary forms, not just the documents?

## Anti-Patterns Specific to This Tier

- **Funding failure** — trust created but never funded (accounts still in individual name)
- **Beneficiary forms contradict trust** — beneficiary-designation regime usually controls unless a plan-law overlay or specific exception changes the result
- **Joint accounts with adult child** — creates gift tax problem, exposes to child's creditors
- **Named executor is stale** — best friend from 20 years ago now estranged
- **Guardian nominations not updated** after relationships changed
- **Out-of-state vacation home** not in trust — ancillary probate in that state
- **529 continuity not reviewed** — family guesses at the default successor path and gets the wrong owner/control result
- **Ignoring state estate tax** in NY, MA, etc. — plan only for federal, owe state tax
- **SECURE Act 10-year rule ignored** — heirs face 10-year taxable distribution
- **Pre-2018 QTIP/AB trust on autopilot** — forces portability-sensitive structure in post-2018 world
- **Ex-spouse still on life insurance** from old employer

## Milestones to Graduate to Tier 3

- Net worth approaching $3M-$5M
- Started or bought business (significant value)
- Concentrated stock position from employer (RSUs, ISO, private company equity)
- Meaningful inheritance received
- High income generating significant savings
- Moved to high-tax state and need planning
