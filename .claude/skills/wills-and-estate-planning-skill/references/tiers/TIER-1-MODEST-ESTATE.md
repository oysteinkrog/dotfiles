# Tier 1: Modest Estate

## Profile

- **Net worth:** under ~$200K
- **Typical profile:** renter or modest-home owner; primary wealth is retirement accounts and home equity; single job income; no business; may have minor children
- **Primary risks:** intestacy default governs family; minor children unguarded; financial accounts frozen; family conflict over items of sentimental (not monetary) value

## Core Document Checklist

**Default starting package for most Tier 1 households:**
- [x] Simple will (testamentary; governs probate)
- [x] Durable financial power of attorney (immediate)
- [x] Healthcare power of attorney
- [x] Living will / advance directive
- [x] HIPAA authorization
- [x] Beneficiary designations verified on all retirement accounts, life insurance, bank accounts
- [x] Guardian nomination for minor children (in will)
- [x] Letter of instruction (practical matters)
- [x] Basic digital-asset inventory (accounts, devices, recovery paths)

**Nice-to-have but often overkill at this tier:**
- Revocable living trust (only if avoiding probate is a clear priority; many states probate is fast/cheap; TOD deed often sufficient)
- Specific bequests (if significant sentimental items)

**Usually overkill at this tier in a plain-vanilla family:**
- Credit shelter / most QTIP-style tax architecture
- Irrevocable trusts
- Asset protection strategies

## Decisions to Make at This Tier

### 1. Probate avoidance strategy
**Options:**
- Do nothing (probate governs) — may be fine in simple-probate states (e.g., WI, simple family, no complex assets)
- TOD deed on home — cheap, effective in many states
- Revocable living trust — overkill unless home is in a high-probate-cost state (CA, FL, NY) or multiple properties

**Recommendation:** Start by comparing actual probate friction in the user's state against simpler titling and beneficiary cleanup. For many Tier 1 households, POD/TOD designations and, where appropriate, a TOD deed solve most of the avoidable hassle; use a revocable trust when the facts justify it rather than by reflex.

### 2. Guardian for minor children
**Decisions:**
- Who is the primary guardian? (typically sibling, close friend, or parent)
- Who is the backup guardian? (in case primary can't)
- Is guardian same as person managing money (guardian of estate)? (often not, but the right answer depends on family capacity and trustworthiness)
- Is there a trust for the kids' inheritance? (typically yes, even at this tier, to avoid UTMA release at 18 or 21)

### 3. Beneficiary designations
**Check:**
- 401(k) / IRA: primary and contingent named?
- Life insurance: primary and contingent named?
- Savings/checking: POD beneficiary?
- Taxable brokerage: TOD beneficiary?

**Common error:** ex-spouse still listed on 401(k) from old job.

### 4. Life insurance sufficiency
If minor children: term coverage should be sized from the real support gap: income replacement, childcare, debt payoff, housing stability, and education goals. In some households that lands near $500K-$1M; in others it is materially different.

## Common Profiles at This Tier

### Profile A: Young Renter, No Kids
- 28, single, renting, $15K savings, $25K 401(k), $10K student loans
- **Plan:** usually a simple will, POD on accounts, beneficiary on 401(k), HCPOA + POA (parents as agents if unmarried)
- **Total cost:** $0-$500 DIY; $500-$1,000 attorney

### Profile B: Young Parents, Renting
- 32/30, married, two kids under 5, renting, $40K savings, $50K combined 401(k), $500K term life each
- **Plan:** wills with guardian nominations and simple testamentary trusts for kids; POA, HCPOA, HIPAA; beneficiary audit; letter of instruction
- **Total cost:** $1,500-$3,000 attorney (flat fee common)

### Profile C: Retired Widow, Small Home
- 74, widowed, $150K home (paid off), $60K in savings, Social Security only
- **Plan:** will, TOD deed on home, POD on accounts, POA (adult child), HCPOA, POLST (if declining health), small final-expense insurance, letter of instruction
- **Total cost:** $500-$2,500

### Profile D: Single Parent, Low Income
- 38, divorced, one child 10, $20K savings, $15K 401(k), modest life insurance
- **Plan:** will (critical for guardian), custodial account for child or testamentary trust, beneficiary audit (remove ex-spouse), POA, HCPOA
- **Total cost:** $500-$1,500

## Cost Range

- **DIY (online forms):** $0-$200 — sometimes workable for the simplest no-kids/no-real-estate cases, but still worth attorney review if possible
- **LegalZoom / Rocket Lawyer:** $150-$500 — automated but formulaic
- **Flat-fee estate attorney:** $800-$2,500 for comprehensive package — recommended baseline
- **Complex planning:** not needed

## Attorney Engagement

At this tier, some people still choose DIY forms for the simplest cases. Attorney review remains the safer baseline, and the skill should strongly steer to counsel for:
- Minor children (guardian + trust)
- Blended family (even simple blended)
- Disabled family member
- Real estate with transfer-on-death or title questions
- Digital or crypto assets

**Attorney interview focus:**
- Do they do flat-fee work? (Avoid hourly for simple wills.)
- Do they use e-signing / remote? (Accessibility.)
- Do they offer "limited scope" representation? (Just the will, not a full plan.)

## Anti-Patterns Specific to This Tier

From [ANTI-PATTERNS.md](../anti-patterns/ANTI-PATTERNS.md):

- **Thinking you don't need a will** because you have nothing — you have minor children who need guardians
- **DIY online form that doesn't match state requirements** — $15 savings vs. invalid will at death
- **Joint ownership "for simplicity"** with adult child — creates gift tax issue, exposes home to child's creditors
- **Beneficiary on 401(k) from an old employer** still pointing to ex-spouse
- **No guardian nomination** — court decides under best-interest standards, which can create conflict and outcomes the parent would never have chosen
- **Not telling guardian** that they're named — shock at death

## Milestones to Graduate to Tier 2

- Net worth passes $200K-$300K
- Home purchased with significant equity
- Children approaching college age (529 planning integrates with estate)
- Spouse with complex employer benefits
- Inheritance received
- Business started
