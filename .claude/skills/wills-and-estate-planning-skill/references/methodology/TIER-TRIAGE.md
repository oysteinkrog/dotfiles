# Tier Triage — Route by Wealth + Complexity

The same skill serves a 25-year-old renter and a $400M industrialist. The tier system ensures both get the right depth without overwhelming the former or under-serving the latter.

## Wealth Tiers (Primary Axis)

| Tier | Net Worth (Individual or Couple) | Expected Federal Estate Tax? | Typical State Tax Exposure |
|------|----------------------------------|------------------------------|-----------------------------|
| 1 | < $500K | No | Rare |
| 2 | $500K – $3M | No | Only in very-low-threshold states (OR, MA, RI at low end) |
| 3 | $3M – $15M | No | Very common (NY, MA, OR, IL, MN, CT, DC, HI, ME, RI, VT, WA, plus MD inheritance / estate interactions) |
| 4 | $15M – $100M | Yes, if no planning | Yes; state tax nearly certain |
| 5 | $100M+ | Yes, significant | Yes plus state income tax on ongoing trust income |

## Complexity Overlay (Secondary Axis)

Add +1 tier of complexity (not net worth, but depth of structure needed) for each:

- Blended family (prior marriage with children)
- Non-U.S.-citizen spouse (triggers QDOT / treaty / portability analysis)
- Privately held business or professional practice
- Partnership interests, private fund interests, carried interests
- Disabled heir requiring special needs planning
- Heir with addiction, mental illness, predatory spouse, creditor exposure
- Real property in multiple states (ancillary probate)
- Assets or heirs in multiple countries
- Forced-heirship jurisdiction real estate (Louisiana, France, most civil-law countries)
- Pre-IPO stock, concentrated equity, large RSU positions
- Active IP/royalty portfolio (author, musician, inventor, monetized creator)
- NFA firearms
- Significant cryptocurrency self-custody
- Conservation or development-restricted real estate
- Known capacity/psychiatric/substance-abuse history
- Ongoing caregiving obligations (aging parent, adult disabled child)

**A "Tier 2 family with a disabled child" gets Tier-3 depth on the SNT section but stays Tier-2 on tax planning.** Don't blindly escalate — escalate only the relevant subsection.

## Tier 1 — Modest Estate (<$500K)

**Typical profile:** Young adult or early-career family. Renter or small homeowner. 401(k) balance under $300K. 1–2 children, possibly young. Few complications.

**Core deliverables (the 90% solution):**

1. Simple will with executor, guardian nomination, residuary clause, backup beneficiaries
2. Durable financial power of attorney (immediate, not springing)
3. Healthcare proxy and living will
4. HIPAA authorization
5. Beneficiary designations reviewed and updated on every account
6. Life insurance if dependents rely on the user's income (term insurance is often sufficient; size coverage from actual spending-gap analysis rather than a canned multiple)
7. Digital asset inventory + password manager with emergency access
8. Letter of instruction (not binding — just instructions)
9. Minor-children trust within the will (a "testamentary trust") if children under 18 — prevents UTMA/UGMA outright at 18

**What to skip at this tier:**

- Revocable living trust — often unnecessary in a simple, single-state estate where beneficiary designations and TOD/POD planning already do most of the work; use it when probate, privacy, incapacity, or family-structure benefits justify the extra machinery
- QTIP, SLAT, GRAT, IDGT, ILIT, dynasty trust — usually unnecessary in the plain-vanilla case; family-structure exceptions still matter
- Anything "sophisticated"

**Special situations at this tier:**

- **Unmarried partner** — usually needs explicit planning; if the couple is truly unmarried and not protected by some state-specific status, the partner often has little or no default inheritance or decision-making authority. See [UNMARRIED-PARTNERS.md](../family-structures/UNMARRIED-PARTNERS.md).
- **Single, no kids** — often needs especially intentional beneficiary and incapacity planning despite lower wealth. See [SINGLE-NO-KIDS.md](../family-structures/SINGLE-NO-KIDS.md).
- **Ex-spouse still on 401(k) after divorce** — prioritize immediate beneficiary-form cleanup.
- **Young parents** — guardianship is usually one of the highest-stakes decisions, and adequate term life insurance is usually close behind.

**Typical cost:** $500–$1,500 with a local attorney, or free via employer-provided legal services, military legal assistance, veterans services, legal aid, or union legal plans. Online services (LegalZoom, Trust & Will, Nolo) acceptable for the simplest cases but verify state-specific execution requirements.

**Full checklist:** [TIER-1-MODEST-ESTATE.md](../tiers/TIER-1-MODEST-ESTATE.md)

## Tier 2 — Middle-Class ($500K – $3M)

**Typical profile:** Home owner with equity, 401(k) of $300K–$1.5M, maybe a rental property or a small side business, 2–3 kids (some still minors, some adult). First serious estate planning.

**Core deliverables (everything in Tier 1, plus):**

10. **Revocable living trust** with pour-over will when the family's probate, privacy, incapacity, or multi-property goals justify it; if used, it must actually be funded during life
11. TOD deed for primary residence (in states that allow)
12. Beneficiary designations usually route retirement accounts directly rather than dumping them into the revocable trust; if a trust is used, it should be deliberate retirement-account planning rather than a reflex
13. **529 plans** with successor-owner continuity reviewed and documented
14. Testamentary or sub-trust for minor children with HEMS + staged distributions (e.g., 1/3 at 25, 1/3 at 30, 1/3 at 35)
15. **Term life insurance** sized to replace lost income and pay off mortgage
16. Basic long-term care insurance analysis (especially if user is 50+ or has parents needing care)
17. Community property character audit if in a community-property state
18. Timely Form 706 / portability analysis after first spouse's death when DSUE preservation could matter
19. If state estate tax threshold is approached: credit-shelter trust at first death

**What to evaluate carefully:**

- **Whether the trust is actually needed.** For a couple in Texas with a $1M house (homestead-protected), $600K in 401(k)s (non-probate by beneficiary designation), and TOD on brokerage — probate is already mostly avoided. A trust adds work without benefit.
- **Small business.** Even a "side hustle" LLC needs a succession plan. Who runs it? Who buys out? If it's worth $0 if the owner dies, the plan is to dissolve — document the path.
- **Rental property.** Consider LLC for liability separation; adds deed work. Revocable trust can hold LLC membership interests.

**Skip at this tier (usually):**

- ILIT (unless state estate tax issue)
- GRAT, IDGT (too small)
- Dynasty trust (exemption unused)
- FLP (no valuation-discount benefit)

**Typical cost:** $2,500–$7,500 for a couple's comprehensive package with revocable trusts.

**Full checklist:** [TIER-2-MIDDLE-CLASS.md](../tiers/TIER-2-MIDDLE-CLASS.md)

## Tier 3 — High Net Worth ($3M – $15M)

**Typical profile:** Executives, successful professionals (surgeons, partners, engineers), small-business owners, inheritors, real-estate investors. Federal estate tax unlikely but state estate tax is a real issue. Often the tier where a first generation of wealth-planning is seriously engaged.

**Core deliverables (everything in Tier 2, plus):**

20. **State estate tax strategy.** If in NY, MA, OR, IL, MN, CT, DC, HI, ME, RI, VT, WA + MD: project state tax, design credit-shelter trust at first death, consider domicile relocation for retirees.
21. **Evaluate portability election / Form 706 filing** after first spouse's death. If preserving DSUE matters, timely filing is required.
22. **QTIP / protected-remainder marital-trust analysis** often deserves early consideration in blended-family cases.
23. **ILIT** if state estate tax exposure and insurance is appropriate (often the cheapest way to fund state estate tax).
24. **Buy-sell agreement** with life-insurance funding for any closely held business.
25. **Concentration risk** planning — exchange funds, collars, structured sales, CRT funding for highly appreciated single-stock positions.
26. **Domicile planning** — if retiring, consider relocation from high-tax state to no-estate-tax state well in advance of death (≥2 years, ideally).
27. **Long-term care planning** usually moves onto the front burner here; evaluate hybrid life/LTC, self-insurance, traditional LTC insurance, and Medicaid-backup strategy as appropriate.
28. **Trust protector provisions** in any irrevocable trust.
29. **Coordinated advisor team** — attorney + CPA + financial advisor meeting annually.
30. **Basis-maximization audit** — identify any old irrevocable trusts that should be decanted/unwound to pull assets back for step-up.

**Key decisions:**

- **Federal exemption strategy:** do not use a hard net-worth trigger. Model basis, projected growth, state tax, control needs, liquidity, and future-law risk. Some families below $15M should still explore targeted gifting; some families near the line should preserve basis and control instead.
- **State tax vs. federal basis.** In a low-state-tax setting, preserving basis by holding appreciated assets until death is often the starting posture, but not an automatic answer if creditor, remarriage, or control concerns dominate. In New York and other state-estate-tax jurisdictions, a credit-shelter trust at first death may justify itself by using the state exemption, but that benefit has to be modeled against the loss of later basis adjustment on the bypassed assets.
- **Roth conversions** during low-income years to reduce the IRA the heir inherits.

**Full checklist:** [TIER-3-HNW.md](../tiers/TIER-3-HNW.md)

## Tier 4 — Ultra-High Net Worth ($15M – $100M)

**Typical profile:** Successful business founders, partners in major firms, senior tech executives with concentrated stock, multi-generational family wealth. Federal estate tax is a real issue even with planning. Complexity is the norm.

**Core deliverables (everything in Tier 3, plus):**

31. **Federal estate tax planning.** Above the exemption, every dollar transferred out at low gift-tax cost saves 40¢ of estate tax.
32. **GRAT program** — rolling short-term GRATs funded with high-growth or volatile assets.
33. **Sale to IDGT** — installment sale of appreciating assets to a defective grantor trust; grantor pays income tax (tax burn) without gift.
34. **SLAT (non-reciprocal if both spouses use)** — removes assets from estate while preserving indirect access through beneficiary spouse.
35. **ILIT** sized to fund expected estate tax — avoids forced asset sales.
36. **Dynasty trust** — GST-exempt, multi-generational, situs in SD/NV/DE/WY.
37. **Family LLC / FLP** for investment assets — valuation discounts for minority/non-marketable interests (20–40% typical), with proper structure and business purpose.
38. **Charitable planning** integrated with family transfer — CRT for appreciated assets, DAF for flexible giving, private foundation for mission.
39. **§1202 QSBS analysis** for founders — acquisition-date-specific Section 1202 modeling. Post-enactment stock can follow a 3 / 4 / 5-year phased regime with a higher cap, while older stock can remain on the older timeline and cap.
40. **Section 6166 installment payment** of estate tax attributable to closely held business.
41. **Trust situs** — SD/NV/DE/WY for dynasty trust administration; DING/NING analysis before large liquidity event.
42. **Family governance** — family meetings, formal structures for next generation preparation.
43. **Multi-family office** or dedicated wealth-management relationship.
44. **Asset protection** — umbrella insurance ($5–10M), DAPTs, homestead planning, FLP charging-order protection.

**Watch-outs:**

- **Reciprocal trust doctrine** for spouses creating SLATs for each other — must be materially different.
- **Three-year lookback** on ILIT — buy new policies inside the ILIT, don't transfer existing.
- **§2036 inclusion risk** — retained interests or implicit agreements re-include assets in the estate.
- **Valuation audits** for FLPs and closely held business — aggressive discounts invite IRS challenge.

**Typical cost:** $25,000–$100,000+ for initial plan with sophisticated structures; ongoing administration $10,000–$50,000/year.

**Full checklist:** [TIER-4-UHNW.md](../tiers/TIER-4-UHNW.md)

## Tier 5 — Industrialist / Dynasty ($100M+)

**Typical profile:** Founders of operating businesses, serial entrepreneurs, families with multi-generational operating wealth, philanthropists. Not a number of people — a number of *families*. Planning extends beyond the individual to the family as institution.

**Core deliverables (everything in Tier 4, plus):**

45. **Dedicated family office** or close relationship with a top-tier multi-family office.
46. **Private foundation or family-controlled DAF network** — typically $25M+ in committed philanthropic assets.
47. **Multi-generation governance:**
    - Family council — separate from the investment committee
    - Family constitution or charter defining values, decision-making, next-generation participation
    - Formal next-generation education program (financial literacy, philanthropy, business exposure, governance)
48. **Trust situs arbitrage** across generations — SD/NV/DE situs for ongoing trusts
49. **Offshore asset protection** — Cook Islands, Nevis, Belize trust structures where appropriate, with full FBAR/FATCA/Form 3520/3520-A compliance
50. **Business succession plan** that is **operational, not just legal** — named successor CEO, 10-year mentorship, family employment policies, shareholders' agreement with drag/tag/ROFR, voting trust if voting control dispersion is a risk
51. **§1042 ESOP rollover** if selling a business to employees
52. **Impact investing** from the foundation — PRIs and MRIs
53. **Concentration management** — if wealth is still in a single business, diversification strategies (exchange funds, hedging, partial sales) without breaking §1202 or other positions
54. **Political / reputational / security planning** — for families with public profile, including security consulting, reputation management, and crisis-response protocols coordinated with the family office
55. **Next-generation inheritance preparation** — inheritors receiving $20M+ at inheritance face sudden wealth syndrome; structured trusts with professional trustees, staged responsibility, and financial-therapist support are the norm
56. **International tax planning** — for families with cross-border presence (dual citizenship, international beneficiaries, operating businesses abroad)
57. **Cross-border estate tax treaties** — most relevant for UK, Germany, France, Canada cross-border families
58. **Insurance wrappers (PPLI)** and other tax-efficient investment structures
59. **Private trust companies** for families with enough ongoing trust activity to justify their own entity
60. **Annual "coordinated advisor" summit** — attorney, CPA, financial advisor, family-office CIO, insurance, trust company all in one room

**Watch-outs:**

- **Scrutiny.** Every aggressive position is audit-visible. Conservation easements, DING/NING, valuation discounts — all under regulatory microscope.
- **Second-generation breakdown.** Most family fortunes don't survive the third generation not because of tax but because of poor next-gen preparation.
- **Philanthropy drift.** Foundation founders' intent routinely abandoned by grandchildren — explicit governance hedges.
- **Concentration in illiquid assets.** A $300M estate that is 85% the family operating business needs ILIT-funded estate tax *and* §6166 *and* potentially §303 redemption *and* an orderly monetization plan.

**Typical cost:** Not comparable to lower tiers — this is ongoing operational expense. Family office budgets run $500K–$10M/year depending on assets under management and complexity.

**Full checklist:** [TIER-5-INDUSTRIALIST.md](../tiers/TIER-5-INDUSTRIALIST.md)

## Routing Decision Tree

```
1. Ask: approximate net worth?
   < $500K            → Tier 1
   $500K – $3M        → Tier 2
   $3M – $15M         → Tier 3
   $15M – $100M       → Tier 4
   $100M+             → Tier 5

2. Then ask each complexity overlay one at a time.
   For each "yes":
     - Pull in the relevant reference file
     - Do NOT bump the primary tier unless the overlay dominates
     - Flag for attorney-level review
```

**Example triage:**

- Age 35, married, 2 kids, net worth $600K, renter, W-2 income $180K → Tier 2, simple
- Age 52, blended family, second marriage, $4M estate, two kids from first marriage, vacation home in second state → Tier 3 + blended family + multi-state overlay
- Age 68, retired founder, $80M, non-citizen spouse, disabled adult son on Medicaid → Tier 4 + non-citizen spouse + SNT overlays
- Age 74, industrialist family, $350M, private business, family in three countries, philanthropic trust existing → Tier 5 + international + business succession + family governance

## Bumping Logic

Skills should **never ask the user to self-identify a tier**. Instead, route based on intake answers:

```
IF net_worth < $500K AND no_business AND no_real_estate_multi_state AND no_disability AND no_addiction AND no_blended_family:
   → Tier 1 flow, usually a relatively short, low-complexity engagement
ELIF net_worth < $3M AND no_business_over_500K:
   → Tier 2 flow, usually moderate depth with focused drafting / coordination work
ELIF net_worth < $15M:
   → Tier 3 flow, often multi-session with basis and state-tax modeling
ELIF net_worth < $100M:
   → Tier 4 flow, usually multi-session with heavy attorney involvement
ELSE:
   → Tier 5 flow, family-office / multi-advisor operating model
```

When uncertain, widen diligence on the risky subsection instead of blindly promoting the entire matter to a more burdensome tier.
