# Cross-Border and Conflict-of-Laws Escalation

Cross-border estate planning is where otherwise sound domestic logic fails.
This file exists to force escalation rather than casual extrapolation.

Output to:

- `analyses/foreign-and-conflict-of-laws-review.md`

---

## Trigger Conditions

Run this review when any of the following appears:

- foreign real estate
- foreign bank or brokerage account
- foreign trust or foundation
- non-U.S.-citizen spouse
- foreign beneficiary
- expatriate or expected relocation abroad
- dual-state domicile dispute
- concern about forced heirship
- need for a separate situs will

---

## Questions To Resolve

1. Which jurisdiction governs the asset at death?
2. Which jurisdiction governs the person's status, capacity, or marriage?
3. Does the local jurisdiction impose forced-heirship or reserved-share rules?
4. Does the asset require a separate local will, deed, or transfer process?
5. Could the U.S. plan accidentally create tax, ownership, or probate problems abroad?
6. Does local counsel need to be engaged before final recommendations are treated as reliable?

---

## Required Sections

1. foreign elements present
2. governing-law uncertainty
3. separate counsel / separate will / separate probate risk
4. immediate issues that block confident planning
5. questions for local and U.S. counsel to coordinate

---

## Operating Rule

Do not present a cross-border recommendation as settled unless:

- the controlling jurisdiction is clear
- the relevant law is verified
- and attorney escalation has been explicitly considered

---

## Anti-Pattern

Do not assume a U.S. revocable trust, U.S. will clause, or domestic beneficiary pattern
will control foreign real estate or civil-law inheritance regimes.
