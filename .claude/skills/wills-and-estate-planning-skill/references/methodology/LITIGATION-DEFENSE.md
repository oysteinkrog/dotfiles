# Litigation Defense

Good estate planning is not only "what should happen?" but also "how would an angry,
motivated beneficiary attack this?" This file forces an adversarial review before the
plan is treated as stable.

---

## When This Review Should Be Triggered By Default

Run litigation-defense review by default when any of the following appears:

- unequal treatment of children or siblings
- disinheritance
- blended family / second marriage
- vulnerable testator or recent cognitive decline
- caregiver or favored child in the room
- very recent plan changes
- high-value lumpy assets
- business succession disputes
- family members with known hostility or litigation history

Output to:

- `analyses/litigation-risk-memo.md`

---

## Main Attack Surfaces

### 1. Capacity

Questions:

- Was the testator competent at the time of execution?
- Is there fluctuating cognition, dementia, delirium, heavy medication, stroke, or
  psychiatric history?
- Is there contemporaneous medical evidence?

### 2. Undue Influence

Questions:

- Who is driving the process?
- Is a beneficiary isolating the testator?
- Is the favored person arranging the lawyer, witnesses, transport, or storage?
- Is there dependency, fear, or coercive leverage?

### 3. Improper Execution

Questions:

- Were the right witnesses present?
- Was the ceremony state-compliant?
- Is self-proving available and used correctly?
- Is there any remote / bedside / interstate wrinkle?

### 4. Ambiguity and Internal Conflict

Questions:

- Do the will, trust, deed, and beneficiary forms tell one story?
- Are contingent provisions clear?
- Are class terms like "children" or "issue" likely to be contested?

### 5. Fiduciary Conflict

Questions:

- Is the chosen executor / trustee also the most controversial beneficiary?
- Will co-fiduciaries deadlock?
- Is a neutral or professional fiduciary needed?

### 6. Procedural Surprise

Questions:

- Will heirs feel ambushed?
- Is there a family communication strategy?
- Is there explanatory writing in the user's own words?

## Required Sections For `analyses/litigation-risk-memo.md`

1. **Facts that increase contest risk**
2. **Likely challenge theories**
3. **Documents or evidence already supporting the plan**
4. **Missing evidence or procedural weaknesses**
5. **Execution safeguards to request from counsel**
6. **Family communication steps that reduce surprise**
7. **Specific attorney questions**

Where relevant, include:

- capacity memo need
- doctor letter possibility
- videotaped signing discussion with counsel
- neutral witness selection
- separate representation / conflict handling

---

## Special Hardening Moves

### Unequal Treatment / Disinheritance

- explain the why in the decision ledger and letter of wishes
- avoid vague moral language
- state whether lifetime support or prior advances justify the difference
- ask counsel how to draft the clause cleanly under local law

### Blended Family

- model remarriage and survivor-control risk
- test whether children of the first marriage remain protected
- avoid relying on "good intentions" where QTIP / trust structure is needed

### Elder / Bedside Execution

- keep interested beneficiaries away from execution logistics where possible
- record who was present and why
- verify ceremony compliance for the exact state

---

## Anti-Pattern

Do not mistake a no-contest clause for a full litigation-defense strategy.
In some states it is weak, limited, or context-sensitive. The real defense is:

- valid execution
- clean documents
- coherent beneficiary / title alignment
- good evidence
- low surprise
