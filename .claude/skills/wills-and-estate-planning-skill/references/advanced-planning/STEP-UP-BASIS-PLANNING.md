# Step-Up Basis Planning

For estates BELOW the federal exemption landscape (most American estates under the $15M single-person exclusion, and many married estates that are still below their realistically preserved combined shelter), basis maximization often produces more wealth for heirs than transfer-tax avoidance.

## The Core Principle

At death, heirs inherit assets at fair market value (FMV) on date of death. Any unrealized appreciation during the decedent's lifetime is **erased for income tax purposes**.

**Compare:**
- Lifetime gift of appreciated stock: recipient inherits donor's basis; capital gains tax due on sale
- Inheritance at death: recipient takes stepped-up basis; sells with minimal/zero capital gain

## When Step-Up Dominates

For estates BELOW federal exemption + state exemption:
- Removing assets from estate often saves little or no transfer tax
- Lost step-up can create a meaningful future capital-gains burden
- Holding until death is often the starting presumption, but still needs to be checked against state tax, liquidity needs, creditor risk, and family goals

For estates ABOVE federal exemption:
- Removing appreciation from the estate can create very large transfer-tax savings
- Lost step-up still matters and should be modeled rather than hand-waved away
- Transfer-tax planning often becomes more compelling, but the crossover is a modeling exercise, not a slogan

The crossover happens at the exemption. State estate tax shifts the line.

## Maximizing Step-Up Strategies

### 1. Hold Appreciated Assets Until Death

Resist gifting appreciated assets to children during life unless you've modeled a real reason to trade away basis step-up, such as transfer-tax exposure, creditor or remarriage protection, or another non-tax objective strong enough to justify it.

**Bad pattern**: Parent gifts $500K of low-basis stock to child. Child sells; pays $80K+ capital gains tax. Under the modeled facts, the parent's estate likely would not have owed federal estate tax anyway. Net loss: roughly $80K+.

**Good pattern**: Parent holds appreciated stock until death. Child inherits with stepped-up basis. If sold promptly, there may be little or no capital gain.

### 2. Community Property With Right of Survivorship (CPWROS)

In community-property states that recognize the form, spouses can often use CPWROS for jointly held real estate.

At first spouse's death:
- properly classified community property can receive a full basis adjustment on the entire property
- by contrast, ordinary joint-survivorship ownership in common-law states often adjusts only the decedent's share

**Potentially very large basis benefit.** A $2M house bought for $500K in California may produce a dramatically different tax result at first death depending on whether the property is actually community property and how it is titled.

### 3. Community Property Trusts (in non-CP states)

A small but growing set of non-community-property jurisdictions have community-property-trust regimes. In the right fact pattern, they may help preserve community-property-style basis treatment even outside a classic community-property state, but this is highly state-specific and should never be pitched from memory.

Advanced; verify current state law, resident/nonresident eligibility, funding mechanics, creditor tradeoffs, and tax treatment with specialized counsel.

### 4. "Gift-and-Die-Back" Strategy

In common-law states, healthier spouse gifts appreciated assets to dying spouse who wills them back to healthier spouse.
- Step-up at first death (when assets pass back)
- Surviving spouse owns at stepped-up basis

**Critical: IRC §1014(e)** denies the step-up if assets pass back to original donor (or donor's spouse) within ONE YEAR of the gift. Plan at least a year ahead.

### 5. QTIP Trust for Potential Later Basis Adjustment

QTIP assets are included in the surviving spouse's estate at second death (unlike classic credit-shelter-trust assets). That often preserves a later basis adjustment for includible appreciated assets at the surviving spouse's death.

Trade-off:
- QTIP: often preserves a later basis adjustment, but keeps the assets in the survivor's estate (which can push the plan back into transfer-tax exposure)
- Credit shelter: bypasses the survivor's estate, but usually gives up that later basis-adjustment opportunity

For below-exemption estates, QTIP often deserves a serious look because of the possible second basis adjustment.
For above-exemption estates, credit-shelter planning often deserves a serious look because of transfer-tax containment.
Do not present either as an automatic winner without modeling state estate tax, basis, control, remarriage risk, and asset mix.

### 6. Unwinding Old Irrevocable Trusts

Trusts created decades ago when exemption was $1M, $2M, or $5.43M may now be counterproductive:
- Assets in irrevocable trust DON'T get step-up at grantor's death
- Heirs sell with carryover basis, paying capital gains
- If the estate is now below the relevant federal and state thresholds, the trust may no longer be pulling its weight relative to the basis cost

**Decanting** allows trustee to move assets from old irrevocable trust into new trust with improved terms (including potentially pulling assets back into estate for step-up).

**Granting power of appointment** to grantor's spouse can pull trust assets back into estate at spouse's death (causing inclusion → step-up).

**Trust modification** through court or non-judicial settlement agreement can adjust outdated provisions.

These are sophisticated; engage specialized counsel.

### 7. Reviewing Old FLP/FLLC Discounts

FLPs created with aggressive valuation discounts (lack of marketability, lack of control) reduce gift/estate tax — but also reduce heirs' stepped-up basis.

If estate now below federal exemption, the discount cost (lost step-up) may exceed the discount benefit (transfer-tax savings).

Consider unwinding or restructuring the FLP/FLLC.

### 8. Roth Conversions Before Death

Convert traditional IRA → Roth during low-income years, paying income tax now.
- Roth assets can be significantly more heir-friendly than traditional IRA assets, and may pass income-tax-free if the qualified-distribution requirements are satisfied
- Many non-spouse heirs still face a year-10 outside deadline, but the exact payout schedule depends on beneficiary type and required-distribution rules in force at death
- Reduces "tax bomb" that traditional IRA inheritance creates

Especially valuable when:
- Decedent expects to be in lower bracket than heirs
- Decedent has cash to pay tax on conversion
- Estate above exemption (Roth is removed from estate by tax payment)

## Asset-Specific Step-Up Treatment

| Asset | Gets Step-Up at Death? |
|-------|------------------------|
| Real estate | ✅ Yes (full FMV) |
| Stocks (taxable account) | ✅ Yes |
| Bonds (taxable account) | ✅ Yes |
| Mutual funds | ✅ Yes |
| Business interests | ✅ Yes (with valuation) |
| Collectibles | ✅ Yes |
| Cryptocurrency | ✅ Yes (date-of-death FMV) |
| **Traditional IRA / 401(k)** | ❌ NO (IRD - income in respect of decedent) |
| **Annuities (deferred)** | ❌ NO |
| **HSA** | ❌ NO |
| **Series EE/I bonds** | Special rules |
| **Cash** | N/A (always at FMV) |
| **Roth IRA** | Usually income-tax-light for heirs, but qualified-distribution and timing rules still matter |

## Documentation Required at Death

For every appreciated asset, the executor needs:
- Date-of-death FMV (publicly traded: average of high/low; private: appraisal)
- Form 8971 analysis if Form 706 is filed and basis-consistency reporting applies
- Records preserved for beneficiaries' eventual sale (years later)

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Lifetime gifting of appreciated assets below exemption** — often a near-pure loss of step-up once federal and state exposure are modeled
2. **Old irrevocable trust never decanted** — heirs inherit carryover basis
3. **No CPWROS or equivalent community-property titling review in a community-property setting** — basis opportunity missed
4. **Aggressive FLP discounts in below-exemption estate** — step-up reduced
5. **Failure to document date-of-death values** — basis support degrades and later tax reporting becomes much harder
6. **§1014(e) violation** — gift-and-die-back within one year — step-up denied
7. **Form 8971 not filed when required** — penalties + downstream basis confusion
8. **Roth conversion not considered** — heirs hit with IRA tax bomb
