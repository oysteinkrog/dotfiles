# Trust Situs Selection

For long-term trusts (dynasty, ILIT, IDGT, asset-protection), state of administration meaningfully affects taxation, duration, creditor protection, and flexibility.

## What Situs Affects

1. **State income tax on trust income** (when undistributed or distributed to non-resident beneficiary)
2. **Duration** (rule against perpetuities)
3. **Asset-protection statutes** (DAPT, dynasty)
4. **Trust modification flexibility** (decanting, NJSAs, judicial)
5. **Directed-trust statutes** (separate trustee, investment, distribution functions)
6. **Quiet/silent trust statutes** (beneficiary doesn't have to know about trust)
7. **Trust protector statutes**
8. **Privacy** (court records)

## When Situs Matters Most

### Long-term trust with income generation

If trust earns $500K/year of dividends and the grantor's home state taxes that:
- California (13.3% top rate): $66K/year state tax
- South Dakota (0%): $0/year state tax
- Over 30 years: $2M+ savings

**For long-duration trusts, situs in no-tax state is significant.**

### Beneficiary in different state from grantor

State income tax on trust income depends on a complex matrix:
- State of trust administration (situs)
- State of grantor (sometimes, depending on trust type)
- State of beneficiary (sometimes)
- Source of income (real estate vs. portfolio)

Aggressive states (CA, NY, NJ) try to tax trusts based on grantor's residence at creation OR trustee residence OR beneficiary residence. Some of these positions have been struck down (Kaestner v. NC Dept. of Revenue, 2019).

### DING / NING Trusts (Aggressive)

Delaware/Nevada Incomplete Non-Grantor trusts:
- Structured as nongrantor for income tax (trust pays its own tax in the no-tax state)
- Structured as incomplete gift for gift tax (no exemption use)
- Used before liquidity events (IPO, business sale) to shelter the gain from grantor's high-tax state

**California's response (2023+)**: SB 131 treats NING/DING as grantor trusts for CA income tax purposes if grantor is a CA resident. Effectively eliminated for CA residents.

**Other aggressive states (NY, NJ)**: similar pushback.

**Still works for**: residents of moderate-tax states or those who can establish domicile in low-tax state before liquidity event.

## Establishing Situs

Not enough to just say "this trust is governed by South Dakota law." Must actually administer there:

### Required Elements

- **Trustee in the state** (corporate trustee in SD, NV, etc.)
- **Trust assets located in the state** (or held by trustee there)
- **Trust administration in the state** (records, decisions)
- **Trust document specifies state law** governs

### Optional but Strengthening

- Investment committee in the state
- Distribution committee in the state
- Beneficiary meetings in the state
- Trust protector in the state

## Changing Situs

Three mechanisms:

### 1. Trust Document Provides

Many modern trusts include "trust protector" or "trustee" power to change situs. Simplest method.

### 2. Decanting

Trustee "decants" assets from old trust to new trust in different state. Most states allow with various restrictions.

### 3. Court Modification

Judicial proceeding to change governing law. More expensive; sometimes required.

## Coordination With Estate Planning

- **Dynasty trust** funded with GST exemption: situs in SD/NV for perpetual duration + tax efficiency
- **ILIT**: situs in SD/NV for asset protection + tax efficiency
- **DAPT**: must be in DAPT state (NV/SD/AK/DE/WY)
- **IDGT**: situs in low-tax state to minimize state income tax burden on grantor
- **CRT**: situs less critical (grantor pays tax during life)

## Selecting a Trustee

For situs in another state, options:

### Corporate Trustees

Bank trust departments and independent trust companies in SD, NV, DE specialize in non-resident trusts.

Examples: South Dakota Trust Company, Bryn Mawr Trust (DE), various Nevada companies.

Cost: 50-100 basis points/year of assets, often with minimums ($5K-$25K/year).

### Family-Member Trustee + Corporate Co-Trustee

Family member for context; corporate trustee for administrative compliance.

### Trust Protector

Third party with limited powers (remove/replace trustees, change situs, modify provisions). Often an attorney with no other connection.

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Situs claimed but not administered there** — home-state taxation continues
2. **Choosing wrong state** for the specific need (e.g., DAPT in non-DAPT state)
3. **Forgetting about home state aggressive rules** (CA SB 131)
4. **Cost of corporate trustee** exceeds tax savings for small trust
5. **Trust protector with too much power** — IRS may treat as grantor
6. **No power to change situs** — frozen as drafted
7. **State of administration changes** but not documented properly
8. **Beneficiary moves to high-tax state** — may attract state taxation
