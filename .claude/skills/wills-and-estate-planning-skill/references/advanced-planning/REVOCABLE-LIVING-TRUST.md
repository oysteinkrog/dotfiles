# Revocable Living Trust

The revocable living trust ("RLT") is often the planning hub for estates of meaningful complexity. Created during life, fully amendable, it can hold assets while you're alive and distribute them at death without probate.

## When to Use

**Strong indicators:**
- Real estate in two or more states (avoids ancillary probate in each)
- Substantial real estate (avoids slow + public probate)
- Privacy concerns (probate file is public; trust is private)
- Incapacity continuity needs (successor trustee can step in immediately)
- Blended family complexity
- Vulnerable heirs requiring trust protection
- High-conflict family dynamics

**Lower-yield / not automatic:**
- Tier 1 estate, single state, simple family, no real estate
- Many Texas residents with simple facts (independent administration is relatively efficient)
- Estates that pass mostly by beneficiary designation anyway

## How It Works

1. **Grantor creates trust** — signs trust document; appoints self as initial trustee
2. **Grantor funds trust** — re-titles assets ("John Doe, Trustee of the John Doe Revocable Trust dated 5/1/2026")
3. **Grantor lives normally** — same control, same tax treatment, same access
4. **Incapacity** — successor trustee takes over per trust terms
5. **Death** — successor trustee distributes per trust terms; no probate for trust-owned assets
6. **Pour-over will** as backup — catches anything not titled to trust

## Tax Treatment During Life

**No tax change.** Revocable trust is "grantor trust" for income tax — all income reported on grantor's 1040, same as if owned individually. Uses grantor's SSN, not separate EIN.

**No estate tax savings during life.** Assets in revocable trust are still in the grantor's taxable estate.

**At death.** Trust becomes irrevocable. Successor trustee gets EIN. Assets included in the taxable estate generally receive basis adjustment at death; the revocable trust itself is not what creates that result. Trust files Form 1041 for income earned during administration.

## Key Drafting Decisions

### Successor Trustee Selection

- **Primary** — typically spouse if married
- **Successor** — adult child, sibling, friend, or corporate trustee
- **Backup successor** — usually at least one, and often more when age, health, or family dynamics justify it
- **Co-trustees** — possible but creates coordination overhead
- **Corporate trustee** — bank trust department, $5K-$25K+/year; objective; useful for complex or contested estates

### Incapacity Triggers

- "Two physicians certify in writing that I am unable to manage my financial affairs"
- "Family council determination" — softer, more flexible
- "Designated trust protector determination"

### Trust Protector Provisions

A third party with limited powers:
- Remove and replace trustees
- Change trust situs (e.g., move from CA to NV for income tax)
- Modify administrative provisions
- Adapt to tax law changes

### Distribution Provisions

- Specific bequests on death
- Residuary distributions
- Trust continues for vulnerable beneficiaries (becomes irrevocable continuing trust)
- Per stirpes vs. per capita
- Disinheritance with explicit language

### Funding Provisions

- "Pour-over" from will catches anything missed
- Schedule A listing initial funding assets
- Provisions for adding assets over time

## Funding the Trust (THE MOST COMMON FAILURE)

**For assets you expect the trust to control, an unfunded trust does very little.** Major probate-exposed assets must actually be re-titled or otherwise coordinated.

| Asset Type | How to Fund |
|------------|-------------|
| Real estate | Deed transfer ("John Doe, Trustee...") + record at county |
| Bank accounts | Re-title at bank; new account or change existing |
| Brokerage | Re-title at brokerage; complete trustee certification |
| Vehicles | DMV title transfer (some states allow this) |
| Personal property | Schedule A in trust; bill of sale optional |
| Business interests | Coordinate with operating agreement; assign membership/shares |
| Retirement accounts | DO NOT re-title into the trust; coordinate beneficiary designations separately |
| Life insurance | Generally beneficiary designation, not ownership transfer |

**Recurring failure:** Revocable trust signed in 2018; user buys new house in 2022 in own name, never re-titles to trust. House goes through probate at death.

**Mitigation:** Annual funding audit. Every new acquisition routinely re-titled.

## Pour-Over Will

Backup safety net. Says "everything I own at death pours into my revocable trust."

- Still requires probate for unfunded assets (defeats purpose if trust unfunded)
- But ensures the trust's distribution provisions ultimately control
- Standard component of any revocable-trust-based plan

## Pros and Cons

**Pros:**
- Probate avoidance (huge in CA, FL; meaningful in NY, IL; less critical in TX)
- Privacy
- Multi-state real estate consolidation
- Incapacity continuity
- Continuity of management (no waiting for letters testamentary)
- Foundation for sub-trusts (bypass, QTIP, special needs)

**Cons:**
- Setup cost ($2K-$7K typical for couple's package)
- Funding burden (real cost is ongoing discipline)
- No creditor protection during life
- No estate tax savings during life
- More complexity for simple estates

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Trust created, never funded in any meaningful way.** Pour-over cleanup can still leave key assets in probate.
2. **New asset acquired, never re-titled.** Probate for that asset.
3. **No successor trustee designated.** Family conflict over who serves.
4. **Joint trust for couple, never split at first death.** Tax complications.
5. **Trust drafted in old state, family moves.** New state's rules may differ.
6. **Sub-trust provisions outdated** as tax law changed.
7. **No trust protector provisions** — frozen as drafted forever.
8. **Co-trustees in conflict** — gridlock during incapacity.
9. **Confused with irrevocable trust** — grantor surprised when realizing they can amend (or vice versa).
10. **Used as probate-avoidance band-aid** without addressing underlying coordination issues.

## Worked Example: Married Couple in NY

For this simplified example, assume the couple's revocable-trust assets total about **$16M** (house $3M, brokerage $12M, bank accounts $1M), each spouse is treated as having contributed roughly half for first-death funding purposes, and retirement accounts / life insurance pass separately by beneficiary designation.

At first spouse's death:
- Roughly the then-current New York exclusion amount can fund the deceased spouse's **Family Trust** (credit-shelter share), with any excess of the decedent's share routed under the marital / survivor design
- Surviving spouse continues with survivor-side assets plus whatever access the trust instrument gives to Family Trust income or principal
- Federal portability and the New York filing are reviewed and filed as appropriate
- Assets parked in the Family Trust stay outside the survivor's later taxable estate

This structure is useful because New York's exemption is not portable. Without a deliberate first-death split, a larger married estate can leave the full combined pool exposed in the survivor's later New York taxable estate, effectively wasting the first spouse's New York exclusion.

## Coordination Required

- Will (pour-over)
- Beneficiary designations (route around trust for retirement; route through trust for life insurance/large taxable accounts as appropriate)
- Healthcare proxy + financial POA
- Letter of instruction
- Funding audit annually

## State-Specific Notes

- **CA**: Revocable trust often deserves especially serious consideration for homeowners because probate cost and publicity are severe
- **FL**: Often recommended; probate is paperwork-heavy
- **TX**: Often optional; independent administration is good
- **NY**: Useful for multi-state real estate, privacy, sub-trust planning
- **OR/MA**: Useful for credit-shelter sub-trust to preserve state exemption
