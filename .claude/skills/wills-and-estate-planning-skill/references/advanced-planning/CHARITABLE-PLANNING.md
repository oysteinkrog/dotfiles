# Charitable Planning (DAFs, Foundations, CRTs, CLTs, QCDs, More)

For families with genuine charitable intent. Combines philanthropic goals with tax efficiency. In the right fact pattern, charitable planning can produce unusually strong after-tax leverage relative to many other planning moves.

## Vehicle Selection Decision Tree

```
Charitable intent?
├─ Modest annual giving ($1K-$25K/year)
│   └─ Direct gifts of cash or appreciated securities to public charities
│
├─ Substantial annual giving + want flexibility ($25K-$500K/year)
│   └─ DONOR-ADVISED FUND (DAF)
│
├─ Want family involvement + some control ($500K-$5M+ committed)
│   └─ DAF with family advisor structure, OR small private foundation
│
├─ Mission-driven, multi-generational, $5M-$25M+ committed
│   └─ PRIVATE FOUNDATION (with succession plan)
│
├─ Have appreciated asset to sell + want lifetime income
│   └─ CHARITABLE REMAINDER TRUST (CRT)
│
├─ Want children to inherit eventually + provide for charity now
│   └─ CHARITABLE LEAD TRUST (CLT)
│
├─ Age 70½+ with IRA distributions
│   └─ QUALIFIED CHARITABLE DISTRIBUTION (QCD) annually
│
├─ Want IRA out of estate at death + have charitable intent
│   └─ Designate charity as IRA beneficiary (often among the most tax-efficient bequests)
│
└─ Land with development restrictions you'd accept
    └─ CONSERVATION EASEMENT (carefully — IRS audits aggressively)
```

## Donor-Advised Fund (DAF)

### Mechanics

1. Contribute appreciated assets to a sponsoring organization (Fidelity Charitable, Schwab Charitable, Vanguard Charitable, community foundation)
2. Take immediate income tax deduction (FMV for long-term appreciated assets, up to AGI limits)
3. Sponsoring organization sells appreciated assets tax-free (no capital gain)
4. Funds invested per donor-recommended strategy
5. Donor advises grants to charities over time (no required timeline)
6. At donor's death, successor advisor (often spouse, then children) continues granting

### Pros

- Immediate income tax deduction
- Tax-free liquidation of appreciated assets
- Investment growth before distribution to charity
- Privacy (vs. private foundation reporting)
- Sponsor minimums, fees, grant rules, and succession options vary materially; some national sponsors are highly accessible, while others start at much higher balances
- Successor advisor provisions for multi-generational philanthropy

### Cons

- No direct family salaries (vs. private foundation)
- No private benefit beyond grants
- Sponsoring organization technically owns the funds
- DAF balances may face proposed distribution requirements (legislative risk)

### Common Use

- Bunching strategy: contribute multiple years' giving in one year for itemization
- Liquidity event year: large contribution to offset windfall income
- Family teaching tool: kids serve as advisors, learn philanthropy
- Estate-planning bequest: name DAF as IRA beneficiary

## Private Foundation

### Mechanics

1. Form 501(c)(3) private foundation (typically 12-18 month process)
2. Fund with cash, securities, or other assets
3. Take income tax deduction (with stricter limits than public charity gifts)
4. Distribute at least 5% of assets annually for charitable purposes
5. Pay 1.39% excise tax on net investment income
6. Comply with self-dealing, jeopardizing investment, and other rules

### Pros

- Full control over giving direction
- Family employment (with proper "reasonable compensation")
- Multi-generational governance vehicle
- Distinct identity (e.g., "The Smith Family Foundation")
- Coordination with broader family philanthropy

### Cons

- Significant administrative burden
- 5% annual distribution requirement
- Excise tax (1.39%)
- Self-dealing rules limit family transactions
- Lower deduction limits than public charity
- Public Form 990-PF (full transparency)

### When Justified

Generally requires $5M+ committed assets for cost-effectiveness. Below that, DAF + family advisor structure usually serves the same goals more efficiently.

## Charitable Remainder Trust (CRT)

### Mechanics

1. Donor transfers appreciated assets to CRT
2. CRT can often sell appreciated assets without immediate entity-level capital-gain recognition at the trust level
3. CRT pays donor (or named non-charity) income for life or term up to 20 years
4. Income taxed under "tier" rules (ordinary income, capital gain, tax-free, return of principal)
5. At end of term, remainder passes to charity (often DAF for flexibility)
6. Donor takes immediate income tax deduction for present value of charitable remainder

### Variants

- **CRAT** (Charitable Remainder Annuity Trust): fixed annuity payment
- **CRUT** (Charitable Remainder Unitrust): fixed % of annual asset value
- **NIMCRUT** (Net Income with Makeup Charitable Remainder Unitrust): pays lesser of net income or fixed %
- **Flip CRUT**: starts as NIMCRUT, switches to standard CRUT on triggering event

### Use Case: Pre-Liquidity Event

Donor owns $5M of low-basis stock, plans to sell. Without CRT: $4M+ capital gain triggers ~$1M tax.

With CRT:
1. Transfer stock to CRT before sale
2. CRT sells stock tax-free
3. Donor receives income for life (~$300K/year for 20 years)
4. Donor takes immediate income tax deduction (~$1.5M present value of remainder)
5. At term end, ~$3M passes to designated charity (or DAF)

Net result: same charitable mission + lifetime income + immediate deduction + the ability to avoid taking the full capital-gain hit in the sale year, with later payments taxed under the CRT tier rules.

## Charitable Lead Trust (CLT)

### Mechanics

Mirror image of CRT:
- Charity receives income stream for term
- Remainder passes to family at end of term
- Donor takes deduction for present value of charitable lead interest (with caveats)

### Use Case: Wealth Transfer with Charitable Component

Family wants to transfer $10M to children with low gift-tax cost AND benefit charity:

1. Transfer $10M to CLT for 20 years
2. CLT pays $500K/year to charity
3. After 20 years, remainder (potentially $15M+ if assets grow) passes to children
4. Gift-tax cost: present value of remainder interest (low if interest rates low)

Best in low-interest-rate environments.

## Qualified Charitable Distribution (QCD)

### Mechanics

After age 70½:
- Direct transfer from IRA to qualified public charity
- Up to the current IRS-indexed annual limit (**$111K in 2026**; verify later-year amounts)
- Counts toward Required Minimum Distribution (RMD)
- NOT included in donor's income
- More tax-efficient than receiving distribution and donating (avoids AGI inflation, reduces Medicare IRMAA, avoids Social Security taxability triggers)

### Coordination

- Annual recurring use for charitable giving in retirement years
- Particularly valuable for taxpayers who don't itemize
- Cannot go to DAF or private foundation (must be operating charity)

## Charitable Bequest of Retirement Account

Often one of the most tax-efficient charitable bequest structures:

- Charity pays NO income tax on inherited IRA
- Children inherit other (basis-stepped-up) assets
- Compare to children inheriting IRA: 10-year payout at ordinary income rates (often 30-40% combined federal+state)

**Example**: $1M IRA + $1M brokerage. Charitable intent = $1M.
- **Bad**: Leave $1M IRA to children (~$350K to taxes), $1M brokerage to charity ($1M to charity, kids net $650K)
- **Good**: Leave $1M IRA to charity ($1M to charity), $1M brokerage to children ($1M to kids, no tax due to step-up)
- **Result**: $350K saved by reversing the assignment

## Conservation Easement

Donate development rights on land to qualified land trust.
- Charitable deduction = appraised value of restriction (often 30-50% of land value)
- Highly scrutinized by IRS
- Syndicated easements aggressively challenged (avoid)
- Legitimate easements still valuable but require scrupulous appraisal and structuring

## Bargain Sale

Sell appreciated property to charity below FMV. Difference between FMV and sale price is charitable contribution; sale portion recognizes proportional gain.

## Pooled Income Fund

Like a CRT but commingled with other donors' funds at a sponsoring charity. Simpler to administer for smaller gifts ($25K-$250K).

## Charitable Gift Annuity

Direct contract with a charity:
- Transfer assets in exchange for lifetime annuity
- Charity keeps remainder
- Simpler than CRT
- Smaller charities offer; rates set by ACGA

## State Coordination

Most states allow charitable bequest deduction parallel to federal. Some states have limited deduction; verify state-specific treatment.

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Naming charity by general description** without legal name + EIN — bequest fails
2. **Using IRA for non-charitable bequest** when charitable intent exists — tax inefficient
3. **CRT funded with mortgaged property** — complications
4. **Conservation easement appraisal too aggressive** — IRS audit
5. **Private foundation with family-employment compliance issues** — penalties
6. **DAF with no successor advisor named** — distributes per sponsoring org's defaults
7. **Naming rights without morals clause** — institution drift problem
8. **Restricted gifts too narrow** — cy pres action needed eventually
