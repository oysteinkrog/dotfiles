# ILIT (Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust)

The standard solution for keeping life insurance proceeds out of the taxable estate. Especially valuable for estates with illiquid assets needing liquidity for estate tax.

## When to Use

**Strong candidates when:**
- Federal estate-tax exposure AND life insurance > exemption gap
- Illiquid estate (business, real estate, farm) needing liquidity for tax payment
- Blended family — life insurance to first-marriage children at first death
- State estate tax above-exemption AND life insurance proceeds substantial

**Often poor fits when:**
- Estate clearly below federal exemption AND below state exemption
- Insurance is small employer group life only
- No estate-tax exposure expected

## How It Works

1. **Grantor establishes ILIT** (irrevocable trust)
2. **ILIT owns the life insurance policy** (NOT the insured)
3. **Insured names ILIT as beneficiary**
4. **Insured makes annual gifts to ILIT** to fund premium payments
5. **Beneficiaries receive Crummey notices** giving them temporary right to withdraw the gifts (this qualifies the gifts for the annual exclusion)
6. **At insured's death**: death benefit paid to ILIT, NOT to estate
7. **Trustee administers per ILIT terms** (often: distribute to beneficiaries OR use to provide estate liquidity by buying assets from estate or lending to estate)

## Key Tax Treatment

- **During life**: premium gifts qualify for annual exclusion (currently $19K per beneficiary in 2026)
- **At death**: death benefit OUTSIDE insured's taxable estate
- **No income tax** on death benefit to ILIT or beneficiaries
- **Trustee's discretion** to use proceeds for estate liquidity if beneficial

## Critical Drafting Elements

### Crummey Powers

- Each beneficiary has temporary right to withdraw the annual gift contribution
- Notice must be given (Crummey letter)
- Withdrawal right typically 30-90 days
- After lapse, gift is "complete" for trust purposes
- Without Crummey provisions, gifts don't qualify for annual exclusion

### Trustee Selection

- Grantor / insured should not serve in a way that creates retained powers or incidents of ownership
- Spouse may be workable in some structures, but review powers carefully so the insurance is not dragged back into the taxable estate
- Adult child: OK if not primary beneficiary
- Independent / corporate: best for objectivity
- Co-trustees common: family member + independent

### Distribution Provisions

- Outright to beneficiaries at insured's death? OR
- Continue trust for next generation (dynasty)?
- HEMS standard for distributions during trust term?
- Power to buy assets from estate (provides liquidity to estate)?
- Power to lend to estate (alternative liquidity mechanism)?

## Three-Year Lookback Rule (CRITICAL)

If grantor transfers an EXISTING policy to ILIT within 3 years of death, the death benefit is INCLUDED in the estate (defeats the purpose).

**Mitigation:**
- **Buy a NEW policy inside the ILIT** (no transfer; no lookback issue)
- **Wait 3+ years** after transferring existing policy
- **Use 3-year-rule-free strategies** (sell policy to ILIT for fair value; complex)

## Funding Mechanics

### Annual Crummey Cycle

```
Each year (typically January):
1. Grantor wires premium amount to ILIT bank account
2. Trustee sends Crummey notices to each beneficiary
3. Beneficiaries do NOT exercise withdrawal right
4. After 30-90 day window, trustee pays insurance premium
5. Annual gift documented for tax purposes (Form 709 if exceeds annual exclusion)
```

### Funding Limits

- Annual exclusion: $19K × number of Crummey beneficiaries
- Example: 4 beneficiaries → up to $76K/year of premium funded gift-tax-free
- For higher premiums: use lifetime exemption or pay-as-you-go

## Liquidity Use Cases

### Use 1: Direct Distribution

ILIT pays out to beneficiaries at insured's death; beneficiaries use proceeds however they choose. Simple but no estate tax help if heirs don't share proceeds with estate.

### Use 2: Buy Illiquid Assets from Estate

ILIT uses death benefit to purchase shares of family business or real estate from the estate. Estate gets cash to pay tax; ILIT gets the assets (which then continue to grow outside transfer-tax system).

### Use 3: Lend to Estate

ILIT lends death benefit to estate at AFR. Estate uses cash for tax payment. Estate repays note over time (interest-only or amortized). Less aggressive than purchase; preserves family ownership of assets.

## Coordination with Buy-Sell Agreements

For closely held businesses with cross-purchase buy-sell:
- Buy-sell funded by life insurance
- ILIT can own the policies for tax efficiency
- Trustee receives proceeds, executes buy-sell purchase
- Coordinate carefully — ILIT trustee acts on behalf of trust, not grantor

## State Tax Considerations

- Some states tax life insurance proceeds for state estate tax even if federal-exempt via ILIT
- Verify state-specific treatment
- ILIT generally still helps for state tax with proper structure

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Existing policy transferred** within 3 years of death — included in estate
2. **No Crummey notices sent** — annual exclusion lost
3. **Insured keeps incidents of ownership** — policy pulled back into estate
4. **Premium funding delays** — policy lapses
5. **Crummey beneficiary actually withdraws** — premium cycle breaks and family expectations get messy
6. **Trust drafted poorly** — ownership, notice, or liquidity provisions fail when they are actually needed
7. **Trustee fails to maintain insurance** — policy lapses; coverage lost
8. **Family member trustee with no oversight** — administrative errors
9. **No liquidity provisions** — proceeds distributed but estate can't pay tax
10. **Reciprocal trust doctrine** if both spouses fund ILITs for each other

## Cost

- Setup: $3K-$10K legal
- Annual administration: $500-$2K (trustee fees, Crummey notice administration, tax filings)
- Premium payments: per insurance policy
- Trustee fees if corporate trustee: $1K-$5K/year typically

## Worked Example

**Estate**: $30M, mostly illiquid (family business worth $20M).
**Federal estate tax**: ($30M - $15M exemption) × 40% = $6M.
**No ILIT**: family may be forced to sell business assets or borrow heavily within 9 months.
**With ILIT**: $10M life insurance policy on insured's life owned by ILIT.
**Death**: $10M death benefit to ILIT (not in estate, no estate tax on insurance).
**Trustee uses $6M** to purchase business shares from estate; estate pays tax with cash.
**Family retains business**; trustee holds purchased shares for next generation.
**Outcome**: business preserved; estate tax paid; ILIT proceeds + business now outside transfer-tax system.
