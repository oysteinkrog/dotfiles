# SLAT and QPRT (Spouse and Residence Trust Strategies)

Two estate-freeze techniques that use the lifetime gift exemption while preserving family use.

## SLAT (Spousal Lifetime Access Trust)

### Mechanics

1. Spouse 1 ("grantor") creates irrevocable trust for benefit of Spouse 2 + descendants
2. Grantor uses lifetime gift exemption (up to $15M in 2026) to fund SLAT
3. Spouse 2 has access to income + principal (HEMS or broader discretion)
4. Assets grow OUTSIDE grantor's estate
5. Grantor retains indirect access through Spouse 2's distributions
6. At Spouse 2's death, remainder passes to descendants (or stays in trust for them)

### When to Use

**Strong indicators:**
- Estate above federal exemption (especially in HNW range $15M-$50M)
- Both spouses healthy
- Marriage stable
- Want to use $15M exemption now (clawback risk if exemption reduced later)
- Beneficiary spouse has reasonable spending needs

**Skip if:**
- Marriage unstable (divorce risk)
- Beneficiary spouse much older (death before grantor leaves grantor without access)
- Estate below exemption

### Worked Example

- 2026: Husband (Tier 4 estate) creates SLAT, transfers $15M of stock to it (uses entire exemption)
- Wife is beneficiary; children are remainder beneficiaries
- 2046: SLAT has grown to $60M (4x growth)
- Husband dies. SLAT is OUTSIDE his estate. $60M passes to wife or children without estate tax.
- Without SLAT: $60M would have been taxable at 40% = $24M estate tax. **Saved $24M.**

### Critical Risk: Reciprocal Trust Doctrine

If BOTH spouses create SLATs for each other, IRS may "uncross" them and include each in the respective grantor's estate.

**Mitigation:**
- Materially different terms (different beneficiaries, different distribution standards, different trustees, different funding dates, different assets)
- Sequence the funding (years apart, not same day)
- Document the differences

### Other Risks

- **Divorce**: grantor loses indirect access through ex-spouse beneficiary
- **Death of beneficiary spouse**: grantor loses indirect access
- **Investment performance**: can't be undone if poorly invested
- **IRS challenge**: aggressive funding scrutinized

## QPRT (Qualified Personal Residence Trust)

### Mechanics

1. Grantor transfers personal residence (primary or vacation) to QPRT for fixed term
2. Grantor retains right to live in home for the term
3. Gift-tax value = present value of remainder interest (discounted by retained-use term)
4. After term: home passes to children (or trust for them)
5. Grantor can lease home from children at fair-market rent (continued use)
6. **CRITICAL: If grantor dies during term, home reverts to estate** — technique fails

### When to Use

**Strong indicators:**
- High-value home expected to appreciate
- Grantor in good health (low mortality risk for term)
- Grantor comfortable paying rent to children after term
- Estate-tax exposure

**Skip if:**
- Federal exemption is sufficient (less common at $15M)
- Grantor in poor health
- Family conflict over home use
- Grantor unable to pay post-term rent

### Worked Example

- 2026: Grantor (age 60) transfers $4M vacation home to QPRT for 12-year term
- Gift-tax value (discounted): ~$2M (uses some lifetime exemption)
- 2038: Grantor (age 72) survives term. Home (now worth $6M) passes to children.
- Grantor leases home from children at $5,000/month rent. (Rent payments are additional wealth transfer to children, also gift-tax-efficient.)
- Net effect: $6M home + future appreciation transferred at $2M gift-tax cost.

### Risks

- **Mortality during term**: technique fails (no harm done other than legal fees)
- **Inability to pay rent post-term**: home is no longer available; family conflict
- **Children sell the home**: grantor loses use
- **Children inherit and stuck with home**: maintenance, taxes, etc.

## QPRT vs. Outright Gifting Under Higher Exemption

Under 2026's $15M exemption, simple outright gifts of homes (or interest in them) often suffice without QPRT complexity. QPRT remains useful when:
- Large estate with high home value
- Want to capture discount for retained-use term
- Coordinated with broader plan that uses GST exemption

## Coordination

- **Insurance**: maintain on QPRT-held home; trustee may be required to insure
- **Property tax**: continues; grantor pays during term, beneficiaries after
- **Mortgage**: continues; grantor must pay during term; complications if mortgage transfers with title
- **§2036 risk**: if grantor continues to use without paying rent post-term, IRS may include home back in estate
- **State tax**: gift may be state-taxable in some states
