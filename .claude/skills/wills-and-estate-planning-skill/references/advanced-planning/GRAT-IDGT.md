# GRATs and Sales to IDGTs (Estate Freeze Techniques)

For UHNW estates with appreciating assets. Both transfer future appreciation to next generation at low gift-tax cost. Use both — they're complementary.

## When to Use

**Strong indicators:**
- Federal estate exposure (single-person estate materially above $15M, or married-couple exposure materially above the family's realistically preserved combined federal shelter)
- Asset expected to appreciate substantially (pre-IPO stock, real estate in growth market, family business)
- Healthy grantor (low mortality risk for GRAT)
- Sufficient remaining gift exemption for IDGT seed gift
- Family willing to lock up wealth for next generation

**Skip if:**
- Estate well below federal exemption (no transfer-tax savings to capture)
- Asset is appreciated and you'll lose more in step-up than save in transfer tax
- Grantor is in poor health (GRATs fail if grantor dies during term)
- Asset is illiquid and unable to support GRAT annuity payments

## GRAT (Grantor Retained Annuity Trust)

### Mechanics

1. Grantor transfers assets to GRAT for fixed term (typically 2-10 years)
2. Grantor receives annual annuity payment back
3. Annuity = (transferred value × IRS §7520 hurdle rate, present value)
4. "Zeroed-out GRAT": annuity calibrated so gift-tax value = $0
5. If asset outperforms 7520 rate: excess passes to remainder beneficiaries gift-tax-free
6. If asset underperforms: nothing passes; no harm except setup costs
7. **CRITICAL: If grantor dies during term, assets revert to estate** — technique fails

### Optimal Use

- **Pre-IPO stock** with imminent expected appreciation
- **Volatile assets** with high upside (concentrated tech stock)
- **Low §7520 rate environments** (low hurdle to clear)
- **Short terms** (2-3 years) to minimize mortality risk
- **Rolling GRATs**: reinvest annuity payments into new GRATs (each gets fresh chance at appreciation)

### Worked Example

- 2026: Grantor transfers $5M of pre-IPO stock to 2-year GRAT
- §7520 rate at funding: 4.0%
- Annuity: $2,651,961/year for 2 years (zeroes out gift)
- IPO occurs year 2; stock value = $20M
- After 2 years of annuities ($5.3M back to grantor), $14.7M remains in GRAT
- $14.7M passes to children gift-tax-free
- Grantor used $0 of lifetime exemption

### Failure Modes

- Grantor dies during term — assets back to estate (no harm but no benefit)
- Asset doesn't appreciate above hurdle — no transfer
- Insufficient liquidity to fund annuity — must distribute illiquid assets
- IRS challenges valuation — gift tax on excess
- Trust drafted poorly — disqualified

## Sale to IDGT (Intentionally Defective Grantor Trust)

### Mechanics

1. Grantor creates irrevocable trust (IDGT) — grantor for income tax, but NOT for estate tax
2. Grantor makes "seed gift" to IDGT (typically 10% of contemplated sale value) using exemption
3. Grantor sells appreciating assets to IDGT in exchange for installment note (AFR rate)
4. Income tax: NO gain (grantor sells to himself for income tax purposes — disregarded)
5. Estate tax: Asset is OUT of grantor's estate (IDGT is separate for estate tax)
6. Grantor pays income tax on IDGT's earnings ("tax burn") — further depletes estate without gift
7. IDGT repays note from asset cash flow + appreciation
8. Excess appreciation stays in IDGT for beneficiaries

### Advantages Over GRAT

- **No mortality risk** (technique survives grantor's death)
- **GST-friendly** (can allocate GST exemption to IDGT for dynasty planning)
- **Lower interest rate** (AFR is typically lower than §7520)
- **Tax burn**: grantor pays IDGT's income tax = additional wealth transfer with no gift consequence
- **Flexibility**: long-term (vs. fixed GRAT term)

### Disadvantages

- Requires **seed gift** (10% of sale value) using exemption
- Requires **legitimate sale** (must respect arm's-length terms)
- Subject to **IRS scrutiny** (substance over form)
- Grantor's continued income tax burden (could be high)

### Worked Example

- 2026: Grantor creates IDGT, makes $1M seed gift using exemption
- Grantor sells $10M of LLC interests to IDGT in exchange for 9-year promissory note at AFR (4%)
- Annual interest payment: $400K (AFR x $10M)
- LLC produces $800K/year cash flow + appreciates to $20M over 9 years
- IDGT pays $400K interest annually + $10M principal at maturity from cash flow + asset sales
- After 9 years: IDGT owns $10M+ in residual value
- Grantor's estate: only the original note payments received (mostly already spent or in estate)
- Net wealth transferred out of estate (during life): ~$10M+ at minimal gift tax cost

### Failure Modes

- IRS recharacterizes sale as gift (insufficient substance)
- Note interest rate below AFR — gift element
- Grantor consumes annuity payments — estate wins
- Trust loses grantor status during grantor's life — gain recognition
- Grantor dies during note term — note becomes asset of estate (still better than original asset, but transfer not complete)

## GRAT vs. IDGT — When to Use Which

| Factor | GRAT | IDGT |
|--------|------|------|
| Grantor in poor health | ❌ Mortality risk | ✅ Survives death |
| GST exemption use | ❌ Hard | ✅ Easy |
| Interest rate | §7520 (typically higher) | AFR (typically lower) |
| Setup cost | Low | Higher |
| Required exemption use | None (zeroed-out) | 10% seed gift |
| Asset must produce cash flow | Yes (for annuity) | Yes (for note repayment) |
| Best for | Public stock with imminent IPO | Family business, real estate |

**Common pattern**: Use GRATs for pre-IPO public stock (no GST need); use IDGTs for family business and dynasty planning (GST allocation).

## Coordination Issues

- **Reciprocal trust doctrine** if both spouses use IDGT — must be materially different
- **§2036 inclusion** if grantor retains too much control or implicit agreement
- **§754 election** for partnership interests sold to IDGT
- **Grantor trust toggle** (§672(e)) — careful not to lose grantor status accidentally
- **State income tax** — choose situs (NV/SD/DE) to avoid grantor's home state tax on IDGT income
- **Power of substitution** must be properly drafted (§675) for grantor trust status
