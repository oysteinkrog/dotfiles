# Privately Held Business Interests

The family business is often the estate's largest asset and the hardest to handle. Estate tax is due in cash within 9 months. Successors may not be the equal-share beneficiaries. Valuation is uncertain. The business itself may not survive transition.

## Core Issues

1. **Liquidity** — estate tax on a $20M business is due 9 months after death. Where does the cash come from?
2. **Valuation** — the IRS scrutinizes business valuations; aggressive discounts invite audit
3. **Succession** — who runs the business? Are the qualified child and the equal-ownership children the same?
4. **Equalization** — if one child gets the business and others don't, how to fair-share?
5. **S-corp shareholder eligibility** — only certain trusts can hold S-corp stock without terminating the election

## Tools

### Buy-Sell Agreement (Funded by Life Insurance)

Among shareholders/partners. Sets:
- **Triggering events** — death, disability, divorce, retirement, voluntary exit
- **Valuation method** — agreed formula, periodic appraisal, fixed price
- **Funding mechanism** — life insurance owned cross-purchase or by entity (redemption)
- **Payment terms** — lump sum, installments, combination

At death, life insurance proceeds fund the buyout. Survivors get the business; deceased's family gets cash.

### Key-Person Insurance

Separate from buy-sell. Covers loss of revenue/value from a critical employee (often the founder). Proceeds go to the business to cover transition costs.

### ILIT-Owned Life Insurance for Estate-Tax Liquidity

Death benefit pays estate tax without forcing business sale. See [LIFE-INSURANCE.md](LIFE-INSURANCE.md).

### Section 6166 Deferral

Estates where a closely held business represents **more than 35% of the adjusted gross estate** can elect to pay the attributable estate tax over **14 years**:
- 4-5 years interest-only
- Then principal + interest
- Special low interest rate on the first $X (inflation-adjusted)

Allows family to retain business while paying tax over time.

### Section 303 Redemption

A closely held corporation can **redeem stock from the estate** sufficient to pay estate tax and administration expenses, **without** the distribution being treated as a dividend. Tax-efficient way to get cash from the business to the estate.

### Section 1202 QSBS

Under current law, Section 1202 is **acquisition-date-specific**, not a single blanket rule:
- Stock acquired after the OBBBA applicable date can follow a **3 / 4 / 5-year phased exclusion regime** rather than the old all-or-nothing 5-year rule
- Post-applicable-date stock can have a **higher per-issuer dollar cap** than older stock
- Older / grandfathered stock can remain on the older 5-year / older-cap framework
- Founders and early employees of qualifying C-corporations need original-issue, active-business, gross-assets, redemption, and trust-transfer rules checked before assuming any exclusion

### Family Limited Partnerships (FLPs) and Family LLCs

For valuation discounts:
- **Lack of marketability** discount (10-30%)
- **Lack of control / minority interest** discount (10-30%)
- Combined discounts often 20-40%
- Subject to substantial IRS scrutiny — must have business purpose, follow formalities, avoid implied agreements

### Voting vs. Non-Voting Shares

Recapitalize so the working child gets **100% of voting shares** (operational control), while all children split **non-voting shares** (economic dividends). Separates control from economics. Critical when one child runs the business and others don't.

### Trust Structures for S-Corp Shares

Only specific trust types can hold S-corp stock without terminating the election:
- **Grantor trust** (during grantor's life)
- **QSST** (Qualified Subchapter S Trust) — single beneficiary, all income to that beneficiary
- **ESBT** (Electing Small Business Trust) — multiple beneficiaries, but trust pays tax at top rate

A poorly drafted trust receiving S-corp stock can **terminate the S election**, converting to C-corp with disastrous tax consequences.

### GRATs and IDGT Sales

For high-growth businesses, transferring shares via GRAT or sale-to-IDGT removes future appreciation from the estate at low gift-tax cost. See [GRAT-IDGT.md](../advanced-planning/GRAT-IDGT.md).

## Succession Planning — Beyond the Mechanics

The legal structure is only half. The other half:

### Successor Identification vs. Preparation

The son who will inherit the business may not be ready to run it. Identifying the successor at 35 and giving them 15 years of mentorship, progressive responsibility, and outside experience produces a different result than dying at 70 with a will saying "to my son."

### Family Employment Policies

Written rules about:
- Who can work in the business
- For what compensation
- Reporting to whom
- Promotion process
- Exit conditions

Prevents many family fights that develop when family members feel unfairly treated.

### Family Council and Family Constitution

For multi-generation businesses, formal governance structures separate family decisions (values, next-gen prep, philanthropy mission) from business decisions (strategy, compensation, capital allocation).

### Exit Options Other Than Family Succession

Not every family business should pass to the next generation. Alternatives:

- **Sale to management (MBO)**
- **ESOP** (Employee Stock Ownership Plan) — tax-advantaged with §1042 rollover; increasingly popular for founders without family successors
- **Sale to strategic or PE buyer** — maximizes price but changes the business
- **Private placement to growth investors** while maintaining control

### Earnouts and Installment Sales Straddling Death

Common: founder sells with 5-year earnout. Founder dies in year 3.
- Tax acceleration possible
- Modified payments per contract
- Earnout may terminate or continue per terms
- All depends on the contract

### Shareholders' Agreements

With drag-along, tag-along, ROFR, buy-sell triggers — coordinated with estate plan.

### Voting Trust

Keeps voting control consolidated even as economic ownership disperses among multiple family members.

## Common Failure Modes

1. **No buy-sell agreement** — surviving partners and family in conflict
2. **Buy-sell exists but unfunded** — partners can't afford to buy out
3. **Trust receiving S-corp stock terminates election** — C-corp double tax
4. **No successor identified or prepared** — business collapses without founder
5. **Equal shares to working and non-working children** — non-working siblings can fire CEO sibling
6. **No §6166 election considered** — estate tax forces sale
7. **Aggressive valuation discounts without business purpose** — IRS audit, deficiency, penalties
8. **Earnout straddling death without contract review** — surprise tax acceleration
9. **Founder personally guaranteed business loans** — estate becomes liable
10. **No business power of attorney** — no one signs payroll checks during probate

## The Conversation to Have

> "Your business is the largest single asset in your estate. We need to plan for: (1) who runs it after you, (2) how the IRS gets paid without forcing a sale, (3) how non-business children get their fair share, and (4) what happens if the buyer in your existing buy-sell agreement can't pay. This usually requires several pieces working together: a current buy-sell agreement, life insurance funding, possibly an ILIT, possibly §6166 planning, and a coordinated successor-development plan. Let's start with: who is the most likely successor, and how prepared are they?"
