# Taxable Investment Accounts, Margin, and Concentrated Stock

Taxable brokerage accounts are among the simplest assets *if* set up properly. They become complex when leverage, concentration, or executive compensation enters.

## Step-Up in Basis

Often one of the most powerful tax benefits. At death, basis generally becomes **FMV on date of death** (or alternate valuation date 6 months later if elected). Pre-death unrealized gains are generally wiped out for basis purposes.

**For publicly traded securities** valued for IRS purposes: average of high and low trading price on date of death.

**Implications:**
- Hold appreciated assets until death rather than selling during life
- A $5M portfolio with $4M built-in gain → $0 capital gain to heirs if held until death
- Sell during life → ~24% federal + state capital gains tax on the gain

## Beneficiary Designations

- **TOD (Transfer-on-Death)** designations let the account pass directly to named beneficiaries outside probate
- **Joint accounts with right of survivorship** pass automatically — basis treatment depends on marital status, contribution history, and governing federal/state tax rules; for many married couples in common-law states the adjustment is effectively around one-half, but nonspousal contribution cases can differ
- **Individual accounts** without TOD pass through the will

**TOD limitations:**
- Generally one set of beneficiaries; complex contingencies hard
- Beneficiary takes outright (not in trust)
- For substantial accounts with vulnerable beneficiaries, **revocable trust** is better

## Margin Loans — The Often-Overlooked Trap

Margin debt is secured by the securities in the account. The brokerage will **typically refuse to release or distribute** the account until the margin loan is satisfied.

### Critical Risks

- **Account frozen** during estate administration but interest continues to accrue
- **Market downturn → margin call** on a frozen account; brokerage may **force-liquidate** to meet the call
- **Forced liquidation after death** can crystallize avoidable post-death gain or loss; **forced liquidation before death** can also blow up the family's expected basis reset
- TOD beneficiary instructions cannot execute until margin debt cleared

### Pre-Death Planning (If Terminally Ill)

1. **Pay down margin while alive** — but selling appreciated securities triggers capital gains tax that step-up would have erased; weigh tradeoff
2. **Pay down margin from other sources** — cash, less-appreciated securities, short-term personal loan
3. **Transfer securities to non-margin account** or to a trust

### Post-Death Handling

1. **Obtain exact payoff** from brokerage immediately
2. **Pay from estate liquidity if available** — preserves stepped-up securities
3. If selling securities is necessary, **select holdings with smallest post-death appreciation** (minimize the narrow window between date of death and sale)
4. **Document everything** to defend against beneficiary disputes

### The Liquidity Strategy

For a family with substantial low-basis holdings and modest margin debt, paying off margin from other estate liquidity often deserves first consideration. That can preserve optionality around stepped-up assets and reduce the need to realize post-death appreciation just because the broker is forcing the timeline.

## Pledged-Asset Lines

Similar to margin: securities-backed loan from a private bank. Same liquidity issues at death. Coordinate with the lender immediately; usually has more flexibility than retail margin.

## Concentrated Stock and Executive Compensation

Executives, founders, and long-tenured employees often hold concentrated positions plus various equity awards.

### Stock Options (ISOs and NQSOs)

- **Vesting** usually accelerates at death (per plan)
- **Exercise period** typically shortens dramatically post-death (often 1 year)
- Failing to exercise in time **forfeits the option**
- **ISO tax treatment** may change post-death — consult plan and tax advisor

### Restricted Stock Units (RSUs)

- Unvested RSUs may **accelerate vesting at death**, OR may forfeit — varies by plan
- Vested but unsettled RSUs treated as IRD (income in respect of decedent), no step-up

### Employee Stock Purchase Plans (ESPPs)

- Generally liquidated at death with specific tax treatment depending on holding period

### Deferred Compensation (NQDC, 409A)

- Often pay out at death under specific schedules
- General **unsecured claims** against employer — at risk if employer financially distressed
- Subject to ordinary income tax (no step-up)

### SERPs (Supplemental Executive Retirement Plans)

- Similar — payout schedules and tax treatment depend on plan terms

### Pensions and Defined Benefit Plans

- Survivor benefit elections made at retirement typically cannot be changed
- Joint-and-survivor vs. single-life decision is **permanent** at retirement

### Blackout Periods and 10b5-1 Plans

- Executives may be unable to sell during blackout
- Estate inherits these restrictions
- **10b5-1 plans** generally continue post-death, allowing ongoing sales by estate without insider-trading concerns

### Concentration Risk

Holding a single stock at >50% of net worth is concentration risk. Pre-death management strategies:

- **Exchange funds** (qualified opportunity to diversify without immediate gain recognition)
- **Structured sales** (collar transactions limit downside while allowing some upside participation)
- **Charitable Remainder Trust** — sell inside CRT tax-free, diversify, take income
- **GRAT** — transfer concentrated stock with high growth expectation; estate-tax-efficient
- **Section 1202 QSBS** — use acquisition-date-specific modeling. Post-enactment stock can follow a phased 3 / 4 / 5-year regime with a higher cap, while older stock can remain on the older timeline and cap

## Executor Checklist for Brokerage Accounts

1. **Notify firm of death** immediately; obtain current statements valued at date of death
2. **Establish estate account** for non-beneficiary-designated assets
3. **Direct transfer** of beneficiary-designated assets directly to named beneficiaries
4. **Document date-of-death values** for basis purposes (high/low average for publicly traded; appraisal for private)
5. **Consider alternate valuation date** election (6 months after death) for large estates if values have fallen
6. **Address margin or pledged-asset debt** immediately; coordinate with broker on freeze and protection from force-liquidation
7. **Identify concentrated positions** and develop liquidation strategy
8. **Coordinate with tax preparer** on basis reporting and Form 8971 if Form 706 required

## Form 8971 and Basis Consistency Reporting

If Form 8971 applies, the executor should file it using the current rule: generally by 30 days after the earlier of the Form 706 due date (including extensions) or the actual filing date. It reports basis-consistency information to the IRS and affected beneficiaries, and penalties can apply for non-filing.

Form 8971 drives basis-consistency reporting for affected property and becomes important downstream documentation when beneficiaries later sell.

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Margin loan force-liquidation during probate** — creates an avoidable liquidity crisis and can force sales on a bad timetable
2. **No TOD on brokerage account** — unnecessary probate
3. **Stock options not exercised** within post-death window — forfeited
4. **Concentrated position held without protection** — single-stock crash devastates estate
5. **Pension joint-and-survivor not elected** — surviving spouse loses income
6. **Form 8971 not filed** — penalties + downstream basis confusion
7. **Alternate valuation election made without modeling** — sometimes reduces estate tax but increases future capital gains by more

## The Conversation to Have

For an executive with concentrated stock:

> "Your concentration in [Company] stock is significant. If something happens to you, your estate has a complex set of awards (RSUs, options, ESPP, deferred comp) plus the underlying shares. The exercise periods on options shorten dramatically at death, and forced selling could trigger taxes that a patient process would avoid. Let's identify each award, its vesting schedule, post-death rules, and the right action sequence for the executor. We'll also discuss diversification strategies during your life — exchange funds, CRT, structured sales — to reduce the concentration risk for your estate."
