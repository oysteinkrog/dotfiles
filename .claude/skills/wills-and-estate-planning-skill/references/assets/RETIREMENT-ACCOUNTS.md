# Retirement Accounts (IRA, 401(k), Roth, HSA, Pension)

Retirement accounts are governed by **contract** (beneficiary designation), not the will. They have unique tax rules that compound the importance of getting designations right.

## The 2026 Inheritance Framework (SECURE Act)

For accounts inherited from someone who died **after December 31, 2019**, many non-spouse designated beneficiaries are subject to a **year-10 outside deadline**, but annual-distribution requirements and exceptions still depend on beneficiary type and whether the decedent had already reached the required beginning date.

### Eligible Designated Beneficiaries (EDBs) — Stretch Available

These beneficiaries can use their life expectancy instead of the ordinary 10-year rule:
- **Surviving spouses**
- **Minor children of the account owner** — but only until they reach the age of majority under the applicable rules; current IRS guidance generally starts the 10-year clock at age 21, subject to edge-case analysis
- **Disabled or chronically ill individuals**
- **Beneficiaries not more than 10 years younger than the decedent** (including older beneficiaries)

### Annual RMDs During the 10 Years (For Beneficiaries Actually on the 10-Year Track)

Depends on whether the decedent had reached their **Required Beginning Date (RBD)** for RMDs:

- **Decedent died before RBD** → no annual RMDs during the 10 years; beneficiary can wait until year 10
- **Decedent died after RBD** → annual RMDs may be required during years 1-9, and the account still must be emptied by year 10

### Non-Designated Beneficiaries

- **Estate** as beneficiary → often a worse payout regime; 5-year or remaining-life-expectancy rules may apply depending on the facts
- **Charity** as beneficiary → income tax exempt; cleanest charitable bequest
- **Non-see-through trust** → often loses designated-beneficiary treatment and can fall into a less favorable payout regime

### Spousal Options (Often the Most Flexible Treatment)

- **Roll over to own IRA** — most flexibility; reset RMDs to spouse's age
- **Maintain as inherited IRA** — flexibility for early distributions before 59½ without penalty
- **Disclaim** to allow contingent beneficiary to take

## Income Tax Implications

### Traditional IRA / 401(k)

- **Pre-tax dollars** — beneficiary pays ordinary income tax on every distribution
- 10-year rule **compresses** what could have been a 30-year stretch into 10 years of taxable distributions
- For a $1M IRA inherited by adult child: ~$100K/year of taxable distributions, often pushing them into top brackets
- Effective tax rate often **higher than the decedent's rate**

### Roth IRA / Roth 401(k)

- **Already-taxed dollars** — distributions are tax-free if account held > 5 years
- Many non-spouse beneficiaries are subject to a year-10 outside deadline, but the pattern depends on beneficiary type and account facts
- **Strategy:** preserve tax-free compounding where possible, while checking whether annual distributions are required

### Pension (Defined Benefit)

- Survivor benefit elections made at retirement typically cannot be changed
- Difference between single-life annuity and joint-and-survivor election can swing surviving spouse's income by 30%+ for life
- Review elections **at retirement**, not just at death — too late then

### HSA (Health Savings Account)

- **Spouse beneficiary** — continues as spouse's own HSA, retains all tax advantages
- **Non-spouse beneficiary** — HSA terminates; full balance becomes **taxable income** to beneficiary in year of death; significant tax hit
- **Estate beneficiary** — included in decedent's final 1040 as taxable income
- **Strategy:** spend down HSA during life if non-spouse is heir

## Strategic Planning Levers

### 1. Roth Conversions During Life

Convert traditional IRA → Roth during low-income years (early retirement, sabbatical, before Social Security). Pay income tax now at lower rate; heir inherits tax-free.

Especially valuable when:
- Decedent in lower bracket than heir will be
- Estate above federal exemption (Roth assets removed from estate but tax was paid from "other money")
- Beneficiary expected to be in top bracket

### 2. Charitable Bequest of Traditional IRA

Charities pay no income tax. Leave the IRA to charity; leave step-up-eligible assets (taxable brokerage, real estate) to children.

For families with charitable intent, this is often one of the cleanest and most tax-efficient bequest structures.

### 3. Qualified Charitable Distribution (QCD)

After age 70½, transfer up to the current IRS-indexed annual limit directly from IRA to qualified charity (**$111K in 2026; verify later-year amounts**). Counts toward RMD; excluded from income.

### 4. Charitable Remainder Trust Funded with IRA

CRT receives IRA at death. CRT pays income to family beneficiaries for term (up to 20 years), then remainder to charity. "Stretches" the distribution period beyond 10 years and produces income for family.

### 5. See-Through Trust as Beneficiary

For protection of vulnerable beneficiaries (minor, disabled, addicted, creditor risk), name a properly drafted see-through trust:

- **Conduit trust** — passes RMDs straight through to beneficiary (simple, beneficiary receives directly, less protection)
- **Accumulation trust** — holds distributions in trust (protection, but trust pays high tax rates on retained income — quickly into 37% bracket)

Drafting requires current SECURE Act expertise. Pre-2024 trust drafts often deserve a fresh review under the final regulations and current IRS guidance.

### 6. EDB Status for Disabled Heir

If a disabled heir qualifies as EDB, they can stretch over their life expectancy — a major advantage. Coordinate with SNT design.

## Beneficiary Designation Checklist

For every retirement account:

- [ ] Primary beneficiary named
- [ ] Contingent beneficiary named
- [ ] Tertiary beneficiary considered
- [ ] Designation reflects current relationship (post-divorce, post-remarriage, post-death of named person)
- [ ] Trust beneficiary, if used, drafted as see-through under post-2024 regs
- [ ] Charity considered for traditional accounts if charitable intent exists
- [ ] HSA designation considered (special tax treatment)
- [ ] 401(k) coordinator confirmed has current form on file (paper forms get lost)
- [ ] For any workplace plan that is actually subject to ERISA survivor-benefit rules, spouse-default / waiver requirements checked and documented

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Ex-spouse still named on a workplace plan** — on ERISA-governed plans especially, do not assume divorce alone fixes the beneficiary form; update it directly
2. **Estate as beneficiary** — often a worse payout regime and income-tax acceleration
3. **Minor child as direct beneficiary** — court-supervised property-management risk, and direct control can arrive at the applicable age of majority or custodial-release age
4. **Trust drafted pre-2024** — may not satisfy post-SECURE Act see-through rules
5. **Non-spouse beneficiary on HSA** — surprise tax on full balance
6. **Pension joint-and-survivor not elected** — surviving spouse loses pension income
7. **No QCD coordination during life** — wasted tax efficiency
8. **No Roth conversion during low-income years** — heir inherits big tax bomb

## Inherited IRA Withdrawal Strategy (Heir Side)

For heirs subject to 10-year rule:

- **Don't take a lump sum** — spikes income into top brackets
- **Smooth withdrawals** over 10 years to fill up lower brackets year by year
- **Front-load in low-income years** (sabbatical, maternity leave, early retirement before Social Security)
- **Watch IRMAA** — Medicare premium surcharges based on income from 2 years prior
- **Watch state tax** — high-tax-state residents may want to back-load before moving low

## Coordination With Trust Plan

A funded revocable trust is a great planning hub but is **usually not the default IRA beneficiary** unless drafted and coordinated specifically for retirement-account rules. Otherwise the trust may be treated as a non-designated beneficiary and lose favorable payout treatment.

**Standard structure:**
- Spouse as primary beneficiary (often the most flexible default if that fits the family and tax plan)
- Children as contingent (often subject to a year-10 outside deadline, subject to beneficiary-status exceptions)
- See-through trust for any minor or vulnerable contingent beneficiary
- Charity as tertiary if charitable intent

## The Conversation to Have

> "Your retirement accounts have specific rules that don't match the rest of your estate. They pass by beneficiary designation — your will doesn't control them. And the tax rules for inherited IRAs are harsh: many non-spouse heirs face a year-10 outside deadline, often with ordinary income tax along the way and sometimes annual-distribution overlays. Let's plan: do we leave the IRA to charity (often highly tax-efficient if you have charitable intent), to your spouse (often the most flexible outcome), or to children (taxable but biggest dollars)? The right answer depends on what you want."
