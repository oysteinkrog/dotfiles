# Firearms and NFA Items

Firearms require specialized estate planning. A mishandled transfer can create serious federal and state criminal exposure.

## Standard Firearms

### Federal Restrictions on Transfer

Firearms cannot be transferred to **prohibited persons**:

- Convicted felons
- Persons subject to domestic violence protective orders
- Persons adjudicated mentally defective
- Persons with disqualifying misdemeanor convictions (DV)
- Illegal drug users (including marijuana even in state-legal jurisdictions per federal law)
- Fugitives from justice
- Persons dishonorably discharged from military
- Persons who renounced U.S. citizenship
- Illegal aliens / unauthorized immigrants

**Executor liability:** An executor who distributes a firearm without verifying eligibility and transfer mechanics can create serious personal and estate-level risk. Treat every transfer as a verify-first exercise.

### State-by-State Variation

- Some states require background checks **even for inheritance transfers**
- Some require executor be licensed
- Some require permits, owner ID cards, registrations, or extra paperwork for certain firearms
- California, New York, Massachusetts, New Jersey have particularly restrictive rules

Know the rules in **both** the decedent's state AND the heir's state, and verify current law rather than relying on a stale checklist.

### Standard Firearm Inheritance Process

1. **Inventory** the firearms (make, model, serial number, location, condition)
2. **Verify heir eligibility** in their state of residence
3. **Comply with state-specific transfer requirements** (background check, paperwork)
4. **Document the transfer** for the executor's records
5. **Insure during administration** — secure storage, theft prevention

## NFA Items (National Firearms Act)

NFA items include:

- **Suppressors** (silencers)
- **Short-barreled rifles (SBR)**
- **Short-barreled shotguns (SBS)**
- **Machine guns**
- **Destructive devices** (explosives, large-bore weapons)
- **Any other weapons** (AOW) — disguised firearms

These are heavily regulated under federal law and require **ATF approval** for transfer.

### Inheritance of NFA Items

- **Form 5** application required for inheritance transfer (not Form 4 used for purchase)
- Form 5 transfers are **tax-free** (no $200 stamp tax for purchase)
- ATF processing time varies; check current ATF timelines before promising a schedule
- Transfer can only occur where the item may be lawfully possessed under federal, state, and local law
- For an individual heir, the Form 5 package generally includes fingerprints and ATF approval before distribution
- Executor generally maintains custody during probate while arranging the lawful transfer

### State Restrictions

Some states ban specific NFA items entirely:
- **Suppressors** — illegal in CA, DE, HI, IL, MA, NJ, NY, RI, DC
- **SBRs/SBSs** — restricted in some states
- **Machine guns** — heavily restricted; pre-1986 transferable only

A named heir may be **legally unable to possess** the NFA item in their state. The estate may need to:
- Sell the item to a lawful transferee using the proper transfer path
- Redirect it to a different lawful heir or other permitted recipient
- Transfer through a qualified dealer or other approved channel where required
- Abandon or surrender it if no lawful transfer route exists

### Gun Trusts

One common tool for NFA collections is an **NFA Gun Trust** (also called a Title II trust).

**Benefits:**
- Trust (not individual) owns the firearms
- Multiple **co-trustees** can legally possess and use trust items
- Can make shared possession and succession easier in some families
- **41F compliance** — Form 5320.23 required for trust transfers; all "responsible persons" (co-trustees) must submit fingerprints and photos
- Continued use across generations

**Drafting requirements:**
- Properly drafted by attorney experienced with NFA trusts (not generic revocable trust)
- Compliance with 41F rule (effective June 2016 — older trusts should be reviewed)
- State-specific provisions
- Successor trustee provisions

**Limitations:**
- Cannot make NFA items legal in states where they're illegal
- Trust co-trustees subject to federal eligibility requirements
- Sale or transfer outside the trust still requires ATF approval
- Not automatically better than individual ownership plus a Form 5 transfer at death; compare both paths before recommending one

### Pre-1986 Machine Guns

Special category. Manufactured before May 1986. Only these can still be transferred to civilians under Hughes Amendment. Values $20K-$200K+ depending on model. Estate planning critical because:
- Transfer requires ATF approval
- Pricing volatility
- Specialized appraisal required
- Prohibited recipients (felons etc.) cannot receive

## Other Specialized Categories

### Military and Commemorative Items

- Service weapons retained legally
- Foreign weapons brought back from service decades ago (often legally questionable)
- Flintlocks and antiques (pre-1899) — generally exempt from federal law
- War trophies — documentation required

### Antique Firearms

Generally exempt from federal regulation if manufactured before 1899 OR using ammunition no longer commercially available. State law may still apply.

## Storage During Administration

The administration period (6-18+ months) requires secure storage:

- Protect against theft (firearms attractive to burglars)
- Protect against access by unauthorized persons (especially minors or vulnerable family members in the home)
- Insurance — verify firearms are covered or schedule separately
- Climate-controlled storage for valuable collectibles

## Coordination With Other Planning

### Voluntary Surrender Directive

For gun owners with depression, cognitive decline, recent significant loss, or substance issues: a voluntary firearm surrender directive lets the user pre-authorize a friend, family member, or law enforcement to **temporarily** take possession during a crisis.

Different from extreme risk protection orders ("red flag" laws):
- ERPO is involuntary, court-issued
- Voluntary surrender is consensual, pre-authorized

For at-risk gun owners, this is potentially life-saving. Coordinate with mental-health professional and family.

### Letter to Executor

The executor of a gun-owner's estate needs:
- Inventory of all firearms with serial numbers and locations
- Identification of any NFA items and their registration documents
- Identification of the gun trust (if any) with location of trust documents
- Eligibility verification process for each named heir
- Storage plan during administration
- Sale process for items heirs cannot or won't accept
- Federal/state legal references

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Executor transfers firearm to prohibited heir** — severe civil/criminal exposure
2. **NFA item passed without ATF approval** — unlawful transfer, item subject to seizure
3. **Heir in state where NFA item is illegal** — estate needs a different lawful transfer or disposition plan
4. **Pre-41F gun trust never updated** — co-trustees may have compliance issues
5. **No inventory** — firearms misplaced, possibly stolen during administration
6. **No insurance during administration** — theft uncovered
7. **War trophy firearms** with questionable provenance — discovered during inventory
8. **Antique handed down without documentation** — subsequent transfer questioned

## The Conversation to Have

For a serious gun owner:

> "Your firearms collection — especially the NFA items — needs special planning. We need to: (1) inventory everything with serial numbers, (2) check whether each named heir can legally receive each item where they live, (3) decide whether individual ownership, an NFA trust, or a sale/disposition plan fits the collection you actually have, (4) coordinate with your executor on storage and transfer process, and (5) consider a voluntary surrender directive if mental-health risk is part of the picture. Firearm-transfer rules are technical enough that we should assume every handoff needs verification before anything changes possession."
