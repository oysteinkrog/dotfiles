# Life Insurance

Life insurance proceeds are generally **income-tax-free** to the beneficiary. But if the insured owned the policy at death, the death benefit is **included in the taxable estate** — a major issue for large estates.

## When Life Insurance Is the Right Tool

1. **Income replacement** for young families (term insurance often fits, but model the actual spending gap rather than relying on a folklore multiple)
2. **Mortgage payoff** for surviving family
3. **Estate-tax liquidity** for large estates with illiquid assets (ILIT)
4. **Equalization** when one heir gets the business or the house
5. **Buy-sell funding** for closely held businesses
6. **State estate tax funding** (estates above state but below federal exemption)
7. **Final expenses** for end-of-life and funeral costs

## Types

| Type | Characteristics | Best For |
|------|----------------|----------|
| **Term** | Death benefit only; no cash value; expires at term end (10/20/30 yr) | Income replacement during working years; cheapest |
| **Whole life** | Permanent coverage; cash value grows; level premium | Estate-tax liquidity; long-term need |
| **Universal life** | Permanent; flexible premium; variable cash value | Flexibility |
| **Variable universal** | Permanent; investment-linked cash value | High-tolerance investors |
| **Indexed universal** | Permanent; cash value linked to index returns with floors | Conservative growth |
| **Hybrid life/LTC** | Combines life insurance with long-term care benefit | Long-term care concern + legacy |

## Beneficiary Designations

One of the most error-prone aspects of life-insurance planning.

- **Spouse primary** is most common
- **Trust as beneficiary** for protection of vulnerable heirs; the drafting issues are different from retirement-account beneficiary rules
- **Children as contingent** with appropriate structure for minors
- **Estate as beneficiary** — generally **bad**: forces probate, exposes to creditors

### After Divorce

State revocation statutes typically apply to life insurance — but verify. Update the form regardless. If divorce decree requires maintaining specific coverage for children/alimony, do not remove those beneficiaries.

### For Minor Children

Direct-to-minor designation commonly pushes the proceeds into court-supervised property management or a custodial handoff at the release age. For meaningful proceeds, usually name a trust or other deliberate structure instead of the child directly.

## ILIT — Irrevocable Life Insurance Trust

For estates with federal or state estate-tax exposure, the **ILIT** removes the death benefit from the taxable estate.

### How It Works

1. ILIT is the **owner** of the policy (not the insured)
2. ILIT is the **beneficiary** of the policy
3. Insured makes annual **gifts to the ILIT** to fund premium payments
4. Gifts qualify for the **annual exclusion** if the trust includes "Crummey" withdrawal powers for beneficiaries
5. At death, **death benefit pays to the trust outside the insured's estate** — no estate tax
6. Trustee can use proceeds to:
   - Buy illiquid assets from the estate (providing estate liquidity)
   - Lend to the estate to pay estate tax
   - Distribute to beneficiaries per trust terms

### Critical Rules

- **Three-year lookback:** transferring an *existing* policy to an ILIT triggers a 3-year period during which the policy is included in the estate if insured dies. **Buy a new policy inside the ILIT** to avoid.
- **Crummey notices** — annual notification to beneficiaries of withdrawal right; required for annual-exclusion treatment
- **Separate bank account** for the ILIT
- **Arms-length transactions** — no commingling
- **Irrevocable** — once established, cannot reach back into it

### When ILIT Is Appropriate

- Federal estate tax exposure
- State estate tax exposure with significant insurance
- Closely held business or illiquid assets needing liquidity for tax
- Blended families needing immediate liquidity for first-marriage children
- Large estate with charitable goals (insurance to family, residue to charity)

### When ILIT Is Not Appropriate

- Estate well below federal and state exemptions (overcomplicated; loss of flexibility)
- Insured needs access to cash value during life
- Premium funding becomes a hardship

## Equalization Use Case

A family business worth $5M is going to son Alex. Two other children get nothing of the business. To equalize:

- ILIT buys $5M policy on parent's life
- Premium funded by annual gifts (within annual exclusion)
- At parent's death, $5M tax-free to ILIT, distributed to non-business children
- Net effect: each child gets roughly equal value

## Buy-Sell Funding

In a closely held business:
- Each owner takes out insurance on the other(s) (cross-purchase) OR
- Business takes out insurance on each owner (redemption)
- At an owner's death, insurance proceeds fund the buyout per the buy-sell agreement
- Surviving owners get the deceased's share; deceased's family gets cash

## Group Life Insurance Through Employer

Common gap: employer-provided life insurance is significant ($500K+ for some executives) but **defaults to "estate" or to a stale beneficiary** because the employee never updated.

- Update employer-provided policy beneficiary
- Coordinate with overall plan
- At job change, group coverage typically terminates — replace with portable coverage if needed

## Common Failure Modes

1. **Estate as beneficiary** — probate exposure, creditor exposure, and loss of minor/trust protection
2. **Ex-spouse on policy after divorce** — often state-revoked but verify
3. **Minor as beneficiary** — court-appointed guardian
4. **Trust drafted without proper Crummey provisions** — ILIT premium gifts don't qualify for annual exclusion
5. **Existing policy transferred to ILIT** within 3 years of death — included in estate
6. **ILIT created but never actually implemented** — policy never transferred or purchased into the trust, premium funding never set up, or ongoing administration ignored
7. **Group life lapsed at job change** without replacement
8. **Coverage amount mismatched to need** — insufficient to pay off mortgage or replace income
9. **No annual ILIT administration** — Crummey notices not sent; tax issues

## The Conversation to Have

For young parents:

> "If something happens to either of you, the survivor needs enough liquidity to keep the household running, cover childcare, and avoid a forced financial reset in the middle of grief. We'll model a realistic coverage range from actual spending, debts, and childcare/education needs, then coordinate the beneficiary structure through the plan instead of guessing from a canned multiplier."

For estates with tax exposure:

> "Your estate is large enough that there will be a meaningful estate tax bill — and a lot of your wealth is in [the business / real estate / other illiquid assets]. We need life insurance owned outside your estate (in an ILIT) to fund that tax bill. Without it, your kids may have to fire-sale assets to pay the IRS within 9 months of your death. Let's talk about how much insurance and how to set up the ILIT."
