# Vacation Homes — Disproportionate Conflict Source

Vacation homes combine high emotional value, ongoing carrying costs, and the need for coordination among multiple owners. They are disproportionately common sources of family conflict.

## Five Options at Death

### 1. Sell

Cleanest. Proceeds split per the will. Best for families not committed to keeping it.

### 2. One Heir Buys Out Others

- Independent appraisal
- Buyout agreement
- Often financed by family promissory note (5-10 year installment)
- Right of first refusal among siblings

### 3. Family LLC With Operating Agreement

Membership interests held by heirs. Operating agreement governs:
- **Use weeks** — allocation method (rotating draft, seniority, paid usage)
- **Expenses** — utilities, maintenance, taxes, insurance
- **Capital contributions** for major repairs
- **Buyout formula** — agreed appraiser, payment terms
- **Right of first refusal** among members
- **Exit triggers** — what happens when a member wants out
- **Forced sale** — supermajority vote or specific trigger

### 4. Qualified Personal Residence Trust (QPRT)

For pre-death gifting at discounted gift value. Owner retains use during term; remainder to children. Mortality risk: if grantor dies during term, full value returns to estate. See [QPRT in advanced planning].

### 5. Separate the Property From the Liquidity

Leave the vacation home to one child (often the most emotionally attached) along with a cash reserve sufficient to cover 10-20 years of carrying costs. Equalize other children with cash or other assets.

## Avoiding the Partition Action

If joint owners cannot agree, any co-owner can file a **partition action** — a court-driven process that often ends in sale and division of proceeds. In practice it can be expensive, slow, and relationship-destroying, and forced-sale dynamics can hurt value.

**Prevention:**

- Don't force joint ownership on heirs who don't want it
- Include right of first refusal
- Include buy-sell formula with agreed appraisal method
- Consider testamentary trust for the vacation home
- Give executor explicit power to sell real estate and distribute cash

## The "Do You Actually Want It?" Conversation

Before designing structure, ask each heir individually:

> "If we keep the cabin, you'd own a third of it with your siblings. That means agreeing on use, paying your share of maintenance, and being stuck with it long-term. If you'd rather have cash, that's an honest answer. What do you actually want?"

A frequent revelation: **none of the kids actually want the vacation home**. Selling and dividing cash is often the right answer. Forcing co-ownership can create years of conflict.

## Carrying Cost Planning

Vacation homes cost real money even when nobody uses them:
- Property tax
- Insurance (often higher for vacation homes — flood, hurricane, vacant property)
- Utilities
- Maintenance and repairs
- HOA fees
- Periodic major capex (roof, septic, dock)

For 3 heirs to share a $1M lakehouse: $30-50K/year of carrying costs split three ways. If one heir cannot afford their share, conflict erupts.

**Endowment fund:** Trust with funded reserve specifically for vacation home expenses. Funds 10-20 years of maintenance. When depleted, owners contribute or sell.

## State Tax Considerations

Vacation home in a state with estate tax exposes that property to that state's estate tax — even for non-resident decedent. See [STATE-ESTATE-TAX.md](../foundations/STATE-ESTATE-TAX.md).

Use revocable trust or LLC ownership where possible — some authorities treat LLC interests as intangibles following owner's domicile.

## Multi-Generational Vacation Properties

For families intending generational ownership:
- LLC or family limited partnership structure
- Operating agreement that survives generations
- Reserve fund replenishment plan
- Use-allocation mechanism that scales with family size
- Buyout provisions for departing branches
- Sale triggers (supermajority, deadlock, financial inability)
- Trust protector to handle changed circumstances

## Cabin Endowment Calculation

To fund a vacation home properly through estate:

```
Annual carrying cost              $30,000
Major capex (roof, etc.) /year    $5,000
Family meeting / governance       $2,000
Total annual                     $37,000

20-year endowment (4% draw)     $925,000
30-year endowment              $1,400,000
```

For high-value vacation property, the endowment may dwarf the property value. This is why many families end up selling — they didn't fund the carrying costs.

## Common Failure Modes

1. **"To my children equally"** with no governance — high risk of deadlock and conflict
2. **Forcing co-ownership when no child wants it** — partition risk becomes very high
3. **Inheriting without funding carrying costs** — children sell within 3 years anyway
4. **Out-of-state vacation home in own name** — ancillary probate
5. **No buyout mechanism** — sibling who wants out has no path
6. **Operating agreement signed but never followed** — informal practice diverges; conflict eventually
7. **No trust protector** — generational structure has no flexibility

## The Conversation to Have

> "The lakehouse is one of the harder pieces of this plan. Your three kids could either share it, have one buy out the others, or sell and split. Families often imagine shared ownership will feel sentimental and fair, then discover the carrying costs and governance problems too late. Let's talk to each kid separately about what they actually want, then design accordingly."
