# Primary Residence

The family home is usually the largest single asset and the one with the most emotional weight.

## Titling Options

| Form | Probate at Death | Survivor Step-Up | Notes |
|------|-----------------|------------------|-------|
| **Sole** | Yes | N/A | Simple but full probate |
| **Joint tenancy w/ right of survivorship** | No (passes to survivor) | Often about one-half in common-law married cases; contribution rules can change the result in other joint-tenancy situations | Survivor takes all; loss of control during life |
| **Tenancy by the entirety** | No | Often similar to married-joint-tenancy treatment for basis purposes, but confirm local/federal treatment in the actual fact pattern | Married couples only (in some states); creditor protection from one spouse's creditors |
| **Community property** (AZ, CA, ID, LA, NV, NM, TX, WA, WI) | Depends on title and state procedure | Generally full basis adjustment on the entire community asset at first death if the federal requirements are satisfied | Each spouse owns 1/2 economically, but income-tax basis treatment is different from ordinary joint tenancy |
| **Community property w/ right of survivorship** | No | Same community-property basis benefit if the classification is actually valid; main added feature is survivorship / probate avoidance | Useful for married couples in CP states when the classification is clean and the local form is appropriate |
| **TOD deed** | No | Same as sole at death | Available in growing list of states (~30); revocable; names beneficiary |
| **Revocable trust** | No | Yes (if grantor trust at death) | Best probate avoidance; multi-state coverage |

## Mortgage at Death

The mortgage does not disappear at death. To preserve options, the family usually needs to keep payments current or engage the servicer quickly while title, assumption, refinance, sale, or surrender decisions are being made.

**Federal / servicing posture:** death of the borrower does not automatically mean the family must refinance immediately. Heirs and other successors in interest can often continue paying while they document title and work with the servicer. Depending on the loan and servicer process, they may be able to:

1. **Continue payments** and keep the property
2. **Seek formal assumption or borrower status** on the existing loan where the servicer and loan documents allow
3. **Refinance** in heir's name
4. **Sell** and pay off mortgage from proceeds
5. **Allow foreclosure** if underwater or strategically not worth saving

**Successor in interest** rules under CFPB give surviving spouses and confirmed heirs important servicing protections. They can request loan information, discuss payment options, and pursue loss-mitigation paths without treating the situation as an ordinary new-origination problem.

## Coordinating Real Estate With the Plan

### For Married Couples in a Community-Property State

Take advantage of the **community property with right of survivorship** election where available and where the home's classification as community property is actually solid. It can provide:
- Probate avoidance at first death
- A major basis benefit at first death
- Smooth transition to surviving spouse

### For Married Couples in a Common-Law State

Common patterns:
- **Tenancy by the entirety** — probate avoidance + creditor protection from one spouse's creditors
- **Joint tenancy with right of survivorship** — probate avoidance, no creditor protection
- **Revocable trust** — probate avoidance + flexibility + multi-state coverage

For a **second home in another state**: a revocable trust is often a strong first option for probate avoidance, but compare it against LLC ownership, TOD-deed availability, liability goals, and local tax/homestead quirks before treating it as automatic.

### For Singles

- **Revocable trust** if probate avoidance valued
- **TOD deed** in states that allow — cheap and effective
- **Sole title with will** — probate but simple

### For Unmarried Partners

- **Joint tenancy with right of survivorship** — partner takes automatically
- But consider gift-tax issue if one partner contributed all the funding
- And consider creditor exposure
- Trust ownership often cleaner

## Heirs' Tax Treatment

**Stepped-up basis at death.** The basis is the FMV on date of death. If heirs sell soon after death, capital gains tax is minimal.

**Section 121 exclusion** — if heir lives in inherited house for 2 of 5 years before selling, qualifies for $250K (single) / $500K (married) exclusion on top of stepped-up basis.

**Rental conversion** — heir can rent the inherited house. Depreciation begins from stepped-up basis. Significant tax benefit if held as rental.

## Pre-Death Planning Levers

### If Significant Appreciation Expected

- **Hold for step-up** rather than selling during life
- Married couple in CP state: confirm the home's actual community-property classification and whether CPWROS or another local form best matches the probate and basis goals
- Consider QPRT (Qualified Personal Residence Trust) if estate-tax exposure — but factor mortality and step-up loss

### If Estate Tax Exposure

- QPRT removes future appreciation from estate
- ILIT can fund tax liability without forcing sale

### If Multi-State Real Estate

- Each out-of-state property in trust or LLC to avoid ancillary probate

### If Heir Will Live in Home

- Confirm titling allows transition (revocable trust, TOD deed)
- Provide for ongoing carrying costs in the trust until heir takes title

## Common Failure Modes

1. **House titled JTROS with one child** — disinherits other children
2. **Out-of-state vacation home in own name** — triggers ancillary probate in second state
3. **Mortgage at death with no liquidity** — heirs default
4. **No community-property titling / classification review in a community-property state** — family misses basis or probate advantages because the form of ownership was never cleaned up
5. **TOD deed signed but not recorded** — invalid; doesn't transfer
6. **Revocable trust created but house never re-titled** — pour-over will triggers probate
