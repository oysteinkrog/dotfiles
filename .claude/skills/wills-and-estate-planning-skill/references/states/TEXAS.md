# Texas Estate Planning

## Headline Posture

- **No state income tax, no state estate tax, no state gift tax, no state inheritance tax**
- **Community property** state
- **Strong homestead protection** (unlimited value; acreage limits)
- **Common domicile destination**
- **Friendly probate** ("independent administration" option)
- **Strong creditor protection**, but analyze community-property creditor exposure separately rather than assuming Florida-style tenancy-by-the-entirety results

## Texas Estate Tax

None. Federal only.

## Texas as Community Property State

Property acquired during marriage is community (half each spouse's), unless:
- Inherited
- Gifted to one spouse
- Premarital assets kept separate
- Specific transmutation

### Implications
- **Double step-up in basis** at first death
- **Divorce:** community property is subject to a "just and right" division, not an automatic 50/50 split in every case
- **Estate planning:** each spouse can devise only their half of community property
- **Creditor:** community property reachable for either spouse's debts (generally)

### Survivorship Community Property (Unique to TX)
Spouses can designate community property with "right of survivorship" — passes to surviving spouse at first death outside probate (not standard community).

## Texas Homestead

### Protection Against Creditors
- **Unlimited value** (like FL)
- **Acreage:**
  - Urban homestead: up to 10 acres (can be contiguous or non-contiguous)
  - Rural homestead (family): up to 200 acres
  - Rural homestead (single adult): up to 100 acres
- **Exceptions:** purchase-money mortgage, taxes, home improvement, home equity loan (with spousal consent)

### Forced Heirship
Texas has homestead rules but less restrictive than FL:
- Surviving spouse has homestead right (lifetime right of use)
- Minor children have rights

Cannot fully devise around spouse for homestead during spouse's life.

## Probate

Texas probate is unusually efficient:

### Independent Administration
- Default in Texas if will specifies (and most do)
- Executor acts without court supervision
- Fast and inexpensive
- Similar to trust administration

### Dependent Administration
- Court-supervised (rare, used if conflict)
- More expensive, slower

### Muniment of Title
- Simple procedure for estates with only real estate and no debts
- Will admitted to probate; no executor appointed
- Real estate passes per will

### Small Estate Affidavit
- For estates under $75K in non-homestead probate assets

## Intestacy

Texas Estates Code §§ 201 et seq. (community property jurisdiction adds complexity):

### Community Property
- **Spouse + all kids from marriage:** all community to spouse
- **Spouse + some kids from prior relationship:** 1/2 community to spouse, other 1/2 to kids

### Separate Property
- **Spouse + no descendants:** all real to spouse; 2/3 of personal to spouse, 1/3 to parents/siblings
- **Spouse + descendants:** 1/3 of personal to spouse for life; 2/3 to descendants; real estate 1/3 life estate to spouse

Intestacy in Texas is complicated — wills strongly recommended.

## Power of Attorney

Texas statutory durable POA:
- Texas Estates Code §§ 751 et seq.
- Statutory form provided
- Both springing and immediate permitted
- Ban on unreasonable third-party rejection (2017 reform)
- Good-faith reliance protection

## Medical Power of Attorney and Directive to Physicians

Separate documents:
- **Medical power of attorney** (Texas Health and Safety Code § 166)
- **Directive to Physicians / Living Will** (statutory form)
- **Out-of-Hospital DNR** (similar to POLST)

## Medicaid

Texas specifics:
- **Income cap state** — Miller Trust needed if over cap
- **5-year lookback**
- **MERP** in effect
- **Strict community spouse protections** (at federal minimums)
- TX is a relatively conservative Medicaid program

## Asset Protection

Texas has strong creditor protection:
- Unlimited homestead
- Community property structure
- Retirement plans (unlimited)
- Insurance cash value (protected)
- Annuities (protected under certain structures)
- Current wages for personal services generally are not subject to garnishment except for child support and spousal maintenance

Texas does NOT have a DAPT statute (no self-settled asset-protection trust).

## Transfer on Death Deed

Texas recognizes TOD deed for real estate (enacted 2015):
- Transfer on Death Deed
- Revocable during life
- Avoids probate for real estate
- Cheap alternative to trust for simple real-estate cases

## Unique Texas Features

### Muniment of Title (above) — elegant probate alternative

### Partition Agreement (spouses)
- Spouses can agree to partition community property into separate
- Pre- or post-marriage
- Affects both divorce and estate planning

### Cattle / Ranch Planning
- Texas farm and ranch planning significant
- Agricultural valuation under Section 2032A
- Family limited partnerships for ranchland
- Mineral rights planning

### Energy / Mineral Rights
- Texas has significant mineral wealth
- Surface estate vs. mineral estate separate
- Royalty planning
- Oil and gas succession

### Firearm Planning
- Texas large firearm population
- NFA trusts for NFA-regulated items
- State-law considerations

### Horses, Livestock
- Texas equine operations common in HNW families
- Breeding operations; business planning

## Domicile Establishment from Other States

Similar to FL but easier in some ways (no state income tax either state):
- Texas driver's license
- Voter registration
- Vehicle registration
- Real estate ownership
- Declaration of domicile (optional formal document)

Less audit pressure than NY/CA because Texas doesn't have income tax to defend.

## Trust Situs Considerations

- Texas has 300-year rule against perpetuities (modern, good for dynasty planning)
- Texas no trust income tax
- Texas has decanting statute (with restrictions)
- Texas does NOT have DAPT
- Texas does NOT have strongest directed-trust provisions

For long-duration trusts, Texas residents often use SD or NV situs for stronger features.

## Pre- / Post-Marital Agreements

Texas strongly honors pre/postnuptial agreements:
- Must be in writing and signed voluntarily
- Disclosure and knowledge facts still matter; enforcement fights often turn on whether the challenger lacked fair disclosure, waived disclosure, or lacked adequate knowledge
- Can alter community-property character

Critical for business-owning spouses, second marriages, inherited wealth.

## Cost of Planning

- Simple will: $500-$2,000
- Revocable trust: $2,000-$5,000
- Complex HNW planning: $10K-$75K+
- Texas attorneys generally reasonable rates outside major cities

## Common Failure Modes in Texas

1. **No will with community property + separate property** — intestacy tangles
2. **Spouse + kids from prior relationship** — intestate result often surprises
3. **Homestead claimed incorrectly** — lose protection
4. **Trying to devise around spouse for homestead** — invalid for life
5. **Independent administration not specified in will** — forced into dependent admin
6. **Miller Trust missed** — Medicaid denied
7. **Mineral rights not addressed** in estate plan — complex post-death tangles
8. **Moving to TX without closing former-state ties** — former state (esp. CA, NY) still claims
9. **No partition agreement for premarital separate property** — commingled during marriage
10. **Texas-specific firearm/NFA trust not used** — inheritance issues with NFA items
