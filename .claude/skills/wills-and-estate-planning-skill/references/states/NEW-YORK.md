# New York Estate Planning

## Headline Posture

- **State estate tax:** **$7,350,000** basic exclusion amount for dates of death in **2026**
- **NY cliff remains the central planning feature:** if the estate exceeds **105%** of the exclusion amount, the benefit of the exclusion is effectively lost
- **No state gift tax** (but 3-year lookback on gifts)
- **High income tax on trusts** — aggressive rules
- **Slow/expensive probate** in NYC especially
- **Medicaid:** unusually favorable spousal-impoverishment terrain, plus real spousal-refusal leverage in the right fact patterns

Verified from the New York Department of Taxation and Finance estate-tax page on **2026-04-16**:
`https://www.tax.ny.gov/pit/estate/etidx.htm`

## Estate Tax — The "Cliff"

NY state estate tax has the infamous "cliff":

- Basic exclusion amount: **$7,350,000** for dates of death January 1, 2026 through December 31, 2026
- 105% cliff line: **$7,717,500**
- Once the estate moves beyond that band, the New York exclusion is effectively lost and tax is computed without the shelter the family expected

### Cliff Example
- Estate: $7.2M → below the 2026 exclusion, generally no New York estate tax
- Estate: $7.6M → in the danger zone; model carefully
- Estate: $7.8M → beyond 105% of the 2026 exclusion; the cliff can make the tax dramatically worse than a family expects

**"Santa Claus Clause"** — bequest to charity at the cliff margin can save large amounts.

### Planning Strategies
- Use federal exemption via lifetime gifts (NY no gift tax); then estate under NY threshold
- Credit-shelter / bypass trust at first death
- Charitable bequest to bring estate under threshold
- Annual gifting program (reduces taxable estate over time)
- 3-year lookback: gifts within 3 years of death added back to NY estate

## State Income Tax on Trusts

NY taxes trust income aggressively:

- **Resident-trust status** turns primarily on New York domicile rules for the decedent or grantor when the estate or trust became resident/irrevocable; the trustee's residence alone does not determine resident-trust status
- **Trustee residence still matters** because a resident trust may escape New York income tax only if the statutory exempt-resident-trust conditions are satisfied
- **New York source income still matters** even when situs or administration has been moved elsewhere

### Strategies
- Move trust situs to SD, NV, DE only as part of a full tax and administration analysis
- Use out-of-state trustees where appropriate
- Exempt Resident Trust (ERT): the familiar safe harbor requires all trustees outside New York, corpus located outside New York, and no New York-source income; do not summarize it as a simple "non-NY trustee = no NY tax" rule

## Probate

New York probate via Surrogate's Court:

- Moderately expensive; takes 6-18 months typically
- Kinship hearings (expensive and slow when relationships unclear)
- Will contests more common than in many states
- Surrogates in each county; NY County (Manhattan) especially busy

**Revocable trust widely used in NY** for privacy and probate avoidance, though not as cost-driven as CA.

## Intestacy

NY EPTL § 4-1.1:

- **Spouse + no issue:** all to spouse
- **Spouse + issue:** $50,000 + half to spouse; remainder to issue per stirpes
- **Issue, no spouse:** all to issue per stirpes
- No issue or spouse: parents, then siblings

Half-bloods treated as whole blood. Adopted children included. Stepchildren NOT unless adopted.

## Non-Community Property State

New York is common-law property. Spouses have separate ownership of what they earn/inherit. Marital property concepts arise primarily at divorce (equitable distribution).

## Power of Attorney — 2021 Reform

**Significant change:** NY POA law substantially reformed in 2021.

- New statutory short-form required for new POAs (post-June 2021)
- Old POAs still valid but cannot be amended
- Penalty for unreasonable third-party rejection — banks risk liability for refusing valid POA
- Two witnesses required now (one can be notary)
- "Statutory Gifts Rider" eliminated; gifting authority included in main form

### Implications
- POAs drafted before 2021 should be reviewed (though still valid)
- NY banks can no longer reject POAs without reason
- NY form is specific; generic POA forms may not work for NY property

## Healthcare Proxy and Living Will

- **Healthcare proxy** (POA for medical decisions) — Public Health Law Art. 29-C
- **Living will / advance directive** — no statutory form but widely honored
- NY uses **MOLST** (not POLST), with **eMOLST** electronic version

## Medicaid

New York specifics:

- **Protected-spouse resource rules are favorable, but not magic** — New York DOH still describes the allowance using the greater-of-minimum-or-spousal-share formula, not a literal "always full max CSRA" rule
- **Why the folklore exists:** when total countable resources are at or below the New York minimum protected amount, the community spouse may effectively keep all of them; above that, the analysis still runs through the minimum / half-share / federal-maximum framework
- **Spousal refusal is real, but procedural** — it can preserve Medicaid eligibility in the right case, yet information-sharing, assignment-of-support, and later contribution / recovery actions still matter
- **Nursing home lookback:** 60 months
- **Home-care lookback / implementation timing has shifted repeatedly** — do not rely on stale summaries; verify current New York Medicaid practice before presenting timing-sensitive recommendations
- **Medicaid Asset Protection Trusts (MAPTs)** widely used
- **NYC/Long Island Medicaid cost** among highest in nation: ~$15K-$20K/month

## Elective Share

NY EPTL § 5-1.1-A:

- Surviving spouse entitled to $50,000 or 1/3 of net estate (whichever greater)
- Can be waived by prenup / postnup
- Do not assume a revocable trust defeats the elective share; New York pulls many testamentary substitutes into the analysis, and the one-year transfer rule also matters

## Domicile

NY is notoriously aggressive about residence claims:

- **Statutory residence**: 183 days + permanent abode
- **Domicile**: subjective; home state of taxpayer's affection
- **Audit pattern**: NY regularly audits alleged move to FL/TX; must prove actual move

**Evidence required:**
- Sale of NY residence (or renting out)
- Driver's license, voter registration, vehicle registration in new state
- Doctors, clubs, religious affiliations moved
- Family in new state
- Mail forwarding
- Tax returns filed in new state

Partial-year residents still subject to NY tax on NY source income.

## Unique NY Features

### Closely-Held Business Valuation
- NY aggressive about business valuations at death
- Minority and marketability discounts scrutinized

### Art and Collectibles
- NY residents own significant art; valuation issues at death
- NY sales tax implications for art transfers

### Rent-Stabilized Apartments
- Tenant rights under succession law — family member can inherit occupancy
- Not a property interest but valuable

### Cooperative Apartments
- Board approval issues at death
- Proprietary lease structure

### NY Pets
- NY pet trusts are valid under EPTL § 7-8.1
- Up to reasonable amount (not extravagant)

## Recent Legislation / Updates

- POA reform (effective 2021)
- Direct New York QTIP election required on the New York estate tax return for decedents dying on or after April 1, 2019
- Home care Medicaid lookback delayed repeatedly; verify live status before relying on any countdown or transfer schedule
- Digital assets (RUFADAA) adopted

## Cost of Planning in New York

- Simple will: $1,500-$3,500
- Revocable trust package: $4,000-$10,000
- HNW / tax planning: $15K-$75K+
- NY attorneys tend to bill at higher rates

## Common Failure Modes in New York

1. **Estate just over cliff threshold** — massive tax; charitable bequest could have eliminated
2. **Old POA used after 2021** — some banks reject; newer standard applies
3. **Situs moved but resident-trust tax status not re-analyzed** — expected NY tax savings never materialize
4. **Domicile claim to FL not supported** — NY audits, reasserts residency
5. **3-year gift lookback ignored** — gifts added back to NY estate
6. **No NY-specific POA** — third-party rejection (historically; less now)
7. **Ignoring elective share** in estate plan — spouse claims 1/3
8. **Medicaid plan assumes a stale home-care lookback timeline** — New York implementation has shifted repeatedly; verify live rules before using countdown-style advice
9. **Rent-stabilized apartment treated as property interest** — not owned, not devisable
