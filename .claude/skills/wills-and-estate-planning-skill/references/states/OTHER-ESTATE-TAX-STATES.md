## Inheritance Tax States — Quick Matrix

| State | Current practical summary | Why it surprises people |
|-------|----------------------------|-------------------------|
| Kentucky | Class A beneficiaries exempt; Class B generally 4% to 16%; Class C generally 6% to 16% | Siblings are exempt, but nieces/nephews and many in-laws are not |
| Maryland | 10% on many non-exempt transfers | Maryland can hit a plan with both estate tax and inheritance tax |
| Nebraska | County-level tax; post-2023 structure generally uses 1% / 11% / 15% tiers with larger exemptions | Many advisors still quote the old pre-2023 Nebraska rules |
| New Jersey | Estate tax gone, inheritance tax remains for some classes | People remember the old estate tax but miss the current inheritance tax |
| Pennsylvania | 0% spouse, 4.5% lineal heirs, 12% siblings, 15% others | Children and grandchildren can still pay inheritance tax |

## State-by-State Notes

### Connecticut

- Official DRS page currently lists **$13.99M** for 2025 taxable gifts and estates.
- Connecticut remains the major outlier because it still has a meaningful **state gift-tax framework** integrated with the estate tax.
- If the recommendation turns on a Connecticut threshold, re-check the live DRS page because the value may be updated again.

### District of Columbia

- OTR's current published threshold found for deaths in **2025** is **$4,873,200**.
- The same page confirms a **10-month** filing/payment timeline for DC estate tax returns.
- Because the live 2026 booklet may not yet be posted when planning begins in 2026, log whether you are using the latest published 2025 number pending a 2026 update.

### Hawaii

- Hawaii's official July 1, 2025 tax outline shows a **$5.49M** exclusion amount.
- Hawaii also recognizes civil unions for state tax purposes, which can materially affect spousal planning.
- Non-citizen-spouse and QDOT issues should still be routed through live federal verification.

### Illinois

- Illinois' Attorney General materials continue to show a **$4.0M** exclusion amount.
- The Illinois exclusion is a **threshold**, not a credit. That makes the effective burden feel cliff-like in practice.
- Illinois portability does not rescue casual "everything to spouse" planning.

### Maine

- Maine Revenue Services now publishes a **2026** exclusion amount of **$7.16M**.
- Rates remain in the **8% to 12%** band.
- Maine second homes and coastal property often create unsuspected situs exposure for nonresidents.

### Maryland

- Maryland still has **both** estate tax and inheritance tax.
- MET-1 materials confirm a **$5.0M** DSUE cap and portability mechanics for Maryland estate tax, but that does not remove the need to analyze inheritance tax on non-exempt transfers.
- If a Maryland plan leaves meaningful property to non-close relatives, model both taxes explicitly.

### Minnesota

- Minnesota's official filing-threshold page continues to list **$3.0M** for **2020-present**.
- Minnesota also publishes its current federal threshold table on the same page, which is useful for side-by-side comparison.

### Oregon

- Oregon DOR continues to require filing at **$1.0M** gross-estate value where Oregon-taxable property is present.
- Oregon's threshold remains a major planning driver for ordinary middle- and upper-middle-class households.

### Rhode Island

- Rhode Island's Division of Taxation currently publishes a **2026** threshold of **$1,838,056**.
- RI's indexed thresholds change frequently enough that using an old memo is an avoidable error.

### Vermont

- The latest accessible Vermont official estate-tax instructions remain older, but the official materials indicate a **$5.0M** threshold for deaths on and after 2021.
- Treat Vermont as a state where a fresh live verification is required before a final client-facing conclusion.

## Inheritance Tax Detail

### Kentucky

- Kentucky DOR classifies spouse, parent, child, grandchild, brother, sister, and certain equivalent relatives as **Class A** and exempt.
- Class B beneficiaries generally receive a small exemption and then face **4% to 16%** rates.
- Class C beneficiaries generally receive a smaller exemption and then face **6% to 16%** rates.

### Maryland

- Maryland inheritance tax generally runs at **10%** on taxable transfers to non-exempt beneficiaries.
- Close-relative exemptions matter. Do not summarize Maryland with a single headline if the beneficiary classes differ.

### Nebraska

- Nebraska inheritance tax is county-level, but the Nebraska DOR legislative summary is the cleanest official reference point for the modern rate structure.
- For decedents dying on or after January 1, 2023, the DOR summary reflects:
  - close relatives: **1%** over a **$100,000** exemption,
  - remote relatives: **11%** over a **$40,000** exemption,
  - nonrelatives: **15%** over a **$25,000** exemption.
- Beneficiaries under age 22 are not subject to Nebraska inheritance tax.

### New Jersey

- New Jersey's official tax page states that **no estate tax is imposed for deaths on or after January 1, 2018**.
- Inheritance tax still applies depending on beneficiary classification.
- Where the beneficiary lives is not the key variable; the beneficiary's relationship to the decedent is.

### Pennsylvania

- Pennsylvania's current official materials summarize the rates as:
  - **0%** spouse and certain parent/minor-child transfers,
  - **4.5%** direct descendants and lineal heirs,
  - **12%** siblings,
  - **15%** other heirs.
- Early payment discounts still matter in administration planning.

## Cross-State Failure Modes

1. Assuming federal portability solves the state problem.
2. Using stale state thresholds from an old planner memo.
3. Ignoring situs taxation for out-of-state real estate.
4. Forgetting that Connecticut gift tax is part of the state transfer-tax system.
5. Missing Pennsylvania inheritance tax on children.
6. Missing Maryland's two-tax problem.
7. Using pre-2023 Nebraska inheritance-tax rates.
8. Treating Vermont numbers as static without live verification.
