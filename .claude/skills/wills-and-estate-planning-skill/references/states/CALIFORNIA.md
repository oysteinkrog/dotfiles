# California Estate Planning

## Headline Posture

- **No state estate tax; no state gift tax; no state inheritance tax**
- **Very aggressive state income tax (13.3% top rate)** — hits trust income in many cases
- **Slow/expensive probate** — revocable trust widely used
- **Community property** state
- **Medi-Cal rules changed materially again starting January 1, 2026** — asset limits returned for certain Non-MAGI populations, and LTC transfer-review rules phase back in over 2026-2028; transfer and estate-recovery advice must be verified from current DHCS guidance

## Estate and Gift Tax

- **State estate tax:** none (repealed 1982)
- **Gift tax:** none
- **Inheritance tax:** none
- **Pick-up tax:** no (even federal pick-up tax is $0)

California is a favorable state for estate taxation. The issue is income tax.

## California Income Tax on Trusts

California trust-tax exposure usually turns on:
- **Residence of trustee(s)**
- **Residence of non-contingent beneficiary(ies)**
- **California-source income**

The apportionment rules are technical. Do not reduce this to a bumper sticker. Use the current FTB Form 541 guidance when the allocation matters.

**Planning posture:**
- Move trust situs to no-tax state (SD, NV, DE) with out-of-state trustee
- **Kaestner v. NC Dept of Revenue (2019)** is relevant background, but do not assume it cleanly neutralizes California's trust-income reach
- California now treats ING / NING / DING planning much more aggressively than older marketing materials suggest; verify the current FTB treatment before presenting any such strategy as viable for a California resident
- Domicile planning to move grantor out of CA before liquidity event

## Probate

- **Expensive:** statutory fees (personal representative + attorney) based on gross estate value: 4% of first $100K, 3% of next $100K, 2% of next $800K, 1% of next $9M, 0.5% of next $15M
- **Example:** $1M estate → $23,000 + $23,000 = $46,000 total statutory fees
- **Slow:** typically 9-24 months minimum
- **Public:** records publicly accessible

**Why revocable trusts are standard in CA:**
- Avoids all statutory probate fees
- Privacy
- Faster settlement

**Small estate procedures:**
- Affidavit / simplified-transfer procedures apply only below the current California small-estate thresholds; verify the current Judicial Council maximum values and Probate Code route before relying on a dollar amount
- Spousal property petition for assets passing to spouse

## Community Property

CA is a community property state. Property acquired during marriage is community (half each spouse's) unless gift/inheritance.

### Implications
- **Double step-up in basis** at first death (both halves stepped up) — major benefit
- **Divorce:** 50/50 split of community property
- **Estate planning:** only each spouse's half is theirs to devise
- **Creditor rights:** community property subject to either spouse's debts (usually)

### Transmutation
Spouses can convert:
- Separate → community
- Community → separate
- Requires written transmutation agreement with specific formalities

## Homestead

California homestead exemption:
- Indexed statutory band; verify the current amount rather than relying on an older range
- Protects against some creditors in bankruptcy

## Intestate Succession (No Will)

California Probate Code § 6401-6402:

**Community property:** all to surviving spouse

**Separate property:**
- Spouse + no kids: all to spouse
- Spouse + 1 kid: 1/2 to spouse, 1/2 to kid
- Spouse + 2+ kids: 1/3 to spouse, 2/3 to kids
- No spouse: kids equally
- No spouse, no kids: parents, then siblings, etc.

**Note:** CA recognizes parentage through many modern paths (assisted reproduction, same-sex marriage, etc.).

## Power of Attorney

- California Probate Code §§ 4000 et seq. governs
- California has a statutory short-form (optional)
- Third-party acceptance issue is real; some banks prefer their own form
- Springing POA valid but often fails in practice (same problems as elsewhere)
- RUFADAA-type language needed for digital assets

## Advance Healthcare Directive (AHCD)

California has a specific statutory form:
- California Probate Code §§ 4600 et seq.
- Combines healthcare-agent appointment with advance-instruction provisions; it is not the same thing as a POLST
- Can specify organ donation, autopsy, disposition of remains

**POLST form:** California's POLST is specific and widely used for seriously ill patients.

## Medi-Cal (California Medicaid) — Verify Current DHCS Rules

California's Medi-Cal planning landscape changed materially in 2024 and again in 2026. Do not rely on older California shorthand.

**Current posture to verify from DHCS:**
- Asset limits were eliminated in 2024 but reinstated effective **January 1, 2026** for certain Non-MAGI populations
- DHCS currently states the 2026 asset limit as **$130,000 for one person plus $65,000 for each additional household member** for the affected programs
- For nursing-home situations, DHCS now warns that transfers made on or after **January 1, 2026** can trigger LTC transfer penalties, but the review window phases in over time rather than jumping instantly to a full 30-month review; DHCS guidance indicates the full 30-month review applies to LTC applications / entries on or after **July 1, 2028**
- Estate recovery still matters, but for deaths on or after **January 1, 2017** California generally limits recovery to **probate assets** and specified long-term-care-related benefits, subject to statutory exceptions

**Planning implications:**
- Do not repeat the old "California has no asset test" summary without checking the current eligibility category
- Do not repeat the old "California has no lookback" summary in long-term-care cases; the transfer-review regime is back, but the implementation is phased and timing-sensitive
- California does **not** use Florida/Texas-style Lady Bird deeds; use California-specific tools such as revocable trusts, community-property-with-right-of-survivorship titling, TOD deeds where appropriate, and probate-aware estate-recovery planning
- California planning is still often more favorable than the old pre-reform regime, but it is no longer safe to treat Medi-Cal as a simplified no-asset-limit system

## Digital Assets (RUFADAA)

California adopted the Revised Uniform Fiduciary Access to Digital Assets Act (2016). Include RUFADAA language in POA, will, trust.

## Elective Share

California does NOT have an elective share statute (unique among common-law states — because CA is community property).

**Instead:** omitted spouse doctrine under Probate Code § 21610 — if spouse omitted from will executed before marriage, spouse receives intestate share.

## Unique Features

### Transfer on Death (TOD) Deed for Real Estate
- California added TOD deed in 2016
- **Available under a statute scheduled to remain operative only until January 1, 2032 unless extended again**; deeds executed before that date remain effective under the current statute
- Useful for modest estates avoiding trust

### Small Estate Affidavit
- Personal property can be collected by affidavit only if the estate is under the current Probate Code / Judicial Council threshold
- Simple, fast, cheap

### Probate-Avoidance Alternatives
- Revocable trust (most common)
- Joint tenancy with right of survivorship
- Community property with right of survivorship
- TOD deed for real estate
- POD for bank accounts
- TOD for brokerage

### Professional Fiduciaries
California has robust industry of licensed professional fiduciaries — alternative to family trustee.

## Recent Legislation to Watch

- **SB 131 (2023):** anti-NING/DING measures
- **AB 1755 (2023):** Medi-Cal asset limit elimination
- **Electronic wills / remote-execution legislation:** verify current enactment status before assuming California recognizes e-wills
- **Ongoing:** cannabis trust planning (still federally illegal, so planning complexity)

## Cost of Planning in California

- Simple will package: $1,500-$3,000 (flat fee)
- Revocable trust package: $3,500-$8,000
- Complex HNW planning: $10K-$100K+
- Trust administration: varies; statutory fee schedule available but negotiable

## Common Failure Modes in California

1. **No revocable trust** — family hit with statutory probate fees on $1M estate: $46K+
2. **Trust created but not funded** — defeats purpose; goes to probate anyway
3. **California resident with out-of-state trust** — CA still taxes grantor
4. **NING/DING trust for CA resident** — closed by SB 131
5. **Moving out of CA without following through** — CA reasserts residence
6. **Missing the community-property basis-adjustment opportunity** by retitling community property incorrectly
7. **Ignoring Medi-Cal reform** — following old complex planning advice
8. **Missing TOD deed opportunity** — forces probate for simple real-estate-only estate
