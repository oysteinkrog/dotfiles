# Brenner Operators (13 Total)

Sydney Brenner's signature research moves, operationalized.

## 1. Model Organism Selection

> "The choice of the experimental object remains one of the most important things to do in biology."

Choose the simplest system that preserves the phenomenon. C. elegans for development, E. coli for genetics. In software: pick the minimal reproduction case.

## 2. Reduction to One Dimension

> "I always like to reduce things to one dimension... 'Let me get this right: A turns on B, B turns on C.'"

Strip away complexity until you have a linear causal chain. If you can't draw it as A→B→C, you don't understand it yet.

## 3. Third Alternative Injection

> "We proposed three models... 'You've forgotten there's a third alternative.' 'What's that?' 'Both could be wrong.'"

When presented with A vs B debates, always ask: could both be wrong? What would a completely different framing look like?

## 4. The Potency Test (Chastity vs Impotence)

> "It's not that it doesn't bind, it's that it can't bind to anything."

Distinguish between "didn't happen" (negative result, possibly meaningful) and "couldn't happen" (broken experiment, uninformative). Always include positive controls.

## 5. Reconstruction Criterion

> "You have not explained a phenomenon until you can specify how to build it from primitives."

Description and prediction are not understanding. Can you write the code that generates the behavior? Can you build the circuit?

## 6. Working Out of Phase

> "The best thing in science is to work out of phase."

When everyone is doing X, do Y. Contrarian positioning creates opportunity for novel discovery.

## 7. Exclusion Over Confirmation

> "Exclusion is always a tremendously good thing in science."

Design experiments to kill hypotheses, not confirm them. A theory that survives elimination is stronger than one with supporting evidence.

## 8. Machine Language Simulation

> "A proper simulation must be done in the machine language of the object being simulated."

Simulate at the right level of abstraction. Simulating neurons with differential equations may miss the computational logic. Simulate in the native "instruction set."

## 9. The Mistress Principle

> "One should not fall in love with one's theories. They should be treated as mistresses to be discarded once the pleasure is over."

Emotional attachment to hypotheses is a scientific liability. Be ready to abandon any theory when evidence demands it.

## 10. Decisive Experiments

> "The experiment should be designed so that whatever answer it gives will be informative."

Design experiments where every outcome teaches something. Avoid experiments where "no result" is uninformative.

## 11. Anomaly Quarantine

> "Put the anomalies in a box, label it, and come back later."

Don't let unexplained data paralyze progress. Document anomalies explicitly, acknowledge them, but don't let them block forward motion.

## 12. Scale and Physics Checks

> "Before you explain how something works, check if it could work at all."

Verify biophysical plausibility. Check timescales, energy budgets, diffusion limits. Many theories fail basic physics.

## 13. Discriminative Predictions

> "A hypothesis is only as good as the predictions that distinguish it from alternatives."

Enumerate predictions that differentiate competing hypotheses. Shared predictions don't help. Only discriminative predictions matter for testing.
